## ACT FOUR.

@stage (At Lutetia in Gaul. A hall in Caesar's palace «The Warm Baths» outside the city. Entrance door at the background; to the right another, smaller door; in front, on the left side, is a window with a curtain.)

@stage (Princess Helena, richly adorned, with pearls in her hair, sits in an armchair and looks out through the window. Myrrha the slave-girl stands above her and holds the curtain aside.)

@ Princess Helena.
What a throng! The whole city streams out to meet them. – Hush! Myrrha, – do you not hear flutes and drums?

@ Myrrha the slave-girl.
Yes, I do think –

@ Princess Helena.
You are lying! The din is too loud; you can hear nothing. *(springs up.)* Oh, this tormenting uncertainty! Not to know whether he comes as victor or as fugitive.

@ Myrrha the slave-girl.
Do not be anxious, my mistress; Caesar has always come as victor, after all.

@ {{{ 146 }}} Princess Helena.
Yes, before; after all the smaller clashes. But this time, Myrrha! This great, terrible battle. All these conflicting rumours. If Caesar had come as victor, why should he then have sent that letter to the city magistrates and forbidden them to meet him with marks of honour outside the gates?

@ Myrrha the slave-girl.
Oh, you know, mistress, how little your exalted husband strives after such things.

@ Princess Helena.
Yes, yes, that is true enough. And if he had suffered some defeat – in Rome they would surely have to know it – would the emperor then have sent us this envoy, who may well be here this very day, and whose express courier has brought me all these rich jewels and gifts? Ah, Eutherius! Well, well?

@ Eutherius the steward *(from the background)*.
My princess, it is quite impossible to learn anything reliable –

@ Princess Helena.
Impossible? You deceive me! The soldiers themselves must surely know –

@ Eutherius the steward.
They are only barbarian auxiliary troops that are marching in, – Batavians and others, – and they know nothing.

@ {{{ 147 }}} Princess Helena *(wrings her hands)*.
Oh, have I deserved this anguish! Sweet, holy Christ, have I not called upon you night and day –

*(listens and cries out.)*

Ah, my Julian! I hear him! – Julian; my beloved!

@ Caesar Julian *(in dusty armour, comes quickly from the background)*.
Helena!

@ Eutherius the steward.
My exalted Caesar!

@ Caesar Julian *(embraces the princess with vehemence)*.
Helena! – Bar all the doors, Eutherius!

@ Princess Helena.
Beaten! Pursued!

@ Eutherius the steward.
My lord!

@ Caesar Julian.
Double guards at the doors; let no one in! Listen; has any envoy come from the emperor?

@ Eutherius the steward.
No, my lord; but an envoy is expected.

@ Caesar Julian.
Go, go! *(to the slave-girl)* Away with you.

@stage (Eutherius and Myrrha go out at the background.)

@ {{{ 148 }}} Princess Helena *(sinks down into the armchair)*.
So it is all over with us, then!

@ Caesar Julian *(draws the curtain together)*
Who knows. Only be wary, and the storm may yet –

@ Princess Helena.
After such a defeat –?

@ Caesar Julian.
Defeat? What are you talking about, my beloved?

@ Princess Helena.
Have the Alemanni not beaten you?

@ Caesar Julian.
Had they beaten me, you would not have seen me alive again.

@ Princess Helena *(springs up)*.
But, Lord of heaven, what has happened, then?

@ Caesar Julian *(softly)*.
The worst, Helena; – an immeasurable victory.

@ Princess Helena.
A victory, you say! An immeasurable victory? You have conquered, and yet –?

@ Caesar Julian.
You cannot grasp how I stand. You know only the golden outer side of all a Caesar's misery.

@ {{{ 149 }}} Princess Helena.
Julian!

@ Caesar Julian.
Can you blame me for having concealed such things from you? Did not both duty and shame bid me –? Ah, – what is *that*? What a change –!

@ Princess Helena.
What? What?

@ Caesar Julian.
What a change in you these months! Helena, have you been ill?

@ Princess Helena.
No, no; but tell me –?

@ Caesar Julian.
Yes, you have been ill! You must be ill still; – these fever-hot temples, these blue-brown rings about the eyes –

@ Princess Helena.
Oh, nothing, my beloved! Do not look at me, Julian! Only anguish and night-watches for your sake; burning prayers to the most highly blessed one upon the tree of the cross –

@ Caesar Julian.
Spare yourself, you precious one; it is, after all, quite uncertain whether this zeal bears so much fruit.

@ Princess Helena.
Fie; your mind is not pious. – But speak of your own affairs, Julian! I beg you, conceal nothing from me.

@ {{{ 150 }}} Caesar Julian.
There *can* be nothing concealed any longer. Since the empress's death I have not been able to take a single step here in Gaul without its being ill-interpreted at the court. If I went cautiously to work against the Alemanni, then it was said that I was timorous and inactive. They mocked at the lover of wisdom, who could not rightly accustom himself to bearing war-armour. If I won an advantage over the barbarians, then I had to hear that I ought to have been able to achieve more.

@ Princess Helena.
But all your friends in the army –

@ Caesar Julian.
Who do you think are my friends in the army? Not *one*, my beloved Helena! Yes, a single one, – that Perusian knight, Sallust, to whom, during our wedding festivities in Milan, I had to refuse a modest request. He has magnanimously come to me in the camp, has reminded me of our old friendship in Athens and begged to be allowed to follow me into all dangers. But what does Sallust count for at the imperial court? He is, after all, one of those whom they there call heathens. He can profit me nothing. – And then the others! The commander Arbetio, who left me in the lurch when I lay shut up in Sennones! Old Severus, who is weighed down under the feeling of his own incompetence, and who yet cannot reconcile himself to my new conduct of war! Or do you think I can lean upon {{{ 151 }}} Florentius, – the praetorian prefect? I tell you, this restless man strives after the highest things.

@ Princess Helena.
Ah, Julian!

@ Caesar Julian *(walks up and down)*.
If only I could get on the track of their intrigues! Every week secret letters go from the camp to Rome. Everything I undertake is reported and distorted. No thrall in the realm is fettered like Caesar. Do you know, Helena, that the bill of fare my cook has to abide by, it has been sent to him from the emperor, and I am not permitted to alter anything in it, neither to add nor to take away!

@ Princess Helena.
And all this you have borne secretly –!

@ Caesar Julian.
Everyone knows it, except you. Everyone mocks at Caesar's impotence. I will bear it no longer! I will not bear it!

@ Princess Helena.
But the great battle –? Tell me, though, – has the rumour exaggerated –?

@ Caesar Julian.
The rumour has not *been able* to exaggerate. – Hush; what was that? *(listens toward the door.)* No, no; I only thought –.

{{{ 152 }}} I dare say that in these months I have accomplished all that stood within human power. Step by step, and in spite of all hindrances in my own camp, I drove the barbarians back toward the eastern frontier. Before Argentoratum, with the Rhine at his back, King Knodomar drew all his fighting forces together. Five kings and ten lesser princes joined him. But before he had managed to gather the boats needful for crossing in case of need, I let my army advance to the attack.

@ Princess Helena.
My hero, my Julian!

@ Caesar Julian.
Lupicinus with the javelin-throwers and the light-armed went round the enemy in the north; the old legions under Severus drove the barbarians more and more eastward toward the river; the Batavians, our confederates, under the faithful Bainabaudes, stood honourably by the legions, and when Knodomar perceived that all was in peril, he sought to escape toward the south to reach over to the islands. But before that could happen I let Florentius meet him with the praetorians and the horsemen. Helena, I do not care to say it aloud, but certain it is that treachery or envy had nearly robbed me of the fruits of victory. The Roman riders gave way time after time before the barbarians, who threw themselves to the ground and stabbed the horses in the belly. I saw our defeat before my eyes –

@ {{{ 153 }}} Princess Helena.
But the God of battles was with you!

@ Caesar Julian.
I seized a banner, fired the imperial household troops with my shouts, held in all haste a speech to them, a speech that perhaps would not have been quite unworthy even of a more enlightened circle of hearers, and thereupon, as soon as the soldiers' shouts of applause had rewarded me, I plunged straight into the thickest tumult of battle.

@ Princess Helena.
Julian! Oh, you do not love me!

@ Caesar Julian.
In that moment you were not in my thoughts. I wished to die; I knew no other way out. But it came, my beloved! It was as if a lightning-striking terror flashed out of our lance-points. I saw Knodomar, that terrible warrior – yes, you have indeed seen him yourself – I saw him flee on foot from the battle, and with him fled his brother Vestralp and the kings Hortar and Suomar and all who did not fall beneath our swords.

@ Princess Helena.
Oh, I see it; I see it! Blessed saviour; it was you who again sent forth the angels of murder from the Milvian bridge!

@ Caesar Julian.
Never have I heard such cries of woe; never have I seen such gaping wounds as those we trod in, {{{ 154 }}} as we waded over the fallen. The river did the rest; the drowning fought among themselves until they rolled over and went to the bottom. Most of the princes fell alive into our hands; Knodomar himself had sought refuge in a clump of reeds; one of his companions betrayed him, our men sent a rain of arrows in toward the hidden one, but without striking him. Then he came forward of his own free will and gave himself up.

@ Princess Helena.
And after such a victory you should not feel secure?

@ Caesar Julian *(hesitating)*.
After the victory, that same evening, there occurred an incident, a trifle –

@ Princess Helena.
An incident?

@ Caesar Julian.
I would rather call it so. In Athens we brooded so much over Nemesis. – My victory was so overwhelmingly great, Helena; my position had, as it were, come out of equilibrium; I do not know –

@ Princess Helena.
Oh, speak then; you frighten me!

@ Caesar Julian.
It was a trifle, I tell you. I had the captive Knodomar led before me in the sight of the army. Before the battle he had threatened that I {{{ 155 }}} should be flayed alive when I fell into his hands. Now he came toward me with uncertain steps, trembling over his whole body; broken by misfortune, in such a way as is the manner of barbarians, he cast himself down before me, clasped my knees, poured out tears and begged for his life.

@ Princess Helena.
With a shudder of terror through his powerful limbs. I see the prostrate Knodomar. – Did you kill him, my beloved?

@ Caesar Julian.
I could not kill this man. I assured him of safety and promised to send him as a prisoner to Rome.

@ Princess Helena.
Without torturing him?

@ Caesar Julian.
Prudence bade me act mildly. But then, – I cannot grasp how it came about, – in overflowing joy, and with a howl upon his lips, the barbarian sprang up, stretched his bound hands into the air and, little versed as he was in our tongue, cried with a loud voice: praised be you, Julian, you mighty emperor!

@ Princess Helena.
Ah!

@ Caesar Julian.
My companions were about to laugh; but the barbarian king's cry struck down like a kindling lightning among {{{ 156 }}} the clusters of soldiers. «Long live emperor Julian», cried the bystanders; and the cry spread in wider and wider rings to the remotest distance; – it was as if a Titan had hurled a mountain out into the world-ocean; – oh, my beloved, forgive me this heathen simile, but –

@ Princess Helena.
Emperor Julian! He said: emperor Julian!

@ Caesar Julian.
What did the rude Aleman know of Constantius, whom he had never seen? I, his vanquisher, was to him the greatest –

@ Princess Helena.
Yes, yes; but the soldiers –?

@ Caesar Julian.
I rebuked them sharply; for I saw it well, – Florentius, Severus and certain others stood about in silence, white with terror and wrath.

@ Princess Helena.
Yes, yes, – *they*, but not the soldiers.

@ Caesar Julian.
Scarcely had a single night run to an end before my secret enemies had distorted the matter. Caesar has let Knodomar proclaim him emperor, it was said, and in return he has granted the barbarian king his life. – Thus, turned upside down, it has also been reported to Rome.

@ {{{ 157 }}} Princess Helena.
Are you sure of it? And by whom?

@ Caesar Julian.
Yes, by whom? By whom? I myself wrote in haste to the emperor and told him the whole of it, but –

@ Princess Helena.
Well, – and what has he answered?

@ Caesar Julian.
As usual. You know that ill-boding silence when he means to strike someone.

@ Princess Helena.
Yet I think you misinterpret all this. It cannot be otherwise. See now; the envoy will soon bring you assurance that –

@ Caesar Julian.
I *have* assurance, Helena! Here on my breast I keep intercepted letters, which –

@ Princess Helena.
Oh, Lord my God, let me see!

@ Caesar Julian.
Later, later.

*(he walks up and down.)*

And all this after the services I have shown him! Here I have cast the Alemanni's onslaught to the ground for long times to come, while he himself has suffered defeat upon defeat at the Danube and while the army in Asia does not seem to gain a single step's advance {{{ 158 }}} against the Persians. Shame and ill-luck on all sides, except here, where a reluctant friend of wisdom was set at the head of affairs. And yet I am mocked all the same at the court. Yes, even after the last great victory they have made a lampoon on me and called me Victorinus! This must have an end.

@ Princess Helena.
Yes, I think so too.

@ Caesar Julian.
What is a Caesar's dignity on such terms!

@ Princess Helena.
No, you are right, Julian; we cannot remain standing at this.

@ Caesar Julian *(stops)*.
Helena, could you follow me?

@ Princess Helena *(softly)*.
Fear not for me; I shall not draw back.

@ Caesar Julian.
Away, then, from all this thankless toil; away to the so long coveted solitude –!

@ Princess Helena.
What do you say? Solitude!

@ Caesar Julian.
With you, my beloved; and with my dear books, which here I have so seldom been able to open, {{{ 159 }}} and which I have only dared to devote my restless nights to.

@ Princess Helena *(looks down at him)*.
Ah, so!

@ Caesar Julian.
What else?

@ Princess Helena.
Well yes; what else?

@ Caesar Julian.
Yes, yes, – I ask, what else?

@ Princess Helena *(closer)*.
Julian, – how did the barbarian king greet you?

@ Caesar Julian *(drawing back)*.
Helena!

@ Princess Helena *(closer again)*.
What name was it that resounded in the soldiers' ranks?

@ Caesar Julian.
Imprudent one; here perhaps a listener stands outside every door!

@ Princess Helena.
What do you fear from listeners? Is not God's grace over you? Have you not been the fortunate one in all encounters? – I see the admonishing saviour; I see the angel with the flaming sword, who made a way for my father when he cast Maxentius into the Tiber!

@ {{{ 160 }}} Caesar Julian.
I should rise up against the ruler of the realm!

@ Princess Helena.
Only against those who stand between you two. Oh, go, go; smite them with the lightning of your wrath; make an end of this consuming, joyless life! Gaul is a wasteland. I freeze here, Julian! I want to go home again, to the sun's warmth, to Rome and Hellas.

@ Caesar Julian.
Home again to your brother?

@ Princess Helena *(softly)*.
Constantius is decrepit.

@ Caesar Julian.
Helena!

@ Princess Helena.
I can endure it no longer, I tell you. Time passes. Eusebia is gone; her empty seat stands beckoning toward honour and glory while I grow old –

@ Caesar Julian.
You do not grow old; you are young and fair!

@ Princess Helena.
No, no, no! Time passes; I cannot bear this with patience; life is slipping from me!

@ Caesar Julian *(looks at her)*.
How temptingly fair, how godlike you are!

@ {{{ 161 }}} Princess Helena *(clings to him)*.
*Am* I that, Julian?

@ Caesar Julian *(embraces her)*.
You are the only woman I have loved, – the only one who has loved me.

@ Princess Helena.
I am older than you. I will grow no older. When all is over, then –

@ Caesar Julian *(tears himself loose)*.
Be still! I will hear no more.

@ Princess Helena *(goes after him)*.
Constantius dies a little each day; he hangs by a hair over the grave. Oh, my beloved Julian, you have the soldiers on your side, after all –

@ Caesar Julian.
Hush, hush!

@ Princess Helena.
He can bear no agitations of the mind. What, then, is there to shrink back from? I mean nothing bloody, after all. Fie, how could you think it? The terror will be enough; it will take him into its arms and lovingly end his sufferings.

@ Caesar Julian.
Do you forget the invisible bodyguard about the anointed one?

@ {{{ 162 }}} Princess Helena.
Christ is good. Oh, be pious, Julian, and he will forgive much. I will help. Prayers shall be sent up for you. Praised be the holy men! Praised be the blood-witnesses! Believe me, we shall atone for everything afterward. Grant me the Alemanni for conversion; I will send out priests among them; they shall bow down beneath the grace of the cross.

@ Caesar Julian.
The Alemanni do not bow down under it.

@ Princess Helena.
Then they shall die! Like a sweet smoke shall the blood rise up to him, the most highly blessed. We will increase his glory; his praise shall be proclaimed through us. I will be there myself. The Alemannic women for me! If they do not bow, then they shall be sacrificed! And then, my Julian, – when you see me again –; made young again, made young again! Grant me the Alemannic women, you beloved! Blood –, it is no murder, after all, and the means shall be unfailing –, a bath in young virgin-blood –

@ Caesar Julian.
Helena, you are sinning!

@ Princess Helena.
Is it a crime to sin for your sake?

@ Caesar Julian.
You fair one, you only one!

@ {{{ 163 }}} Princess Helena *(bends down over his hands)*.
My lord before God and men! – Do not yield this time, Julian! My hero, my emperor! I see heaven open. The priests shall sing praises to Christ; my women shall gather in prayer. *(with uplifted arms.)* Oh, you blessed one! Oh, you God of hosts, – you who hold grace and victory in your hand –

@ Caesar Julian *(with a glance toward the door, cries out)*:
Helena!

@ Princess Helena.
Ah!

@ Eutherius the steward *(from the background)*.
My lord, the emperor's messenger –

@ Caesar Julian.
Has he come?

@ Eutherius the steward.
Yes, my lord!

@ Caesar Julian.
His name? Who is he?

@ Eutherius the steward.
Decentius the tribune.

@ Princess Helena.
Really? The pious Decentius!

@ Caesar Julian.
Whom has he spoken with?

@ {{{ 164 }}} Eutherius the steward.
With no one, my lord; he came this very moment.

@ Caesar Julian.
I will see him at once. And listen; one thing more. The generals and the commanders shall present themselves to me.

@ Eutherius the steward.
Good, most gracious sire!

*(he goes out at the background.)*

@ Caesar Julian.
Now, my Helena, now it will show itself –

@ Princess Helena *(softly)*.
Whatever shows itself, do not forget that you can rely on the soldiers.

@ Caesar Julian.
Ah, rely, rely –; I do not know whether I can rely on anyone.

@stage (Decentius the tribune comes from the background.)

@ Princess Helena *(toward him)*.
Welcome, noble Decentius! A Roman face, – and above all, this face, – oh, it lays a living gleam of sunshine over our unkind Gaul.

@ Decentius the tribune.
The emperor meets your longing and your hope halfway, exalted princess! We may believe that Gaul shall not fetter you much longer.

@ {{{ 165 }}} Princess Helena.
Do you say so, you bringer of joy? The emperor thinks of me always with love, then? How is it with his health?

@ Caesar Julian.
Go, go, my beloved Helena!

@ Decentius the tribune.
The emperor's health has by no means worsened.

@ Princess Helena.
No, is it not so? That was what I knew. All these alarming rumours –; God be praised that it was only rumours! Thank him most lovingly, you pious Decentius! And be thanked yourself. With what rich gifts have you not heralded your arrival! Imperial gifts –; no, no, brotherly gifts in truth! Two shining black Nubians, – you should see them, my Julian! – and pearls! Those I am wearing already. And fruits, – sweet, swelling; ah; peaches from Damascus, peaches in golden bowls! How they will refresh me; – fruit, fruit; I languish here in Gaul.

@ Caesar Julian.
A feast of joy shall end the day; but business first. Go, my precious wife!

@ Princess Helena.
I go to the church, – to pray for my brother and for all hopes.

*(she goes out to the right.)*

@ {{{ 166 }}} Caesar Julian *(after a moment's pause)*.
Message or dispatches?

@ Decentius the tribune.
Dispatches.

*(he hands him a roll of paper.)*

@ Caesar Julian *(reads, suppresses a smile and stretches out his hand)*.
More!

@ Decentius the tribune.
Exalted Caesar, that is about all.

@ Caesar Julian.
Really? Has the emperor sent his friend the long way only to –?

*(he bursts into a short laugh; then he walks up and down.)*

Had the Aleman king Knodomar come to Rome before your departure?

@ Decentius the tribune.
Yes, exalted Caesar!

@ Caesar Julian.
And how does he manage in the foreign land, unversed in the language as he is? Yes, he is most unversed, Decentius! He was downright a laughing-stock for my soldiers. Just think, he confused such common words as emperor and Caesar.

@ Decentius the tribune *(shrugs his shoulders)*.
A barbarian. What is there to say?

@ {{{ 167 }}} Caesar Julian.
No, what is there to say? But the emperor is gracious to him, though?

@ Decentius the tribune.
Knodomar is dead, my lord!

@ Caesar Julian *(stops)*.
Knodomar is dead?

@ Decentius the tribune.
In the strangers' camp on the Caelian hill.

@ Caesar Julian.
Dead? So? – Yes, the air in Rome is unhealthy.

@ Decentius the tribune.
The Aleman king died of homesickness, my lord! The longing for kin and freedom –

@ Caesar Julian.
– it consumes, Decentius; yes, yes, I know it. – I should not have sent him living to Rome; I should have had him killed here.

@ Decentius the tribune.
Caesar's mind is mild.

@ Caesar Julian.
Hm –! Homesickness? Indeed!

*(To Sintula the master of the horse, who comes from the background.)*

Are you there, old faun? Tempt me no more. *(to Decentius.)* Ever since the battle of Argentoratum he is always talking to me of the victory-chariot and the white team. *(to Sintula.)* It became Phaethon's ride with {{{ 168 }}} the bright sun-horses. How did it end? Have you forgotten – have you forgotten your heathenism, I was about to say. – Forgive me, Decentius, that I wound your pious ear.

@ Decentius the tribune.
Caesar tickles his servant's ear; but he does not wound it.

@ Caesar Julian.
Yes, yes; bear with Caesar if he jests. I really do not know how to take it otherwise. – There we have them.

@stage (Severus the general, Florentius the praetorian prefect, together with several commanders and gentlemen of Caesar's court come from the background).

@ Caesar Julian *(goes to meet them)*.
Good morning, you brothers in arms and friends! Do not be too vexed that I call you straight here from the dirt and toil of the highroad; I should surely have granted you some hours' rest; but –

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
Has anything particular happened, my lord?

@ Caesar Julian.
Yes, in truth, there is. Can you tell me, – what was lacking in Caesar's fortune?

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
What should be lacking in Caesar's fortune?

@ Caesar Julian.
*Now* nothing. *(to Decentius.)* The army has demanded that I should hold a victory-entry into the city. At the head {{{ 169 }}} of the legions they wished that I should ride in through Lutetia's gates. Captive barbarian princes with bound hands were to walk by the wheels of the chariot; women and slaves from twenty conquered peoples were to follow after, closely crowded, head by head – *(breaks off suddenly.)* Rejoice, my brave fellow-fighters; here you see Decentius the tribune, the emperor's confidential friend and counsellor. He came this morning hour with gifts and greetings from Rome.

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
Ah, then surely nothing is lacking in Caesar's fortune.

@ Severus the general *(softly to Florentius)*.
Inconceivable! So once more in the emperor's favour!

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect *(softly)*.
Oh, this fickle emperor!

@ Caesar Julian.
It seems as if a wondering muteness had struck you all. – They find that the emperor has done too much, good Decentius!

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
How can such a thing come into Caesar's mind?

@ Severus the general.
Too much, exalted Caesar? By no means. Should the emperor not know how to choose the right bounds for his grace?

@ {{{ 170 }}} Florentius the praetorian prefect.
To be sure, this is a great and rare distinction –

@ Severus the general.
I would call it exceedingly great and rare.

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
And in particular it gives a shining testimony that our exalted emperor's mind is free of all envy –

@ Severus the general.
A testimony without precedent; that I may well say.

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
But what, then, has Caesar not also accomplished in these few years in Gaul?

@ Caesar Julian.
A year-long dream, you precious friends! I have accomplished nothing at all. Nothing, nothing!

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
And your modesty reckons that as nothing? What was the army when you took it over? A disordered rabble –

@ Severus the general.
– without cohesion, without obedience, without leadership –

@ Caesar Julian.
Exaggeration, Severus!

@ {{{ 171 }}} Florentius the praetorian prefect.
And was it not with this unbridled rabble that you went against the Alemanni; did you not beat them with these bands, which through victories you transformed into an army of victors; did you not take Colonia Agrippina back –?

@ Caesar Julian.
Ah, ah, you see with friendship's eye, my Florentius! – Or is it really true? Is it so, that I drove the barbarians away from the islands in the Rhine? That I set the decayed Tres Tabernae in a state of defence for the good of the realm's security? Is it really so?

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
How, my lord? Can you doubt such great things?

@ Caesar Julian.
No, in truth, I think –. And the battle of Argentoratum? Was I not there? It hovers before me so surely that I was victorious over Knodomar. And after the victory –; Florentius, have I dreamed; or did I not build Trajan's fort up again, when we marched into the Germanic regions?

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect.
Exalted Caesar, is there any man so insane that he would dispute you this honour?

@ {{{ 172 }}} Severus the general *(to Decentius)*.
I praise fate, which granted me in my old days to follow so fortunate a leader.

@ Florentius the praetorian prefect *(likewise to the tribune)*.
What turn the Alemannic assault might have taken without Caesar's valour and prudence, I scarcely dare to think of.

@ Several courtiers *(press forward).*
Yes, my lord, Caesar is great!

@ Other gentlemen *(clap their hands)*.
Caesar is without equal!

@ Caesar Julian *(looks for a while by turns at Decentius and the others; then he bursts out in a loud short laugh)*.
So blind is friendship, Decentius! So blind, blind!

*(he turns to the others and strikes the roll of paper that he holds in his hand.)*

Here stand quite other things! Listen, and drink it in like a refreshing dew of recognition. It is the emperor's announcement to all the governors round about in the realm; – our excellent Decentius has brought a copy with him. Here it stands: I have accomplished nothing in Gaul. It was a dream, as I just said. Here we have the emperor's own words: it was under the emperor's fortunate auspices that the peril overhanging the realm was averted.

@ {{{ 173 }}} Florentius the praetorian prefect.
All the affairs of the realm enjoy success under the emperor's auspices.

@ Caesar Julian.
More, more! Here it is reported that it is the *emperor* who has fought and conquered at the Rhine; it is the emperor who has raised up the humbly pleading Aleman king who cast himself down before him. *My* name I do not succeed in finding anywhere in this document, – nor yours, Florentius, nor yours either, Severus!
