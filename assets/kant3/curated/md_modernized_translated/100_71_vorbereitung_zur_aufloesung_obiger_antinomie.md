## {{ 317 }} § 71. Preparation for the resolution of the above antinomy

We can in no way prove that organized products of nature cannot be produced by nature's mere mechanism, for we lack any insight into the first inner ground of the endless multiplicity of the particular laws of nature — laws that, being known to us only through experience, remain contingent for us — and hence we simply cannot reach the inner, thoroughly sufficient principle of the possibility of a nature (which lies in the supersensible). Hence whether nature's productive power extends just as far to what we judge to be shaped or linked according to the idea of ends as it does to that for which we believe a mere machinery of nature suffices; and whether in fact, for things taken as genuine natural ends (as we are bound to judge them), an entirely different sort of original causality — one that could not possibly reside within material nature or within its intelligible substrate, namely an architectonic understanding — lies at the {{{ 389 }}} basis of them: about all this our reason, so narrowly restricted where the concept of causality is concerned, once that concept is to be specified _a priori_, can yield no information whatever. — But that, in relation to our faculty of cognition, the mere mechanism of nature can likewise supply no ground of explanation for the generation {{ 318 }} of organized beings is just as indubitably certain. ***For the reflecting power of judgment*** this is therefore a wholly correct principle: that, for the so manifest connection of things according to final causes, a causality distinct from mechanism must be thought, namely that of a world-cause acting according to ends (an intelligent one); however rash and indemonstrable it would ***be for the determining*** power of judgment. In the first case this counts only as a maxim of the power of judgment, where the concept of such a causality is nothing but an idea, to which one does not at all venture to concede reality, using it merely as a guiding thread for reflection — which throughout stays receptive to every mechanical explanatory ground and does not lose itself out of the sensible world; in the second case the principle would be an objective principle, dictated by reason, under which the power of judgment would have to place itself determinately, though in doing so it would pass beyond the sensible world and lose itself in the extravagant, and might well be led astray.

Every semblance of an antinomy between the maxims of the strictly physical (mechanical) mode of explanation and the teleological (technical) one therefore rests on this: that one mistakes a principle belonging to the reflecting power of judgment for one belonging to the determining power, and the ***autonomy*** of the former (which is valid merely subjectively, for our exercise of reason regarding particular {{ 319 }} laws of experience) for the ***heteronomy*** of the latter, which has to conform to the laws — general or particular — supplied by the understanding.
