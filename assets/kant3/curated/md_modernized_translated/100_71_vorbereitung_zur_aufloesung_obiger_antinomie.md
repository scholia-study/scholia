## {{ 317 }} § 71. Preparation for the Solution of the Above Antinomy

We can by no means prove the impossibility of the generation of organized natural products through the mere mechanism of nature, because we do not see into the first inner ground of the infinite manifoldness of particular laws of nature, which are contingent for us because they are only empirically cognized, and thus we can absolutely not reach the inner, thoroughly sufficient principle of the possibility of a nature (which lies in the supersensible). Whether, therefore, the productive faculty of nature does not suffice just as well for that which we estimate as formed or connected according to the idea of ends as for that for which we believe we require merely a mechanical arrangement of nature; and whether, in fact, there lies at the basis of things as actual natural ends (as we must necessarily estimate them) a completely different kind of original causality which cannot be contained at all in material nature or in its intelligible substrate, namely an architectural understanding, as its {{{ 389 }}} ground: on this our reason, which is very narrowly restricted in regard to the concept of causality when it is to be specified _a priori_, can absolutely give no information. — But that, in relation to our cognitive faculty, the mere mechanism of nature can also provide no ground of explanation for the generation {{ 318 }} of organized beings is just as undoubtedly certain. ***For the reflecting power of judgment***, therefore, this is a completely correct principle: that for the so obvious connection of things according to final causes, a causality distinct from mechanism, namely that of a world-cause acting according to ends (an intelligent one), must be thought; however rash and unprovable it would be ***for the determining one***. In the former case, it is a mere maxim of the power of judgment, wherein the concept of that causality is a mere idea which one by no means undertakes to concede reality to, but rather uses only as a guide for reflection, which thereby always remains open to all mechanical grounds of explanation and does not lose itself from the world of sense; in the second case, the principle would be an objective principle which reason prescribes and to which the power of judgment would have to subject itself determiningly, whereby, however, it loses itself beyond the world of sense into the transcendent and is perhaps led astray.

All appearance of an antinomy between the maxims of the properly physical (mechanical) and the teleological (technical) modes of explanation thus rests on this: that one confounds a principle of the reflecting power of judgment with that of the determining one, and the ***autonomy*** of the former (which holds merely subjectively for our use of reason in regard to the particular {{ 319 }} empirical laws) with the ***heteronomy*** of the latter, which must conform to the (general or particular) laws given by the understanding.
