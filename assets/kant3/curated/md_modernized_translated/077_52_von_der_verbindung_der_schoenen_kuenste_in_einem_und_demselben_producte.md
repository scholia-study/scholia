## § 52. On the combination of the fine arts in one and the same product

Eloquence, in a ***play***, admits of union with a pictorial rendering of its subjects and no less of objects; poetry unites with music in ***song***, and song, in turn, with pictorial (theatrical) staging in an ***opera***; while in a ***dance*** the play of sensations within music is matched with the play of figures — and so on. So too the presentation of the sublime, so far as it counts as fine art, may be joined with beauty in a ***rhymed tragedy***, a ***didactic poem***, or an ***oratorio*** {{ 214 }}; and though such couplings render fine art still more ingenious, whether they render it more beautiful as well — given that so many differing sorts of satisfaction here intersect — may in several of these instances be called into question. In all fine {{{ 326 }}} art, however, what is essential resides in the form that proves purposive for observing and appraising it — where the pleasure is at the same time culture and tunes the spirit toward ideas, thereby rendering it open to yet more such pleasure and diversion — and not in the matter of sensation (in charm or emotion), where the aim is mere enjoyment, which leaves nothing lasting in the idea, blunts the spirit, comes to sicken one of the object by degrees, and, through the mind's awareness of a disposition that reason's judgment finds counter-purposive, turns it discontented with itself and capricious.

Should the fine arts fail to be linked, whether nearly or distantly, to moral ideas — which alone bring with them a satisfaction that stands on its own — then this latter outcome proves their final destiny. Thereafter they merely furnish diversion, a need that grows all the greater the more one resorts to it so as to banish the mind's discontent with itself — and one thereby only grows ever more useless and still more discontented with oneself. On the whole, nature's beauties best serve that first purpose when one is trained from early on {{ 215 }} to watch them, to appraise them, and to admire them.
