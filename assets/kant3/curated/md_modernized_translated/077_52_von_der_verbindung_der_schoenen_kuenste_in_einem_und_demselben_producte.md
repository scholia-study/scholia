## § 52. On the combination of the fine arts in one and the same product

Eloquence can be combined with a painterly presentation of its subjects as well as of objects in a ***play***, poetry with music in ***song***, and the latter in turn together with painterly (theatrical) presentation in an ***opera***, the play of sensations in a piece of music with the play of shapes in ***dance***, etc. The presentation of the sublime, too, insofar as it belongs to fine art, can unite itself with beauty in a ***rhymed tragedy***, a ***didactic poem***, an ***oratorio*** {{ 214 }}; and in these combinations fine art is even more artful: whether it is also more beautiful (since so many diverse kinds of satisfaction cross one another), can, in some of these cases, be doubted. Yet in all fine {{{ 326 }}} art the essential thing lies in the form, which is purposive for observation and judging, where the pleasure is at once culture and attunes the spirit to ideas, and consequently makes it receptive to more of such pleasure and entertainment; not in the matter of sensation (charm or emotion), where it is aimed merely at enjoyment, which leaves nothing behind in the idea, dulls the spirit, gradually renders the object loathsome, and, through the mind's consciousness of its own disposition — counter-purposive in the judgment of reason — makes it dissatisfied with itself and moody.

If the fine arts are not brought, closely or remotely, into connection with moral ideas, which alone carry with them a self-subsistent satisfaction, then the latter is their ultimate fate. They then serve only for distraction, of which one becomes ever more in need the more one avails oneself of it in order to drive away the mind's dissatisfaction with itself, by making oneself still more useless and still more dissatisfied with oneself. In general the beauties of nature are most conducive to the former aim, if one is accustomed early on {{ 215 }} to observe, judge, and admire them.
