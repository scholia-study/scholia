## § 52. On the combination of the beautiful arts in one and the same product

Rhetoric can be combined with a painterly presentation of its subjects as well as of objects in a ***play***, poetry with music in ***song***, but this in turn with a painterly (theatrical) presentation in an ***opera***, the play of sensations in a piece of music with the play of shapes in ***dance***, etc. Also, the presentation of the sublime, insofar as it belongs to beautiful art, can unite itself with beauty in a ***rhymed tragedy***, a ***didactic poem***, an ***oratorio*** {{ 214 }}; and in these combinations beautiful art is even more artful: but whether it is also more beautiful (since so many manifold different kinds of satisfaction cross one another) can be doubted in some of these cases. Yet in all beautiful {{{ 326 }}} art the essential consists in the form, which is purposive for observation and judging, where the pleasure is at the same time culture and attunes the spirit to ideas, hence makes it receptive to more such pleasure and entertainment; not in the matter of the sensation (in charm or emotion), where it is laid out merely for enjoyment, which leaves nothing behind in the idea, makes the spirit dull, the object gradually loathsome, and the mind dissatisfied with itself and moody through the consciousness of its disposition, which is contrary to purpose in the judgment of reason.

If the beautiful arts are not brought, closely or remotely, into combination with moral ideas, which alone carry with them a self-sufficient satisfaction, then the latter is their final fate. They then serve only for diversion, of which one becomes ever more needful the more one makes use of it in order thereby to drive away the dissatisfaction of the mind with itself, by which one makes oneself ever more useless and more dissatisfied with oneself. In general the beauties of nature are most conducive to the first aim, if one is early {{ 215 }} accustomed to observe, to judge, and to admire them.
