## {{ 44 }} § 15. The judgment of taste is entirely independent of the concept of perfection

***Objective*** purposiveness can be cognized only by means of the relation of the manifold to a determinate end, hence only through a concept. From this alone it already becomes clear: that the beautiful, the judging of which has a merely formal purposiveness, i.e., a purposiveness without an end, at its ground, is entirely independent of the representation of the good, because the latter presupposes an objective purposiveness, i.e., the relation of the object to a determinate end.

Objective purposiveness is either the outer, i.e., the ***usefulness***, or the inner, i.e., the ***perfection*** of the object. That the satisfaction in an object, on account of which we call it beautiful, cannot rest on the representation of its usefulness can be sufficiently seen from both preceding sections: because then it would not {{{ 227 }}} be an immediate satisfaction in the object, which latter is the essential condition of the judgment about beauty. But an objective inner purposiveness, i.e., perfection, comes nearer to the predicate of beauty and has therefore also been held to be identical with beauty by reputable philosophers, though with the addition ***when it is thought confusedly {{ 45 }}***. It is of the greatest importance, in a critique of taste, to decide whether beauty too can really be resolved into the concept of perfection.

To judge objective purposiveness we always need the concept of an end and (if that purposiveness is to be not an outer one but an inner one) the concept of an inner end that contains the ground of the inner possibility of the object. Now just as an end in general is that whose ***concept*** can be regarded as the ground of the possibility of the object itself: so, in order to represent an objective purposiveness in a thing, the concept of ***what sort of thing it is supposed to be*** will go first; and the accordance of the manifold in it with this concept (which gives the rule for its combination in it) is the ***qualitative perfection*** of a thing. From this the ***quantitative***, as the completeness of every thing in its kind, is entirely distinct and a mere concept of magnitude (of allness), in which ***what the thing is supposed to be*** is already thought of in advance as determined and it is only asked whether ***everything*** requisite for it is present in it. The formal in the representation of a thing, i.e., the accordance of the manifold into one (undetermined what it is supposed to be), gives {{ 46 }} of itself absolutely no objective purposiveness to be cognized: because, since one abstracts from this one ***as an end*** (what the thing is supposed to be), nothing remains but the subjective purposiveness of the representations in the mind of the one intuiting, which indeed indicates a certain purposiveness of the representational state in the subject and in this a comfort of the subject in apprehending a given form into the imagination, but no perfection of any object that is here thought through no concept of an end. As, e.g., when I come upon a lawn in the forest around which the trees stand in a circle, and I do not thereby represent to myself an end, namely that it is supposed to serve perhaps for rustic dancing, not the least concept of {{{ 228 }}} perfection is given through the mere form. But to represent to oneself a formal ***objective*** purposiveness without an end, i.e., the mere form of a ***perfection*** (without all matter and ***concept*** of that with which the accordance is made, even if it were merely the idea of a lawfulness in general), is a true contradiction.

Now the judgment of taste is an aesthetic judgment, i.e., one that rests on subjective grounds, and whose determining ground cannot be a concept, hence also not that of a determinate end. Thus by means of beauty, as a formal subjective purposiveness, there is by no means thought a perfection of the object as an allegedly {{ 47 }} formal yet nevertheless objective purposiveness; and the distinction between the concepts of the beautiful and the good, as if both were distinguished only in logical form, the first being merely a confused, the second a distinct concept of perfection, but otherwise identical in content and origin, is null: because then there would be no ***specific*** distinction between them, but a judgment of taste would be just as much a cognitive judgment as the judgment by which something is declared good; just as, for instance, the common man, when he says that deceit is wrong, grounds his judgment on confused, the philosopher his on distinct, but at bottom both on the same principles of reason. But I have already noted that an aesthetic judgment is unique of its kind and gives absolutely no cognition (not even a confused one) of the object: which latter happens only through a logical judgment; whereas the former relates the representation through which an object is given solely to the subject and gives one to notice no constitution of the object, but only the purposive form in the determination of the powers of representation that occupy themselves with it. The judgment is also called aesthetic precisely because its determining ground is no concept but the feeling (of inner sense) of that unanimity in the play of the powers of mind, insofar as it can only be felt. By contrast, if one wanted to call confused concepts and the objective {{ 48 }} judgment that has them at its ground aesthetic, one would have an understanding that judges sensibly, or a sense that represented its objects through concepts, both of which contradict themselves. The faculty of concepts, whether they be confused or distinct, is the understanding; and although understanding also belongs to the judgment of taste, as an aesthetic judgment (as it does to all judgments), it belongs to it {{{ 229 }}} not, however, as a faculty of the cognition of an object, but as a faculty of the determination of the judgment and its representation (without a concept) according to the relation of the latter to the subject and its inner feeling, and indeed insofar as this judgment is possible according to a universal rule.
