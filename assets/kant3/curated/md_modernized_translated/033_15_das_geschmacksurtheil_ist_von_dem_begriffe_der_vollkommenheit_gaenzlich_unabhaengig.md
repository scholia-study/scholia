## {{ 44 }} § 15. The judgment of taste is entirely independent of the concept of perfection

***Objective*** purposiveness is something we can cognize solely through the manifold's relation to some determinate end, and thus solely by way of a concept. This by itself already makes clear: that the beautiful—whose judging has as its basis a purposiveness that is merely formal, that is, a purposiveness devoid of any end—stands wholly apart from the representation of the good, since this latter presupposes a purposiveness that is objective, that is, the object's relation to some determinate end.

Objective purposiveness comes in two forms: the external kind, that is, ***usefulness***, and the internal kind, that is, an object's ***perfection***. That the satisfaction whereby we pronounce an object beautiful cannot be founded upon any representation of its usefulness is sufficiently evident from the two foregoing sections: for were it otherwise, the satisfaction would then not {{{ 227 }}} be an immediate one taken in the object, and it is immediacy of just this kind that forms the indispensable condition of any judgment about beauty. An internal objective purposiveness, however—that is, perfection—already lies nearer to the predicate of beauty, and for that reason renowned philosophers have even equated it with beauty, albeit under the proviso ***when it is confusedly conceived {{ 45 }}***. For a critique of taste, it is a matter of the utmost importance to settle whether beauty too may genuinely be reduced to the concept of perfection.

To judge an objective purposiveness we invariably need the concept of an end, and—supposing this purposiveness is meant to be internal rather than external [usefulness]—the concept of an inner end that would hold the ground of the object's inner possibility. Since an end as such is precisely that whose ***concept*** may be taken for the very ground on which the object's own possibility rests: so too, if one is to picture an objective purposiveness in a given thing, there must come first the concept of ***what kind of thing it is intended to be***; and the accord of the manifold within that thing with this concept (which lays down the rule for combining the manifold in it) makes up the ***qualitative perfection*** of a thing. Wholly different from this is ***quantitative*** perfection, namely the completeness that any thing possesses within its own kind—a bare concept of quantity (of allness), one in which ***what the thing is to be*** is already presupposed as settled beforehand, so that the only question is whether ***everything*** demanded for it is actually there in it. The formal aspect in the representation of a thing—that is, the agreement of the manifold into a unity (with what that unity is to be left undetermined)—discloses {{ 46 }} of itself no objective purposiveness whatsoever: for once abstraction is made from this unity taken ***as an end*** (what the thing is to be), there is left over nothing beyond the subjective purposiveness belonging to the representations in the mind of the one who beholds them, a purposiveness that does indeed betoken a certain purposiveness of the representational state within the subject, and within that state an ease it feels in taking up a given form into the imagination, yet that points to no perfection of any object, the object being thought here by means of no concept of an end. So, for instance, should I chance upon a patch of turf in the woods, ringed about by trees standing in a circle, and yet form for myself no end there—say, that the spot might be used for country dancing—then not the slightest concept of {{{ 228 }}} perfection is furnished by the bare form alone. But to form for oneself a formal ***objective*** purposiveness that has no end—that is, the naked form of a ***perfection*** (stripped of all matter and of any ***concept*** of that toward which the harmonizing is directed, even were this nothing more than the idea of a conformity to law in general)—is a downright contradiction.

Now the judgment of taste is an aesthetic judgment, that is, one resting upon subjective grounds, whose determining ground can be no concept at all, and therefore not the concept of a determinate end. Hence beauty, as a formal subjective purposiveness, in no way brings to mind any perfection of the object under the guise of an allegedly {{ 47 }} formal, yet for all that objective, purposiveness; and the difference between the concept of the beautiful and that of the good—as though the two were set apart merely by logical form, the former a confused and the latter a distinct concept of perfection while otherwise agreeing in content and origin—amounts to nothing: for on that supposition no ***specific*** difference would separate them, and a judgment of taste would count as a cognitive judgment just as fully as the judgment whereby a thing is pronounced good, much as the common man, when he calls deceit wrong, grounds his judgment on confused, and the philosopher his on distinct, yet both at bottom on one and the same principles of reason. Yet I have already remarked that an aesthetic judgment is one of a kind and delivers no cognition at all (not even a confused one) of the object: the latter coming about only through a logical judgment, whereas an aesthetic judgment instead refers the representation through which an object is given entirely to the subject and calls attention to no property of the object, but solely to the purposive form in the determination of the powers of representation that busy themselves with that object. It is for just this reason, too, that the judgment is termed aesthetic: because what determines it is no concept but rather the feeling (belonging to the inner sense) of that harmony in the play of the mental powers, so far as such harmony admits only of being felt. Were one, on the other hand, to give the name aesthetic to confused concepts and to the objective {{ 48 }} judgment that rests upon them, one would be positing an understanding that judges by way of the senses, or else a sense that pictured its objects through concepts—each of which is a contradiction in itself. The power that deals in concepts, be they confused or distinct, is the understanding; and although the judgment of taste, as an aesthetic judgment, does call upon the understanding too (as every judgment does), it draws on it {{{ 229 }}} not as the power to cognize an object, but as the power to determine the judgment together with its representation (apart from any concept) according to the bearing this has upon the subject and the subject's inner feeling—and this only so far as such a judgment is possible under a universal rule.
