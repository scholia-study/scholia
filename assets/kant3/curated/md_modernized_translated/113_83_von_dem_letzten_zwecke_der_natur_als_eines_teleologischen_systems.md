## {{ 388 }} § 83. On the ultimate end of nature as a teleological system

In what preceded, we demonstrated that, on principles of reason, we have cause enough to appraise the human being—not merely as a natural end, in the way that every organized being is one, but here on earth as the ***ultimate*** end of nature, in relation to which all the rest of nature's things make up a system of ends—valid, to be sure, not for the determining but only for the reflecting power of judgment. Now if what is to be advanced as an end by way of his connection with nature has to be met with in the human being himself, then either this end must be {{{ 430 }}} of such a sort that nature, through its benevolence, can bring it to satisfaction of itself, or it lies in the fitness and skill for ends of every kind, toward which nature may be put to use by him (outwardly and inwardly). Nature's first end would be the human being's ***happiness***, its second his ***culture***.

Happiness is not a concept that the human being somehow abstracts from his instincts, thereby lifting it out of the animality within him; rather, it is a mere ***idea*** of a condition to which, under conditions that are merely empirical (which is impossible), {{ 389 }} he would have the latter conform. He sketches it out for himself, and indeed in such varied ways, through his understanding as entangled with imagination and the senses; and he alters this understanding so often that nature, even were it wholly subject to his choice, could nonetheless adopt no determinate, universal, and fixed law by which to accord with this fluctuating concept and thus with the end that each person arbitrarily sets before himself. Yet even were we, on the one side, to bring this down to the genuine need of nature, in respect of which our species is throughout at one with itself, or, on the other, to heighten to any degree whatever the skill of furnishing oneself with imagined ends, that which a human being means by happiness—and which is in truth his own ultimate natural end (an end not of freedom)—would nonetheless never be reached by him; since his nature is not such as to halt at any point in possession and enjoyment and there arrive at satisfaction. On the contrary, it is so far from true that nature has adopted him as its peculiar darling and, above all the animals, showered benefits upon him, that in its destructive workings—plague, famine, peril of flood, frost, the onslaught of other creatures large and small, and suchlike—it spares him just as little as any other animal; and worse still, that the perversity of the ***natural predispositions*** in him casts him into self-contrived torments, and casts yet others of his own kind, through the pressure of {{ 390 }} lordship, the savagery of wars, and so on, into like distress, while he for his part, so far as lies in him, toils at the ruin of his own kind—so that even were the nature outside us as benevolent as could be, its end, had it been fixed upon the happiness of our species, would go unattained in any system of it on earth, because the nature within us has no receptivity for it. He therefore remains forever no more than one link within the chain of natural ends: a principle, admittedly, in regard to many an {{{ 431 }}} end for which nature, by its arrangement, appears to have destined him, since he makes himself into that; but withal also a means toward preserving purposiveness in the mechanism of the remaining links. Being the sole creature on earth endowed with understanding, and hence with a capacity to set ends for itself at will, he is indeed the titular lord of nature and, if one regards nature as a teleological system, by his vocation the ultimate end of nature; but always only conditionally, namely that he understand it and have the will to give to nature and to himself such a relation of ends as can be sufficient unto itself independently of nature, and hence be a final end—one that, however, need not be sought in nature at all.

But to discover in the human being where, at the least, we ought to place that ***ultimate*** end of nature, we must single out whatever {{ 391 }} nature has it in its power to accomplish in readying him for what falls to him alone to do if he is to be a final end, and set this apart from all those ends the possibility of which depends on conditions that nature by itself may be counted upon to supply. To this latter sort belongs earthly happiness, meaning the totality of all those ends of his that nature makes possible both outside and within the human being; such is the matter of every end he has on earth, and should he make it his entire end, it deprives him of the capacity to appoint a final end for his own existence and to bring himself into accord with it. Of all his ends in nature, then, there is left over only the formal, subjective condition—namely that of the fitness to set himself ends at all and (independently of nature in the way he determines his ends) to make use of nature as a means suited to the maxims of his free ends generally—which is precisely what nature is able to bring about in view of the final end that lies beyond it, and which may accordingly count as nature's ultimate end. ***Culture*** is the name for the bringing-forth of a rational being's fitness for ends of any sort whatsoever (and hence in its freedom). Culture alone, accordingly, is capable of being the ultimate end that we have reason to ascribe to nature in regard to the human species (not the human being's own earthly happiness, and still less his serving merely as the chief tool for founding order {{ 392 }} and unanimity in the reasonless nature outside him).

But not every culture is sufficient for this ultimate end of nature. That of ***skill*** is, to be sure, the chief subjective condition of a fitness for advancing ends at large; yet {{{ 432 }}} it does not thereby suffice to advance the ***will*** in the determining and choosing of its ends—something that nonetheless belongs essentially to a fitness for ends taken in its full compass. This latter condition of fitness—which one could designate the culture of training (discipline)—has a negative character, residing in freeing the will from the despotism of the desires; for, riveted to certain natural things, we are thereby made unable to make our own choice, since we allow the drives to serve us as shackles that nature appended to us merely in the stead of guiding threads, in order that we not neglect, or even do harm to, the vocation of the animality within us, though we remain free enough to draw them tighter or to relax them, to prolong or to curtail them, in keeping with what the ends of reason call for.

Skill can hardly be developed in the human species otherwise than by means of inequality among human beings: for the greatest number provide, as it were mechanically and without requiring any special art for it, the necessities of life for the comfort and leisure of others, who work at the less necessary parts of culture, science {{ 393 }} and art, and are kept by these in a condition of oppression, bitter labor, and little enjoyment—a class to which, however, much of the culture of the higher one does gradually spread as well. The plagues, however, mount up as that progress advances (its summit, once the leaning toward the superfluous starts to curtail the indispensable, going by the name of luxury), growing on both sides with equal force—on the one from the violence of others, on the other from an inner want of contentment; yet this resplendent wretchedness does after all go together with the unfolding of the natural predispositions in the human species, and nature's own end, granted it is not an end of ours, is for all that reached in the process. That formal condition whereby nature is able, and only so, to reach this ultimate intention of hers is the sort of constitution among human beings in their mutual relations in which a lawful power within a whole—termed ***civil society***—is opposed to the mutual curtailment of their conflicting freedoms; for it is within such a whole alone that the fullest unfolding of the natural predispositions can come about. For this, however, there would still be required—even were human beings clever enough to discover it and wise enough to submit willingly to its constraint—a ***cosmopolitan*** whole, that is, a system of all states that are in danger of acting detrimentally upon one another. Where such a whole is wanting, and in the face of the obstacle that {{{ 433 }}} the cravings for honor, for mastery, and for possessions—chiefly among those {{ 394 }} in whose hands power lies—throw up against the mere possibility of any such plan, ***war*** (in part the kind wherein states fracture and break up into smaller ones, in part the kind wherein one state strives to annex others lesser than itself and to shape a greater whole) becomes inescapable: war which, however much it is an unintended attempt on the part of human beings (spurred by ungoverned passions), is yet a deeply concealed and perhaps designed attempt of the highest wisdom—to prepare, if not indeed to establish, lawfulness together with the freedom of states, and by that route the unity of a system of them founded on morality, and which, for all the most dreadful afflictions it lays upon the human race, and the perhaps greater ones still that the unceasing readiness for it imposes in time of peace, remains nevertheless one more spring driving all the talents that serve culture to their highest pitch of development (even as the hope for the tranquility of a people's happiness withdraws ever farther off).

As for the discipline of the inclinations—for which the natural predisposition is, in view of our vocation as an animal species, quite purposive, but which greatly hinder the development of humanity—here too, in respect of this second requirement for culture, there shows itself a purposive striving of nature toward a cultivation that makes us receptive to higher ends than nature itself can supply. The preponderance of the ills that the refinement of taste, carried to the point of its {{ 395 }} idealization, and even luxury in the sciences, as a nourishment for vanity, pour out over us through the insatiable multitude of the inclinations thereby engendered, is not to be disputed; but on the other hand the end of nature is likewise not to be mistaken—to win ever more ground away from the rawness and vehemence of those inclinations that belong more to the animality in us and are most opposed to our cultivation toward our higher vocation (the inclinations of enjoyment), and to make room for the development of humanity. Fine art and the sciences, which through a pleasure that admits of universal communication, and through polish and refinement for society, make human beings, if not morally better, then at least civilized, win a great deal away from the tyranny of sensuous propensity and thereby prepare human beings for a sovereignty in which reason alone is to hold power: while the ills with which nature on the one part, and the incompatible selfishness of human beings on the other, afflict us at the same time summon up, heighten, and steel the powers of the soul {{{ 434 }}} not to succumb to those ills, and thus let us feel a fitness for higher ends that lies hidden within us.[^*]

[^*]: It is easily decided what worth life holds for us if this worth is reckoned solely by ***what one enjoys*** (happiness, that is, the natural end formed by the aggregate of all our inclinations). It falls below zero; for who would care to take up life once more on the very same terms, or even by a fresh plan of one's own devising (yet conformable to nature's course) that was, however, likewise pitched merely toward enjoyment? What worth life has according to what it contains within itself, when conducted in accordance with the end that nature has with us, and which consists in ***what one does*** (not merely enjoys)—where we are, however, always only a means to an indeterminate final end—has been shown above. Nothing else, then, is likely left over but the value we ourselves confer upon our life by means of what we not merely do, but do in a manner so purposive and so free of nature that the very existence of nature can count as an end on this condition alone.
