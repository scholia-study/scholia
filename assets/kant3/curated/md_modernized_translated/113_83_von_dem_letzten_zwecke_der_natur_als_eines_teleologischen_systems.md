## {{ 388 }} § 83. On the ultimate end of nature as a teleological system

We have shown in the foregoing that we have, according to principles of reason, cause sufficient for judging the human being not merely, like all organized beings, as a natural end, but also, here on earth, as the ***ultimate*** end of nature, in relation to which all the other natural things constitute a system of ends—sufficient, to be sure, not for the determining, but for the reflecting power of judgment. If now that must be encountered in the human being himself which, as an end, is to be promoted through his connection with nature: then either the end {{{ 430 }}} must be of such a kind that it can itself be satisfied by nature in its beneficence; or it is the aptitude and skill for all sorts of ends for which nature (externally and internally) could be used by him. The first end of nature would be the ***happiness***, the second the ***culture*** of the human being.

The concept of happiness is not one that the human being, say, abstracts from his instincts and so derives from the animality in himself; rather it is a mere ***idea*** of a state to which he wants to make the latter adequate under merely empirical conditions (which is impossible). {{ 389 }} He projects it for himself, and indeed in so various a way through his understanding entangled with imagination and the senses; he even alters it so often that nature, even if it were entirely subjected to his choice, could yet absolutely adopt no determinate, universal, and fixed law in order to agree with this wavering concept and so with the end that each arbitrarily sets for himself. But even if we wanted either to reduce this to the true need of nature, in which our species throughout agrees with itself, or on the other hand to raise ever so high the skill for procuring imagined ends: yet what the human being understands by happiness, and what is in fact his own ultimate natural end (not end of freedom), would never be attained by him; for his nature is not of such a kind as to stop and be satisfied anywhere in possession and enjoyment. On the other hand, it is so far from being the case that nature has taken him up as its special favorite and favored him with beneficence above all animals, that it has rather spared him as little as any other animal in its destructive effects, in plague, hunger, danger from water, frost, attack by other large and small animals, and the like; even more, however, that the perversity of the ***natural predispositions*** in him puts him into self-devised plagues and yet others, by the pressure of {{ 390 }} domination, the barbarity of wars, etc., into such distress at the hands of his own species, and he himself, as far as it is in him, labors at the destruction of his own species, that, even with the most beneficent nature outside us, the end of it, if it were set on the happiness of our species, would not be attained in a system of it on earth, because the nature within us is not receptive to it. He is therefore always only a member in the chain of natural ends: a principle, to be sure, with regard to many an {{{ 431 }}} end to which nature seems to have determined him in its predisposition, in that he makes himself so; but yet also a means to the preservation of the purposiveness in the mechanism of the other members. As the only being on earth that has understanding, hence a faculty of arbitrarily setting ends for itself, he is indeed entitled the lord of nature and, if one regards nature as a teleological system, by his vocation the ultimate end of nature; but always only conditionally, namely on condition that he understand it and have the will to give to it and to himself such a relation to an end as can be self-sufficient independently of nature, hence be a final end—which, however, must not be sought at all in nature.

But in order to find out wherein, in the human being, we have at least to set that ***ultimate*** end of nature, we must seek out that which {{ 391 }} nature is capable of performing in order to prepare him for what he himself must do in order to be a final end, and we must separate it from all those ends whose possibility rests on conditions that one may expect from nature alone. Of the latter kind is happiness on earth, by which is understood the sum total of all the ends of the human being possible through nature outside and within him; that is the matter of all his ends on earth, which, if he makes it into his entire end, makes him incapable of setting a final end for his own existence and of harmonizing therewith. There thus remains, of all his ends in nature, only the formal, subjective condition, namely that of the aptitude for setting ends for himself at all and (independently of nature in his determination of ends) for using nature as a means appropriate to the maxims of his free ends in general—which nature can accomplish with a view to the final end that lies outside it and which can therefore be regarded as its ultimate end. The production of the aptitude of a rational being for arbitrary ends in general (consequently in his freedom) is ***culture***. Thus only culture can be the ultimate end that one has cause to ascribe to nature with regard to the human species (not his own happiness on earth, or even merely his being the foremost instrument for establishing order {{ 392 }} and concord in the nature without reason outside him).

But not every culture is sufficient for this ultimate end of nature. That of ***skill*** is indeed the foremost subjective condition of aptitude for the promotion of ends in general; but {{{ 432 }}} yet it is not sufficient to promote the ***will*** in the determination and choice of its ends, which yet essentially belongs to the whole extent of an aptitude for ends. The latter condition of aptitude, which one could call the culture of training (discipline), is negative and consists in the liberation of the will from the despotism of the desires, by which we, attached to certain natural things, are made incapable of choosing for ourselves, in that we let the drives serve us as fetters which nature has given us only in place of guiding threads, in order that we should not neglect or even injure the vocation of the animality in us, while we are yet free enough to tighten or relax them, to lengthen or shorten them, according as the ends of reason require.

Skill cannot well be developed in the human species except by means of inequality among human beings: since the greatest number provide for the necessity of life, as it were mechanically, without needing any special art for it, for the comfort and leisure of others who work at the less necessary parts of culture, science {{ 393 }} and art, and are kept by these in a state of oppression, bitter labor, and little enjoyment, although to this class too something of the culture of the higher gradually also spreads. But the plagues grow, in the progress of culture (the peak of which, when the propensity for the dispensable begins to do harm to the indispensable, is called luxury), on both sides with equal force, on the one side through alien violence, on the other through inner insatiability; yet this glittering misery is bound up with the development of the natural predispositions in the human species, and the end of nature itself, even if it is not our end, is yet thereby attained. The formal condition under which alone nature can attain this its final aim is that constitution in the relation of human beings to one another in which lawful force in a whole, which is called ***civil society***, is opposed to the harm of the mutually conflicting freedom; for only in it can the greatest development of the natural predispositions take place. For this same, however, even if human beings were clever enough to discover it and wise enough to submit themselves willingly to its coercion, there would yet be required also a ***cosmopolitan*** whole, i.e., a system of all states that are in danger of acting detrimentally upon one another. In the lack of this, and given the hindrance {{{ 433 }}} which ambition, lust for domination, and avarice, chiefly among those {{ 394 }} who have force in their hands, oppose even to the possibility of such a project, ***war*** (partly that in which states split apart and dissolve into smaller ones, partly that in which one state unites others, smaller ones, with itself and strives to form a greater whole) is unavoidable: which, just as it is an unintentional (provoked by unbridled passions) attempt of human beings, yet a deeply hidden, perhaps intentional attempt of the highest wisdom, if not to establish, yet to prepare lawfulness together with the freedom of states and thereby unity of a morally grounded system of them, and which, notwithstanding the most frightful tribulations with which it afflicts the human race, and the perhaps still greater ones with which the constant readiness for it oppresses in peacetime, is nevertheless one more incentive (while the hope for the resting place of a national happiness recedes ever further) to develop to the highest degree all the talents that serve culture.

As concerns the discipline of the inclinations, for which the natural predisposition is, with a view to our vocation as an animal species, quite purposive, but which greatly impede the development of humanity: there shows itself, also with regard to this second requirement for culture, a purposive striving of nature toward a cultivation that makes us receptive to higher ends than nature itself can supply. The preponderance of the evils which the refinement of taste to the point of its {{ 395 }} idealization, and even the luxury in the sciences, as a nourishment for vanity, pours out over us through the insatiable multitude of the inclinations thereby produced, is not to be disputed: yet against this the end of nature is also not to be mistaken, namely, ever more to wrest away from the rawness and impetuosity of those inclinations which belong more to the animality in us and are most opposed to the cultivation toward our higher vocation (the inclinations of enjoyment), and to make room for the development of humanity. Beautiful art and sciences, which through a pleasure that can be universally communicated and through refinement and polish for society, even if they do not make the human being morally better, yet make him civilized, gain a very great deal over the tyranny of sensual propensity and thereby prepare the human being for a sovereignty in which reason alone is to have force: while the evils with which nature on the one hand, and the unsociable selfishness of human beings on the other, afflict us, at the same time summon up, heighten, and steel the powers of the soul {{{ 434 }}} so as not to succumb to them, and thus let us feel an aptitude for higher ends that lies hidden in us.[^*]

[^*]: What value life has for us, if this is estimated merely according to ***what one enjoys*** (the natural end of the sum of all inclinations, happiness), is easy to decide. It sinks below zero; for who would want to begin life anew under the same conditions, or even according to a new, self-projected (but in accordance with the course of nature) plan, which was likewise set merely on enjoyment? What value life has in accordance with what it contains in itself, conducted in accordance with the end that nature has with us, and which consists in that ***which one does*** (not merely enjoys), where we are however always only means to an indeterminate final end, has been shown above. There thus remains nothing else, then, but the value that we give to our own life through that which we not only do, but do so purposively independently of nature, that even the existence of nature can be an end only under this condition.
