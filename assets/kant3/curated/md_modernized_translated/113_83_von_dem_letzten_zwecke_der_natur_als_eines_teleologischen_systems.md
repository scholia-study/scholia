## {{ 388 }} § 83. On the ultimate end of nature as a teleological system

We showed in the foregoing that we have grounds sufficient—according to principles of reason, though only for the reflecting and not for the determining power of judgment—to judge the human being not merely, like all organized beings, as a natural end, but also, here on earth, as the ***ultimate*** end of nature, in relation to which all the remaining things of nature constitute a system of ends. Now if that which is to be promoted as an end through the human being's connection with nature must be found within the human being himself, then either the end must be {{{ 430 }}} of such a kind that it can itself be satisfied by nature in its beneficence, or it is the fitness and skill for all sorts of ends for which nature (externally and internally) might be used by him. The first end of nature would be the ***happiness***, the second the ***culture*** of the human being.

The concept of happiness is not one that the human being, say, abstracts from his instincts and thereby derives from the animality within him; it is rather a mere ***idea*** of a state to which, under merely empirical conditions (which is impossible), {{ 389 }} he wants to make the latter adequate. He sketches it out for himself, and indeed in such varied ways, through his understanding as entangled with imagination and the senses; and he alters this understanding so often that nature, even were it wholly subject to his choice, could nonetheless adopt no determinate, universal, and fixed law by which to accord with this fluctuating concept and thus with the end that each person arbitrarily sets before himself. But even if we were either to reduce this to the true natural need in which our species is thoroughly in agreement with itself, or on the other hand to raise ever so high the skill for procuring imagined ends, still what the human being understands by happiness, and what is in fact his own ultimate natural end (not an end of freedom), would never be attained by him; for his nature is not of the kind to leave off anywhere in possession and enjoyment and to be satisfied. On the other hand, so far is it from being the case that nature has taken him up as its special favorite and favored him with beneficence above all animals, that it rather spares him no more than any other animal in its ruinous effects—in plague, hunger, danger of flood, frost, assault by other animals great and small, and the like; still more, the absurdity of the ***natural predispositions*** in him drives him into self-devised torments and drives still others of his own species, through the oppression of {{ 390 }} domination, the barbarism of wars, and so forth, into such distress, while he himself, so far as in him lies, labors at the destruction of his own species, so that even with the most beneficent nature outside us its end, were it set upon the happiness of our species, would not be attained within a system of that nature on earth, because the nature within us is not receptive to it. He is thus always only a link in the chain of natural ends: a principle, to be sure, with respect to many an {{{ 431 }}} end for which nature seems to have determined him in its arrangement, inasmuch as he makes himself so; yet at the same time a means for maintaining purposiveness within the mechanism of the other links. Being the sole creature on earth endowed with understanding, and hence with a capacity to set ends for itself at will, he is indeed the titular lord of nature and, if one regards nature as a teleological system, by his vocation the ultimate end of nature; but always only conditionally, namely that he understand it and have the will to give to nature and to himself such a relation of ends as can be sufficient unto itself independently of nature, and hence be a final end—one that, however, need not be sought in nature at all.

But in order to find out where, at least, we are to locate in the human being that ***ultimate*** end of nature, we must seek out what {{ 391 }} nature is capable of providing in order to prepare him for what he himself must do in order to be a final end, and separate it from all those ends whose possibility rests on conditions that may be expected of nature alone. Of the latter kind is happiness on earth, by which is understood the sum total of all his ends made possible through nature outside and within the human being; that is the matter of all his ends on earth, which, if he makes it his whole end, renders him incapable of setting a final end for his own existence and of harmonizing with it. There thus remains, of all his ends in nature, only the formal, subjective condition, namely the fitness for setting ends for himself at all, and (independently of nature in his determination of ends) for using nature, in conformity with the maxims of his free ends in general, as a means—which is what nature can accomplish with a view to the final end lying outside it, and which can therefore be regarded as its ultimate end. The production of the fitness of a rational being for ends of its own choosing in general (consequently in its freedom) is ***culture***. Only culture, therefore, can be the ultimate end that one has cause to attribute to nature with respect to the human species (not his own happiness on earth, nor even his being merely the foremost instrument for establishing order {{ 392 }} and concord in the reasonless nature outside him).

But not every culture is sufficient for this ultimate end of nature. That of ***skill*** is indeed the foremost subjective condition of fitness for the promotion of ends in general; but {{{ 432 }}} it is nonetheless not sufficient to promote the ***will*** in the determination and choice of its ends, which yet belongs essentially to fitness for ends in its whole extent. The latter condition of fitness, which one might call the culture of training (discipline), is negative and consists in the liberation of the will from the despotism of desires, by which we, riveted to certain natural things, are made incapable of choosing for ourselves, since we let the impulses serve as fetters that nature has given us only in place of guiding threads, so that we should not neglect or even injure the vocation of animality within us, while we are yet free enough to tighten or slacken, lengthen or shorten them according as the ends of reason require.

Skill can hardly be developed in the human species otherwise than by means of inequality among human beings: for the greatest number provide, as it were mechanically and without requiring any special art for it, the necessities of life for the comfort and leisure of others, who work at the less necessary parts of culture, science {{ 393 }} and art, and are kept by these in a condition of oppression, bitter labor, and little enjoyment—a class to which, however, much of the culture of the higher one does gradually spread as well. But the torments grow, in the course of that progress (the peak of which, when the propensity for the dispensable begins to encroach upon the indispensable, is called luxury), equally mighty on both sides—on the one through violence from without, on the other through inner discontent; yet this glittering misery is bound up with the development of the natural predispositions in the human species, and the end of nature itself, even though it is not our own end, is thereby nonetheless attained. The formal condition under which alone nature can attain this its final aim is that constitution in the relations of human beings among one another wherein lawful force in a whole—which is called ***civil society***—is set against the encroachment of the mutually conflicting freedoms; for only within it can the greatest development of the natural predispositions take place. For this, however, there would still be required—even were human beings clever enough to discover it and wise enough to submit willingly to its constraint—a ***cosmopolitan*** whole, that is, a system of all states that are in danger of acting detrimentally upon one another. In the absence of this, and given the obstacle that {{{ 433 }}} ambition, lust for domination, and avarice—above all in those {{ 394 }} who hold power in their hands—set against the very possibility of such a design, ***war*** (partly one in which states split apart and dissolve into smaller ones, partly one in which a state seeks to unite others, smaller than itself, with itself and to form a larger whole) is unavoidable: war which—being an unintentional endeavor of human beings (aroused by unbridled passions), yet a deeply hidden and perhaps intentional one of the supreme wisdom—serves, if not to establish, then at least to prepare lawfulness together with the freedom of states, and thereby the unity of a morally grounded system of them, and which, notwithstanding the most frightful tribulations with which it burdens the human race, and the perhaps still greater ones with which the constant readiness for it presses in peacetime, is nonetheless one incentive the more (while the hope for the repose of a people's happiness recedes ever further) to develop to the highest degree all the talents that serve culture.

As for the discipline of the inclinations—for which the natural predisposition is, in view of our vocation as an animal species, quite purposive, but which greatly hinder the development of humanity—here too, in respect of this second requirement for culture, there shows itself a purposive striving of nature toward a cultivation that makes us receptive to higher ends than nature itself can supply. The preponderance of the ills that the refinement of taste, carried to the point of its {{ 395 }} idealization, and even luxury in the sciences, as a nourishment for vanity, pour out over us through the insatiable multitude of the inclinations thereby engendered, is not to be disputed; but on the other hand the end of nature is likewise not to be mistaken—to win ever more ground away from the rawness and vehemence of those inclinations that belong more to the animality in us and are most opposed to our cultivation toward our higher vocation (the inclinations of enjoyment), and to make room for the development of humanity. Fine art and the sciences, which through a pleasure that admits of universal communication, and through polish and refinement for society, make human beings, if not morally better, then at least civilized, win a great deal away from the tyranny of sensuous propensity and thereby prepare human beings for a sovereignty in which reason alone is to hold power: while the ills with which nature on the one part, and the incompatible selfishness of human beings on the other, afflict us at the same time summon up, heighten, and steel the powers of the soul {{{ 434 }}} not to succumb to those ills, and thus let us feel a fitness for higher ends that lies hidden within us.[^*]

[^*]: What worth life has for us, if this is estimated merely according to that ***which one enjoys*** (the natural end of the sum of all inclinations, happiness), is easy to decide. It sinks below zero; for who, indeed, would want to begin life anew under the same conditions, or even according to a new, self-devised plan (though in keeping with the course of nature) that was likewise set merely upon enjoyment? What worth life has according to what it contains within itself, when conducted in accordance with the end that nature has with us, and which consists in ***what one does*** (not merely enjoys)—where we are, however, always only a means to an indeterminate final end—has been shown above. There thus remains, presumably, nothing but the worth that we ourselves give to our life through what we not only do, but do so purposively and independently of nature that even the existence of nature can be an end only under this condition.
