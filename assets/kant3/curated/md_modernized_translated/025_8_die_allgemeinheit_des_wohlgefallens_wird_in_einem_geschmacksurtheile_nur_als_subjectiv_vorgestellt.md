## § 8. The universality of satisfaction is represented in a judgment of taste only as subjective

This peculiar determination of the universality of an aesthetic judgment, one that can be met with in a judgment of taste, is a remarkable thing—not, to be sure, for the logician, but certainly for the transcendental philosopher—which demands of him no slight effort to discover its origin, but which in return also uncovers a property of our faculty of cognition that would have remained unknown without this analysis.

First one must convince oneself of this completely: that through the {{{ 214 }}} judgment of taste (about the beautiful) one imputes the satisfaction in an object to ***everyone***, yet without however basing it on a concept (for in that case it would be the good); and that this claim to universal validity {{ 22 }} belongs so essentially to a judgment by which we declare something to be ***beautiful*** that, without thinking it along with the judgment, it would occur to no one to use this expression, but rather everything that gives pleasure apart from a concept would be reckoned as agreeable, with respect to which one lets each person keep his own opinion, and no one requires of another agreement with his judgment of taste—which nonetheless invariably happens in the case of a judgment of taste concerning beauty. The first of these I may term the taste of sense and the second the taste of reflection, insofar as the former passes merely private judgments while the second passes putatively commonly-valid (public) ones, though both pass aesthetic (not practical) judgments about an object merely in view of how its representation relates to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure. Now it is indeed strange that, whereas in the case of the taste of sense experience not only shows that its judgment (of pleasure or displeasure in something or other) is not universally valid, but everyone is also of his own accord so modest as not to impute this agreement to others (even though a very widespread unanimity is in fact often enough found even in these judgments), the taste of reflection—which, as experience teaches, is likewise often enough rejected when it claims universal validity for its judgment (about the beautiful) for everyone—can nonetheless find it possible (which it {{ 23 }} indeed does) to represent to itself judgments that could universally demand this agreement, and does in fact require it of everyone for each of its judgments of taste, without those who judge being in dispute over the possibility of such a claim, and being unable only in particular cases to reach agreement about the correct application of this faculty.

Here it is now to be noted, first of all, that a universality not grounded on concepts of the object (even if only empirical ones) is not logical at all but aesthetic, i.e., contains no objective quantity of the judgment but only a subjective one, for which I also use the expression ***common validity***, which designates the validity not of the relation of a representation to the faculty of cognition, but of its relation to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure for every subject. (But one may also employ this very expression for the logical quantity of a judgment, {{{ 215 }}} provided one only adds ***objective*** universal validity, to distinguish it from the merely subjective, which is always aesthetic.)

Now an ***objectively universally valid*** judgment is also always subjective, i.e., if a judgment is valid for everything falling under a given concept, then it also holds for everyone who represents an object to himself by means of that concept. From a ***subjective universal validity***, however—that is, the aesthetic one, which rests on no concept {{ 24 }}—the logical cannot be inferred: because that kind of judgment does not bear on the object at all. Yet precisely for this reason the aesthetic universality that is ascribed to a judgment must also be of a special kind, since it does not connect the predicate of beauty with the concept of the ***object***, taken in its whole logical sphere, and yet extends that same predicate over the whole sphere ***of those who judge***.

With respect to logical quantity, all judgments of taste are ***singular*** judgments. For, since I must hold the object immediately up to my feeling of pleasure and displeasure, and yet not through concepts, those judgments cannot have the quantity of objectively commonly-valid judgments; although, if the object's singular representation in the judgment of taste is transformed by comparison into a concept, in accordance with the conditions that determine the latter, a logically universal judgment can arise from it: e.g., the rose at which I am looking, I declare through a judgment of taste to be beautiful. By contrast, the judgment that arises through the comparison of many singular ones—roses in general are beautiful—is now asserted no longer merely as an aesthetic judgment, but as a logical judgment grounded on an aesthetic one. Now the judgment—the rose is agreeable (in its scent)—is, to be sure, also aesthetic and singular, yet not a judgment of taste but a judgment of sense. It differs from the former, namely, in this: that the judgment of taste carries with it an ***aesthetic {{ 25 }} quantity*** of universality—that is, validity holding for everyone, which cannot be met with in a judgment about the agreeable. The judgments about the good alone, although they likewise determine the satisfaction taken in an object, possess a logical and not merely aesthetic universality; for they hold of the object, as cognitions of it, and therefore for everyone.

When objects are judged solely by means of concepts, every representation of beauty is lost. Hence there can also be no rule by which anyone might be compelled to acknowledge something as beautiful. Whether {{{ 216 }}} a garment, a house, a flower is beautiful: about this one will not let one's judgment be talked into one by any grounds or principles. One wants to submit the object to one's own eyes, just as if one's satisfaction depended on the sensation; and yet, when one then calls the object beautiful, one believes oneself to have a universal voice on one's side and lays claim to the assent of everyone, whereas by contrast every private sensation would decide only for the observer alone and his satisfaction.

Here it is now to be seen that in a judgment of taste nothing more is postulated than such a ***universal voice*** with respect to the satisfaction, without the mediation of concepts; hence the ***possibility*** of an {{ 26 }} aesthetic judgment that may at once count as valid for everyone. The judgment of taste itself does not ***postulate*** everyone's agreement (for that only a logically universal judgment can do, since it can adduce grounds); it only ***imputes*** this agreement to everyone, as a case of the rule, with respect to which it expects confirmation not from concepts but from the assent of others. The universal voice is thus only an idea (on what it rests is not yet investigated here). Whether the one who believes himself to be passing a judgment of taste is in fact judging in conformity with this idea may be uncertain; but that he nonetheless relates it to this idea, and hence that it is meant to be a judgment of taste, he announces through the expression of beauty. For himself, however, he can become certain of it through the mere awareness that he has set apart everything belonging to the agreeable and the good from the satisfaction that still remains to him; and that is the whole extent to which he pledges himself the agreement of everyone: a claim to which, under these conditions, he would also be entitled, if only he did not so often offend against them and thereby pass an erroneous judgment of taste.
