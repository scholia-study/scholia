## § 8. The universality of satisfaction is represented in a judgment of taste only as subjective

This peculiar determination of the universality of an aesthetic judgment, one that can be met with in a judgment of taste, is a remarkable thing—not, to be sure, for the logician, but certainly for the transcendental philosopher—which demands of him no slight effort to discover its origin, but which in return also uncovers a property of our faculty of cognition that would have remained unknown without this analysis.

To begin with, one has to become entirely convinced of the following: that by means of the {{{ 214 }}} judgment of taste (regarding the beautiful) the satisfaction taken in an object is demanded of ***everyone***, though not on the strength of any concept (since, were that so, it would be the good); and that such a claim to universal validity {{ 22 }} belongs so intrinsically to any judgment whereby we pronounce a thing ***beautiful*** that, were this claim not thought together with it, no one would ever hit upon employing that word, and instead whatever pleases without a concept would be counted as agreeable—a domain in which each is allowed his own view for himself, and none expects of anyone else concurrence with his judgment of taste, whereas in the judgment of taste about beauty this expectation is present at all times. The first of these I may term the taste of sense and the second the taste of reflection, insofar as the former passes merely private judgments while the second passes putatively commonly-valid (public) ones, though both pass aesthetic (not practical) judgments about an object merely in view of how its representation relates to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure. Now it is genuinely puzzling that, while with the taste of sense not merely does experience make plain that its judgment (of pleasure or displeasure in this or that) fails to hold universally, but each person is besides of his own accord modest enough not to press this agreement upon others (even if a really quite widespread concord is, in truth, frequently to be met with in such judgments too), the taste of reflection—which, as experience shows, is itself often enough turned away when it claims universal validity for its judgment (about the beautiful) on everyone's behalf—can nonetheless deem it possible (as {{ 23 }} it really does) to conceive of judgments that could demand this agreement universally, and indeed exacts it from everyone for each of its judgments of taste, without those who judge quarreling over whether such a claim is possible, being unable merely in particular instances to settle on the correct application of this faculty.

Here the very first thing to observe is that a universality resting not upon concepts of the object (albeit merely empirical ones) is by no means logical but rather aesthetic—that is, it holds no objective quantity of the judgment within it, only a subjective one, and it is for this that I likewise employ the term ***common validity***, a term marking the validity that pertains not to a representation's relation to the faculty of cognition, but to its bearing on the feeling of pleasure and displeasure in every subject. (Yet one is equally free to apply the same term to the logical quantity of a judgment, {{{ 215 }}} so long as one appends the qualification ***objective*** universal validity, thereby setting it apart from the purely subjective sort, which is invariably aesthetic.)

Now an ***objectively universally valid*** judgment is also always subjective, i.e., if a judgment is valid for everything falling under a given concept, then it also holds for everyone who represents an object to himself by means of that concept. From a ***subjective universal validity***, however—that is, the aesthetic one, which rests on no concept {{ 24 }}—the logical cannot be inferred: because that kind of judgment does not bear on the object at all. But for just that reason the aesthetic universality attributed to a judgment has likewise to be of a peculiar sort, inasmuch as it does not link the predicate of beauty to the concept of the ***object*** considered throughout its entire logical sphere, while nevertheless stretching that very predicate across the whole sphere ***of those who judge***.

Regarded in terms of their logical quantity, all judgments of taste count as ***singular*** judgments. For inasmuch as I have to measure the object directly against my feeling of pleasure and displeasure, and precisely not by way of concepts, such judgments cannot possess the quantity belonging to objectively commonly-valid judgments; and yet, should the singular representation of the object of the judgment of taste be converted through comparison into a concept, following the conditions that fix the latter, there can emerge from it a logically universal judgment: for instance, the rose before my eyes I pronounce, by a judgment of taste, to be beautiful. By contrast, the judgment that arises through the comparison of many singular ones—roses in general are beautiful—is now asserted no longer merely as an aesthetic judgment, but as a logical judgment grounded on an aesthetic one. Now the judgment—the rose is agreeable (in its scent)—is, to be sure, also aesthetic and singular, yet not a judgment of taste but a judgment of sense. It differs from the former, namely, in this: that the judgment of taste carries with it an ***aesthetic {{ 25 }} quantity*** of universality—that is, validity holding for everyone, which cannot be met with in a judgment about the agreeable. The judgments about the good alone, although they likewise determine the satisfaction taken in an object, possess a logical and not merely aesthetic universality; for they hold of the object, as cognitions of it, and therefore for everyone.

When objects are judged solely by means of concepts, every representation of beauty is lost. There can therefore be no rule either that would oblige a person to recognize something as beautiful. As to whether {{{ 216 }}} a robe, a house, or a flower is beautiful: on that point one refuses to have one's judgment argued into one by any reasons or principles. One seeks to bring the object before one's own eyes, as though one's satisfaction hung upon the sensation; and nonetheless, once one calls the object beautiful at that point, one takes oneself to have a universal voice behind one and stakes a claim on the concurrence of everyone, while on the other hand a merely private sensation would be decisive for the beholder by himself and for his satisfaction alone.

What must be seen here is that a judgment of taste posits nothing beyond such a ***universal voice*** concerning the satisfaction, with no concepts intervening; and thus the ***possibility*** of an {{ 26 }} aesthetic judgment that can at the same time be regarded as valid for everyone. The judgment of taste itself does not ***postulate*** everyone's agreement (for that only a logically universal judgment can do, since it can adduce grounds); rather it merely ***imputes*** to everyone that agreement, treating it as an instance of the rule, and in this regard it looks for its confirmation not from concepts but from the concurrence of others. The universal voice is thus only an idea (on what it rests is not yet investigated here). It may well remain unsettled whether someone who takes himself to be rendering a judgment of taste is really judging in keeping with this idea; but that he does refer it to that idea, and so that it is supposed to be a judgment of taste, he makes known by the very word beauty. On his own account, however, he can arrive at certainty of this merely by being conscious that he has separated out, from the satisfaction still left over for him, everything that pertains to the agreeable and to the good; and this is the full measure of what he promises himself as the assent of everyone: an entitlement he would indeed possess under such conditions, provided only that he did not transgress against them too frequently and thereby deliver a mistaken judgment of taste.
