## § 8. The Universality of Satisfaction is Represented in a Judgment of Taste Only as Subjective

This particular determination of the universality of an aesthetic judgment, which is to be found in a judgment of taste, is a remarkable thing, not indeed for the logician, but certainly for the transcendental philosopher, which demands of him no small effort to discover its origin, but in return also uncovers a property of our cognitive faculty which without this analysis would have remained unknown.

First, one must convince oneself completely of this: that by a {{{ 214 }}} judgment of taste (on the beautiful) one attributes the satisfaction in an object to ***everyone***, without yet founding it on a concept (for then it would be the good); and that this claim to universal validity {{ 22 }} belongs so essentially to a judgment by which we declare something to be ***beautiful***, that without thinking this along with it, it would occur to no one to use this expression, but rather everything that pleases without a concept would be counted as the agreeable, in respect of which one allows everyone to have his own head, and no one expects another to agree with his judgment of taste, which is nevertheless always the case in a judgment of taste about beauty. I can call the first the taste of sense, the second the taste of reflection: insofar as the former makes merely private judgments, but the latter makes supposedly universally valid (public), but in both cases aesthetic (not practical) judgments about an object merely in respect of the relation of its representation to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure. Now it is indeed strange that, whereas of the taste of sense not only does experience show that its judgment (of pleasure or displeasure in something) is not universally valid, but everyone is also of his own accord so modest as not even to attribute this agreement to others (although indeed a very widespread unanimity is often actually found in these judgments too), the taste of reflection, which is nevertheless often enough rejected in its claim to the universal validity of its judgment (on the beautiful) for everyone, as experience teaches, can nevertheless find it possible (which it {{ 23 }} also actually does) to represent to itself judgments that could demand this agreement universally, and indeed expects it of everyone for each of its judgments of taste, without the judges being in dispute over the possibility of such a claim, but only being unable to agree in particular cases on the correct application of this faculty.

Here it is now first of all to be noted that a universality which does not rest on concepts of the object (even if only empirical ones) is not logical at all, but aesthetic, i.e., contains no objective quantity of judgment, but only a subjective one, for which I also use the expression ***common validity***, which designates the validity not of the relation of a representation to the cognitive faculty, but to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure for every subject. (One can, however, also use the same expression for the logical quantity of judgment, {{{ 215 }}} if only one adds ***objective*** universal validity, to distinguish it from the merely subjective one, which is always aesthetic.)

Now an ***objectively universally valid*** judgment is also always subjective, i.e., if the judgment is valid for everything that is contained under a given concept, then it is also valid for everyone who represents an object to himself through this concept. But from a ***subjective universal validity***, i.e., the aesthetic, which rests on no concept {{ 24 }}, one cannot infer the logical: because that kind of judgment does not go to the object at all. Just for this reason, however, the aesthetic universality which is attributed to a judgment must also be of a special kind, because it does not connect the predicate of beauty with the concept of the ***object***, considered in its entire logical sphere, and yet extends this very same predicate over the entire sphere ***of the judges***.

In respect of logical quantity, all judgments of taste are ***singular*** judgments. For because I must refer the object directly to my feeling of pleasure and displeasure, and yet not by means of concepts, these judgments cannot have the quantity of objectively universally valid judgments; although, if the singular representation of the object of the judgment of taste is converted by comparison into a concept according to the conditions that determine the latter, a logically universal judgment can arise from it: e.g., the rose that I look at, I declare beautiful through a judgment of taste. By contrast, the judgment which arises from the comparison of many singular ones: roses in general are beautiful, is now stated not merely as an aesthetic, but as a logical judgment founded upon an aesthetic one. Now the judgment: the rose is (in its smell) agreeable, is indeed also an aesthetic and singular judgment, but not a judgment of taste, but rather a judgment of sense. Namely, it differs from the former in this: that the judgment of taste carries with it an ***aesthetic {{ 25 }} quantity*** of universality, i.e., of validity for everyone, which cannot be found in the judgment on the agreeable. Only judgments on the good, although they likewise determine the satisfaction in an object, have logical, and not merely aesthetic universality; for they hold of the object as cognitions of it, and therefore for everyone.

If one judges objects merely according to concepts, all representation of beauty is lost. Hence there can also be no rule according to which anyone should be compelled to recognize something as beautiful. Whether {{{ 216 }}} a dress, a house, a flower is beautiful: to that one does not allow one's judgment to be talked into by any reasons or principles. One wants to submit the object to one's own eyes, just as if one's satisfaction depended on sensation; and yet, if one then calls the object beautiful, one believes oneself to have a universal voice on one's side and claims the assent of everyone, whereas, by contrast, every private sensation would decide for the observer alone and his satisfaction.

Here it is now to be seen that in the judgment of taste nothing is postulated but such a ***universal voice*** in respect of satisfaction without the mediation of concepts; hence the ***possibility*** of an {{ 26 }} aesthetic judgment that can at the same time be considered valid for everyone. The judgment of taste itself does not ***postulate*** everyone's agreement (for only a logically universal one can do that, because it can adduce reasons); it only ***attributes*** this agreement to everyone, as an instance of the rule, in respect of which it expects confirmation not from concepts but from the assent of others. The universal voice is therefore only an idea (on what it rests is not yet investigated here). That he who believes he is making a judgment of taste is in fact judging in accordance with this idea can be uncertain; but that he nevertheless relates it to it, hence that it is to be a judgment of taste, he announces through the expression of beauty. For himself, however, he can become certain of this through the mere consciousness of the separation of everything that belongs to the agreeable and the good from the satisfaction that still remains to him; and that is all for which he promises himself the agreement of everyone: a claim which under these conditions he would also be justified in making, if only he did not often offend against them and thereby make an erroneous judgment of taste.
