## {{{ 338 }}} § 56. Representation of the antinomy of taste

The first of taste's commonplaces is contained in that saying by which anyone devoid of taste thinks to guard himself against reproach: ***everyone has his own taste***. This amounts to saying: the determining ground of this judgment is merely subjective (gratification or pain); and the judgment has no claim on the necessary assent of others.

The second commonplace about it, which is used even by those who grant the judgment of taste the right to pronounce {{ 233 }} validly for everyone, is: ***about taste there can be no disputing***. This amounts to saying: the determining ground of a judgment of taste may indeed also be objective, but it cannot be brought under determinate concepts; hence nothing can be ***decided*** about the judgment itself by means of proofs, although one may very well and rightly ***contend*** about it. For ***contending*** and ***disputing*** are indeed alike in that they seek to bring about agreement of the judgments through their mutual opposition, yet differ in that the latter hopes to accomplish this in accordance with determinate concepts serving as grounds of proof, and thus assumes ***objective concepts*** as grounds of the judgment. But where this is regarded as impracticable, disputing too is judged to be equally impracticable.

One readily sees that between these two commonplaces a proposition is missing, one that is not indeed in proverbial circulation but is nonetheless contained in everyone's mind, namely: ***about taste one can contend*** (though not dispute). This proposition, however, contains the contrary of the topmost proposition. For where contending is to be permitted, there must be hope of coming to an understanding with one another; hence one must be able to count on grounds of the judgment that do not have merely private validity and so are not merely subjective; yet to this the principle ***everyone has his own taste*** stands directly opposed.

Thus with regard to the principle of taste the following {{ 234 }} antinomy shows itself:

1) ***Thesis.*** The judgment of taste is not based on concepts; for otherwise it would admit of disputing (of being decided by means of proofs).

2) ***Antithesis.*** The judgment of taste is based on concepts; for otherwise, its variety notwithstanding, one could not even {{{ 339 }}} so much as contend about it (lay claim to the necessary concurrence of others with this judgment).
