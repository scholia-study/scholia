## {{{ 338 }}} § 56. Representation of the Antinomy of Taste

The first commonplace of taste is contained in the proposition with which everyone who lacks taste thinks to protect himself against censure: ***everyone has his own taste***. This is as much as to say: the determining ground of this judgment is merely subjective (pleasure or pain); and the judgment has no right to the necessary assent of others.

The second commonplace of taste, which is used even by those who concede to the judgment of taste the right {{ 233 }} to speak validly for everyone, is: ***there is no disputing about taste***. This is as much as to say: the determining ground of a judgment of taste may indeed be objective, but it cannot be reduced to determinate concepts; consequently, nothing can be ***decided*** about the judgment itself by means of proofs, although it can very well and with right be ***quarreled*** about. For ***quarreling*** and ***disputing*** are indeed alike in that they seek to produce unanimity between judgments through their mutual resistance, but different in that the latter hopes to effect this according to determinate concepts as grounds of proof, and thus assumes ***objective concepts*** as grounds of the judgment. But where this is regarded as impracticable, there disputing is likewise judged to be impracticable.

One easily sees that between these two commonplaces a proposition is missing, which, though not proverbially in circulation, is nevertheless present in everyone's mind, namely: ***there is quarreling about taste*** (although no disputing). This proposition, however, contains the opposite of the first proposition. For that about which it is to be allowed to quarrel, there must be hope of coming to agreement with one another; consequently, one must be able to count on grounds of judgment that do not have merely private validity and are thus not merely subjective; to which, nevertheless, that principle: ***everyone has his own taste***, is directly opposed.

There is thus revealed, in regard to the principle of taste, the following {{ 234 }} antinomy:

1) ***Thesis.*** The judgment of taste is not based on concepts; for otherwise there would be disputing about it (deciding by means of proofs).

2) ***Antithesis.*** The judgment of taste is based on concepts; for otherwise, despite its diversity, it would {{{ 339 }}} not even be possible to quarrel about it (to make a claim to the necessary agreement of others with this judgment).
