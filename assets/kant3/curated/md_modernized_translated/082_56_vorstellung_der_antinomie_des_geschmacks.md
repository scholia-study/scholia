## {{{ 338 }}} § 56. Representation of the antinomy of taste

The first of taste's commonplaces is contained in that saying by which anyone devoid of taste thinks to guard himself against reproach: ***everyone has his own taste***. This is as much as to say: what determines this judgment is purely subjective (gratification or pain), and the judgment can claim no necessary assent from others.

The second commonplace on the same subject, drawn upon even by those who allow the judgment of taste the right of pronouncing itself {{ 233 }} valid for everyone, is this: ***about taste there can be no disputing***. This is as much as to say: the ground determining a judgment of taste might well be objective too, yet it cannot be reduced to determinate concepts; and so, by means of proofs, nothing can be ***decided*** regarding the judgment itself, even though one may quite rightly and with good reason ***contend*** about it. For ***contending*** and ***disputing*** are indeed one and the same in this, that through the mutual resistance of judgments they seek to produce unanimity among them, but they diverge in this, that disputing hopes to effect it by determinate concepts taken as grounds of proof, and hence posits ***objective concepts*** as the grounds of the judgment. But where this is regarded as impracticable, disputing too is judged to be equally impracticable.

One readily sees that between these two commonplaces a proposition is missing, one that is not indeed in proverbial circulation but is nonetheless contained in everyone's mind, namely: ***about taste one can contend*** (though not dispute). This proposition, however, contains the contrary of the topmost proposition. For wherever contending is to be allowed, there must be some hope of reaching mutual agreement; and thus one has to be able to rely on grounds of the judgment which possess more than a merely private validity and are therefore not purely subjective, a demand to which that very principle, ***everyone has his own taste***, runs directly counter.

Thus with regard to the principle of taste the following {{ 234 }} antinomy shows itself:

1) ***Thesis.*** The judgment of taste rests upon no concepts; for were that not so, one could dispute about it (settle it by means of proofs).

2) ***Antithesis.*** The judgment of taste does rest upon concepts; for were that not so, then, whatever its diversity, no contention over it would {{{ 339 }}} even be possible (to make claim to the necessary concurrence of others in this judgment).
