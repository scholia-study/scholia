## § 47. Elucidation and confirmation of the above explanation of genius

On this point everyone is agreed: that genius stands wholly opposed to the ***spirit of imitation***. Now since learning is nothing but imitating, even the greatest aptitude—teachableness (capacity) taken as teachableness—still cannot count as genius. But even when one thinks or composes for oneself and does not merely take up what others have thought, indeed even invents a good deal for art and science, this is still not the proper ground for calling such an (often great) ***head*** (as opposed to one who, because he can never do more than merely learn and imitate, is called a ***dullard***) a ***genius***: for precisely this too ***could*** have been learned, and so still lies along the natural path of inquiry and reflection according to rules, and is not specifically distinct from what can be acquired through diligence by way of imitation. Thus everything that ***Newton*** laid out in that immortal work of his on the principles of the philosophy of nature—no matter how great a head was {{ 184 }} needed to invent such things—can be learned quite well; but one cannot learn to write poetry with spirit, however exhaustive {{{ 309 }}} all the precepts for the art of poetry, and however excellent its models, may be. The reason is that Newton was able to demonstrate every one of his steps, from the first elements of geometry all the way to his great and profound discoveries, not only to himself but to everyone else, fully intuitively and determinately enough to be followed; whereas no ***Homer*** or ***Wieland*** can show how his imaginative and yet at the same time thought-laden ideas arise and come together in his head, for the reason that he himself does not know it and therefore cannot teach it to anyone else either. In scientific matters, then, the greatest discoverer differs from the most painstaking imitator and apprentice only in degree, whereas he differs specifically from one whom nature has endowed for fine art. Yet in this there lies no disparagement of those great men, to whom the human race owes so much, as against the favorites of nature with respect to their talent for fine art. Precisely in this—that the former talent is made for the ever-advancing greater perfection of cognitions and of every benefit dependent on them, and likewise for the instruction of others in these very same items of knowledge—consists a great advantage of theirs over those who deserve the honor of being called geniuses: for with the latter, art somewhere {{ 185 }} comes to a standstill, since a limit is set to it beyond which it cannot go, one that has presumably long since been reached and can no longer be extended; and moreover such a skill cannot be communicated either, but rather wants to be imparted to each individual directly by the hand of nature, and so dies with him, until nature one day endows another in the very same way, one who needs nothing more than an example in order to set to work, in a similar manner, the talent of which he is conscious.

Since the natural endowment must give art (as fine art) its rule, of what kind, then, is this rule? It cannot be couched in any formula so as to serve as a prescription; for otherwise the judgment about the beautiful would be determinable in accordance with concepts: rather, the rule must be abstracted from the deed, that is, from the product, against which others may test their own talent, letting the former serve them as a model not for ***copying*** but for ***imitation***. How this is possible is hard to explain. The artist's ideas arouse similar ideas in his apprentice, provided nature has furnished the latter with a similar proportion of the powers of mind. The models of fine art are therefore {{{ 310 }}} the only means of transmission for carrying it on to posterity: something that could not be done through mere descriptions (above all not in the field of the verbal arts); and even among these, only the {{ 186 }} ones in ancient, dead languages, now preserved merely as learned tongues, can become classical.

Although mechanical and fine art are very different from one another—the first as a mere art of diligence and of learning, the second as that of genius—there is nevertheless no fine art in which something mechanical, capable of being grasped and followed according to rules, and hence something ***academic***, does not constitute the essential condition of the art. For something must therein be conceived as an end, otherwise one could not ascribe its product to any art at all; it would be a mere product of chance. But to bring an end into a work, determinate rules are required from which one may not absolve oneself. Now since the originality of talent constitutes one (though not the only) essential component of the character of genius, shallow minds suppose that they can show themselves to be budding geniuses in no better way than by casting off the scholastic constraint of all rules, believing that one makes a finer show on a frenzied horse than on one trained in the riding school. Genius can supply only rich ***material*** for the products of fine art; the working-up of that material and the ***form*** require a talent schooled through training, so as to make a use of it that can hold up before the power of judgment. But when someone, even in matters of the most careful inquiry of reason, speaks {{ 187 }} and pronounces like a genius, then it becomes utterly ridiculous; one hardly knows whether to laugh more at the charlatan who spreads so much haze about himself—amid which one can judge nothing distinctly, yet imagine all the more—or more at the public that guilelessly persuades itself that its inability to recognize and grasp this masterpiece of insight distinctly comes about because new truths are being tossed at it in whole heaps, against which the detail (by way of measured explanations and methodical examination of the principles) seems to it to be mere botchwork.
