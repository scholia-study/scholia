## § 47. Elucidation and confirmation of the above explanation of genius

On this point everyone is agreed: that genius stands wholly opposed to the ***spirit of imitation***. Now since learning is nothing but imitating, even the greatest aptitude—teachableness (capacity) taken as teachableness—still cannot count as genius. Yet even where a person thinks or writes poetry on his own, rather than simply absorbing what others have already thought, and indeed contributes various inventions of his own to art and science, this still furnishes no proper ground for calling such an (often eminent) ***head*** (as against the one who, since he is never able to do anything beyond mere learning and copying, goes by the name of ***dullard***) a ***genius***: for even that ***could*** have been picked up by learning, and hence still lies upon the natural road of investigation and reflection guided by rules, being in no way specifically distinct from what industriousness can acquire through imitation. Thus everything that ***Newton*** laid out in that immortal work of his on the principles of the philosophy of nature—no matter how great a head was {{ 184 }} needed to invent such things—can be learned quite well; but one cannot learn to write poetry with spirit, however exhaustive {{{ 309 }}} all the precepts for the art of poetry, and however excellent its models, may be. The reason is that Newton was able to demonstrate every one of his steps, from the first elements of geometry all the way to his great and profound discoveries, not only to himself but to everyone else, fully intuitively and determinately enough to be followed; whereas no ***Homer*** or ***Wieland*** can show how his imaginative and yet at the same time thought-laden ideas arise and come together in his head, for the reason that he himself does not know it and therefore cannot teach it to anyone else either. In scientific matters, then, the greatest discoverer differs from the most painstaking imitator and apprentice only in degree, whereas he differs specifically from one whom nature has endowed for fine art. This, however, involves no belittling of those great men—men to whom humankind is indebted for so much—as set against nature's favorites in view of their talent for fine art. That very fact—namely, that the former talent is directed toward the steadily progressing, ever fuller perfection of our cognitions, together with every benefit that hangs upon them, and equally toward instructing others in these self-same branches of knowledge—constitutes a signal superiority they hold over those who earn the honor of bearing the name of geniuses: for in the case of the latter, art at some point {{ 185 }} halts, a boundary having been fixed for it past which it can advance no further, a boundary in all likelihood attained long ago and incapable of any further widening; and besides, a proficiency of this sort admits of no communication, but insists rather on being conferred upon each person straight from nature's own hand, so that it perishes along with him, until such time as nature again bestows an equal endowment upon someone else, who requires no more than a single example to set the talent he is aware of possessing into operation in a like fashion.

Since the natural endowment must give art (as fine art) its rule, of what kind, then, is this rule? Set down in no formula, it can serve as no prescription; for were it otherwise, the judgment about the beautiful would admit of determination by way of concepts: instead, the rule has to be drawn from the deed, that is, from the product—on which others may put their own talent to the test, in order to let that product stand for them as a model, not of ***copying*** but of ***imitation***. How this is possible is hard to explain. The artist's ideas arouse similar ideas in his apprentice, provided nature has furnished the latter with a similar proportion of the powers of mind. The models of fine art are therefore {{{ 310 }}} the only means of transmission for carrying it on to posterity: something that could not be done through mere descriptions (above all not in the field of the verbal arts); and even among these, only the {{ 186 }} ones in ancient, dead languages, now preserved merely as learned tongues, can become classical.

Although mechanical and fine art are very different from one another—the first as a mere art of diligence and of learning, the second as that of genius—there is nevertheless no fine art in which something mechanical, capable of being grasped and followed according to rules, and hence something ***academic***, does not constitute the essential condition of the art. For in it something has to be thought of as a purpose, since otherwise its product could be credited to no art whatever; it would be nothing but a product of accident. Yet in order to carry a purpose into execution, definite rules are called for, and from these one is not at liberty to release oneself. Since, then, the originality of talent makes up one—though not the sole—essential ingredient in the character of genius, superficial minds imagine that they cannot better display themselves as blossoming geniuses than by renouncing the scholastic compulsion of all rules, convinced that one cuts a finer figure on a skittish horse than upon one broken in at the riding school. Genius can supply only rich ***material*** for the products of fine art; the working-up of that material and the ***form*** require a talent schooled through training, so as to make a use of it that can hold up before the power of judgment. But when someone, even in matters of the most careful inquiry of reason, speaks {{ 187 }} and pronounces like a genius, then it becomes utterly ridiculous; one hardly knows whether to laugh more at the charlatan who spreads so much haze about himself—amid which one can judge nothing distinctly, yet imagine all the more—or more at the public that guilelessly persuades itself that its inability to recognize and grasp this masterpiece of insight distinctly comes about because new truths are being tossed at it in whole heaps, against which the detail (by way of measured explanations and methodical examination of the principles) seems to it to be mere botchwork.
