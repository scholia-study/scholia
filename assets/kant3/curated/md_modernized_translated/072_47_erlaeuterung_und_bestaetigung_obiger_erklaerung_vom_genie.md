## § 47. Elucidation and Confirmation of the Above Explanation of Genius

Everyone is agreed on this, that genius is to be completely opposed to the ***spirit of imitation***. Since learning is nothing but imitation, the greatest capability, docility (capacity) as docility, can still not count as genius. But even if one thinks or writes poetry for oneself, and does not merely grasp what others have thought, indeed even invents many a thing for art and science: this is still not the right ground for calling such a (frequently great) ***mind*** (in contrast with him who, because he can never do anything more than merely learn and imitate, is called a ***blockhead***) a ***genius***: because that very same thing could also have ***been*** learned, and thus still lies on the natural path of investigation and reflection according to rules, and is not specifically distinguished from what can be acquired through industry by means of imitation. Thus one can very well learn everything that ***Newton*** has set forth in his immortal work, the Principles of Natural Philosophy, however great a mind {{ 184 }} was required to invent such things; but one cannot learn to write poetry with genius, however detailed {{{ 309 }}} all the precepts for the art of poetry, and however excellent the models of it may be. The reason is that Newton could make all his steps, which he had to take from the first elements of geometry to his great and profound discoveries, entirely intuitive and determined for succession not only to himself but to everyone else; but no ***Homer*** or ***Wieland*** can show how his ideas, rich in fantasy and yet at the same time full of thought, arise and come together in his head, because he himself does not know it, and therefore also cannot teach it to anyone else. In the scientific field, therefore, the greatest inventor differs only in degree from the most laborious imitator and apprentice, whereas he differs specifically from him whom nature has endowed for beautiful art. Yet this implies no depreciation of those great men, to whom the human race owes so much, in comparison with nature's favorites in respect of their talent for beautiful art. In this very fact, that their talent is designed for the ever-progressive greater perfection of cognitions and of all the utility dependent upon them, as well as for the instruction of others in these same cognitions, consists their great advantage over those who deserve the honor of being called geniuses: because for the latter, art somewhere {{ 185 }} comes to a standstill, since a limit is set for it beyond which it cannot go, which has presumably already been reached long ago and cannot be extended further; and besides, such a skill cannot be communicated either, but wants to be granted directly to each person from the hand of nature, and thus dies with him, until nature once again endows another in the same way, who needs nothing more than an example in order to let the talent, of which he is conscious, work in a similar manner.

Since the natural endowment of art (as beautiful art) must give the rule, what kind of rule is this? It cannot, drawn up in a formula, serve as a precept; for otherwise the judgment on the beautiful would be determinable according to concepts: rather, the rule must be abstracted from the deed, i.e., from the product, on which others may test their own talent, letting that product serve them as a model, not for ***copying***, but for ***imitation***. How this is possible is difficult to explain. The ideas of the artist arouse similar ideas in his apprentice, if nature has provided the latter with a similar proportion of mental powers. The models of beautiful art are therefore {{{ 310 }}} the sole means of transmitting these to posterity: which could not happen through mere descriptions (especially not in the field of the speech arts); and even in the latter, only those {{ 186 }} in ancient, dead languages, which are now preserved only as learned languages, can become classical.

Although mechanical and beautiful art, the first as a mere art of industry and learning, the second as that of genius, are very distinct from one another: yet there is no beautiful art in which something mechanical, which can be comprehended and followed according to rules, and thus something ***academic***, does not constitute the essential condition of the art. For something must be thought as a purpose in it, otherwise one cannot ascribe its product to any art at all; it would be a mere product of chance. But in order to carry out a purpose, definite rules are required, from which one may not exempt oneself. Since the originality of talent constitutes an (but not the only) essential piece of the character of genius: shallow minds believe that they can show that they are budding geniuses in no better way than if they release themselves from the academic constraint of all rules, and believe that one parades better on an unruly horse than on a school-horse. Genius can only provide rich ***material*** for products of beautiful art; the processing of it and the ***form*** require a talent trained by the school, in order to make a use of it that can stand before the power of judgment. But if someone speaks {{ 187 }} and decides like a genius even in matters of the most careful rational investigation, it is completely ridiculous; one does not quite know whether one should laugh more at the charlatan who spreads so much haze around himself, where one can judge nothing clearly but can imagine all the more, or more at the public, which simplemindedly imagines that its inability clearly to recognize and grasp the masterpiece of insight arises from the fact that new truths are being thrown at it in whole masses, by contrast with which the detail (by means of measured explanations and academic examination of the principles) seems to it to be only bungling work.
