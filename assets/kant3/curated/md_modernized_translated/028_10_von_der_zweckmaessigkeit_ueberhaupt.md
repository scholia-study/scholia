## § 10. On purposiveness in general

If we set out to explain what an end is according to its transcendental determinations (presupposing nothing empirical, of the sort that the feeling of pleasure is, {{{ 220 }}}), then an end is a concept's object, in so far as this concept is regarded as the cause of that object (the real ground of the latter's possibility); and purposiveness (_forma finalis_) is the causality of a ***concept*** in view of its ***object***. Hence, wherever that which is thought to be possible as an effect only through a concept of that effect is not the mere cognition of an object but the very object — namely its form or existence — there one is thinking of an end. In this case the representation of the effect functions as the ground that determines its cause {{ 33 }}, preceding the cause itself. The consciousness of the causality of a representation with regard to the subject's state, so as ***to maintain*** the subject in this state, may here designate in general what is called pleasure; displeasure, by contrast, is that representation which contains in itself the ground for determining the representations' state into their own opposite (for warding them off or doing away with them).

The faculty of desire, to the extent that it can be determined solely by concepts — that is, to act in conformity with the representation of an end — would amount to the will. An object, a state of mind, or an action is likewise called purposive even when the representation of an end is not necessarily presupposed for its possibility, and this merely because its possibility can be explained and grasped by us only in so far as we lay at its ground a causality according to ends — that is, a will that, following the representation of a certain rule, would have arranged it in just this way. Purposiveness may thus be present even in the absence of an end, provided we do not situate the causes of such a form within a will, while nevertheless being able to render the account of its possibility comprehensible to ourselves only by tracing it back to a will. Now we do not always have need to grasp through reason (as regards its possibility) that which we observe. Thus we can at least observe a purposiveness as to form, even without placing an end {{ 34 }} (taken as the matter of the _nexus finalis_) at its ground, and can remark it in objects, although not otherwise than by means of reflection.
