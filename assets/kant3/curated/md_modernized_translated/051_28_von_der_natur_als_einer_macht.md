## § 28. On Nature as a Might

***Might*** is a capacity that is superior to great obstacles. The very same is called a ***dominion*** if it is also superior to the resistance of that which itself possesses might. Nature, considered in the aesthetic judgment as a might that has no dominion over us, is ***dynamically sublime***.

If nature is to be judged by us dynamically as sublime, then it must be represented as arousing fear (although, conversely, not every object that arouses fear is found to be sublime in our aesthetic judgment). For in aesthetic appraisal (without a concept), superiority over obstacles can be judged only according to the magnitude of the resistance. Now that which we strive to resist is an evil and, if we do not find our capacity a match for it, an object of fear. Thus for the aesthetic power of judgment nature can count as a might, hence as dynamically sublime, only insofar as it is considered an object of fear. {{ 103 }}

But one can consider an object as ***fearful*** without being afraid ***of*** it, namely if we judge it in such a way that we merely ***think*** of the case in which we might want to offer it resistance, and that then all resistance would be by far in vain. Thus the virtuous person fears God without being afraid of him, because he does not think of wanting to resist him and his commandments as a case that is to be apprehended on ***his*** part. {{{ 261 }}} But in every such case, which he thinks of as not in itself impossible, he recognizes him as fearful.

Whoever is afraid can no more judge about the sublime of nature than one who is taken up by inclination and appetite can judge about the beautiful. The former flees the sight of an object that instills dread in him; and it is impossible to find satisfaction in a terror that is meant in earnest. Hence the agreeableness arising from the cessation of a hardship is ***gladness***. This gladness, however, on account of the deliverance from a danger, is a gladness with the resolve never to expose oneself to it again; indeed one does not even like to think back on that sensation, far be it from one to seek out the occasion for it oneself.

Bold, overhanging, as it were threatening cliffs, thunderclouds towering up {{ 104 }} in the sky, advancing with lightning and thunderclaps, volcanoes in their full destructive violence, hurricanes with the devastation they leave behind, the boundless ocean set into a rage, a lofty waterfall of a mighty river, and the like, make our capacity to resist into an insignificant trifle in comparison with their might. But their sight only becomes all the more attractive the more fearful it is, provided only that we find ourselves in safety; and we gladly call these objects sublime, because they raise the strength of our soul above its usual middle measure and let us discover within ourselves a capacity to resist of an entirely different kind, which gives us the courage to be able to measure ourselves against the apparent omnipotence of nature.

For just as we found, in the immeasurability of nature and the insufficiency of our capacity to take a standard proportioned to the aesthetic estimation of the magnitude of its ***domain***, our own limitation, yet nevertheless also found in our faculty of reason at the same time another, non-sensible standard which has that very infinity under itself as a unity, against which everything in nature is small, and hence found in our mind a superiority over nature itself in its immeasurability: so too the irresistibility of its might, while making us, considered as natural beings, recognize our physical powerlessness, {{ 105 }} at the same time discloses a capacity for judging ourselves as independent of it, and a superiority over nature, on which is grounded a self-preservation of an entirely different kind than that which can be assailed and endangered by the nature outside us, {{{ 262 }}} whereby the humanity in our person remains undemeaned even though the human being would have to succumb to that dominion. In such a way nature is judged as sublime in our aesthetic judgment not insofar as it arouses fear, but rather because it calls forth our power (which is not nature) within us, to regard that for which we are concerned (goods, health, and life) as small and hence to regard its might (to which we are indeed subjected in respect of these things) as nevertheless not such a dominion over us and our personality to which we would have to bow if it came down to our highest principles and their assertion or abandonment. Thus here nature is called sublime merely because it raises the imagination to the presentation of those cases in which the mind can make palpable to itself the proper sublimity of its own vocation, even over nature.

This self-esteem loses nothing through the fact that we must see ourselves safe in order to feel this inspiring satisfaction; and hence, because the danger is not in earnest, the sublimity of our spiritual faculty might also (as it may seem) be just as little in earnest. {{ 106 }} For the satisfaction here concerns only the ***vocation*** of our capacity disclosed in such a case, just as the predisposition to it lies in our nature; while the development and exercise of it remains left to us and incumbent upon us. And in this there is truth, however much the human being, if he extends his reflection that far, may be conscious of his present actual powerlessness.

This principle does indeed seem too far-fetched and over-subtle, hence to be overstrained for an aesthetic judgment: yet the observation of the human being proves the contrary, and that it can lie at the ground of the most common appraisals, even though one is not always conscious of it. For what is it that is even to the savage an object of the greatest admiration? A human being who is not terrified, who is not afraid, hence who does not yield to danger, but at the same time sets to work with full deliberation and vigor. Even in the most civilized condition this preeminent esteem for the warrior remains; only that one in addition demands that he at the same time display all the virtues of peace, gentleness, compassion, and even fitting care for his own person: precisely because by this the indomitability of his mind in the face of danger is recognized. Hence one may dispute as much as one likes, in the comparison of the statesman with the general, about the preeminence of the {{ 107 }} {{{ 263 }}} respect that the one deserves over the other; the aesthetic judgment decides in favor of the latter. War itself, if it is conducted with order and reverence for civil rights, has something sublime about it, and at the same time makes the way of thinking of the people who conduct it in this way all the more sublime the more dangers it has been exposed to and has been able to assert itself courageously under: whereas a long peace tends to make the mere commercial spirit predominant, and with it base self-interest, cowardice, and effeminacy, and to debase the way of thinking of the people.

Against this resolution of the concept of the sublime, insofar as it is attributed to might, there seems to militate: that we are accustomed to represent God as presenting himself in the storm, in the tempest, in the earthquake, and the like, as in wrath, but at the same time also in his sublimity, where yet the conceit of a superiority of our mind over the effects, and, as it seems, even over the intentions, of such a might would be folly and sacrilege at once. Here it seems that no feeling of the sublimity of our own nature, but rather submission, dejection, and a feeling of utter powerlessness, is the disposition of mind befitting the appearance of such an object, and which is also usually accustomed to be bound up with the idea of it on the occasion of such a natural event. In religion in general, prostration, {{ 108 }} worship with a bowed head, with contrite, anxious gestures and voices, seems to be the only fitting conduct in the presence of the divinity, which most peoples have therefore also adopted and still observe. Yet this disposition of mind is also by far not bound up in itself and necessarily with the idea of the ***sublimity*** of a religion and its object. The human being who is really afraid, because he finds cause for it within himself, in that he is conscious of offending with his reprehensible disposition against a might whose will is irresistible and at the same time just, finds himself by no means in the frame of mind to admire the divine greatness, for which a disposition for calm contemplation and an entirely free judgment is required. Only then, when he is conscious of his upright disposition pleasing to God, do those effects of might serve to awaken in him the idea of the sublimity of this being, insofar as he recognizes in himself a sublimity of disposition conformable to its will, and is thereby raised above the fear of such effects of nature, which he does not regard as outbreaks {{{ 264 }}} of its wrath. Even humility, as a relentless appraisal of one's deficiencies, which otherwise, given the consciousness of good dispositions, could easily be cloaked over with the frailty of human nature, is a sublime disposition of mind, voluntarily {{ 109 }} to subject oneself to the pain of self-reproach in order gradually to eradicate the cause of it. In such a way alone is religion inwardly distinguished from superstition, the latter of which grounds in the mind not reverence for the sublime, but fear and dread of the overpowering being to whose will the terrified human being sees himself subjected without yet holding it in esteem: from which, of course, nothing but the soliciting of favor and ingratiation can spring, instead of a religion of a good course of life.

Thus sublimity is contained in no thing of nature, but only in our mind, insofar as we can become conscious of being superior to the nature within us and thereby also to the nature outside us (insofar as it has influence upon us). Everything that arouses this feeling in us, to which belongs the might of nature that calls forth our powers, is then called (although improperly) sublime; and only under the presupposition of this idea in us and in relation to it are we capable of attaining the idea of the sublimity of that being which produces deep respect in us not merely through its might, which it proves in nature, but still more through the capacity, placed in us, of judging that without fear and of thinking of our vocation as sublime above it.
