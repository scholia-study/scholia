## § 28. On nature as a might

***Might*** is a capacity that surpasses great obstacles. This same thing is called a ***dominion*** when it also surpasses the resistance of something that itself possesses might. Nature, regarded in aesthetic judgment as a might over us that yet wields no dominion, is ***dynamically sublime***.

For us to judge nature as dynamically sublime, it has to be represented as arousing fear (though not, conversely, that every object which arouses fear is found sublime in our aesthetic judgment). For in aesthetic judging (without a concept) superiority over obstacles can be judged only according to the magnitude of the resistance. Now that which we strive to resist is an ill, and, if we find our capacity unequal to it, an object of fear. Thus for the aesthetic power of judgment nature can count as a might, and hence as dynamically sublime, only insofar as it is regarded as an object of fear. {{ 103 }}

One can, however, regard an object as ***fearful*** without being afraid ***of*** it, namely when we judge it in such a way that we merely ***think*** the case in which we might wish to offer it resistance, and that then all resistance would be utterly in vain. So the virtuous person fears God without being afraid of him, since he does not think of wishing to resist him and his commandments as any case worrisome on ***his*** {{{ 261 }}} part. But for every such case that he thinks not impossible in itself, he recognizes it as fearful.

Whoever is afraid can pass no judgment at all on the sublimity of nature, just as little as one taken up by inclination and appetite can judge of the beautiful. The former flees the sight of an object that instills dread in him; and it is impossible to find satisfaction in a terror that were meant in earnest. Hence the agreeableness arising from the cessation of a hardship is ***gladness***. This, however, on account of deliverance from a danger, is a gladness accompanied by the resolve never to expose oneself to it again; indeed, one has no wish even to recall that sensation fondly, far from seeking out the occasion for it oneself.

Bold, overhanging, as it were menacing crags, thunderclouds towering up in the {{ 104 }} heavens, moving on with lightning and thunderclaps, volcanoes in all their destructive violence, hurricanes with the devastation they leave behind, the boundless ocean roused to fury, a lofty waterfall of a mighty river, and the like, make our capacity for resistance, in comparison with their might, into an insignificant trifle. But their sight only becomes the more attractive the more fearful it is, provided we find ourselves in safety; and we gladly call these objects sublime, because they raise the strength of the soul above its usual middling level and let us discover within ourselves a capacity for resistance of an entirely different kind, which gives us the courage to be able to measure ourselves against the seeming omnipotence of nature.

For just as, in the immeasurability of nature and the inadequacy of our capacity, we found our own limitation in taking a standard proportioned to the aesthetic estimation of the magnitude of its ***domain***, yet at the same time found also, in our faculty of reason, another, non-sensible standard, which has that very infinity beneath itself as a unit, against which everything in nature is small, and hence found in our mind a superiority over nature itself in its immeasurability: so too the irresistibility of its might, while it makes us, regarded as natural beings, recognize our physical {{ 105 }} impotence, at the same time discloses a capacity to judge ourselves as independent of it, and a superiority over nature on which is grounded a self-preservation of an entirely different kind from that which can be assailed and endangered by the nature outside {{{ 262 }}} us, whereby the humanity in our person remains undegraded, although the human being would have to succumb to that dominion. In this way nature is judged sublime in our aesthetic judgment not insofar as it arouses fear, but because it summons up in us our power (which is not nature), so as to regard what we are anxious about (possessions, health, and life) as small, and hence to regard its might (to which, in respect of these things, we are indeed subject) as nonetheless, for us and our personality, no such dominion that we should have to bow beneath it, when it came to our highest principles and their affirmation or abandonment. Thus nature is here called sublime merely because it raises the imagination to the presentation of those cases in which the mind can make palpable to itself the proper sublimity of its own vocation even above nature.

This self-esteem loses nothing by the fact that we must see ourselves in safety in order to feel this exhilarating satisfaction, and hence that, because the danger is not in earnest, the sublimity of our spiritual power might also (as it may seem) be just as little in earnest. {{ 106 }} For the satisfaction here concerns only the ***vocation*** of our capacity that reveals itself in such a case, so far as the predisposition to it lies in our nature; while its development and exercise remain left to us and incumbent upon us. And there is truth in this, however conscious the human being may be of his present actual impotence when he extends his reflection this far.

This principle does seem too far-fetched and over-subtle, and hence too extravagant for an aesthetic judgment; yet observation of the human being proves the opposite, and that it can underlie the most ordinary judgings, even if one is not always conscious of it. For what is it that is an object of the greatest admiration even to the savage? A human being who is not startled, who is not afraid, and so does not give way before danger, yet at the same time sets to work vigorously with full deliberation. Even in the most thoroughly civilized condition this preeminent esteem for the warrior remains; only that one additionally demands that he at the same time display all the virtues of peace — gentleness, compassion, and even a fitting care for his own person — precisely because in this the invincibility of his mind against danger is recognized. Hence one may dispute as much as one likes, in comparing the statesman with the general, over the superiority of the {{ 107 }} {{{ 263 }}} respect that the one deserves over the other; the aesthetic judgment decides for the latter. Even war, when it is conducted with order and a sacred regard for civil rights, has something sublime about it, and at the same time makes the way of thinking of a people that conducts it in this manner all the more sublime the more dangers it was exposed to and could courageously hold its ground under; whereas a long peace tends to let the mere spirit of commerce prevail, and along with it base self-interest, cowardice, and softness, and to debase the way of thinking of a people.

Against this resolution of the concept of the sublime, insofar as it is ascribed to might, it seems to count against this that we are accustomed to represent God, in the thunderstorm, the tempest, the earthquake, and the like, as showing himself in wrath yet at the same time in his sublimity, where nonetheless the conceit of a superiority of our mind over the effects, and, as it seems, even over the aims of such a might would be folly and sacrilege at once. Here it seems that no feeling of the sublimity of our own nature, but rather submission, dejection, and a feeling of utter impotence, is the frame of mind befitting the appearance of such an object, and one that is also customarily bound up with the idea of it in the case of such a natural occurrence. In religion generally, prostration, {{ 108 }} adoration with bowed head, with contrite, anxious gestures and voices, seems to be the only fitting conduct in the presence of the deity, which most peoples have therefore adopted and still observe. Yet this frame of mind is by no means bound up in itself and necessarily with the idea of the ***sublimity*** of a religion and its object. The human being who really is afraid, because he finds cause for it within himself, being conscious that with his reprehensible disposition he offends against a might whose will is irresistible and at the same time just, is by no means in the composure of mind required to admire the divine greatness, for which a mood of calm contemplation and an entirely free judgment is requisite. Only then, when he is conscious of his own sincere disposition pleasing to God, do those effects of might come to awaken in him the idea of this being's sublimity, insofar as he recognizes in himself a sublimity of disposition conforming to its will, and is thereby raised above the fear of such effects of nature, which he does not regard as outbursts {{{ 264 }}} of its wrath. Even humility, as an unsparing judging of one's own defects — which, given the consciousness of good dispositions, could otherwise easily be cloaked under the frailty of human nature — is a sublime frame of mind, voluntarily {{ 109 }} to submit oneself to the pain of self-reproach in order gradually to eradicate the cause of it. In this way alone does religion inwardly distinguish itself from superstition, the latter of which grounds in the mind not reverence for the sublime, but fear and dread before the overpowering being to whose will the terrified human being sees himself subjected without, however, holding it in high esteem — from which of course nothing but ingratiation and flattery can spring, in place of a religion of good conduct of life.

Thus sublimity is contained in no thing of nature, but only in our mind, insofar as we can become conscious of being superior to the nature within us, and thereby also to the nature outside us (insofar as it has an influence upon us). Everything that arouses this feeling in us — to which belongs the might of nature that calls forth our powers — is then called (though improperly) sublime; and only under the presupposition of this idea within us, and in relation to it, can we ascend to the idea of the sublimity belonging to that being which works within us a heartfelt respect not merely through its might, as displayed in nature, but still more through the capacity, laid within us, to judge that might without fear and to think of our vocation as sublime above it.
