## § 28. On nature as a might

***Might*** is a capacity that surpasses great obstacles. This same thing is called a ***dominion*** when it also surpasses the resistance of something that itself possesses might. Nature, regarded in aesthetic judgment as a might over us that yet wields no dominion, is ***dynamically sublime***.

If nature is to be judged by us as dynamically sublime, then it must be presented as inspiring fear (though the reverse does not hold, that every object inspiring fear is deemed sublime in our aesthetic judgment). For within aesthetic appraisal (which proceeds without a concept), superiority over obstacles can be gauged solely by the magnitude of the resistance. Now what we strive to withstand is an evil, and, should we find our powers unequal to it, it becomes an object of fear. Hence nature, for the aesthetic power of judgment, can pass as a might — and thus as dynamically sublime — only to the extent that it is looked upon as an object of fear. {{ 103 }}

One can, however, regard an object as ***fearful*** without being afraid ***of*** it, namely when we judge it in such a way that we merely ***think*** the case in which we might wish to offer it resistance, and that then all resistance would be utterly in vain. So the virtuous person fears God without being afraid of him, since he does not think of wishing to resist him and his commandments as any case worrisome on ***his*** {{{ 261 }}} part. But for every such case that he thinks not impossible in itself, he recognizes it as fearful.

Whoever is afraid can pass no judgment at all on the sublimity of nature, just as little as one taken up by inclination and appetite can judge of the beautiful. The former takes flight from the sight of any object that strikes dread into him; nor can any satisfaction be found in a terror meant in earnest. Hence the agreeableness arising from the cessation of a hardship is ***gladness***. This, however, on account of deliverance from a danger, is a gladness accompanied by the resolve never to expose oneself to it again; indeed, one has no wish even to recall that sensation fondly, far from seeking out the occasion for it oneself.

Bold, overhanging crags that all but menace us, thunderclouds heaping themselves up in the {{ 104 }} heavens and rolling onward amid lightning and thunder, volcanoes in the whole of their destroying force, hurricanes that leave devastation behind them, the boundless ocean thrown into an uproar, the lofty cascade of a mighty river, and things of this kind — set against their might, these shrink our power of resistance to an insignificant trifle. Yet the sight of them grows all the more compelling the more frightful it is, so long as we ourselves stand secure; and we readily name such objects sublime, since they lift the soul's fortitude above its ordinary measure and let us uncover within us a power to resist of a wholly different order, one that emboldens us to pit ourselves against the apparent all-power of nature.

For just as, faced with the immeasurability of nature and the insufficiency of our own capacity, we found our own limitation when we sought a measure suited to the aesthetic estimation of its ***domain***'s magnitude, yet at the same time found, in our power of reason, a second, non-sensible standard that keeps that very infinitude beneath it as one unit, before which the whole of nature is small, and so found in our mind a superiority over nature itself even in its immeasurability: so likewise the irresistibility of its might, though it makes us, viewed as beings of nature, acknowledge our physical {{ 105 }} powerlessness, at the same time uncovers in us a capacity to judge ourselves independent of it, together with a superiority over nature upon which rests a self-preservation of a wholly different sort than the one that can be challenged and imperiled by the nature beyond {{{ 262 }}} us; and in all this the humanity in our person is left undegraded, even though the human being would be bound to succumb to that dominion. In this manner nature, in our aesthetic judgment, is deemed sublime not on the ground that it excites fear, but because it calls forth in us a power of our own (which is no part of nature), so that we treat as trifling the things we are anxious about (goods, health, and life), and thereby treat its might (to which, as regards these matters, we are certainly subject) as being nonetheless, for us and our personhood, no such dominion as would require us to bow beneath it whenever our supreme principles and their upholding or surrender were at stake. Nature, then, is styled sublime in this place solely because it elevates the imagination toward exhibiting those very cases in which the mind is able to make felt to itself the sublimity proper to its own vocation, a sublimity even above nature.

This self-esteem loses nothing by the fact that we must see ourselves in safety in order to feel this exhilarating satisfaction, and hence that, because the danger is not in earnest, the sublimity of our spiritual power might also (as it may seem) be just as little in earnest. {{ 106 }} For the satisfaction at issue here bears only upon the ***vocation*** of our capacity that comes to light in such a case, inasmuch as the disposition toward it resides in our nature; whereas the developing and exercising of it are left to us and laid upon us as our charge. And there is truth in this, however conscious the human being may be of his present actual impotence when he extends his reflection this far.

This principle does indeed look too far-fetched and over-refined, and thus too exorbitant for an aesthetic judgment; and yet the study of the human being demonstrates the reverse, and reveals that it can lie at the base of the most commonplace assessments, however little one is always aware of it. For what, after all, is that which even to the savage counts as an object of the greatest admiration? A human being who is not startled, who is not afraid, and so does not give way before danger, yet at the same time sets to work vigorously with full deliberation. Even in the most highly civilized state this exceptional regard for the warrior endures; save that one demands besides that he should, together with it, exhibit every peaceful virtue — mildness, compassion, and even a becoming solicitude for his own person — and this precisely because it is here that the indomitability of his mind in the face of danger comes to be recognized. Hence one may dispute as much as one likes, in comparing the statesman with the general, over the superiority of the {{ 107 }} {{{ 263 }}} respect that the one deserves over the other; the aesthetic judgment decides for the latter. War itself, provided it is waged with order and a hallowed respect for the rights of citizens, carries something sublime within it, and renders the cast of mind of a people that wages it in such a fashion all the more sublime the greater the perils to which it stood exposed and beneath which it managed to bear itself bravely; whereas a protracted peace is wont to give the upper hand to the mere trading spirit, and with it low self-seeking, cowardice, and effeminacy, and to degrade a people's cast of mind.

It appears to militate against this analysis of the concept of the sublime, so far as the sublime is attributed to might, that we are wont to picture God — in the thunderstorm, in the tempest, in the earthquake, and the like — as presenting himself at once in wrath and in his sublimity, whereas the fancy of any ascendancy of our mind over these effects, and, so it appears, even over the designs of such a might, would be at one and the same time folly and sacrilege. In this case the frame of mind suited to the appearance of such an object seems to be, not any feeling of the sublimity belonging to our own nature, but rather submissiveness, despondency, and a sense of complete powerlessness — a disposition that is, moreover, ordinarily associated with the idea of that object whenever a natural event of this kind occurs. In religion generally, prostration, {{ 108 }} adoration with bowed head, with contrite, anxious gestures and voices, seems to be the only fitting conduct in the presence of the deity, which most peoples have therefore adopted and still observe. This disposition of mind, however, is in itself and of necessity very far from being tied to the idea of a religion's ***sublimity*** and of its object. That human being who is genuinely afraid, because he discovers within himself a ground for it — aware, that is, that through his blameworthy cast of mind he transgresses against a might whose will cannot be withstood and is at once just — stands in no way in that settledness of mind requisite for admiring the divine greatness, a thing that calls for a temper disposed to tranquil contemplation and for a wholly unconstrained judgment. It is solely then, once he becomes conscious of his own upright disposition pleasing to God, that those workings of might come to stir the idea of this being's sublimity in him — to the degree that he acknowledges within himself a loftiness of disposition answering to that being's will, and is by this means lifted above any fear of such workings of nature, which he does not take to be eruptions {{{ 264 }}} of its wrath. Humility, too — taken as an unsparing appraisal of one's own shortcomings, which, where the consciousness of good dispositions is present, might otherwise be readily glossed over as the frailty of human nature — is a sublime cast of mind: to subject oneself, of one's own accord, {{ 109 }} to the pain of self-reproach so as by degrees to root out the cause of it. In this way alone does religion inwardly distinguish itself from superstition, the latter of which grounds in the mind not reverence for the sublime, but fear and dread before the overpowering being to whose will the terrified human being sees himself subjected without, however, holding it in high esteem — from which of course nothing but ingratiation and flattery can spring, in place of a religion of good conduct of life.

Sublimity, accordingly, resides in no object of nature, but is contained in our mind alone, to the extent that we can grow conscious of standing superior to the nature within us, and by that very means to the nature outside us as well (insofar as it flows in upon us). Everything that arouses this feeling in us — to which belongs the might of nature that calls forth our powers — is then called (though improperly) sublime; and only under the presupposition of this idea within us, and in relation to it, can we ascend to the idea of the sublimity belonging to that being which works within us a heartfelt respect not merely through its might, as displayed in nature, but still more through the capacity, laid within us, to judge that might without fear and to think of our vocation as sublime above it.
