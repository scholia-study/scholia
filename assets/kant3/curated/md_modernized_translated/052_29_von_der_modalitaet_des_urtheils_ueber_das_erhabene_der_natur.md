## {{ 110 }} § 29. On the Modality of the Judgment about the Sublime of Nature

There are innumerable things of beautiful nature about which we may straightway require of everyone agreement of judgment with our own and also, without erring particularly, expect it; but with our judgment about the sublime in nature we cannot so easily promise ourselves the acceptance of others. For a far greater culture, not merely of the aesthetic power of judgment, but also of the faculties of cognition that lie at its ground, seems to be required in order to be able to pass a judgment about this preeminence of natural objects.

{{{ 265 }}} The disposition of the mind to the feeling of the sublime requires a receptivity of it for ideas; for it is precisely in the inadequacy of nature to the latter, hence only under the presupposition of them and of the straining of the imagination to treat nature as a schema for the latter, that there consists what is repellent for sensibility but is yet at the same time attractive: because it is a dominion that reason exercises over sensibility only in order to expand it commensurately with its own proper domain (the practical) and to let it look out upon the infinite, which is for sensibility an abyss. In fact, without the development of moral ideas, that which we, prepared through culture, {{ 111 }} call sublime will appear to the uncultivated human being merely as repellent. He will see, in the proofs of the dominion of nature in its destruction and in the great scale of its might, against which his own vanishes into nothing, nothing but hardship, danger, and distress that would surround the human being who was banished to such a place. Thus the good and otherwise sensible Savoyard peasant (as Mr. de Saussure relates) called all lovers of the ice-mountains fools, without hesitation. And who knows, too, whether he would have been so entirely wrong if that observer had undertaken the dangers to which he exposed himself here merely, as most travelers are accustomed to do, out of hobby, or in order to be able one day to give pathetic descriptions of them? But as it was, his aim was the instruction of human beings; and the soul-elevating sensation the excellent man both had and gave to the readers of his travels, into the bargain.

But just because the judgment about the sublime of nature requires culture (more than that about the beautiful), it is nevertheless not for that reason first produced by culture and merely introduced into society in a conventional manner; rather it has its foundation in human nature, and indeed in that which one can, together with sound understanding, at the same time require of everyone and demand from him, namely in the predisposition {{ 112 }} to the feeling for (practical) ideas, i.e., to the moral.

On this, now, is grounded the necessity of the assent of the judgment of others about the sublime to our own, which we at the same time include in this latter. For just as we reproach with a lack of ***taste*** the one who is indifferent in the appraisal of an object of nature that we find beautiful: so we say of the one who remains unmoved in the presence of that which we judge to be sublime, that he has no ***feeling***. Both, however, we demand of every human being and presuppose them in him too, if {{{ 266 }}} he has some culture: only with the difference that we demand the former of everyone straightway, because in it the power of judgment relates the imagination merely to the understanding as the faculty of concepts; but the second only under a subjective presupposition (which, however, we believe ourselves justified in requiring of everyone), namely that of the moral feeling in the human being, because in it the power of judgment relates the imagination to reason as the faculty of ideas, and we thereby also attribute necessity to this aesthetic judgment.

In this modality of the aesthetic judgments, namely their presumed necessity, lies a chief moment for the critique of the power of judgment. For it is precisely this that makes recognizable in them a principle a priori and raises them out of empirical psychology, in which they would otherwise remain buried among {{ 113 }} the feelings of gratification and pain (only with the meaningless epithet of a ***finer*** feeling), in order to place them, and by means of them the power of judgment, in the class of those which have principles _a priori_ at their ground, but as such to draw them over into transcendental philosophy.
