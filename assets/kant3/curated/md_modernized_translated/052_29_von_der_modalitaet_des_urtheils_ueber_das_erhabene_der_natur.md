## {{ 110 }} § 29. On the modality of the judgment on the sublime of nature

There are countless objects of beautiful nature regarding which we may straightaway require of everyone an agreement of judgment with our own and, without going much astray, may also expect it; but with our judgment about the sublime in nature we cannot so easily count on gaining acceptance from others. For a far greater culture seems to be required, not merely of the aesthetic power of judgment, but also of the cognitive faculties that underlie it, in order to be able to pass a judgment on this superiority of the objects of nature.

{{{ 265 }}} The attunement of the mind to the feeling of the sublime requires a receptivity of that mind for ideas; for it is precisely in the inadequacy of nature to the latter — hence only under the presupposition of these ideas and of the straining of the imagination in treating nature as a schema for them — that there lies what is repellent to sensibility yet at the same time attractive: because it is a dominion that reason exercises over sensibility, only in order to enlarge it in a manner suited to reason's own proper domain (the practical) and to let it look out toward the infinite, which for sensibility is an abyss. Indeed, without the development of moral ideas, that which we, prepared by culture {{ 111 }}, call sublime will strike the uncultivated person as merely repellent. In the evidences of nature's dominion in its devastation, and in the great scale of its might, against which his own vanishes into nothing, he will see nothing but hardship, danger, and distress that would beset the person banished to such a place. Thus the good, and otherwise sensible, Savoyard peasant (as Mr. de Saussure recounts) unhesitatingly called all lovers of the glaciers fools. And who knows whether he would have been so entirely wrong, if that observer had taken on the dangers to which he exposed himself here merely, as most travelers are wont to do, out of a hobbyist's enthusiasm, or in order one day to be able to give affecting descriptions of them? But as it stood, his intention was the instruction of humankind; and the soul-uplifting sensation was one that the excellent man both had himself and instilled, as something over and above their purchase, in the readers of his travels.

Yet even though the judgment about the sublime in nature requires culture (more than the judgment about the beautiful does), it is not on that account first generated by culture and merely introduced into society as a matter of convention; rather, it has its foundation in human nature, and indeed in that which, together with sound understanding, one may require of everyone and demand of him, namely in the predisposition {{ 112 }} to the feeling for (practical) ideas, that is, to the moral feeling.

On this, now, is grounded the necessity of others' judgment about the sublime agreeing with our own — a necessity that we include within our own judgment at the same time. For just as we charge the person who remains indifferent in judging an object of nature that we find beautiful with a lack of ***taste***, so we say of the person who stays unmoved by what we judge to be sublime that he has no ***feeling***. Both, however, we require of every human being, and we also presuppose it in him, if {{{ 266 }}} he has some degree of culture: only with this difference, that we demand the former straightaway of everyone, because in it the power of judgment refers the imagination merely to the understanding as the faculty of concepts, but the latter — because in it the power of judgment refers the imagination to reason as the faculty of ideas — only under a subjective presupposition (which, however, we believe ourselves entitled to require of everyone), namely that of the moral feeling in the human being, and thereby ascribe necessity to this aesthetic judgment as well.

In this modality of aesthetic judgments — namely their claimed necessity — lies a principal moment for the critique of the power of judgment. For it is precisely this that makes an a priori principle recognizable in them and lifts them out of empirical psychology, in which they would otherwise remain buried among {{ 113 }} the feelings of gratification and pain (distinguished only by the empty epithet of a ***finer*** feeling), so as to place them, and by their means the power of judgment, in the class of those that have principles _a priori_ at their basis, and, as such, to draw them over into transcendental philosophy.
