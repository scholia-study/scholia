## {{ 110 }} § 29. On the modality of the judgment on the sublime of nature

There are countless objects of beautiful nature regarding which we may straightaway require of everyone an agreement of judgment with our own and, without going much astray, may also expect it; but with our judgment about the sublime in nature we cannot so easily count on gaining acceptance from others. For to be capable of rendering a judgment upon this excellence in natural objects, there seems to be needed a culture far greater in degree — one belonging not solely to the aesthetic power of judgment but likewise to the cognitive faculties on which it rests.

{{{ 265 }}} For the mind to be tuned to the feeling of the sublime, it must be receptive to ideas; for what is forbidding to sensibility — though at the same time alluring — consists precisely in nature's incommensurability with those ideas, and thus arises only on the presupposition of them together with an exertion of the imagination that handles nature as a schema for them: since this is a force that reason wields upon sensibility, merely so as to expand it in keeping with reason's proper territory (the practical) and to make it gaze out upon the infinite, which to sensibility yawns as an abyss. In fact, to anyone still crude, what we — schooled beforehand by culture {{ 111 }} — term sublime will, absent any cultivation of moral ideas, appear nothing more than forbidding. In the evidences of nature's dominion in its devastation, and in the great scale of its might, against which his own vanishes into nothing, he will see nothing but hardship, danger, and distress that would beset the person banished to such a place. Thus the good, and otherwise sensible, Savoyard peasant (as Mr. de Saussure recounts) unhesitatingly called all lovers of the glaciers fools. And who knows whether he would have been so entirely wrong, if that observer had taken on the dangers to which he exposed himself here merely, as most travelers are wont to do, out of a hobbyist's enthusiasm, or in order one day to be able to give affecting descriptions of them? But as it stood, his intention was the instruction of humankind; and the soul-uplifting sensation was one that the excellent man both had himself and instilled, as something over and above their purchase, in the readers of his travels.

But although the judging of nature's sublimity calls for culture (to a greater degree than the judging of the beautiful), it is nevertheless not for that reason something that culture originally brought forth, nor a mere convention established within society; its ground lies instead in human nature itself — specifically in what, along with sound understanding, may be expected and demanded of each person alike, that is, in the disposition {{ 112 }} toward a feeling for (practical) ideas, in a word, toward what is moral.

On this, now, is grounded the necessity of others' judgment about the sublime agreeing with our own — a necessity that we include within our own judgment at the same time. For just as we reproach with a want of ***taste*** anyone who stays indifferent when appraising a natural object we find beautiful, so of whoever remains unmoved before something our judgment pronounces sublime we declare that he possesses no ***feeling***. Yet both we demand of every human being, and we likewise assume them present in him once {{{ 266 }}} he possesses some culture — with this one distinction: the first we require flatly of anyone at all, since in this the power of judgment ties the imagination purely to the understanding, taken as the faculty of concepts; the second, however, since therein the power of judgment relates the imagination to reason as the faculty of ideas, we require only on a subjective presupposition (one we nonetheless deem ourselves justified in expecting of anyone), namely the presupposition of moral feeling within the human being, and in this way we attach necessity to this aesthetic judgment too.

It is in this modality of aesthetic judgments — that is, in the necessity they presume to claim — that the critique of the power of judgment finds one of its chief moments. For it is just this that brings to light an a priori principle at work in them, raising them up out of empirical psychology — where they would otherwise stay buried, {{ 113 }} amid the feelings of enjoyment and pain (marked off merely by the vacuous label of a ***finer*** feeling) — in order to set them, and through them the power of judgment, within the class of those grounded upon principles _a priori_, and, as such, to carry them across into transcendental philosophy.
