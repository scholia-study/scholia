## {{ 396 }} § 84. On the final end of a world's existence — that is, of creation itself

***Final end*** is that end which needs no other as the condition of its possibility.

If, to account for the purposiveness of nature, one assumes its mere mechanism as the ground of explanation, then one cannot ask what the things in the world are there for; for on such an idealistic system it is then only a question of the physical possibility of the things (to think of which as ends would be a mere sophistry without an object): whether one now attributes this form of the things to chance or to a blind necessity, in both cases that {{ 397 }} question would remain empty. But if we take the connection of ends in the world to be real, and posit for it a special kind of causality, namely that of a cause ***acting intentionally***, then we cannot halt at the question why the things of the world (organized beings) have this or that form, or are set by nature into these or those relations with others; rather, since we are here positing an understanding that has to be viewed as the cause of the possibility of forms of the sort we genuinely encounter in things, we must likewise ask, in that same understanding, after the {{{ 435 }}} objective ground that could have determined this productive understanding to an effect of such a kind — which ground is then the final end for the sake of which things of that sort exist.

I said above that the final end is no end for effecting which, and for bringing forth in conformity with the idea of which, nature would be sufficient, since it is unconditioned. For within nature (as a being of sense) there is nothing whose determining ground, itself lying in nature, would not always be conditioned anew in its turn; and this is true not only of the nature outside us (the material one) but likewise of the nature within us (the thinking one): with the proviso, of course, that I am regarding in myself only what belongs to nature. But a thing that, by virtue of its objective character, is necessarily to exist as the final end of an intelligent cause must be of such a sort that, in the order of ends, it hangs upon no further condition {{ 398 }} than its idea alone.

Now we have only a single kind of being in the world whose causality is teleological, i.e., directed toward ends, and yet is at the same time so constituted that the law by which they must determine ends for themselves is represented by them as unconditioned and independent of any natural condition, though in itself as necessary. The being of this kind is the human being, though considered as a noumenon; the sole being of nature in which we can nonetheless cognize, as regards its own constitution, a supersensible faculty (***freedom***), and even the law of causality together with the object of that causality which it can set before itself as its highest end (the highest good in the world).

Of the human being, now — and likewise of every rational being in the world — insofar as he is a moral being, one can inquire no further as to the end (_quem in finem_) for which he exists. His existence has the highest end itself within it, to which, so far as he is able, he can subject the whole of nature, or at least in defiance of which he is not permitted to count himself subject to any natural influence. — Now if the things of the world, being dependent as regards their existence, require a supreme cause that acts according to ends, then the human being is creation's final end; for without him the chain of mutually subordinated ends would lack a complete foundation, and it is only in the human being — and even in him only as the subject of morality — {{ 399 }} that we meet with the unconditioned legislation with respect to ends, the legislation that {{{ 436 }}} alone therefore renders him capable of being a final end under which the whole of nature stands teleologically subordinated[^*].

[^*]: It would be possible for the happiness of the rational beings in the world to be an end of nature, and in that case it would also be its ***ultimate*** end. At any rate, one cannot discern _a priori_ why nature ought not to have been so ordered, since through its mechanism such an effect would, as far as we can tell, be entirely possible. But morality, and a causality according to ends subordinated to it, is utterly impossible through natural causes; for the principle of its determination to action is supersensible, and is thus the only possible thing in the order of ends that is absolutely unconditioned with respect to nature, and which alone thereby qualifies its subject to be the ***final end*** of creation, the end under which all of nature stands subordinated. — ***Happiness***, by contrast, as was shown in the preceding § on the testimony of experience, is not even an ***end of nature*** with respect to human beings in preference to other creatures: far from it that it should be a ***final end of creation***. Human beings may always make it into their ultimate subjective end. But when I inquire after the final end of creation: For what purpose did human beings have to exist? then what is in question is an objective, supreme end of the sort that the highest reason would demand for its act of creation. If one now answers this: So that beings may exist to whom that supreme cause can do good, then one contradicts the condition to which the reason of the human being itself subjects his most heartfelt wish for happiness (namely, agreement with his own inner moral legislation). This proves that happiness can be only a conditioned end, and hence that the human being can be the final end of creation only as a moral being; while, as concerns his state, happiness stands connected with it only as a consequence in proportion to his agreement with that end, as the end of his existence.
