## {{ 396 }} § 84. On the final end of the existence of a world, i.e., of creation itself

A ***final end*** is that end which needs no other as the condition of its possibility.

If for the purposiveness of nature the mere mechanism of it is assumed as the ground of explanation, then one cannot ask: what the things in the world are there for; for in that case, according to such an idealistic system, the talk is only of the physical possibility of things (to think of which as ends would be mere sophistry without an object): whether one now interprets this form of things as due to chance or to blind necessity, in both cases {{ 397 }} that question would be empty. But if we assume the connection of ends in the world to be real and assume for it a special kind of causality, namely that of an ***intentionally acting*** cause, then we cannot stop at the question: what the things of the world (organized beings) have this or that form for, are placed by nature into this or that relation to others; but since once an understanding is thought which must be regarded as the cause of the possibility of such forms as are actually found in things, then in this very same understanding the {{{ 435 }}} objective ground must also be inquired after which could have determined this productive understanding to an effect of this kind, which then is the final end for which such things are there.

I have said above: that the final end is no end for the effecting and producing of which, in accordance with the idea of it, nature would be sufficient, because it is unconditioned. For there is nothing in nature (as a being of sense) for which the determining ground present in itself would not always be in turn conditioned; and this holds not merely of the nature outside us (the material), but also within us (the thinking): it being well understood that I consider in myself only that which is nature. But a thing which, on account of its objective constitution, is necessarily to exist as the final end of an intelligent cause must be of such a kind that in the order of ends it is dependent on no further condition {{ 398 }} than merely its idea.

Now we have only a single kind of being in the world whose causality is teleological, i.e., directed toward ends, and yet at the same time so constituted that the law in accordance with which they have to determine ends for themselves is represented by themselves as unconditioned and independent of natural conditions, but in itself as necessary. The being of this kind is the human being, but considered as a noumenon; the only natural being in which we can nevertheless recognize, on the side of its own constitution, a supersensible faculty (***freedom***) and even the law of causality together with the object of it that it can set for itself as the highest end (the highest good in the world).

Now of the human being (and so of every rational being in the world), as a moral being, it cannot be asked further: for what (_quem in finem_) he exists. His existence has the highest end itself within it, to which, as far as he is able, he can subject all of nature, at least against which he may not hold himself subjected to any influence of nature. — If now things of the world, as beings dependent according to their existence, need a supreme cause acting according to ends, then the human being is the final end of creation; for without him the chain of mutually subordinated ends would not be completely grounded; and only in the human being, but in him too only as the subject of morality, {{ 399 }} is the unconditioned legislation with regard to ends to be encountered, which therefore {{{ 436 }}} alone makes him capable of being a final end to which the whole of nature is teleologically subordinated[^*].

[^*]: It would be possible that happiness of the rational beings in the world were an end of nature, and then it would also be its ***ultimate*** end. At least one cannot see _a priori_ why nature should not be so arranged, because through its mechanism this effect would, at least so far as we can see, be quite possible. But morality, and a causality according to ends subordinated to it, is absolutely impossible through natural causes; for the principle of its determination to action is supersensible, is thus the only thing possible in the order of ends that is absolutely unconditioned with regard to nature and that thereby alone qualifies its subject to be the ***final end*** of creation, to which the whole of nature is subordinated. — ***Happiness***, on the contrary, is, as was shown in the preceding § according to the testimony of experience, not even an ***end of nature*** with regard to human beings in preference to other creatures: far from its being supposed to be a ***final end of creation***. Human beings may always make it into their ultimate subjective end. But if I ask after the final end of creation: For what did human beings have to exist? then the talk is of an objective supreme end, such as the highest reason would require for its creation. If one now answers to this: So that there might exist beings to whom that supreme cause could do good, then one contradicts the condition to which the reason of the human being himself subjects his innermost wish for happiness (namely, agreement with his own inner moral legislation). This proves: that happiness can be only a conditioned end, the human being thus can be the final end of creation only as a moral being; but as concerns his state, happiness stands in connection only as a consequence in proportion to the agreement with that end, as the end of his existence.
