## {{ 396 }} § 84. On the final end of a world's existence — that is, of creation itself

***Final end*** is the end that requires no other to furnish the condition of its possibility.

If, to account for the purposiveness of nature, one assumes its mere mechanism as the ground of explanation, then one cannot ask what the things in the world are there for; for on such an idealistic system it is then only a question of the physical possibility of the things (to think of which as ends would be a mere sophistry without an object): whether one now attributes this form of the things to chance or to a blind necessity, in both cases that {{ 397 }} question would remain empty. If, however, we regard the connection of ends in the world as something real and grant it a causality of its own peculiar kind — namely that of a cause ***acting intentionally*** — then the question cannot rest with why the world's things (organized beings) possess this or that form, or stand placed by nature in these or those relations to others; for since an understanding is here being thought — one to which the possibility of the forms actually encountered in things has to be traced back as to their cause — then in that same understanding we must also inquire after the {{{ 435 }}} objective ground capable of having determined this productive understanding toward an effect of that kind — a ground which is then the final end for which things of the like are present.

I said above that the final end is no end for effecting which, and for bringing forth in conformity with the idea of which, nature would be sufficient, since it is unconditioned. For within nature (as a being of sense) there is nothing whose determining ground, itself lying in nature, would not always be conditioned anew in its turn; and this is true not only of the nature outside us (the material one) but likewise of the nature within us (the thinking one): with the proviso, of course, that I am regarding in myself only what belongs to nature. A thing, however, which by reason of its objective constitution must necessarily exist as the final end of a cause endowed with understanding, has to be of such a nature that, in the order of ends, it depends upon no other condition {{ 398 }} than its idea alone.

Now we have only a single kind of being in the world whose causality is teleological, i.e., directed toward ends, and yet is at the same time so constituted that the law by which they must determine ends for themselves is represented by them as unconditioned and independent of any natural condition, though in itself as necessary. A being of this kind is the human being, yet taken as noumenon — the sole natural being in whom we can, from the side of its own constitution, come to cognize a supersensible faculty (***freedom***), and indeed the law of causality along with the object belonging to it, an object it is able to hold before itself as its supreme end (the highest good in the world).

Of the human being, now — and likewise of every rational being in the world — insofar as he is a moral being, one can inquire no further as to the end (_quem in finem_) for which he exists. His existence has the highest end itself within it, to which, so far as he is able, he can subject the whole of nature, or at least in defiance of which he is not permitted to count himself subject to any natural influence. — Now if the things of the world, being dependent as regards their existence, require a supreme cause that acts according to ends, then the human being is creation's final end; for without him the chain of mutually subordinated ends would lack a complete foundation, and it is only in the human being — and even in him only as the subject of morality — {{ 399 }} that we meet with the unconditioned legislation with respect to ends, the legislation that {{{ 436 }}} alone therefore renders him capable of being a final end under which the whole of nature stands teleologically subordinated[^*].

[^*]: It would be possible that the happiness of rational beings within the world should count as an end of nature, and then it would likewise be its ***ultimate*** end. At any rate, one cannot discern _a priori_ why nature ought not to have been so ordered, since through its mechanism such an effect would, as far as we can tell, be entirely possible. But morality, together with a causality in accordance with ends and subordinate to it, is flatly impossible by way of natural causes; for the principle determining it to action is supersensible, and hence is the sole possibility within the order of ends which, in regard to nature, is unconditioned outright, and which for that very reason is what alone makes its subject fit to be the ***final end*** of creation — the end to which nature as a whole stands subordinate. — ***Happiness***, by contrast, as was shown in the preceding § on the testimony of experience, is not even an ***end of nature*** with respect to human beings in preference to other creatures: far from it that it should be a ***final end of creation***. Human beings may always make it into their ultimate subjective end. But when I inquire after the final end of creation: For what purpose did human beings have to exist? then what is in question is an objective, supreme end of the sort that the highest reason would demand for its act of creation. Should one now reply: In order that beings might exist upon whom that supreme cause could confer benefit, one thereby runs counter to the very condition under which the human being's own reason places his innermost longing for happiness (namely, its accord with his own inner moral legislation). This shows that happiness can amount to no more than a conditioned end, so that the human being is qualified as creation's final end solely in his character as a moral being; whereas, so far as his state is concerned, happiness enters in only as a consequence, measured by the degree of his accord with that end, the end of his very existence.
