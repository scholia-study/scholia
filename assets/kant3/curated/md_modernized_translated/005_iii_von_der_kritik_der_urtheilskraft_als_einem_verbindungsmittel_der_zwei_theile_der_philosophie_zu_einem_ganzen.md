## III. On the Critique of the Power of Judgment, as a Means for Combining the Two Parts of Philosophy into a Whole

The critique of the faculties of cognition with regard to what they can accomplish ***a priori*** has properly no domain with regard to objects: because it is not a doctrine, but has only to investigate whether and how a doctrine is possible through them according to the circumstances in which our faculties stand. Its field extends to all the pretensions of these faculties, in order to set them within the bounds of their legitimacy. But what cannot enter into the division of philosophy can yet enter as a {{ XXI }} principal part into the critique of the pure faculty of cognition in general, if, namely, it contains principles that are in themselves serviceable neither for theoretical nor for practical use.

The concepts of nature, which contain the ground for all theoretical cognition a priori, rested on the legislation of the understanding. — The concept of freedom, which contained the ground for all sensibly unconditioned practical precepts _a priori_, rested on the legislation of reason. Both faculties, therefore, besides the fact that they can be applied according to their logical form to principles of whatever origin these may be, have in addition each its own legislation {{{ 177 }}} with respect to content, above which there is no other (_a priori_), and which therefore justifies the division of philosophy into the theoretical and the practical.

But in the family of the higher faculties of cognition there is still a middle term between the understanding and reason. This is the ***power of judgment***, of which one has cause to suppose, by analogy, that it too might just as well contain in itself _a priori_, if not indeed a legislation of its own, yet a principle of its own for seeking laws, an at all events merely subjective one: which, even if no field of objects belonged to it as its domain, can nevertheless have some sort of ground and a certain constitution of it, for which {{ XXII }} precisely this principle alone might be valid.

But to this there is added still (judging by analogy) a new ground for bringing the power of judgment into connection with another order of our powers of representation, which seems to be of even greater importance than that of its kinship with the family of the faculties of cognition. For all powers or capacities of the soul can be reduced to the three that cannot be further derived from a common ground: the ***faculty of cognition***, the ***feeling of pleasure and displeasure***, and the ***faculty of desire***[^*]. {{{ 178 }}} For the faculty of cognition the understanding alone is legislative, if {{ XXIII }} the former (as must also be the case if it is considered for itself, without admixture {{ XXIV }} with the faculty of desire) is related as a faculty of a ***theoretical cognition*** to nature, with regard to which alone (as appearance) it is possible for us to give laws through concepts of nature _a priori_, which are properly pure concepts of the understanding. — For the faculty of desire, as a higher faculty in accordance with the concept of freedom, reason alone (in which alone this concept has its place) is _a priori_ legislative. — Now between the faculty of cognition and the faculty of desire there is contained the feeling of pleasure, just as the power of judgment is contained between the understanding and reason. It is therefore at least provisionally to be supposed that the power of judgment likewise contains for itself a principle _a priori_ and, since with the faculty of desire pleasure or displeasure is necessarily bound up (whether, as in the case of the lower, it precedes the principle of that faculty, or, as in the case of the {{ XXV }} {{{ 179 }}} higher, follows only from the determination of it by the moral law), it will likewise effect a transition from the pure faculty of cognition, i.e., from the domain of the concepts of nature, to the domain of the concept of freedom, just as in its logical use it makes possible the transition from the understanding to reason.

Thus although philosophy can be divided into only two principal parts, the theoretical and the practical; although everything that we might have to say about the proper principles of the power of judgment would have to be reckoned in it to the theoretical part, i.e., to rational cognition according to concepts of nature: yet the critique of pure reason, which must constitute all of this before the undertaking of that system for the sake of its possibility, consists of three parts: the critique of the pure understanding, of the pure power of judgment, and of pure reason, faculties that are called pure because they are _a priori_ legislative.

[^*]: It is useful: to attempt, in the case of concepts that one uses as empirical principles, when one has cause to suppose that they stand in kinship _a priori_ with the pure faculty of cognition, a transcendental definition for the sake of this relation: namely through pure categories, insofar as these alone already sufficiently indicate the distinction of the concept at hand from others. In this one follows the example of the mathematician, who leaves the empirical data of his problem undetermined and brings only their relation in the pure synthesis of them under the concepts of pure arithmetic, and thereby generalizes the solution of it. — One has reproached me on account of a similar procedure (Crit. of Pract. R., p. 16 [9] of the Preface) and has censured the definition of the faculty of desire as ***the faculty for being, through its representations, the cause of the reality of the objects of these representations***: because mere wishes would also be desires, yet of which everyone admits that through them alone he cannot bring forth their object. — But this proves nothing more than that there are also desires in the human being through which he stands in contradiction with himself: in that he works through his representation alone toward the production of the object, from which he can yet expect no success, because he is conscious that his mechanical powers (if I may so call the non-psychological ones), which would have to be determined by that representation in order to bring about the object (and so indirectly), are either not sufficient, or even aim at something impossible, e.g., to make what has happened not to have happened (_O mihi praeteritos, etc._) or in impatient waiting to be able to annihilate the interval until the longed-for moment. — Although in such fantastic desires we are indeed conscious of the insufficiency of our representations (or even of their unfitness) to be the ***cause*** of their objects: yet the relation of them as cause, and hence the representation of their ***causality***, is contained in every ***wish*** and is especially visible when this is an affect, namely ***longing***. For such affects prove, by the fact that they expand the heart and make it languish and thus exhaust the powers, that the powers are repeatedly strained by representations, but allow the mind, in view of the impossibility, to sink back again incessantly into exhaustion. Even prayers for the averting of great and, as far as one can see, unavoidable evils, and many superstitious means for attaining naturally impossible ends, prove the causal relation of representations to their objects, which even by the consciousness of their insufficiency for the effect cannot be held back from the striving for it. — But why the propensity to consciously empty desires has been laid into our nature is an anthropological-teleological question. It seems: that, were we not to be determined to the exertion of our powers before we had assured ourselves of the sufficiency of our faculty for the production of an object, these powers would for the most part remain unused. For commonly we first come to know our powers only by trying them. This deception in empty wishes is therefore only the consequence of a beneficent arrangement in our nature.
