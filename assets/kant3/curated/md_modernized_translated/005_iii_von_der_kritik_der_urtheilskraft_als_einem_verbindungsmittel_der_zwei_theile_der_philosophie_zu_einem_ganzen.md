## III. On the critique of the power of judgment, as a means of connecting philosophy's two parts into one whole

The critique of the faculties of cognition, in view of what they are able to achieve ***a priori***, possesses in truth no domain with respect to objects: for it is no doctrine, but has only to examine whether, and how, given the constitution of our faculties, a doctrine is possible through them. Its field extends to all the pretensions of those faculties, so as to confine them within the bounds of their legitimacy. But something that cannot enter into the division of philosophy may nonetheless enter, as a {{ XXI }} principal part, the critique of the pure faculty of cognition taken generally, provided, that is, that it holds principles which, on their own, serve neither a theoretical nor a practical use.

The concepts of nature, which hold within themselves the a priori ground for all theoretical cognition, had their basis in the legislation of the understanding. — The concept of freedom, for its part, which enclosed the ground of all sensibly-unconditioned practical precepts _a priori_, took its basis from reason's own legislation. Both faculties, accordingly, quite apart from the fact that in their logical form they admit of application to principles of whatever origin, each possess in addition a legislation peculiarly their own {{{ 177 }}} with respect to content—one above which none other (_a priori_) stands, and which for that reason vindicates philosophy's partition into a theoretical and a practical branch.

But within the family that gathers the higher faculties of cognition there still stands, after all, an intermediate member between the understanding and reason. This middle member is the ***power of judgment***, of which one has grounds, reasoning by analogy, to conjecture that it too—though not indeed a legislation of its own—may yet harbor within itself, _a priori_, a principle proper to it for the seeking of laws, at most a merely subjective one: a principle which, even if no field of objects were to fall to it as its domain, can nonetheless possess some ground or other, together with a certain constitution of that ground, for which {{ XXII }} precisely this principle, and it alone, might hold valid.

But here (again to judge by analogy) a fresh ground arises for linking the power of judgment to another order among our powers of representation—an order that appears more important still than the kinship it holds with the family of the faculties of cognition. For every power or capacity of the soul may be led back to those three which permit of no further derivation from any ground held in common: the ***faculty of cognition***, the ***feeling of pleasure and displeasure***, as well as the ***faculty of desire***[^*]. {{{ 178 }}} As regards the faculty of cognition, the understanding by itself does the legislating, whenever {{ XXIII }} that faculty—as must in fact occur when one takes it on its own, unmixed {{ XXIV }} with the faculty of desire—is brought to bear on nature as the faculty of a ***theoretical cognition***, nature being the one thing in regard to which (as appearance) we are able to prescribe laws by means of concepts of nature _a priori_, concepts that are strictly speaking pure concepts of the understanding. — When it comes to the faculty of desire, regarded as a higher faculty that follows the concept of freedom, it is reason alone—the sole seat of that concept—that legislates _a priori_. — Now the feeling of pleasure lies between the faculty of cognition and the faculty of desire, precisely as the power of judgment lies between the understanding and reason. Hence one may at least provisionally conjecture that the power of judgment, too, holds within itself a principle _a priori_, and that—since pleasure or displeasure attaches necessarily to the faculty of desire (be it that, as with the lower faculty, it runs ahead of that faculty's principle, or that, as with the {{ XXV }} {{{ 179 }}} higher one, it issues solely from that faculty's being determined through the moral law)—it will in like manner bring about a transition out of the pure faculty of cognition—that is, out of the domain of the concepts of nature—across into the domain of the concept of freedom, much as, in its logical employment, it renders the passage from the understanding over to reason possible.

And so, although philosophy admits of division into no more than two principal parts, the theoretical and the practical; although everything we might wish to say concerning the power of judgment's own principles would have to be reckoned, within that division, under the theoretical part, that is, under rational cognition governed by concepts of nature: the critique of pure reason, which must establish all of this beforehand, ahead of any attempt at that system and for the sake of its very possibility, is nonetheless made up of three parts—a critique of the pure understanding, a critique of the pure power of judgment, and a critique of the pure reason—these faculties being termed pure precisely because their legislation is _a priori_.

[^*]: It is of use, for concepts that one employs as empirical principles, when one has reason to surmise that they stand in kinship _a priori_ with the pure faculty of cognition, to attempt, on account of this relation, a transcendental definition: namely by means of the pure categories, insofar as these of themselves already indicate sufficiently how the concept in question differs from others. In this one follows the mathematician's example, who leaves the empirical data of his task undetermined and brings only their relation, in the pure synthesis of those data, under the concepts of pure arithmetic, thereby generalizing the solution he reaches. — A similar procedure (Crit. of Pract. R., p. 16 [9] of the preface) has drawn a reproach upon me, and my definition of the faculty of desire—as the ***faculty of being, by way of its representations, the cause of the actual existence of the objects belonging to those representations***—has been faulted: on the grounds that mere wishes, too, would count as desires, though everyone grants that by these alone he can never bring their object into being. — Yet this establishes nothing beyond the fact that human beings harbor desires as well by which a person falls into contradiction with himself: for through his representation alone he strives toward bringing the object forth, while from that striving he can look for no success, since he knows full well that his mechanical forces (if I may so name the non-psychological ones), which would need to be determined by that representation so as to produce the object (and hence indirectly), are either insufficient or else reach for something impossible—for instance, to render what has occurred as though it had not occurred (_O mihi praeteritos, etc._), or, in restless waiting, to be able to blot out the span of time leading up to the longed-for moment. — Although in such fanciful desires we remain conscious that our representations fall short (or are even wholly unfit) for serving as the ***cause*** of their objects: the reference of those representations as cause, and thus the representation of their ***causality***, is nevertheless contained in every ***wish***, and shows itself above all when the wish happens to be an affect—namely ***longing***. For such longings prove, by the very way in which they distend the heart and make it wither, and so exhaust the powers, that the powers are repeatedly tensed anew by representations, but that they let the mind, in view of the impossibility, sink back ceaselessly again into lassitude. Even the prayers offered to avert grievous ills that, so far as we can tell, are unavoidable, along with many a superstitious device for reaching ends impossible by nature, testify to the causal bearing of representations upon their objects—a bearing that not even the awareness of its inadequacy to the effect can deter from the striving toward it. — But why the propensity to consciously empty desires has been placed in our nature is an anthropological-teleological question. It seems that, were we not to be determined to the exertion of our powers until we had first assured ourselves of the adequacy of our capacity for producing an object, these powers would for the most part remain unused. For commonly we first come to know our powers only by trying them out. This deception in empty wishes is thus merely the consequence of a beneficent arrangement in our nature.
