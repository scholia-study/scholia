## IV. On the Power of Judgment, as an _a priori_ Legislative Faculty

The power of judgment in general is the faculty for thinking the particular as contained under the universal. If the universal (the rule, {{ XXVI }} the principle, the law) is given, then the power of judgment, which subsumes the particular under it, (even if, as transcendental power of judgment, it specifies _a priori_ the conditions in accordance with which alone something can be subsumed under that universal) is ***determining***. But if only the particular is given, for which it is to find the universal, then the power of judgment is merely ***reflecting***.

The determining power of judgment, under universal transcendental laws that the understanding gives, is merely subsuming; the law is prescribed to it _a priori_, and it therefore has no need to think out a law for itself in order to be able to subordinate the particular in nature to the universal. — But there are so manifold forms of nature, so many modifications, as it were, of the universal transcendental concepts of nature, which are left undetermined by those laws that the pure understanding gives _a priori_, because these go only to the possibility of a nature (as an object of the senses) in general, that for them there must yet {{{ 180 }}} also be laws, which, although as empirical they may be contingent in accordance with ***our*** insight of the understanding, must yet, if they are to be called laws (as the concept of a nature also requires), be regarded as necessary on the basis of a principle of the unity of the manifold, even if this principle is unknown to us. — The reflecting power of judgment, which has the obligation of ascending from the particular in nature to the universal, {{ XXVII }} therefore requires a principle that it cannot borrow from experience, because it is precisely to ground the unity of all empirical principles under likewise empirical but higher principles, and thus the possibility of the systematic subordination of them to one another. Such a transcendental principle, therefore, the reflecting power of judgment can only give as a law to itself, it cannot take it from elsewhere (because it would then be the determining power of judgment), nor prescribe it to nature: because reflection on the laws of nature is directed according to nature, while nature is not directed according to the conditions in accordance with which we strive to acquire a concept of it that is, with regard to nature, entirely contingent.

Now this principle can be no other than this: that, since universal laws of nature have their ground in our understanding, which prescribes them to nature (although only according to the universal concept of it as nature), the particular empirical laws, with regard to that which is left undetermined in them by the former, must be considered in accordance with such a unity as if an understanding (even if not ours) had likewise given them for the sake of our faculties of cognition, in order to make possible a system of experience according to particular laws of nature. Not as if in this way such an understanding actually had to be assumed (for it is only the reflecting power of judgment to which this idea serves as a principle, for reflecting, not for determining); {{ XXVIII }} rather this faculty thereby gives a law only to itself and not to nature.

Now since the concept of an object, insofar as it at the same time contains the ground of the reality of this object, is called the end, and the agreement of a thing with that constitution of things which is possible only according to ends is called the ***purposiveness*** of its form: so the principle of the power of judgment, with regard to the form of the things of nature under empirical laws in general, is the ***purposiveness of nature*** in its manifoldness. That is, nature is, through this concept, {{{ 181 }}} represented as if an understanding contained the ground of the unity of the manifold of its empirical laws.

The purposiveness of nature is therefore a special concept _a priori_ that has its origin solely in the reflecting power of judgment. For one cannot ascribe to the products of nature anything such as a relation of nature in them to ends, but can only use this concept in order to reflect on them with regard to the connection of appearances in nature that is given according to empirical laws. This concept is also entirely distinct from that of practical purposiveness (of human art or also of morals), although it is indeed thought in accordance with an analogy with the latter.
