## IV. On the power of judgment as a faculty that legislates _a priori_

The power of judgment in general is the faculty of thinking the particular as falling under a universal. When a universal is given (a rule, {{ XXVI }} a principle, a law), the power of judgment that subsumes the particular beneath it (even where, as transcendental power of judgment, it states _a priori_ the conditions under which alone subsumption under that universal can occur) is ***determining***. But where only the particular is given, for which it is to find the universal, the power of judgment is merely ***reflecting***.

The determining power of judgment, working under the universal transcendental laws that the understanding furnishes, does no more than subsume; its law is marked out for it _a priori_, so that it need not think up any law of its own in order to bring the particular in nature under the universal. — Yet nature exhibits such a diversity of forms — so many modifications, as it were, of the universal transcendental concepts of nature, forms left undetermined by the laws that the pure understanding provides _a priori_ (since those laws touch only the possibility of a nature as such, as an object of the senses) — that there must nonetheless {{{ 180 }}} be laws for these forms as well; laws that, being empirical, may well strike ***our*** understanding as contingent, yet which, if they are to count as laws (as indeed the very concept of a nature demands), must all the same be taken as necessary on the strength of some principle that unifies the manifold, even if that principle remains unknown to us. — The reflecting power of judgment, whose {{ XXVII }} charge it is to rise from what is particular in nature to what is universal, accordingly requires a principle that no experience can furnish, since its very office is to ground the unity of all empirical principles under principles that are themselves empirical but higher, and hence to ground the possibility of ordering them systematically one beneath another. Such a transcendental principle, then, the reflecting power of judgment can only prescribe to itself; it may not draw it from any other source (for it would otherwise be a determining power of judgment), nor may it dictate such a principle to nature; for reflection upon nature's laws is directed by nature, while nature is not directed by the conditions under which we labor to form a concept of it — a concept that, measured against those conditions, is wholly contingent.

Now this principle can be none other than this: since the universal laws of nature are grounded in our own understanding, which lays them down for nature (albeit only in accordance with the general concept of nature as such), the particular empirical laws must — with respect to whatever those universal laws leave undetermined in them — be regarded under a unity such as they would possess if some understanding (though not our own) had furnished them for the sake of our own faculties of cognition, in order to render possible a system of experience governed by particular laws of nature. Not that on this basis such an understanding must actually be presupposed (for it is solely the reflecting power of judgment for which this idea functions as a principle — a principle for reflection, not for determination); {{ XXVIII }} rather, by this means the faculty prescribes a law to itself alone, and not to nature.

Since the concept of an object — insofar as it also holds within it the ground of that object's actuality — is termed the end, while the accordance of a thing with that constitution of things which is possible only in accordance with ends is termed the ***purposiveness*** of its form: the principle of the power of judgment, as regards the form of the things of nature under empirical laws in general, is the ***purposiveness of nature*** in its manifold variety. That is to say, by means of this concept nature is {{{ 181 }}} represented as if some understanding contained the ground of the unity of the manifold of its empirical laws.

Nature's purposiveness is thus a special concept _a priori_ whose sole origin lies in the reflecting power of judgment. For one cannot impute anything of this kind — a relating of nature within its products to ends — to the products of nature themselves; one can only put the concept to use in reflecting upon them with a view to the linkage of appearances in nature that is given under empirical laws. This concept, moreover, is wholly distinct from that of practical purposiveness (in human art, or indeed in morals), notwithstanding that it is conceived on an analogy with it.
