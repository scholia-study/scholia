## § 21. Whether one can with good ground presuppose a common sense

Cognitions and judgments must, together with the conviction that accompanies them, be capable of being universally communicated; for otherwise no agreement with the object would belong to them: they would one and all be a merely subjective play of the powers of representation, just as skepticism demands. But if cognitions are to be capable of being communicated, then the state of mind, i.e., the disposition of the powers of cognition for a cognition in general, and indeed that proportion which is fitting for a representation (through which an object is given to us) in order to make a cognition out of it, must also be capable of being universally communicated: because without this as the subjective condition of cognizing, the cognition, as an effect, could not arise. And this also actually happens every time when a given object, by means of the senses, brings the imagination into activity for the composition of the manifold, but the imagination brings the understanding into activity for the unity of the manifold in concepts. But this disposition of the powers of cognition has a different proportion according to the difference of the objects that are given. Nevertheless, there must be one {{ 66 }} in which this inner relation for the animation (of the one through the other) is the most beneficial for both powers of mind with respect to cognition (of given {{{ 239 }}} objects) in general; and this disposition cannot be determined otherwise than through feeling (not according to concepts). Now since this disposition itself must be capable of being universally communicated, hence also the feeling of it (upon a given representation); but the universal communicability of a feeling presupposes a common sense: this can with good ground be assumed, and indeed without thereby being based on psychological observations, but rather as the necessary condition of the universal communicability of our cognition, which must be presupposed in every logic and every principle of cognitions that is not skeptical.
