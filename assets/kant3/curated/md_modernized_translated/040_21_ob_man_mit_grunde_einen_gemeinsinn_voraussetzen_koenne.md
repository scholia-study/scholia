## § 21. Whether one can, with good ground, presuppose a common sense

Cognitions and judgments, together with the conviction that accompanies them, must admit of being universally communicated; for otherwise no agreement with the object would accrue to them: taken all together they would be a merely subjective play of the powers of representation, precisely as skepticism would have it. But if cognitions are to admit of being communicated, then the state of mind as well — i.e. the attunement of the cognitive powers toward a cognition in general, and specifically that proportion which is fitting for a representation (whereby an object is given to us) so as to make cognition out of it — must admit of being universally communicated: because without this, as the subjective condition of cognizing, the cognition, as its effect, could not arise. And this in fact does happen every time, when a given object, by means of the senses, sets the imagination into activity for the composing of the manifold, and this in turn sets the understanding into activity for the unity of that manifold in concepts. But this attunement of the cognitive powers has a different proportion according to the diversity of the objects that are given. Yet there must nonetheless be one {{ 66 }} in which this inner relation for the enlivening (of the one power by the other) is the most conducive for both powers of the mind with a view to cognition (of given {{{ 239 }}} objects) in general; and this attunement cannot be determined otherwise than through feeling (not according to concepts). Now since this attunement itself must admit of being universally communicated, and hence the feeling of it as well (in the case of a given representation), while the universal communicability of a feeling in turn presupposes a common sense: this latter can therefore be assumed with good ground, and indeed without basing oneself in this respect on psychological observations, but rather as the necessary condition of the universal communicability of our cognition, which must be presupposed in every logic and every principle of cognitions that is not skeptical.
