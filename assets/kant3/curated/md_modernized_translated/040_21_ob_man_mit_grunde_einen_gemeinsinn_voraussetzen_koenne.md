## § 21. Whether one can, with good ground, presuppose a common sense

Cognitions and judgments, along with the conviction attending them, have to be capable of universal communication; for were it otherwise, no correspondence with the object would belong to them: one and all, they would amount to nothing but a subjective interplay of the representational powers, exactly as skepticism demands. Yet if cognitions are to be communicable, then the condition of the mind must likewise be capable of universal communication — that is, the attunement of the cognitive powers to a cognition as such, and indeed just that proportion which befits a representation (through which an object is given to us) so that a cognition may be produced from it: for in the absence of this, which is the subjective condition of coming to know, the cognition could never issue forth as its effect. And this in fact does happen every time, when a given object, by means of the senses, sets the imagination into activity for the composing of the manifold, and this in turn sets the understanding into activity for the unity of that manifold in concepts. But this attunement of the cognitive powers has a different proportion according to the diversity of the objects that are given. Yet there must nonetheless be one {{ 66 }} in which this inner relation for the enlivening (of the one power by the other) is the most conducive for both powers of the mind with a view to cognition (of given {{{ 239 }}} objects) in general; and this attunement cannot be determined otherwise than through feeling (not according to concepts). Now, seeing that this attunement must of itself be universally communicable, and consequently the feeling of it too (given some representation), whereas the universal communicability of a feeling for its part presupposes a common sense: the latter may then with good reason be assumed — not, indeed, by grounding oneself on observations drawn from psychology, but as the necessary condition under which our cognition attains universal communicability, a presupposition required by every logic and by every non-skeptical principle of cognitions.
