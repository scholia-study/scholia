## § 31. On the method of the deduction of judgments of taste

A deduction—that is, the warranting of the lawfulness—of a kind of judgment becomes requisite only where that judgment stakes a claim to {{ 134 }} necessity; and this holds too whenever it calls for subjective universality, in other words the concurrence of everyone: although it remains, all the while, no cognitive judgment but only one concerning the pleasure or displeasure taken in a given object, that is, the pretension to a subjective purposiveness that shall hold without exception for each person, a purposiveness meant to rest on no concepts of the matter, since it is a judgment of taste.

For, in the latter of these cases, what lies before us is no cognitive judgment at all—not a theoretical one, which takes as its ground the concept of a ***nature*** as such by way of the understanding, nor a (pure) practical one, which posits as its ground the idea of ***freedom*** as given _a priori_ through reason; and hence, as regards its validity _a priori_, we have to vindicate neither a judgment that sets forth what some thing is, nor the demand that, in order to produce it, I ought to perform something: and so, for the power of judgment in general, it will merely be the ***universal validity*** of a {{{ 281 }}} ***singular*** judgment—one that voices the subjective purposiveness belonging to an empirical representation of an object's form—which has to be exhibited, so as to explain how it may be possible that something should please simply in the appraising of it (apart from sensation of the senses or concept), and how, just as the appraisal of an object on behalf of a ***cognition*** in general is governed by universal rules, so too the {{ 135 }} satisfaction felt by each individual may be held forth as a rule for everyone besides.

Now, supposing that this universal validity is to rest not upon a gathering of votes and an inquiring around among others as to their way of sensing, but as it were upon an autonomy belonging to the subject that passes judgment on the feeling of pleasure (within the given representation)—that is, upon its very own taste—while nevertheless not being derivable from concepts either: then a judgment of this kind—as the judgment of taste indeed is—carries a twofold peculiarity, and a logical one at that: namely, ***first***, a universal validity a priori that is yet not a logical universality proceeding by concepts, but rather the universality proper to a singular judgment; ***second***, a necessity (one that must in every instance rest on grounds _a priori_) which all the same hinges on no grounds of proof _a priori_ by whose representation the assent that the judgment of taste requires of everyone might be extorted.

The unraveling of these logical peculiarities—those wherein a judgment of taste sets itself apart from every cognitive judgment—will of itself be enough for the deduction of this peculiar faculty, so long as we here, to begin with, leave aside the whole of its content, that is, the feeling of pleasure, and set the aesthetic form side by side merely with the form that objective judgments take, as logic lays it down. We therefore wish first of all to bring these characteristic properties of taste before the mind, illustrated by examples {{ 136 }}.
