## § 31. On the method of the deduction of judgments of taste

The obligation to provide a deduction—that is, to guarantee the legitimacy—of a class of judgments arises only when the judgment lays claim to {{ 134 }} necessity; which is likewise the case when it demands subjective universality, that is, everyone's assent: even though it is nonetheless no cognitive judgment but merely one of pleasure or displeasure in a given object, that is, the presumption of a subjective purposiveness holding throughout for everyone, one that is to be grounded on no concepts of the thing, because it is a judgment of taste.

Since in the latter case we have before us no cognitive judgment—neither a theoretical one, which lays down as its ground the concept of a ***nature*** in general through the understanding, nor a (pure) practical one, which lays down the idea of ***freedom*** as given _a priori_ through reason; and thus have to justify according to its validity _a priori_ neither a judgment that represents what a thing is, nor one asserting that, in order to bring it about, I ought to do something: so it will be necessary to establish, for the power of judgment as such, merely the ***universal validity*** of a {{{ 281 }}} ***singular*** judgment which expresses the subjective purposiveness of an empirical representation of the form of an object, so as to make clear how it can come about that something pleases merely in the judging of it (without sensory sensation or concept), and how, just as, with a view to a ***cognition*** in general, the judging of an object is subject to universal rules, the {{ 135 }} satisfaction of anyone may likewise be proclaimed as a rule for everyone else.

Now if this universal validity is to rest not on the collecting of votes and the canvassing of others about their manner of sensing, but rather, as it were, on an autonomy of the subject judging about the feeling of pleasure (in the given representation), that is, on its own taste, yet is nonetheless also not to be derived from concepts: then such a judgment—as the judgment of taste in fact is—has a twofold and indeed logical peculiarity: namely, ***first***, universal validity a priori, and yet not a logical universality according to concepts, but rather the universality of a singular judgment; ***second***, a necessity (which must at all times rest on grounds _a priori_) that nevertheless depends on no proof-grounds _a priori_ through whose representation the assent the judgment of taste demands of everyone could be compelled.

The resolution of these logical peculiarities, by which a judgment of taste is distinguished from all cognitive judgments, will by itself suffice for the deduction of this strange faculty, provided that here we initially abstract from all its content, namely the feeling of pleasure, and merely compare the aesthetic form with the form of objective judgments as logic prescribes it. We therefore wish first of all to bring these characteristic properties of taste before the mind, illustrated by examples {{ 136 }}.
