## § 31. On the Method of the Deduction of Judgments of Taste

The obligation of a deduction, i.e., of guaranteeing the legitimacy of a kind of judgment, arises only when the judgment makes a claim to {{ 134 }} necessity; which is also the case when it demands subjective universality, i.e., everyone's assent: whereas it is nevertheless not a judgment of cognition, but only one of pleasure or displeasure in a given object, i.e., the pretension of a subjective purposiveness holding thoroughly for everyone, which is not to be based on any concepts of the thing, because it is a judgment of taste.

Since in the latter case we have before us no judgment of cognition, neither a theoretical one, which lays the concept of a ***nature*** in general as given by the understanding at its basis, nor a (pure) practical one, which lays the idea of ***freedom*** as given _a priori_ by reason at its basis; and thus have to justify according to its _a priori_ validity neither a judgment that represents what a thing is, nor one asserting that I should perform something in order to produce it: so only the ***universal validity*** of a {{{ 281 }}} ***single*** judgment, which expresses the subjective purposiveness of an empirical representation of the form of an object, will have to be demonstrated for the power of judgment in general, in order to explain how it is possible that something could please merely in the judging (without sensory sensation or concept), and, just as the judging of an object for the sake of a ***cognition*** in general has universal rules, also the {{ 135 }} satisfaction of each may be announced as a rule for every other.

If now this universal validity is not to be based on the gathering of votes and questioning of others regarding their way of feeling, but is to rest, as it were, on an autonomy of the subject judging about the feeling of pleasure (in the given representation), i.e., on its own taste, yet is nevertheless also not to be derived from concepts: then such a judgment — as the judgment of taste in fact is — has a twofold and indeed logical peculiarity: namely, ***firstly***, universality a priori and yet not a logical universality according to concepts, but rather the universality of a single judgment; ***secondly***, a necessity (which must always rest on _a priori_ grounds), which nevertheless does not depend on any _a priori_ proofs, through the representation of which the approval that the judgment of taste requires of everyone could be compelled.

The resolution of these logical peculiarities, in which a judgment of taste distinguishes itself from all judgments of cognition, if we here initially abstract from all its content, namely the feeling of pleasure, and merely compare the aesthetic form with the form of objective judgments as prescribed by logic, will alone be sufficient for the deduction of this singular faculty. We will therefore first present these characteristic properties of taste, illustrated {{ 136 }} by examples.
