## § 4. The satisfaction in the good is connected with an interest

***Good*** is what pleases through the mere concept, by way of reason. We call some things ***good for something*** (the useful), which please only as a means; but others ***good in themselves***, which please for their own sake. Each of these harbors, without exception, the concept of an end, and thus reason's bearing upon a willing that is at least possible, so that a satisfaction attaches to the ***existence*** of an object or of an action—in other words, an interest of some sort.

To find something good, I must in every case know what kind of thing the object is meant to be, that is, have a concept of it. To find beauty in something, I have no need of that. Flowers, free-hand drawings, tracery aimlessly wound into one another under the {{ 11 }} label of foliage—these mean nothing, rest upon no determinate concept, and please all the same. Satisfaction in the beautiful, by contrast, has to depend on reflecting upon an object—a reflection issuing in some concept or another, no matter which—and just here lies its difference from the agreeable, whose whole basis is sensation.

Admittedly, the agreeable seems in many cases to coincide with the good. Thus people will commonly say: every enjoyment (especially the lasting kind) is good in itself; which comes to roughly this, that to be lastingly agreeable or to be good is one and the same. Yet one soon notices that this is merely a faulty exchange of words, since the concepts that properly belong to these expressions can in no way {{{ 208 }}} be substituted for one another. As something agreeable, the agreeable presents an object merely in its bearing on sense; to be termed good as an object of the will, it has to be ranged under principles of reason by means of the concept of an end in the first place. That a quite different relation to the satisfaction is at stake once I call ***good*** the very thing that gives me pleasure becomes evident from the following: in the case of the good one always asks whether the thing is good only in a mediate way or good directly (that is, useful or good in itself), while in the case of the agreeable such a question cannot even arise, for that word always denotes whatever pleases at once. (It stands just the same way, too, with what {{ 12 }} I call beautiful.)

Even in the most ordinary discourse people distinguish the agreeable from the good. Of a dish that heightens the taste through spices and other additions, one says without hesitation that it is agreeable, while at the same time granting that it is not good: for although it immediately ***pleases*** the senses, mediately, that is, considered through reason, which looks to the consequences, it displeases. Even in the judging of health one can still notice this distinction. To everyone who possesses it, it is immediately agreeable (at least negatively, that is, as the keeping away of every bodily pain). But to pronounce it good, one must still, by means of reason, direct it toward ends, namely that it is a state which makes us disposed for all our affairs. With respect to happiness, everyone in the end nonetheless believes that he may call the greatest sum (in quantity as well as duration) of the agreeablenesses of life a true, indeed even the highest, good. Yet against this too reason revolts. Agreeableness is enjoyment. But if it is aimed at this alone, then it would be foolish to be scrupulous about the means that procure it for us, whether it were obtained passively, from the generosity of nature, or through our own activity and doing. But that the existence of a human being should have a worth in itself, one who merely lives (and {{ 13 }} however busily occupied he may be to that end) in order ***to enjoy***, even if in doing so he were, as a means thereto, of the utmost service to others who likewise all aim solely at enjoyment, and this because through sympathy he shared in all their pleasure: of this reason will never let itself be convinced. Only through what he does without regard to enjoyment, in complete freedom and independently of whatever nature might procure for him even passively, does he give to his existence, as the existence of a person, {{{ 209 }}} an absolute worth; and happiness, with the entire fullness of its agreeableness, is far from being an unconditioned good[^*].

Yet for all the ways in which the agreeable and the good diverge, they still agree on one point: each is invariably bound up with an interest taken in its object—not merely the agreeable, § 3, together with what is good in mediate fashion (the useful, pleasing as a means toward one agreeableness or another), but likewise that which is good outright and in every respect, namely the moral, which carries the supreme interest along with it. For the good is the object of the will (that is, {{ 14 }} of a power of desire determined through reason). But to will a thing and to take a satisfaction in the existence of that thing, which is to say, to take an interest in it, amount to one and the same.

[^*]: An obligation to enjoy is a plain absurdity. Precisely this, then, must equally be the case with any alleged obligation binding us to every action whose sole aim is enjoyment: let such enjoyment be conceived (or embellished) in however spiritual a manner one likes, even should it amount to a mystical, so-called heavenly delight.
