## § 4. The satisfaction in the good is connected with an interest

***Good*** is what pleases through the mere concept, by way of reason. We call some things ***good for something*** (the useful), which please only as a means; but others ***good in themselves***, which please for their own sake. In both cases there is always contained the concept of an end, and hence the relation of reason to volition (at least possible volition), and consequently a satisfaction in the ***existence*** of an object or an action, that is, some interest or other.

To find something good, I must in every case know what kind of thing the object is meant to be, that is, have a concept of it. To find beauty in something, I have no need of that. Flowers, free designs, lines interlaced with one another without any aim, going under the {{ 11 }} name of foliage, signify nothing, depend on no determinate concept, and yet please. The satisfaction in the beautiful must rest on reflection concerning an object that leads to some concept or other (it being indeterminate which), and it is thereby distinguished also from the agreeable, which rests wholly on sensation.

Admittedly, the agreeable seems in many cases to coincide with the good. Thus people will commonly say: every enjoyment (especially the lasting kind) is good in itself; which comes to roughly this, that to be lastingly agreeable or to be good is one and the same. Yet one soon notices that this is merely a faulty exchange of words, since the concepts that properly belong to these expressions can in no way {{{ 208 }}} be substituted for one another. The agreeable, which as such represents the object solely in relation to the senses, must first be brought under principles of reason through the concept of an end, in order, as an object of the will, to be called good. But that this then involves a wholly different relation to the satisfaction, when I at the same time call ***good*** what gratifies me, can be seen from this: that with the good the question is always whether it is merely mediately good or immediately good (whether useful or good in itself); whereas with the agreeable there can be no such question at all, since the word invariably signifies something that pleases immediately. (It stands just the same way, too, with what {{ 12 }} I call beautiful.)

Even in the most ordinary discourse people distinguish the agreeable from the good. Of a dish that heightens the taste through spices and other additions, one says without hesitation that it is agreeable, while at the same time granting that it is not good: for although it immediately ***pleases*** the senses, mediately, that is, considered through reason, which looks to the consequences, it displeases. Even in the judging of health one can still notice this distinction. To everyone who possesses it, it is immediately agreeable (at least negatively, that is, as the keeping away of every bodily pain). But to pronounce it good, one must still, by means of reason, direct it toward ends, namely that it is a state which makes us disposed for all our affairs. With respect to happiness, everyone in the end nonetheless believes that he may call the greatest sum (in quantity as well as duration) of the agreeablenesses of life a true, indeed even the highest, good. Yet against this too reason revolts. Agreeableness is enjoyment. But if it is aimed at this alone, then it would be foolish to be scrupulous about the means that procure it for us, whether it were obtained passively, from the generosity of nature, or through our own activity and doing. But that the existence of a human being should have a worth in itself, one who merely lives (and {{ 13 }} however busily occupied he may be to that end) in order ***to enjoy***, even if in doing so he were, as a means thereto, of the utmost service to others who likewise all aim solely at enjoyment, and this because through sympathy he shared in all their pleasure: of this reason will never let itself be convinced. Only through what he does without regard to enjoyment, in complete freedom and independently of whatever nature might procure for him even passively, does he give to his existence, as the existence of a person, {{{ 209 }}} an absolute worth; and happiness, with the entire fullness of its agreeableness, is far from being an unconditioned good[^*].

But regardless of all this difference between the agreeable and the good, the two nonetheless coincide in this: that in every instance they are conjoined with an interest in their object, not only the agreeable, § 3, and the mediately good (the useful), which pleases as a means to some agreeableness or other, but also the good in the absolute sense and in every respect, namely the moral, which brings with it the highest interest. For the good is the object of the will (that is, {{ 14 }} of a power of desire determined through reason). But to will a thing and to take a satisfaction in the existence of that thing, which is to say, to take an interest in it, amount to one and the same.

[^*]: An obligation to enjoy is a plain absurdity. The same, accordingly, must hold also of any purported obligation to all actions that have mere enjoyment as their goal: however spiritually contrived (or dressed up) this may be, and even if it were a mystical, so-called heavenly enjoyment.
