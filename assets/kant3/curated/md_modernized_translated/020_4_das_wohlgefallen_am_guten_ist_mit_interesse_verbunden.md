## § 4. The Satisfaction in the Good is Combined with Interest

***Good*** is that which pleases by means of reason through the mere concept. We call some things ***good for something*** (the useful), which please only as a means; but other things ***good in themselves***, which please for their own sake. In both there is always contained the concept of an end, hence the relation of reason to (at least possible) volition, consequently a satisfaction in the ***existence*** of an object or of an action, i.e., some interest.

In order to find something good, I must always know what sort of thing the object is supposed to be, i.e., have a concept of it. To find beauty in something I do not need that. Flowers, free designs, lines aimlessly intertwined with one another, under the {{ 11 }} name of foliage, signify nothing, depend on no determinate concept, and yet please. The satisfaction in the beautiful must depend on the reflection on an object that leads to some concept (it is undetermined which), and thereby it is also distinguished from the agreeable, which rests entirely on sensation.

To be sure, in many cases the agreeable seems to be one and the same with the good. Thus one will commonly say: all (especially lasting) gratification is in itself good; which means roughly as much as: to be lastingly agreeable and to be good are one and the same. Yet one can soon notice that this is merely a faulty substitution of words, since the concepts that properly attach to these expressions can in no way {{{ 208 }}} be exchanged for one another. The agreeable, which as such represents the object merely in relation to sense, must first be brought under principles of reason through the concept of an end in order to call it, as an object of the will, good. But that this is then a quite different relation to the satisfaction, when I call that which gratifies at the same time ***good***, can be seen from the fact that in the case of the good there is always the question whether it is merely mediately good or immediately good (whether useful or good in itself); whereas in the case of the agreeable there can be no question about this at all, since the word always signifies something that pleases immediately. (It is just the same with that {{ 12 }} which I call beautiful.)

Even in the most ordinary speech one distinguishes the agreeable from the good. Of a dish that elevates the taste through spices and other additives one says without hesitation that it is agreeable, while at the same time conceding that it is not good: because although it immediately ***gratifies*** the senses, it displeases mediately, i.e., considered through reason, which looks ahead to the consequences. Even in the appraisal of health one can still notice this difference. It is immediately agreeable to everyone who possesses it (at least negatively, i.e., as the removal of all bodily pains). But in order to say that it is good, one must still direct it through reason toward ends, namely that it is a state that disposes us to all our affairs. With regard to happiness everyone finally believes, after all, that he can call the greatest sum (in respect of multitude as well as duration) of the agreeablenesses of life a true, indeed even the highest good. Yet reason balks even at this. Agreeableness is enjoyment. But if it is aimed at this alone, it would be foolish to be scrupulous regarding the means that procure it for us, whether it were attained passively, from the bounty of nature, or through self-activity and our own working. But that the existence of a human being who lives merely (and {{ 13 }} however busy he may be in this respect) in order ***to enjoy*** should have a value in itself, even if in doing so he were of the best service as a means to others who all likewise aim only at enjoyment, and indeed because he shared in all gratification through sympathy: of this reason will never let itself be persuaded. Only through that which he does without regard to enjoyment, in full freedom and independently of what nature could passively procure for him, does he give to his existence, as the existence of a person, {{{ 209 }}} an absolute value; and happiness, with the whole abundance of its agreeableness, is by far not an unconditioned good[^*].

But notwithstanding all this difference between the agreeable and the good, both nevertheless agree in this: that they are always combined with an interest in their object, not only the agreeable, § 3, and the mediately good (the useful), which pleases as a means to some agreeableness, but also the absolutely and in every respect good, namely the moral, which carries with it the highest interest. For the good is the object of the will (i.e., {{ 14 }} of a faculty of desire determined through reason). But to will something and to have a satisfaction in its existence, i.e., to take an interest in it, is identical.

[^*]: An obligation to enjoy is a manifest absurdity. The very same must therefore also be the case with a supposed obligation to all actions that have merely enjoyment as their aim: however spiritually this may be conceived (or embellished), and even if it were a mystical, so-called heavenly enjoyment.
