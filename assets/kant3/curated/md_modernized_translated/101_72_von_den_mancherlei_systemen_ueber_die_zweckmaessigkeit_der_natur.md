## § 72. Of the Various Systems Concerning the Purposiveness of Nature

The correctness of the principle that certain things of nature (organized beings) and their possibility must be judged according to the concept of final causes, even if only when one requires a ***guide*** in order to get to know their constitution through observation, without venturing to {{{ 390 }}} investigate their first origin, has not yet been doubted by anyone. The question can therefore only be: whether this principle is merely subjectively valid, i.e., a mere maxim of our power of judgment, or an objective principle of nature, according to which there belongs to it, in addition to its mechanism (according to mere laws of motion), another kind of causality, namely, that of final causes, under which the former (the moving forces) stand only as intermediate causes.

Now one could leave this question or problem completely undecided and unresolved for speculation: because, if we content ourselves with the latter within the limits of the mere cognition of nature, we have enough in those maxims to study nature as far as human {{ 320 }} powers reach, and to track down its most hidden secrets. It is therefore likely a certain suspicion of our reason, or a hint given to us as it were by nature, that by means of that concept of final causes we might even reach beyond nature and connect it itself to the highest point in the series of causes, if we abandoned the investigation of nature (although we have not yet gone far in it), or at least suspended it for a time, and previously tried to explore where that stranger in natural science, namely the concept of natural purposes, might lead.

Here indeed that undisputed maxim would have to pass over into a problem that opens a wide field for controversy: whether the connection of purposes in nature ***proves*** a special kind of causality for it; or whether, considered in itself and according to objective principles, it is not rather identical with the mechanism of nature, or rests on one and the same ground: only that, since for our investigation this ground is often hidden too deeply in many products of nature, we try a subjective principle, namely that of art, i.e., of causality according to ideas, in order to attribute it to nature by analogy; which makeshift indeed succeeds for us in many cases, though in some it seems to fail, but in any case does not entitle us to introduce into natural science a special mode of operation, distinct from {{ 321 }} the causality according to merely mechanical laws of nature itself. We will, in calling the procedure (the causality) of nature 'technique' on account of the similarity to purpose that we find in its products, divide this into the ***intentional*** (_technica intentionalis_) and the ***unintentional*** (_technica naturalis_). The first is to mean: that the productive {{{ 391 }}} power of nature according to final causes must be held to be a special kind of causality; the second: that it is at bottom completely identical with the mechanism of nature, and that the accidental coincidence with our concepts of art and their rules, as a merely subjective condition for judging it, is falsely interpreted as a special kind of natural generation.

If we now speak of the systems of explanation of nature in respect of final causes, it must be noted: that they are altogether dogmatically in dispute with one another over objective principles of the possibility of things, whether through intentionally or purely unintentionally acting causes, but not as it were over the subjective maxim of merely judging of the cause of such purposive products: in which latter case ***disparate*** principles could still indeed be combined, whereas in the former, ***contradictorily opposed*** principles cancel each other {{ 322 }} out and cannot coexist.

The systems in respect of the technique of nature, i.e., of its productive power according to the rule of purposes, are twofold: of the ***idealism***, or of the ***realism*** of natural purposes. The former is the assertion that all purposiveness of nature is ***unintentional***; the latter, that some of it (in organized beings) is ***intentional***; from which indeed the consequence, established as a hypothesis, could also be drawn, that the technique of nature, also as regards all other products of it in relation to the whole of nature, is intentional, i.e., is a purpose.

1) The ***idealism*** of purposiveness (I understand here always the objective one) is now either that of the ***casuality***, or of the ***fatality*** of the determination of nature in the purposive form of its products. The first principle concerns the relation of matter to the physical ground of its form, namely, the laws of motion; the second to its and all nature's ***hyperphysical*** ground. The system of ***casuality***, which is ascribed to Epicurus or Democritus, taken literally, is so obviously absurd that it must not detain us; on the other hand, the system of fatality (of which Spinoza is made the author, although to all appearances it is much older), which appeals to something supersensible into which our insight {{ 323 }} does not reach, is not so easy to refute: because his concept of the original being is not to be understood at all. But this much is clear: that the connection of purposes in the world must in that system be assumed to be unintentional {{{ 392 }}} (because it is derived from an original being, but not from its understanding, hence from no intention of it, but rather from the necessity of its nature and the unity of the world descending from it), and therefore the fatalism of purposiveness is at the same time an idealism of it.

2) The ***realism*** of the purposiveness of nature is also either physical or hyperphysical. The ***first*** grounds purposes in nature on the analogue of a faculty acting according to intention, the ***life of matter*** (in it, or also through an animating inner principle, a world-soul), and is called ***hylozoism***. The ***second*** derives them from the original ground of the universe, as an intelligent being producing with intention (originally living), and is ***theism***.[^*]

[^*]: One sees from this: that in most speculative matters of pure reason, as far as dogmatic assertions are concerned, the philosophical schools have commonly tried all solutions that are possible concerning a certain question. Thus, concerning the purposiveness of nature, one has tried for this purpose now either ***lifeless matter***, or a ***lifeless God***, now ***living matter***, or also a ***living God***. For us nothing remains but, if need should require, to depart from all of these ***objective assertions*** and to consider our judgment ***critically*** merely in relation to our cognitive faculties, in order to procure for its principle a validity of a maxim sufficient for the secure use of reason, if not a dogmatic one.
