## § 72. On the various systems concerning the purposiveness of nature

That the principle is correct — the principle that certain things in nature (organized beings) and their possibility must be judged according to the concept of final causes, even where one requires no more than a ***guiding thread*** for coming to know their constitution through observation, without venturing {{{ 390 }}} to an investigation into their first origin — this no one has yet doubted. The question can therefore only be this: whether this principle is merely subjectively valid, i.e., merely a maxim of our power of judgment, or whether, on the contrary, it is an objective principle belonging to nature, according to which, over and above its mechanism (proceeding by mere laws of motion), there accrues to it yet a further kind of causality, namely that of final causes, under which the former (the moving forces) would stand only as intermediate causes.

Now one could leave this question or task entirely undecided and unresolved for speculation: for if we content ourselves with the latter within the bounds of mere cognition of nature, then those maxims are enough for us to study nature as far as human {{ 320 }} powers extend and to track down its most deeply hidden secrets. It is therefore surely a certain intimation of our reason, or as it were a hint given to us by nature, that by means of that concept of final causes we might indeed reach out beyond nature and even link nature itself to the supreme point within the whole causal series, were we to give up the inquiry into nature (little though we have as yet advanced in it), or at least were to suspend it for a while and first try to find out where that stranger in natural science, namely the concept of natural ends, might lead.

Here, to be sure, that uncontested maxim would have to pass over into a task that opens a wide field for disputes: whether the connection of ends in nature ***proves*** a special kind of causality for it; or whether, considered in itself and according to objective principles, it is not rather one and the same as the mechanism of nature, or rests on one and the same ground: except that we, since this ground in many natural products often lies too deeply concealed for our inquiry, make the attempt with a subjective principle, namely the principle of art, i.e., of a causality governed by ideas, in order to lay it beneath nature by analogy; an emergency aid that does indeed succeed for us in many cases, though in some it seems to fail, but that in no case entitles us to introduce into natural science a special mode of operation, distinct from {{ 321 }} the causality according to merely mechanical laws of nature itself. Since we call the procedure (the causality) of nature a technique on account of the end-like character that we find in its products, we shall divide this technique into the ***intentional*** (_technica intentionalis_) and the ***unintentional*** (_technica naturalis_). The first is to mean: that the productive {{{ 391 }}} capacity of nature in accordance with final causes must be regarded as a special kind of causality; the second: that at bottom it coincides wholly with nature's mechanism, and that the accidental coincidence with our concepts of art and their rules, being merely a subjective condition for judging it, is falsely construed as a distinct kind of natural generation.

When we now discuss the systems for explaining nature in respect of final causes, it must be carefully noted: that they are one and all in dispute with one another dogmatically, i.e., over objective principles concerning the possibility of things — be it through causes acting intentionally or purely unintentionally, but not, say, over the subjective maxim of merely judging about the cause of such purposive products: in which latter case ***disparate*** principles could still well be united, whereas in the former ***contradictorily opposed*** ones cancel one another {{ 322 }} out and cannot subsist alongside one another.

The systems with regard to the technique of nature, that is, of its productive power operating by the rule of ends, are twofold: those of ***idealism***, or of ***realism*** of natural ends. The former is the assertion: that all purposiveness of nature is ***unintentional***; the second: that some of it (in organized beings) is ***intentional***; from which the further consequence, grounded as a hypothesis, could also be drawn, that the technique of nature is intentional, i.e., an end, even as regards all its other products in relation to the whole of nature.

1) The ***idealism*** of purposiveness (by which throughout I mean the objective) is now either that of the ***casuality***, or of the ***fatality*** of nature's determination in the purposive form of its products. The first of these principles concerns the relation of matter to the physical ground of its form, namely the laws of motion; the second, to the ***hyperphysical*** ground of matter and of nature in its entirety. The system of ***casuality***, which is attributed to Epicurus or Democritus, is — taken literally — so patently absurd that we need not dwell upon it; the system of fatality, by contrast (of which Spinoza is made the author, though to all appearances it is much older), which appeals to something supersensible, to which therefore our insight does {{ 323 }} not reach, is not so easily refuted: precisely because its concept of the original being cannot be understood at all. This much, however, is clear: that within it the connection of ends in the world must be assumed {{{ 392 }}} to be unintentional (because it is derived from an original being, but not from its understanding, and hence from no intention on its part, but instead from the necessity inherent in its nature and from the world-unity that descends from it), so that the fatalism of purposiveness is at once an idealism thereof.

2) The ***realism*** of nature's purposiveness is, again, either physical or hyperphysical. The ***first*** grounds the ends in nature on the analogue of a capacity acting according to intention, on the ***life of matter*** (within it, or else through an animating inner principle, a world-soul), and it goes by the name of ***hylozoism***. The ***second*** traces them back to the primordial ground of the cosmos, viewed as an intelligent being that brings them forth with intention (and that is originally living), and it is ***theism***.[^*]

[^*]: From this one sees: that in most speculative matters of pure reason, as far as dogmatic assertions are concerned, the philosophical schools have commonly tried out all the solutions that are possible regarding a given question. Thus, regarding the purposiveness of nature, one has tried out for this purpose now either ***lifeless matter*** or a ***lifeless God***, now ***living matter*** or even a ***living God***. For us nothing remains but, should the need arise, to depart from all these ***objective assertions*** and to weigh our judgment ***critically*** merely in relation to our faculties of cognition, in order to secure for its principle the validity of a maxim that, if not dogmatic, is at least sufficient for a secure use of reason.
