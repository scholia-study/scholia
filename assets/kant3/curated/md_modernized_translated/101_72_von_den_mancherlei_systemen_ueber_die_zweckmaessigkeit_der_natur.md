## § 72. On the various systems concerning the purposiveness of nature

No one has as yet cast doubt on the correctness of the basic proposition that certain natural things (organized beings), along with their possibility, must be judged in accordance with the concept of final causes — and this holds even when, so as to learn their constitution through observation, one demands a ***guiding thread***, without presuming to rise {{{ 390 }}} to an inquiry into their first origin. Thus the only remaining question is this: whether such a principle holds good in a merely subjective way — that is, whether it counts simply as a maxim of our power of judgment — or whether instead it constitutes an objective principle of nature, one by which, beyond her mechanism (governed by bare laws of motion), a causality of yet another sort belongs to her, that of final causes, beneath which the aforesaid moving forces would rank as no more than intermediate causes.

For speculation, then, this question or problem might be left wholly unsettled and open: since, provided we rest content with the latter inside the limits of mere cognition of nature, we possess in those maxims all that is needed to study nature — as far indeed as human {{ 320 }} powers reach — and to hunt out her most deeply concealed mysteries. It is therefore surely a certain intimation of our reason, or as it were a hint given to us by nature, that by means of that concept of final causes we might indeed reach out beyond nature and even link nature itself to the supreme point within the whole causal series, were we to give up the inquiry into nature (little though we have as yet advanced in it), or at least were to suspend it for a while and first try to find out where that stranger in natural science, namely the concept of natural ends, might lead.

At this point, admittedly, that undisputed maxim would have to give way to a problem that throws open a broad arena for controversy: whether, within nature, the linkage of ends ***proves*** for her a causality of a peculiar sort; or whether, taken on its own and viewed under objective principles, it is not rather identical with nature's mechanism, or grounded in one and the very same basis — save only that we, because in many products of nature this basis lies buried too deep for our investigation, resort to a subjective principle, namely that of art, i.e., of a causality operating by ideas, so as to place it under nature by way of analogy; and although this makeshift aid succeeds for us in many instances, and in some admittedly appears to fail, in no case does it authorize us to bring into natural science a distinct mode of efficacy, one set apart from {{ 321 }} the causality that follows the merely mechanical laws of nature herself. Inasmuch as we give the name of technique to nature's procedure (its causality), owing to the end-resembling quality we come upon in her products, we shall sort this technique into the ***intentional*** (_technica intentionalis_) and the ***unintentional*** (_technica naturalis_). By the first is meant this: that nature's productive {{{ 391 }}} power operating through final causes has to be taken for a special species of causality; by the second: that it is at bottom entirely the same as nature's mechanism, and that its fortuitous agreement with our concepts of art and the rules thereof — a merely subjective condition for appraising it — comes to be wrongly interpreted as a peculiar mode of natural production.

When we now discuss the systems for explaining nature in respect of final causes, it must be carefully noted: that they are one and all in dispute with one another dogmatically, i.e., over objective principles concerning the possibility of things — be it through causes acting intentionally or purely unintentionally, but not, say, over the subjective maxim of merely judging about the cause of such purposive products: in which latter case ***disparate*** principles could still well be united, whereas in the former ***contradictorily opposed*** ones cancel one another {{ 322 }} out and cannot subsist alongside one another.

As concerns the technique of nature — that is, her productive power following the rule of ends — the systems fall into two kinds: that of the ***idealism***, or that of the ***realism***, of natural ends. The one holds that the whole purposiveness of nature is ***unintentional***; the other, that a portion of it (in organized beings) is ***intentional*** — whence, indeed, one could draw the further inference, established as a hypothesis, that nature's technique is likewise intentional, i.e., an end, with respect as well to every one of her remaining products in their bearing on nature as a whole.

1) Now the ***idealism*** of purposiveness (throughout, I always understand the objective kind) is either that of the ***casuality*** or that of the ***fatality*** by which nature is determined in the purposive form her products take. This former principle bears upon how matter stands to the physical basis of its form, that is, upon the laws of motion; the latter, upon the ***hyperphysical*** basis of matter and of nature taken in its whole extent. The system of ***casuality***, which is attributed to Epicurus or Democritus, is — taken literally — so patently absurd that we need not dwell upon it; the system of fatality, by contrast (of which Spinoza is made the author, though to all appearances it is much older), which appeals to something supersensible, to which therefore our insight does {{ 323 }} not reach, is not so easily refuted: precisely because its concept of the original being cannot be understood at all. Yet this much stands clear: that within such a system the world's connection of ends has to be taken {{{ 392 }}} as unintentional (for it is drawn from an original being — though not from that being's understanding, and thus from no intention of its own, but rather out of the necessity of its nature and the unity of the world descending therefrom), and hence the fatalism of purposiveness is simultaneously an idealism of it.

2) The ***realism*** of nature's purposiveness is, again, either physical or hyperphysical. The ***first*** grounds the ends in nature on the analogue of a capacity acting according to intention, on the ***life of matter*** (within it, or else through an animating inner principle, a world-soul), and it goes by the name of ***hylozoism***. The ***second*** traces them back to the primordial ground of the cosmos, viewed as an intelligent being that brings them forth with intention (and that is originally living), and it is ***theism***.[^*]

[^*]: From this one sees: that in most speculative matters of pure reason, as far as dogmatic assertions are concerned, the philosophical schools have commonly tried out all the solutions that are possible regarding a given question. Thus, regarding the purposiveness of nature, one has tried out for this purpose now either ***lifeless matter*** or a ***lifeless God***, now ***living matter*** or even a ***living God***. There remains for us no other course than, should necessity so demand, to abandon every one of these ***objective assertions*** and to appraise our judgment ***critically*** solely with respect to our faculties of cognition, thereby procuring for its principle a maxim's validity — one that, if not dogmatic, is nonetheless adequate for a sure employment of reason.
