## VIII. On the logical representation of nature's purposiveness

In an object that is given in experience, a purposiveness can be represented in either of two ways: either on a merely subjective ground — as the accord of its form, in the ***apprehension*** (_apprehensio_) of that object antecedently to any concept, with our faculties of cognition, so as to bring intuition together with concepts into a cognition as such; or on an objective ground — as the accord of its form with the very possibility of the thing, in conformity with a concept of that thing which precedes it {{ XLIX }} and holds within it the ground of this form. We have already seen: that a representation of purposiveness of the first kind is grounded in the immediate pleasure taken in the object's form through mere reflection upon it; the second, by contrast, since it relates the object's form not to the subject's faculties of cognition as they take it up, but to a determinate cognition of the object falling under a given concept, is concerned not with any feeling of pleasure taken in things, but with the understanding as it judges them. Once a concept of some object has been given, the task that falls to the power of judgment, in putting that concept to use for cognition, lies in ***presentation*** (_exhibitio_), that is, in setting beside the concept an intuition that corresponds to it: be it that this takes place through our own power of imagination, as it does in art, {{{ 193 }}} where we bring to realization a concept, framed in advance, of an object that is for us an end, or through nature in its own technique (as with organized bodies), when we lay our concept of an end beneath its product for the purpose of judging it; in which case what is represented is not merely a ***purposiveness*** on nature's part in the thing's form, but rather this product of nature as a ***natural end***. — Even though the concept we form of a subjective purposiveness in nature, in its forms under empirical laws, is not itself a concept of any object, but only a {{ L }} principle whereby the power of judgment furnishes itself with concepts amid nature's overwhelming variety (so as to be able to find its bearings within it): we do nonetheless, by this very means, attribute to nature something like a regard for our faculty of cognition, on the analogy of an end; and in this way ***natural beauty*** admits of being regarded as the ***presentation*** of the concept of a formal (merely subjective) purposiveness, whereas ***natural ends*** may be regarded as the presentation of the concept of a real (objective) purposiveness, the former of which we judge by taste (aesthetically, through the feeling of pleasure), the latter by understanding and reason (logically, by way of concepts).

Upon this rests the division of the critique of the power of judgment into a critique of the ***aesthetic*** and of the ***teleological*** power of judgment: for by the former is meant the faculty for judging formal purposiveness (elsewhere also termed subjective) through the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, and by the latter the faculty for judging the real (objective) purposiveness of nature through understanding and reason.

Within a critique of the power of judgment, the part that contains the aesthetic power of judgment belongs to it essentially, for this part alone holds a principle which the power of judgment places entirely _a priori_ at the foundation of its reflection upon nature, namely that of a formal purposiveness of nature, in respect of its particular (empirical) laws, for the benefit of our faculty of cognition, a purposiveness apart from which the understanding would be unable to orient itself amid it: whereas {{ LI }} no ground can be assigned _a priori_ at all, indeed not even the possibility of such a thing is made evident from the concept of a nature regarded as an object of experience, alike in general and in particular, that there must exist objective ends of nature, that is, things possible solely as natural ends; rather the power of judgment alone, without harboring within itself any _a priori_ principle for this, contains, in the cases that present themselves (of certain products), the rule for putting the concept of ends to use on reason's behalf, once that transcendental {{{ 194 }}} principle has beforehand made the understanding ready to apply the concept of an end (at least in respect of form) to nature.

The transcendental principle, however, of representing to ourselves, in the form of a thing, a purposiveness of nature standing in subjective relation to our faculty of cognition, and this as a principle for judging that thing, leaves it wholly undetermined where and in which cases I am to undertake such judging, as the judging of a product falling under a principle of purposiveness rather than merely under nature's universal laws, and it hands over to the ***aesthetic*** power of judgment the task of settling, in taste, the fitness of that product (of its form) to our faculties of cognition (insofar as this faculty reaches its verdict not through agreement with concepts but through feeling). By contrast, the teleologically employed power of judgment states with precision the conditions under {{ LII }} which something (e.g. an organized body) ought to be judged in accordance with the idea of an end of nature; yet it can adduce no principle, drawn from the concept of nature taken as an object of experience, that would authorize us to ascribe to nature a relation to ends _a priori_, or even, in indeterminate fashion, to assume any such relation from actual experience of products of that sort: the reason being that a great many particular experiences must be undertaken and considered under the unity of their principle if an objective purposiveness in a given object is to be cognized at all, and even then only empirically. — The aesthetic power of judgment is thus a faculty all its own, one for judging things by a rule and not by concepts. The teleological power of judgment is no faculty of its own kind, but simply the reflecting power of judgment as such, insofar as it proceeds, as everywhere in theoretical cognition, according to concepts, yet, with regard to certain natural objects, by special principles, namely those of a merely reflecting power of judgment that does not determine objects, and so, by its application, it falls under the theoretical division of philosophy, and, owing to those special principles which are not determining, as they would have to be in a doctrine, it must likewise make up a distinct part of the critique; whereas the aesthetic power of judgment adds nothing to the cognition of its objects and must therefore be reckoned ***only*** to the critique of the judging subject and of that subject's faculties of cognition, so far as these {{ LIII }} are capable of principles _a priori_, whatever the use (theoretical or practical) to which they may otherwise be put, a critique that forms the propaedeutic of all philosophy.
