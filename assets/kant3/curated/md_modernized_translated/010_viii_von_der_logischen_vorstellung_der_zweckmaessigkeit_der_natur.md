## VIII. On the logical representation of nature's purposiveness

In an object that is given in experience, a purposiveness can be represented in either of two ways: either on a merely subjective ground — as the accord of its form, in the ***apprehension*** (_apprehensio_) of that object antecedently to any concept, with our faculties of cognition, so as to bring intuition together with concepts into a cognition as such; or on an objective ground — as the accord of its form with the very possibility of the thing, in conformity with a concept of that thing which precedes it {{ XLIX }} and holds within it the ground of this form. We have already seen: that a representation of purposiveness of the first kind is grounded in the immediate pleasure taken in the object's form through mere reflection upon it; the second, by contrast, since it relates the object's form not to the subject's faculties of cognition as they take it up, but to a determinate cognition of the object falling under a given concept, is concerned not with any feeling of pleasure taken in things, but with the understanding as it judges them. Where a concept of an object is already given, the business of the power of judgment, when it employs that concept toward cognition, consists in ***presentation*** (_exhibitio_) — that is, in placing alongside the concept an intuition answering to it: whether this comes about through our own power of imagination, as art itself shows, {{{ 193 }}} when we realize a previously formed concept of an object that serves as an end for us, or through nature by way of its technique (as in organized bodies), when we set our concept of an end under nature's product so as to judge it; and in such a case what comes to be represented is not simply a ***purposiveness*** belonging to nature in the form of the thing, but this product of hers regarded as a ***natural end***. — Now although the concept we frame of a subjective purposiveness in nature's forms, following empirical laws, is in no way a concept drawn from the object, but merely a {{ L }} principle by which the power of judgment provides itself with concepts amid this immense manifold of hers (and can thereby orient itself within it): even so, through this very step we ascribe to nature, as it were, a consideration for our faculty of cognition modeled on the analogy of an end; and thus we are able to view ***natural beauty*** as the ***presentation*** of a concept of formal purposiveness (one merely subjective), whereas ***natural ends*** count as the presentation of a concept of real purposiveness (an objective one) — of which we appraise the first through taste (aesthetically, by means of the feeling of pleasure), and the second through understanding and reason (logically, according to concepts).

On this is founded the way in which the critique of the power of judgment is divided — into one of the ***aesthetic*** and one of the ***teleological*** power of judgment: for under the first is understood the faculty that appraises formal purposiveness (called subjective in other contexts) by means of the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, while under the second is understood the faculty that appraises nature's real (objective) purposiveness by means of understanding and reason.

In any critique of the power of judgment, the portion dealing with the aesthetic power of judgment is essential to the whole, since this alone harbors a principle that the power of judgment lays entirely _a priori_ beneath its reflection upon nature — namely the principle of a formal purposiveness of nature, conforming to its particular (empirical) laws, on behalf of our faculty of cognition, and without such purposiveness the understanding could not find its way about in nature: whereas {{ LI }} not a single ground can be given _a priori_ at all, nor is even the mere possibility of it rendered evident from the concept of a nature taken as an object of experience — whether at large or in particular — namely that objective ends of nature must exist, in other words things that are possible only as natural ends; instead, the power of judgment by itself, containing no such _a priori_ principle within it, does hold the rule, in the instances that arise (those of certain products), for making use of the concept of ends on reason's behalf, once that transcendental {{{ 194 }}} principle has already prepared the understanding to bring the concept of an end (at least as regards form) to bear upon nature.

The transcendental principle, on the other hand — the principle of representing to ourselves, upon the form of a thing, a purposiveness of nature that bears subjectively on our faculty of cognition, and of treating this as a principle for appraising that thing — leaves entirely open where and in what cases I am to carry out such appraisal, namely as the appraisal of some product that comes under a principle of purposiveness and not merely under nature's general laws, and it turns over to the ***aesthetic*** power of judgment the business of making out, in taste, how well that product (its form) accords with our faculties of cognition (so far as this faculty decides the matter not by concord with concepts but by way of feeling). The teleologically employed power of judgment, on the contrary, specifies with exactness the conditions under {{ LII }} which a thing (an organized body, say) is to be judged according to the idea of a natural end; yet from the concept of nature as an object of experience it can put forward no fundamental proposition licensing us to assign to nature any relation to ends _a priori_, nor even, in an indeterminate way, to take such a relation from what we actually experience in products of this kind: and the reason for this is that many particular experiences have to be set up and surveyed under the unity of their principle before an objective purposiveness in some given object can be cognized — and even so, only empirically. — The aesthetic power of judgment is thus a faculty all its own, one for judging things by a rule and not by concepts. The teleological power of judgment is not a faculty of a special sort; it is nothing but the reflecting power of judgment in general, so far as it operates, as everywhere within theoretical cognition, by concepts, though in regard to certain objects of nature it works by principles of a special kind — those of a power of judgment that merely reflects and does not determine its objects — and hence, according to its use, it belongs to the theoretical branch of philosophy, while, on account of those special principles which lack the determining character a doctrine would require of them, it must at the same time constitute a part of the critique all its own; the aesthetic power of judgment, by contrast, contributes nothing whatever to the cognition of its objects, and so must be counted ***only*** toward the critique of the subject that judges and of the faculties of cognition that belong to it, so far as these {{ LIII }} prove capable of _a priori_ principles, no matter which use (theoretical or practical) they may in other respects serve — a critique that constitutes the propaedeutic of all philosophy.
