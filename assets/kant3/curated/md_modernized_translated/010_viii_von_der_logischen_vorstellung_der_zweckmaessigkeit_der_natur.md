## VIII. On the Logical Representation of the Purposiveness of Nature

In an object given in experience purposiveness can be represented: either from a merely subjective ground, as the correspondence of its form, in the ***apprehension*** (_apprehensio_) of it prior to any concept, with the faculties of cognition, in order to unite the intuition with concepts for a cognition in general; or from an objective one, as the correspondence of its form with the possibility of the thing itself, in accordance with a concept of it that precedes {{ XLIX }} and contains the ground of this form. We have seen: that the representation of the purposiveness of the first kind rests on the immediate pleasure in the form of the object in the mere reflection upon it; that of the purposiveness of the second kind, therefore, since it relates the form of the object not to the faculties of cognition of the subject in the apprehension of it, but to a determinate cognition of the object under a given concept, has nothing to do with a feeling of pleasure in the things, but with the understanding in the appraisal of them. When the concept of an object is given, then the business of the power of judgment in the use of it for cognition consists in the ***presentation*** (_exhibitio_), i.e., in placing alongside the concept a corresponding intuition: whether this happens through our own imagination, as in art, {{{ 193 }}} when we realize a previously conceived concept of an object that is an end for us, or through nature in its technique (as in the case of organized bodies), when we lay our concept of an end under it for the appraisal of its product; in which case not merely ***purposiveness*** of nature in the form of the thing, but this product of it is represented as a ***natural end***. — Although our concept of a subjective purposiveness of nature in its forms in accordance with empirical laws is no concept of the object at all, but only a {{ L }} principle of the power of judgment for providing itself with concepts in this its excessive manifoldness (for being able to orient itself in it): we nevertheless thereby attribute to it, as it were, a regard for our faculty of cognition in accordance with the analogy of an end; and thus we can regard ***natural beauty*** as the ***presentation*** of the concept of the formal (merely subjective) and the ***natural ends*** as the presentation of the concept of a real (objective) purposiveness, the one of which we appraise through taste (aesthetically, by means of the feeling of pleasure), the other through understanding and reason (logically, according to concepts).

On this is grounded the division of the critique of the power of judgment into that of the ***aesthetic*** and the ***teleological***: by the first being understood the faculty for appraising the formal purposiveness (otherwise also called subjective) through the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, by the second the faculty for appraising the real purposiveness (objective) of nature through understanding and reason.

In a critique of the power of judgment the part which contains the aesthetic power of judgment belongs to it essentially, because this alone contains a principle which the power of judgment lays wholly _a priori_ at the ground of its reflection upon nature, namely that of a formal purposiveness of nature in accordance with its particular (empirical) laws for our faculty of cognition, without which the understanding could not find its way into it: whereas {{ LI }} no ground _a priori_ at all can be given, indeed not even the possibility of it is illuminated from the concept of a nature, as an object of experience in general as well as in particular, that there must be objective ends of nature, i.e., things that are possible only as natural ends; but only the power of judgment, without containing a principle for this _a priori_ in itself, contains the rule in occurring cases (of certain products), in order to make use, for the behoof of reason, of the concept of ends, after that transcendental {{{ 194 }}} principle has already prepared the understanding to apply the concept of an end (at least as regards its form) to nature.

The transcendental principle, however, of representing a purposiveness of nature in subjective relation to our faculty of cognition in the form of a thing as a principle for the appraisal of it leaves it entirely undetermined where and in which cases I am to undertake the appraisal as that of a product in accordance with a principle of purposiveness and not rather merely in accordance with universal laws of nature, and leaves it to the ***aesthetic*** power of judgment to make out, in taste, the suitability of it (of its form) to our faculties of cognition (insofar as this decides not through correspondence with concepts but through the feeling). By contrast, the teleologically used power of judgment specifies determinately the conditions under {{ LII }} which something (e.g., an organized body) is to be appraised in accordance with the idea of an end of nature; but it can adduce no principle from the concept of nature as an object of experience for the warrant to attribute to it a relation to ends _a priori_, and even to assume, though only indeterminately, anything of this sort from actual experience in such products: the ground of which is that many particular experiences must be undertaken and considered under the unity of their principle in order to be able to cognize, only empirically, an objective purposiveness in a certain object. — The aesthetic power of judgment is therefore a particular faculty for appraising things according to a rule, but not according to concepts. The teleological is no particular faculty, but only the reflecting power of judgment in general, insofar as it proceeds, as everywhere in theoretical cognition, according to concepts, but with regard to certain objects of nature according to particular principles, namely those of a merely reflecting, not an object-determining power of judgment, and thus belongs, as regards its application, to the theoretical part of philosophy, and on account of the particular principles, which are not determining as they must be in a doctrine, must also constitute a particular part of the critique; whereas the aesthetic power of judgment contributes nothing to the cognition of its objects and must therefore be counted ***only*** to the critique of the judging subject and of its faculties of cognition, insofar as they {{ LIII }} are capable of principles _a priori_, of whatever use (the theoretical or the practical) these may otherwise be, which critique is the propaedeutic of all philosophy.
