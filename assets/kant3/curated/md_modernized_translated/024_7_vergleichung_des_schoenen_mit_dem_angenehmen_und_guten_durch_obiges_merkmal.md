## § 7. Comparison of the beautiful with the agreeable and the good through the above mark

Concerning the ***agreeable***, everyone acquiesces: that the judgment which he bases on a private feeling, and through which he declares of an object that he finds it pleasing, is likewise restricted merely to his own person. Hence he is quite willing, when he says the Canary wine {{ 19 }} is agreeable, to let another correct his phrasing and remind him that he should say instead it is agreeable ***to me***; and this obtains not only for what the tongue, the palate, and the gullet taste, but also for whatever may prove agreeable to each person's eyes and ears. For one person the violet color counts as soft and endearing, while another finds it dead and extinguished. The sound of wind instruments is dear to one man, whereas another prefers that of the stringed ones. To quarrel over this with the intention of censuring as incorrect the judgment of others which differs from our own, just as if it were logically opposed to it, would be folly; regarding the agreeable, therefore, the principle holds: ***everyone has his own taste*** (of the senses).

With the beautiful matters stand quite otherwise. It would (quite the reverse) be laughable if someone who fancied himself on his taste meant to vindicate himself thus: this object (the building before our eyes, the garment worn by that man, the concert now reaching our ears, the poem set out for judging) is ***for me*** beautiful. For he need not deem it ***beautiful*** if it merely pleases him. Many things may hold charm and pleasantness for him, and with that no one troubles himself; yet once he presents something as beautiful, he demands of others the very same satisfaction: his verdict is issued not for himself alone but for {{ 20 }} everyone, and from then on he treats beauty as though it were a quality belonging to things themselves. He says accordingly: the ***thing*** is beautiful, and does not reckon on others' concurrence in his judgment of satisfaction, {{{ 213 }}} because he has several times found theirs in agreement with his own, but rather ***demands*** it of them. He reproaches them if they judge otherwise, and refuses them the taste that he nonetheless insists they ought to possess; and to that extent one cannot say: everyone has his own peculiar taste. That would come to the same as saying: there is no taste whatever, i.e., no aesthetic judgment able to lay a rightful claim to the assent of everyone.

And yet even with regard to the agreeable one finds that a concordance among human beings can be encountered in the judging of it, in view of which one does after all deny taste to some and allow it to others, and indeed not in the sense of an organic sense, but as a capacity for judging with respect to the agreeable as such. Thus one says of a man who understands how to regale his guests with agreeable things (of enjoyment through every sense) so that they are pleased one and all: this man has taste. But in this case the universality is taken only comparatively; and here there are merely ***generale*** rules (as all empirical ones are), not ***universale*** ones, the latter being what the judgment of taste about the beautiful ventures upon or asserts a claim to. It {{ 21 }} amounts to a judgment bearing on sociability, to the extent that this sociability is grounded in empirical rules. As regards the good, judgments too, and justifiably so, assert a claim to validity for everyone; but the good is presented as an object of universal satisfaction solely ***through a concept***, something that holds true of neither the agreeable nor the beautiful.
