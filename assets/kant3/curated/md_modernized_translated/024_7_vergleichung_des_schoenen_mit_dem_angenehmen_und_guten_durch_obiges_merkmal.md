## § 7. Comparison of the Beautiful with the Agreeable and the Good through the Above Characteristic

With regard to the ***agreeable*** everyone is content: that his judgment, which he grounds on a private feeling, and by which he says of an object that it pleases him, also restricts itself merely to his own person. Hence he is gladly satisfied that, if he says: Canary wine {{ 19 }} is agreeable, another corrects his expression and reminds him that he should say: it is agreeable ***to me***; and this not only in the taste of the tongue, the palate, and the throat, but also in that which may be agreeable to everyone for the eyes and ears. To one the violet color is gentle and lovely, to another dead and lifeless. One loves the tone of wind instruments, the other that of stringed instruments. To dispute about this with the aim of reproaching as incorrect the judgment of others which differs from our own, just as if it were logically opposed to it, would be folly; with regard to the agreeable, then, the principle holds: ***everyone has his own taste*** (of sense).

With the beautiful it is entirely otherwise. It would be (precisely the reverse) ridiculous if someone who prided himself on his taste thought to justify himself thus: this object (the building that we see, the garment that someone wears, the concert that we hear, the poem that is set up for appraisal) is beautiful ***for me***. For he must not call it ***beautiful*** if it merely pleases him. Charm and agreeableness may be present for him in many things, no one is concerned about that; but if he declares something to be beautiful, then he expects the very same satisfaction of others: he judges not merely for himself, but for {{ 20 }} everyone, and speaks then of beauty as if it were a property of things. He says therefore: the ***thing*** is beautiful, and does not count on the agreement of others in his judgment of satisfaction {{{ 213 }}} because he has several times found them in agreement with his own, but rather ***demands*** it of them. He blames them if they judge otherwise, and denies them taste, which he nevertheless requires of them that they ought to have; and to this extent one cannot say: everyone has his own special taste. This would amount to saying: there is no taste at all, i.e., no aesthetic judgment that could make a rightful claim to the assent of everyone.

Nevertheless one also finds, with regard to the agreeable, that in the appraisal of it a unanimity among human beings can be met with, in view of which one nonetheless denies taste to some and concedes it to others, and indeed not in the meaning of an organ-sense, but as a faculty of appraisal with regard to the agreeable in general. Thus one says of someone who knows how to entertain his guests with agreeable things (of enjoyment through all the senses) so that it pleases them all: he has taste. But here the universality is taken only comparatively; and there are only ***general*** (as all the empirical ones are), not ***universal*** rules, the latter of which the judgment of taste about the beautiful undertakes or lays claim to. It {{ 21 }} is a judgment in relation to sociability, insofar as this rests on empirical rules. With regard to the good, judgments also rightly lay claim to validity for everyone; but the good is represented only ***through a concept*** as the object of a universal satisfaction, which is the case neither with the agreeable nor with the beautiful.
