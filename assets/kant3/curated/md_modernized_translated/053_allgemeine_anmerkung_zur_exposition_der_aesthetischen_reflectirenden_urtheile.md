## General remark on the exposition of the aesthetic reflecting judgments

With respect to the feeling of pleasure, an object is to be counted as belonging either to the ***agreeable***, or the ***beautiful***, or the ***sublime***, or the ***good*** (absolutely) (_iucundum_, _pulchrum_, _sublime_, _honestum_).

The ***agreeable***, as an incentive of the desires, is throughout of one and the same kind, no matter from where it comes and however specifically different the representation (of sense and of sensation, considered objectively) may be. Hence, in judging its influence upon the mind, what matters is only the multitude of charms (simultaneous and successive) and, as it were, only the mass of agreeable sensation; and this, accordingly, can be made intelligible through nothing other than ***quantity***. It also does not cultivate, but belongs to mere enjoyment. — The ***beautiful***, by contrast, requires the representation of a certain ***quality*** of the object, which can also be made intelligible and brought under concepts (although in the aesthetic judgment it is not so brought); and it cultivates, in that it teaches us at the same time to heed purposiveness within the feeling of pleasure. — The ***sublime*** consists solely in the ***relation*** in which {{ 114 }} {{{ 267 }}} the sensible in the representation of nature is judged as suitable for a possible supersensible use of it. — The ***absolutely good***, appraised subjectively by the feeling that it instils (the object of moral feeling) as the determinability of the subject's powers through the representation of an ***absolutely necessitating*** law, is distinguished chiefly through the ***modality*** of a necessity resting on concepts _a priori_, which contains within itself not merely a ***claim*** but also a ***command*** of approval for everyone, and belongs in itself not to the aesthetic but to the pure intellectual power of judgment; nor is it ascribed in a merely reflecting but in a determining judgment, not to nature but to freedom. Yet the ***determinability of the subject*** through this idea—and indeed of a subject that can feel within itself ***obstacles*** in sensibility, yet at the same time superiority over it through overcoming it, as a ***modification of its state***—that is, the moral feeling, is nevertheless so far akin to the aesthetic power of judgment and its ***formal conditions*** that it can serve to present the lawfulness of an action done from duty as at the same time aesthetic, that is, as sublime, or even as beautiful, without forfeiting its purity: which does not occur if one were to place it in natural connection with the feeling of the agreeable.

If one draws the result from the foregoing exposition of both kinds of aesthetic judgments, the following brief explanations would follow from it:

***Beautiful*** is that which pleases in the mere judging (thus not by means of the sensation of sense in accordance with a concept of the understanding). {{ 115 }} From this it follows of itself that it must please without any interest whatsoever.

***Sublime*** is that which pleases immediately through its resistance to the interest of the senses.

Both, as explanations of aesthetic universally valid judging, refer to subjective grounds—namely, on the one hand, those of sensibility insofar as it is purposive in favor of the contemplative understanding, and on the other hand insofar as it is purposive against sensibility yet for the ends of practical reason, and both nonetheless united in the same subject, in relation to the moral feeling. The beautiful prepares us to love something, even nature, without interest; the sublime, to hold it in high esteem even against our (sensible) interest.

{{{ 268 }}} One can describe the sublime thus: it is an object (of nature) ***whose representation determines the mind to think of the unattainability of nature as a presentation of ideas***.

Taken literally and considered logically, ideas cannot be presented. But if we extend our empirical faculty of representation (mathematically or dynamically) for the intuition of nature, then reason inevitably comes in, as the faculty of the independence of absolute totality, and brings forth the striving of the mind, though futile, to make the representation of the senses adequate to this. This striving, together with the feeling that the idea stays out of the imagination's reach, is itself a presentation of how our mind is subjectively purposive when it puts the imagination to work for its supersensible vocation, and it obliges us to ***think*** nature itself, subjectively, taken as a whole, as a presentation of something supersensible, {{ 116 }} though we cannot render this presentation ***objectively***.

For we soon become aware that nature in space and time entirely lacks the unconditioned, and hence also absolute magnitude, which is nevertheless demanded by the most common reason. By this very fact we are also reminded that we have to do only with a nature as appearance, and that this itself must still be regarded as a mere presentation of a nature in itself (which reason has in the idea). This idea of the supersensible, however, which we cannot indeed determine any further, and hence cannot ***cognize*** nature as a presentation of it but can only ***think*** it, is awakened in us by an object whose aesthetic judging strains the imagination to its limit, whether of extension (mathematically) or of its might over the mind (dynamically), inasmuch as it is grounded on the feeling (the moral feeling) of a vocation of the mind that entirely oversteps the domain of the former, with respect to which the representation of the object is judged as subjectively purposive.

Indeed, a feeling for the sublime of nature cannot well be thought without connecting with it a disposition of the mind similar to that for the moral; and although an immediate pleasure in nature's beauty likewise both presupposes and fosters a certain ***liberality*** in the manner of thought, that is, an independence of the satisfaction from mere enjoyment of the senses, freedom is thereby represented still more as in ***play*** than under a lawful ***business***: which is the {{{ 269 }}} genuine character of the morality of the human being, where reason must do violence to sensibility, only that in the aesthetic judgment about {{ 117 }} the sublime this violence is represented as exercised by the imagination itself, functioning as an instrument of reason.

The satisfaction taken in the sublime of nature is therefore likewise only ***negative*** (whereas that in the beautiful is ***positive***), namely a feeling of the deprivation of the freedom of the imagination by itself, in that it is purposively determined according to a law other than that of empirical use. Thereby it acquires an extension and might that is greater than the one it sacrifices, but whose ground remains hidden from it, whereas it ***feels*** both the sacrifice, or the deprivation, and, along with it, the cause to which it is thereby made subject. The ***astonishment*** bordering on terror, the horror and the sacred awe that seizes the spectator at the sight of mountain masses ascending to the heavens, deep chasms and the waters raging within them, deeply shadowed wastelands inviting melancholy reflection, etc., is, given the safety in which he knows himself to be, not actual fear, but only an attempt to engage with it by means of the imagination, in order to feel the might of that very faculty, to connect the movement of the mind thereby aroused with the mind's state of rest, and thus to be superior to the nature within us, and hence also to that outside us, insofar as it can have an influence on the feeling of our well-being. For the imagination, according to the law of association, makes our state of contentment physically dependent; but this same imagination, according to principles of the schematism of the power of judgment (and consequently insofar as it is subordinated to freedom), is an instrument of reason and its ideas, and as such a might to assert our independence against the influences of nature, to disparage as small what is great according to the former, and thus to place the {{ 118 }} absolutely great only in its (the subject's) own vocation. This reflection of the aesthetic power of judgment, to raise itself to adequacy with reason (yet without a determinate concept of it), nevertheless presents the object itself as subjectively purposive, through the objective inadequacy of the imagination in its greatest extension for reason (as the faculty of ideas).

Here one must in general attend to what was already recalled above, namely that in the transcendental aesthetic of the power of judgment {{{ 270 }}} the discussion must be solely of pure aesthetic judgments, and consequently the examples must not be drawn from such beautiful or sublime natural objects as already presuppose the concept of an end; for then it would be either a teleological purposiveness, or one grounded on mere sensations of an object (gratification or pain), and hence in the former case not aesthetic and in the latter not merely formal. Thus if one calls the sight of the starry heavens ***sublime***, one must not lay at the ground of its judging concepts of worlds inhabited by rational beings, and then the shining points with which we behold the space over our heads filled up, as their suns moving in orbits arranged very purposively for them, but rather merely as one sees it, as a vast vault that encompasses everything; and merely under this representation must we place the sublimity that a pure aesthetic judgment ascribes to this object. Likewise the sight of the ocean, not as we, enriched with all sorts of knowledge (which, however, is not contained in the immediate intuition), ***think*** it; say as a vast realm of aquatic creatures, as the great treasury of water for the vapors that impregnate the {{ 119 }} air with clouds for the benefit of the lands, or also as an element that indeed separates parts of the world from one another yet nonetheless makes possible the greatest community among them: for that yields nothing but teleological judgments; rather one must, merely as the poets do, according to what the sight of it shows—say, when it is contemplated at rest, as a clear mirror of water bounded only by the sky, but, when it is turbulent, as an abyss threatening to swallow up everything—nevertheless be able to find the ocean sublime. The same is to be said of the sublime and the beautiful in the human figure, where we must not look back to concepts of the ends ***for which*** all its limbs exist as determining grounds of the judgment, nor let the agreement with them ***enter into*** our (then no longer pure) aesthetic judgment, although that they do not conflict with those ends is admittedly a necessary condition of aesthetic satisfaction as well. Aesthetic purposiveness is the lawfulness of the power of judgment in its ***freedom***. The satisfaction in the object depends upon the relation into which we choose to set the imagination: only that, by itself, it should keep the mind occupied in a free activity. If, by contrast, something else—be it sensation of sense or concept of the understanding—{{{ 271 }}} determines the judgment, then it is indeed lawful, but not the judgment of a ***free*** power of judgment.

Thus if one speaks of intellectual beauty or sublimity, then, ***first***, these expressions are not entirely correct, because they are aesthetic kinds of representation that, if we were mere pure intelligences (or even transported ourselves in thought into this quality), would not be met with in us at all; ***second***, although both, as objects of an {{ 120 }} intellectual (moral) satisfaction, are indeed so far compatible with the aesthetic in that they ***rest*** on no interest, they are nonetheless in turn difficult to unite with the latter, because they are supposed to ***produce*** an interest which, if the presentation is to accord with the satisfaction in the aesthetic judging, could in the latter never come about otherwise than through an interest of sense that one connects with it in the presentation, whereby, however, the intellectual purposiveness is impaired and rendered impure.

The object of a pure, unconditioned intellectual satisfaction is the moral law considered in the might that it exercises in us over each and every incentive of the mind ***preceding it***; and since this might actually makes itself aesthetically knowable only through sacrifices (which is a deprivation, though for the sake of inner freedom, while it uncovers in us an unfathomable depth of this supersensible faculty with its consequences extending into the unforeseeable), the satisfaction, from the aesthetic side (in relation to sensibility), is negative, that is, contrary to this interest, but considered from the intellectual side, positive and bound up with an interest. From this it follows that the intellectual good, purposive in itself (the morally good), judged aesthetically, must be represented not so much as beautiful as rather as sublime, so that it arouses more the feeling of respect (which scorns charm) than that of love and intimate affection; because human nature does not accord with that good of itself, but only through the violence that reason does to sensibility. Conversely, that which we call sublime in nature outside us, or also within us (e.g., certain affects), likewise comes to be represented, and thereby to become interesting, only as a {{ 121 }} might of the mind to soar above ***certain*** obstacles of sensibility by means of moral principles.

I will dwell a little on the latter. The idea of the good with {{{ 272 }}} affect is called ***enthusiasm***. This state of mind seems to be sublime, so much so that one commonly professes: without it nothing great can be accomplished. Now every affect[^*], however, is blind, either in the choice of its end, or, even when this has been given by reason, in the execution of it; for it is that movement of the mind which makes it incapable of undertaking free deliberation of principles in order to determine itself accordingly. Thus it can in no way merit a satisfaction of reason. Aesthetically, nonetheless, enthusiasm is sublime, because it is a straining of the powers through ideas that give the mind a momentum which acts far more powerfully and enduringly than the impulse through representations of sense. But (which seems strange) even ***absence of affect*** (_Apatheia, Phlegma in significatu bono_) in a mind that emphatically {{ 122 }} pursues its unalterable principles is sublime, and indeed in a far more excellent way, because it at the same time has the satisfaction of pure reason on its side. A mind of this kind alone is called noble: an expression that is afterward applied also to things, e.g., buildings, a garment, a style of writing, bodily bearing, and the like, when these arouse not so much ***astonishment*** (affect in the representation of novelty that exceeds expectation) as ***admiration*** (an astonishment that does not cease with the loss of novelty), which happens when ideas, in their presentation, accord unintentionally and without art with aesthetic satisfaction.

Every affect of the valiant kind (namely the kind that stirs up the consciousness of our powers to overcome any resistance (_animi strenui_)) is ***aesthetically sublime***, e.g., anger, even despair (namely the ***indignant*** kind, but not the ***despondent***). But the affect of the melting kind (which makes the very striving to resist an object of displeasure (_animum languidum_)) has nothing ***noble*** {{{ 273 }}} about it, though it can be counted as belonging to the beautiful in the manner of sensing. Hence the ***emotions*** that can grow strong enough to become affects are also very diverse. There are ***courageous*** emotions, there are ***tender*** ones. The latter, when they rise to the level of affect, are worth nothing at all; the inclination to them is called ***sentimentality***. A sympathetic pain that will not let itself be consoled, or into which we deliberately enter, when it concerns fictitious evils, to the point of being deceived by fantasy as if they were real, proves and makes a soft but at the same time weak soul, which shows a beautiful side and can indeed be called fanciful, but not even enthusiastic. Novels, tearful plays, {{ 123 }} shallow precepts of morality that toy with (though falsely) so-called noble dispositions, but in fact make the heart withered and insensitive to the strict precept of duty, incapable of all respect for the dignity of humanity in our own person and for the right of human beings (which is something entirely other than their happiness) and, in general, of all firm principles; even a religious discourse that recommends a crawling, low courting of favor and ingratiation, which gives up all trust in one's own capacity for resistance against the evil within us, in place of the sturdy resolve to try the powers that still remain to us, for all our frailty, in overcoming the inclinations; the false humility that locates in self-contempt, in a whining, feigned remorse, and in a merely passive frame of mind the sole manner in which one might become pleasing to the highest being: none of these accord even with what could be counted as belonging to beauty, still less with what could be counted as belonging to the sublimity of the mind's disposition.

But even tumultuous movements of the mind—whether they come to be linked, under the title of edification, with religious ideas, or, as pertaining merely to culture, with ideas that harbor a social interest—however much they may strain the imagination, can by no means lay claim to the honor of a ***sublime*** presentation, if they do not leave behind a disposition of mind that has an influence, however indirect, upon the consciousness of one's strength and resolve toward that which carries with it pure intellectual purposiveness (the supersensible). For otherwise all these emotions amount to nothing but ***motion***, which one is happy to have for the sake of one's health. The agreeable lassitude that {{ 124 }} follows such a shaking-up by the play of the affects is an enjoyment {{{ 274 }}} of well-being arising from the equilibrium of the various vital forces within us that is thereby restored: which in the end amounts to the same thing as that which the voluptuaries of the Orient find so comfortable when they have their body, as it were, thoroughly kneaded and all their muscles and joints gently pressed and bent; only that there the moving principle is for the most part within us, whereas here it is entirely outside us. Thus many a person now believes himself edified by a sermon in which nothing at all is built up (no system of good maxims); or improved by a tragedy, when he is merely glad about boredom happily driven away. Thus the sublime must always have a relation to the ***way of thinking***, that is, to maxims for procuring for the intellectual and the ideas of reason supremacy over sensibility.

One need not worry that the feeling of the sublime will lose by such an abstract manner of presentation, which becomes wholly negative with respect to the sensible; for the imagination, although beyond the sensible it finds nothing to which it can hold fast, nevertheless feels itself unbounded precisely through this removal of its limits: and that separation is thus a presentation of the infinite, which for that very reason can indeed never be anything more than a merely negative presentation, but which nonetheless expands the soul. Perhaps there is no more sublime passage in the book of law of the Jews than the commandment: You shall make for yourself no graven image, nor any likeness whatsoever, neither of that which is in heaven, nor on the earth, nor under the earth, etc. This commandment alone can explain the enthusiasm that the Jewish people, in its civilized epoch, felt for its religion when it compared itself with other peoples, or that pride which Mohammedanism {{ 125 }} inspires. The very same holds also of the representation of the moral law and of the predisposition to morality within us. It is an entirely mistaken worry that, if one deprives it of everything that can recommend it to the senses, it would then carry with it nothing but a cold, lifeless approval and no moving force or emotion. It is precisely the reverse; for there, where the senses now see nothing more before them, and the unmistakable and ineffaceable idea of morality nevertheless remains, it would sooner be necessary to moderate the momentum of an unbounded imagination, so as not to let it rise to the level of enthusiasm, than, from a fear that these ideas lack force, to look for help on their behalf in images and childish contrivances. Hence governments too have {{{ 275 }}} gladly permitted religion to be amply furnished with the latter accessories, and have thus sought to relieve the subject of the trouble, but at the same time also of the capacity, of extending his powers of soul beyond the limits that one arbitrarily sets for him and by means of which one can more easily handle him as merely passive.

This pure, soul-elevating, merely negative presentation of morality, by contrast, brings no danger of ***fanaticism***, which ***is a delusion of wanting to see something beyond every bound of sensibility***, that is, to dream according to principles (to rave with reason), to ***will*** it; precisely because in the former the presentation is merely negative. For ***the inscrutability of the idea of freedom*** entirely cuts off the path to all positive presentation: the moral law, however, is in itself sufficient within us and originally determining, so that it does not even permit us to look about for a determining ground outside of it. If {{ 126 }} enthusiasm is to be compared with ***madness***, then fanaticism is to be compared with ***derangement***, of which the latter is the least compatible of all with the sublime, because it is broodingly ridiculous. In enthusiasm, as an affect, the imagination is unbridled; in fanaticism, as a deep-rooted, brooding passion, it is ruleless. The former is a passing accident that occasionally befalls even the healthiest understanding; the second is a disease that deranges it.

***Simplicity*** (artless purposiveness) is, so to speak, the style of nature in the sublime, and likewise that of morality, which is a second (supersensible) nature, of which we know only the laws, without being able to attain by intuition the supersensible faculty within us that contains the ground of this legislation.

It is further to be noted that, although the satisfaction in the beautiful, just as much as that in the sublime, is not only recognizably distinguished from the other aesthetic judgings through universal ***communicability***, but also, through this property, acquires an interest in relation to society (in which it can be communicated), the ***separation from all society*** is nonetheless likewise regarded as something sublime, if it rests on ideas that look beyond all sensible interest. To be sufficient unto oneself, and hence not to need society, without, however, being unsociable, that is, fleeing it, is something that approaches the sublime, as does every rising above needs. By contrast, to flee human beings out of ***misanthropy***, because one is hostile to them, {{{ 276 }}} or out of ***anthropophobia*** (dread of humankind), because one fears them as one's enemies, is partly ugly, partly contemptible. Nonetheless there is a (very improperly so-called) misanthropy, the predisposition to which {{ 127 }} tends to settle with age in the mind of many a right-thinking person, one who, as regards ***benevolence***, is philanthropic enough, but who through a long, sad experience has been drawn far away from ***satisfaction*** in human beings: to which the inclination to seclusion, the fanciful wish to be able to spend one's life on a remote country estate, or also (in young persons) the dreamed-of happiness of spending it with a small family on an island unknown to the rest of the world—a wish that the novelists or poets of Robinsonades know so well how to exploit—bears witness. Falsity, ingratitude, injustice, the childishness in the ends that we ourselves hold to be important and great, in the pursuit of which human beings inflict upon one another every conceivable evil, stand so in contradiction with the idea of what they could be if they willed it, and are so contrary to the lively wish to see them better, that, in order not to hate them, since one cannot love them, the renunciation of all social joys seems to be but a small sacrifice. This sadness—not over the evils that fate imposes upon other human beings (of which sympathy is the cause), but over those that they inflict upon themselves (which rests on antipathy in principles)—is, because it rests on ideas, sublime, whereas the former can likewise count only as beautiful. — The equally ingenious and thorough ***Saussure*** says, in the description of his Alpine journeys, of ***Bonhomme***, one of the Savoyard mountains: »There prevails there a certain ***insipid sadness***.« He was therefore acquainted, after all, with an ***interesting*** sadness that the sight of a wasteland inspires—into which human beings might well wish to transport themselves in order to hear or learn nothing {{ 128 }} further of the world—which then need not be so entirely inhospitable as to offer human beings only a most toilsome sojourn. — I make this remark only with the intention of recalling that grief too (not dejected sadness) can be counted among the ***vigorous*** affects, when it has its ground in moral ideas; but that, if it is grounded on sympathy and as such is also amiable, it belongs merely to the ***melting*** affects: in order thereby to draw attention to the disposition of mind that is ***sublime*** only in the former case. {{{ 277 }}}

One can now also compare the transcendental exposition of aesthetic judgments here carried out with the physiological one, as a ***Burke*** and many acute men among us have worked it out, in order to see where a merely empirical exposition of the sublime and the beautiful leads. ***Burke***[^**], who deserves to be named as the foremost author in this kind of treatment, brings out along this path (p. 223 of his work): »that the feeling of the sublime is grounded on the drive to self-preservation and on ***fear***, that is, on a pain which, because it does not go so far as the actual derangement of the bodily parts, produces movements that, since they cleanse the finer or coarser vessels of dangerous and burdensome obstructions, are capable of arousing agreeable sensations—not indeed pleasure, but a kind of gratifying shudder, a certain calm that is mingled with {{ 129 }} terror.« The beautiful, which he grounds on love (from which, however, he wants desire to be kept separate), he traces (pp. 251–252) »to the slackening, relaxation, and enfeeblement of the fibers of the body, and hence a softening, dissolution, exhaustion, a sinking away, a dying away, a melting away with pleasure.« And now he confirms this manner of explanation not only through cases in which the imagination in connection with the understanding, but even in connection with sensation of sense, can arouse in us the feeling of the beautiful as well as of the sublime. — As psychological observations, these dissections of the phenomena of our mind are exceedingly fine and provide rich material for the most favored investigations of empirical anthropology. Nor can it be denied that all representations in us, whether they be objectively merely sensible or wholly intellectual, can nevertheless be subjectively bound up with gratification or pain, however imperceptible the one or the other may be (since they one and all affect the feeling of life, and not a single one of them, insofar as it is a modification of the subject, can be indifferent); indeed that, as Epicurus maintained, ***gratification*** and ***pain*** are in the end always corporeal, whether they may begin from imagination or even from representations of the understanding: because life, without the {{{ 278 }}} feeling of the corporeal organ, would be a mere consciousness of one's existence, but no feeling of well- or ill-being, that is, of the promotion or inhibition of the vital forces; because the mind, for itself alone, is wholly life (the life-principle itself), and obstacles or promotions must be sought outside it, and yet within the human being himself, and hence in connection with his body.

But if one places the satisfaction in the object entirely and wholly {{ 130 }} in the fact that it gratifies through charm or through emotion, then one may also not expect any ***other*** person to agree with the aesthetic judgment that ***we*** pass; for about this each rightly consults only his own private sense. But then all censorship of taste also entirely ceases; unless one were to make the example that others give through the accidental agreement of their judgments into a ***command*** of approval for us—a principle against which we would nevertheless presumably resist and appeal to the natural right of subjecting the judgment that rests on the immediate feeling of one's own well-being to one's own sense and not to that of others.

Thus if the judgment of taste must necessarily count not as ***egoistic***, but, according to its inner nature—that is, for its own sake, not for the sake of the examples that others give of their taste—as ***pluralistic***, when one esteems it as one that may at the same time demand that everyone should concur with it: then some principle _a priori_ (be it objective or subjective) must lie at its ground, to which one can never attain by ferreting out empirical laws of the alterations of the mind: because these only make it known how one judges, but do not command how one ought to judge, and indeed in such a way that the command is ***unconditioned***; a thing which the judgments of taste presuppose, in that they want the satisfaction to be connected ***immediately*** with a representation. Thus the empirical exposition of aesthetic judgments may always make the beginning, in order to supply the material for a higher investigation; a transcendental discussion of this faculty is nevertheless possible and essentially belongs to the critique of taste. {{ 131 }} For unless it had principles _a priori_, it could not possibly sit in judgment over the judgments of others and pass verdicts of approval or rejection upon them with even a semblance of right.
