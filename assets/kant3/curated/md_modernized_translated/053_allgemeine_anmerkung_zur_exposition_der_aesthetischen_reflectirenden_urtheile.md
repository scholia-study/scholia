## General remark on the exposition of the aesthetic reflecting judgments

As regards the feeling of pleasure, any object is to be reckoned as belonging to one among the ***agreeable***, the ***beautiful***, the ***sublime***, and the ***good*** (absolutely) (_iucundum_, _pulchrum_, _sublime_, _honestum_).

The ***agreeable***, as an incentive of the desires, is throughout of one and the same kind, no matter from where it comes and however specifically different the representation (of sense and of sensation, considered objectively) may be. Hence, in judging its influence upon the mind, what matters is only the multitude of charms (simultaneous and successive) and, as it were, only the mass of agreeable sensation; and this, accordingly, can be made intelligible through nothing other than ***quantity***. Nor does it cultivate; it pertains solely to enjoyment. — The ***beautiful***, on the other hand, calls for the representation of a certain ***quality*** in the object, one that admits of being made intelligible and brought under concepts (even though the aesthetic judgment does not in fact bring it there); and it cultivates us, since it teaches us at once to attend to purposiveness in the feeling of pleasure. — The ***sublime*** lies purely in the ***relation*** wherein {{ 114 }} {{{ 267 }}} the sensible within nature's representation is appraised as fit for a possible supersensible employment of it. — The ***absolutely good***, when it is appraised subjectively according to the feeling it inspires (as the object of moral feeling)—namely, as the determinability of the subject's powers by the representation of an ***absolutely necessitating*** law—differs above all through the ***modality*** of a necessity that rests upon concepts _a priori_ and that harbors within itself not a mere ***claim*** but likewise a ***command*** of assent binding on everyone; and of itself it pertains not to the aesthetic power of judgment but to the pure intellectual one, being ascribed, moreover, not in a merely reflecting judgment but in a determining one, and not to nature but to freedom. Yet the ***determinability of the subject*** by this idea—that of a subject able to feel within itself ***obstacles*** posed by sensibility, and at the same time, through surmounting them, a superiority over it as a ***modification of its state***—that is to say, the moral feeling, stands nonetheless in such kinship with the aesthetic power of judgment and with its ***formal conditions*** that it can help to exhibit the lawfulness of an action done from duty as itself aesthetic, namely as sublime, or indeed as beautiful, without any loss to its purity: something that fails to happen were one to bring it into natural union with the feeling of the agreeable.

Should one extract the upshot of the exposition given thus far of the two kinds of aesthetic judgment, the following concise explanations would emerge from it:

***Beautiful*** is what gives pleasure in the mere act of judging (hence not through the sensation of sense following a concept of the understanding). {{ 115 }} It follows of its own accord from this that it must please devoid of all interest whatsoever.

***Sublime*** is that which, by its resistance to the interest of the senses, pleases without mediation.

The two of them, taken as explanations of an aesthetic judging that holds universally, point back to subjective grounds—on one side to those of sensibility, so far as it serves the contemplative understanding, on the other to those of sensibility so far as it works against itself yet in behalf of the ends of practical reason, both grounds being nevertheless combined in one and the same subject, and purposive with a view to the moral feeling. The beautiful makes us ready to cherish something—nature itself included—apart from any interest; the sublime, to prize it even in defiance of our (sensible) interest.

{{{ 268 }}} The sublime admits of the following description: it is an object (in nature) ***whose representation determines the mind to conceive nature's unattainability as a presentation of ideas***.

If taken to the letter and viewed logically, ideas admit of no presentation. But once we enlarge our empirical faculty of representation (whether mathematically or dynamically) for the intuition of nature, reason unfailingly enters as the faculty of the independence of absolute totality, and gives rise to the mind's endeavor—vain though it is—to render the representation of the senses commensurate with that totality. This striving, together with the feeling that the idea stays out of the imagination's reach, is itself a presentation of how our mind is subjectively purposive when it puts the imagination to work for its supersensible vocation, and it obliges us to ***think*** nature itself, subjectively, taken as a whole, as a presentation of something supersensible, {{ 116 }} though we cannot render this presentation ***objectively***.

For we soon become aware that nature in space and time entirely lacks the unconditioned, and hence also absolute magnitude, which is nevertheless demanded by the most common reason. Precisely thereby we are further reminded that our concern is only with a nature qua appearance, and that this nature must itself still be looked upon as no more than the presentation of a nature in itself (a nature that reason holds in the idea). This idea of the supersensible, though—one we cannot determine any further, so that we can accordingly not ***cognize*** nature as its presentation but only ***think*** it—is roused in us by an object whose aesthetic judging strains the imagination up to its boundary, be it of extension (mathematically) or of its might over the mind (dynamically), since it rests upon the feeling (the moral feeling) of a determination of the mind that wholly transgresses the province of the former, in view of which the representation of the object comes to be appraised as subjectively purposive.

Indeed, a feeling for the sublime of nature cannot well be thought without connecting with it a disposition of the mind similar to that for the moral; and although an immediate pleasure in nature's beauty likewise both presupposes and fosters a certain ***liberality*** in the manner of thought, that is, an independence of the satisfaction from mere enjoyment of the senses, freedom is thereby represented still more as in ***play*** than under a lawful ***business***: which is the {{{ 269 }}} genuine character of the morality of the human being, where reason must do violence to sensibility, only that in the aesthetic judgment about {{ 117 }} the sublime this violence is represented as exercised by the imagination itself, functioning as an instrument of reason.

Hence, too, the satisfaction felt in nature's sublime is merely ***negative*** (whereas in the beautiful it is ***positive***)—that is, a feeling that the imagination robs itself of its own freedom, in being purposively determined according to a law other than that of its empirical use. Thereby it acquires an extension and might that is greater than the one it sacrifices, but whose ground remains hidden from it, whereas it ***feels*** both the sacrifice, or the deprivation, and, along with it, the cause to which it is thereby made subject. The ***astonishment*** verging on fright, the dread and the holy shudder that grips the beholder before the spectacle of mountain masses climbing skyward, of deep gorges with their raging torrents, of deeply shaded solitudes that invite melancholy meditation, and so forth, is—so secure does he know himself to be—no genuine fear, but merely an endeavor to enter into it through the imagination, so as to feel the might of this very faculty, to bind the stirring of the mind thus aroused together with the mind's repose, and thereby to prevail over the nature within ourselves, and consequently over that outside us as well, so far as it is able to bear upon the feeling of our well-being. For the imagination, in keeping with the law of association, renders our contented condition physically dependent; the same power, however, in keeping with the principles of the schematism of the power of judgment (and hence so far as it stands subordinate to freedom), serves as a tool of reason and of its ideas, and as such as a might for asserting our independence in the face of nature's influences, for belittling as small what counts as great by the former standard, and so for situating the {{ 118 }} absolutely great only within the subject's own determination. This reflective act of the aesthetic power of judgment—the act of lifting itself to a commensurateness with reason (though without any determinate concept of the latter)—nonetheless exhibits the object itself as subjectively purposive by way of the objective incommensurateness of the imagination, at its utmost expansion, with reason (as the faculty of ideas).

One must here in general attend to what was recalled already above—namely, that the transcendental aesthetic of the power of judgment {{{ 270 }}} must treat exclusively of pure aesthetic judgments, and that no examples may accordingly be borrowed from those beautiful or sublime objects in nature which presuppose the concept of an end; for in that case there would be a purposiveness that is either teleological or grounded on mere sensations of an object (gratification or pain), and thus in the former case not aesthetic and in the latter not merely formal. Thus if one calls the sight of the starry heavens ***sublime***, one must not lay at the ground of its judging concepts of worlds inhabited by rational beings, and then the shining points with which we behold the space over our heads filled up, as their suns moving in orbits arranged very purposively for them, but rather merely as one sees it, as a vast vault that encompasses everything; and merely under this representation must we place the sublimity that a pure aesthetic judgment ascribes to this object. Likewise the sight of the ocean, not as we, enriched with all sorts of knowledge (which, however, is not contained in the immediate intuition), ***think*** it; say as a vast realm of aquatic creatures, as the great treasury of water for the vapors that impregnate the {{ 119 }} air with clouds for the benefit of the lands, or also as an element that indeed separates parts of the world from one another yet nonetheless makes possible the greatest community among them: for that yields nothing but teleological judgments; rather one must, merely as the poets do, according to what the sight of it shows—say, when it is contemplated at rest, as a clear mirror of water bounded only by the sky, but, when it is turbulent, as an abyss threatening to swallow up everything—nevertheless be able to find the ocean sublime. Precisely the same is to be said of the sublime and beautiful as found in the human figure, in whose case we must not, as determining grounds of the judgment, revert to concepts of the ends ***for which*** all its members are there, nor allow the agreement with them to ***enter into*** our (then no longer pure) aesthetic judgment—even though the fact that they do not conflict with those ends is, to be sure, a necessary condition of aesthetic satisfaction as well. Aesthetic purposiveness consists in the lawfulness of the power of judgment as it stands in its ***freedom***. The satisfaction taken in the object hangs upon the relation in which we would place the imagination: only that, of itself, it must keep the mind engaged in a free occupation. If instead something else—whether a sensation of sense or a concept of the understanding—{{{ 271 }}} settles the judgment, then, lawful though it be, it is not the verdict of a ***free*** power of judgment.

Hence, whenever one talks of intellectual beauty or of intellectual sublimity, ***first***, these expressions are not fully accurate, since what is at issue are aesthetic modes of representation that would not be encountered in us at all were we mere pure intelligences (or transposed ourselves in thought into that quality); ***second***, although the two of them, being objects of an {{ 120 }} intellectual (moral) satisfaction, are indeed compatible with the aesthetic so far as they ***rest*** upon no interest, they are nonetheless once again hard to reconcile with it, because they are meant to ***produce*** an interest which, supposing the presentation is to accord with the satisfaction in aesthetic judging, could in the latter come about in no other way than through an interest of sense that one connects with it in the presentation, whereby, however, the intellectual purposiveness suffers damage and is rendered impure.

The moral law, in the might it wields within us over any and all incentives of the mind ***preceding it***, is what constitutes the object of a pure, unconditioned intellectual satisfaction; and since this might properly renders itself aesthetically recognizable only by way of sacrifices (a deprivation, albeit one on behalf of inner freedom, which on the other hand discloses in us an unfathomable depth of this supersensible faculty, whose consequences reach out into the unsurveyable), the satisfaction, viewed from the aesthetic side (in reference to sensibility), is negative—that is, opposed to this interest—yet viewed from the intellectual side, positive and joined with an interest. It follows from this that the intellectual good, which is purposive in itself (the morally good), when judged aesthetically, must be represented less as beautiful than rather as sublime, in such a way that it awakens the feeling of respect (which disdains charm) more than that of love and familiar affection; for human nature does not fall into accord with that good on its own, but only through the violence that reason inflicts upon sensibility. Conversely, whatever we name sublime in the nature outside us, or likewise within us (e.g., certain affects), comes to be represented—and thereby to grow interesting—solely as a {{ 121 }} might of the mind to lift itself above ***certain*** obstacles of sensibility through moral principles.

I will dwell a little on the latter. The idea of the good conjoined with {{{ 272 }}} affect goes by the name of ***enthusiasm***. Such a condition of the mind appears to be sublime, to such a degree that people commonly claim: apart from it nothing great can be brought about. But now every affect[^*] is blind—either in choosing its end, or, when the end has indeed been furnished by reason, in carrying it out; for it is that movement of the mind which renders the mind unable to conduct a free deliberation of principles so as to determine itself thereby. Thus it can in no way merit a satisfaction of reason. Aesthetically, nonetheless, enthusiasm is sublime, because it is a straining of the powers through ideas that give the mind a momentum which acts far more powerfully and enduringly than the impulse through representations of sense. But (which seems strange) even ***absence of affect*** (_Apatheia, Phlegma in significatu bono_) in a mind that emphatically {{ 122 }} pursues its unalterable principles is sublime, and indeed in a far more excellent way, because it at the same time has the satisfaction of pure reason on its side. A mind of this kind alone is called noble: an expression that is afterward applied also to things, e.g., buildings, a garment, a style of writing, bodily bearing, and the like, when these arouse not so much ***astonishment*** (affect in the representation of novelty that exceeds expectation) as ***admiration*** (an astonishment that does not cease with the loss of novelty), which happens when ideas, in their presentation, accord unintentionally and without art with aesthetic satisfaction.

Every affect of the brave sort (namely the sort that rouses the awareness of our powers to surmount any resistance whatever (_animi strenui_)) is ***aesthetically sublime***, e.g., anger, even despair (namely the ***indignant*** sort, though not the ***despondent*** one). But the affect of the melting kind (which makes the very striving to resist an object of displeasure (_animum languidum_)) has nothing ***noble*** {{{ 273 }}} about it, though it can be counted as belonging to the beautiful in the manner of sensing. Hence the ***emotions*** that can grow strong enough to become affects are also very diverse. There are ***courageous*** emotions, there are ***tender*** ones. The latter, when they rise to the level of affect, are worth nothing at all; the inclination to them is called ***sentimentality***. A commiserating pain that refuses to be comforted, or into which—when it bears on invented ills—we knowingly let ourselves be drawn, right up to the deception of fancy that treats them as though they were real, both proves and produces a tender yet at the same time feeble soul, one that displays a beautiful side and may indeed be termed fanciful, though not so much as enthusiastic. Novels, maudlin stage-plays, {{ 123 }} vapid moral maxims that trifle with dispositions (falsely, to be sure) called noble, but that in truth leave the heart shriveled and unfeeling toward the stern precept of duty, and render it devoid of every reverence for humanity's dignity in our own person and for the right of human beings (which is quite a different thing from their happiness) and, generally, of all steadfast principles; even a sermon on religion that commends a groveling, base soliciting of favor and fawning, one that surrenders every reliance on one's own capacity to resist the evil in us, in place of the vigorous determination to put to the test those powers that, for all our frailty, still remain to us for mastering the inclinations; the false humility that sets, in self-contempt, in a whimpering and dissembled repentance, and in a merely suffering cast of mind, the one way in which alone one might come to please the supreme being—all of these are not even consonant with what might be reckoned to beauty, far less with what might be reckoned to the sublimity of the mind's cast.

But even stormy agitations of the mind—be they bound up, under the name of edification, with ideas of religion, or, as belonging merely to culture, with ideas that carry a social interest—can, however greatly they may strain the imagination, in no way lay claim to the honor of a ***sublime*** presentation, unless they leave behind a frame of mind that bears, if only indirectly, upon the awareness of one's strength and resolve toward that which brings with it a pure intellectual purposiveness (the supersensible). For otherwise all these emotions amount to nothing but ***motion***, which one is happy to have for the sake of one's health. The pleasant weariness that {{ 124 }} ensues upon such a jostling through the play of the affects is a relishing {{{ 274 }}} of well-being springing from the restored equilibrium of the manifold vital forces within us—an enjoyment that in the end comes down to just the same as the one the voluptuaries of the Orient find so cozy when they have their body, so to speak, kneaded through and through and let all their muscles and joints be gently pressed and flexed; save that there the moving principle lies for the most part within us, while here it lies wholly outside us. Thus one person or another now fancies himself edified by a sermon in which nothing whatever is built up (no system of good maxims), another bettered by a tragedy, though he is merely pleased at boredom fortunately chased away. The sublime, accordingly, must at all times bear a relation to the ***way of thinking***, that is, to maxims that secure for the intellectual and for the ideas of reason a supremacy over sensibility.

There is no cause to fear that the feeling of the sublime stands to lose through such a detached mode of presentation, one that turns wholly negative in regard to the sensible; for the imagination, though beyond the sensible it lights on nothing it can cling to, yet feels itself unbounded precisely by this doing-away with its own barriers: and that setting-apart is therefore a presentation of the infinite, which precisely on that account can never be anything other than a bare negative presentation, and yet enlarges the soul. There is perhaps no passage more sublime in the Jews' book of law than this commandment: You shall make yourself no graven image, nor any likeness at all, whether of what is in heaven, or on the earth, or beneath the earth, and so on. This commandment by itself is able to account for the enthusiasm that the Jewish people, during its civilized era, felt toward its religion whenever it set itself alongside other nations, or for the pride that Mohammedanism {{ 125 }} instills. The very same holds also of the representation of the moral law and of the predisposition to morality within us. It is a wholly erroneous apprehension that, were one to strip it of all that might commend it to the senses, it would then bring with it none other than a cold, lifeless assent, and no stirring force or emotion whatever. The truth is exactly the opposite; for there, where the senses now behold nothing further before them while the unmistakable and inextinguishable idea of morality yet persists, it would be needful rather to temper the impetus of an unbounded imagination, lest it climb as high as enthusiasm, than, out of a fear that these ideas want force, to seek aid on their behalf in pictures and childish apparatus. Hence governments too have {{{ 275 }}} gladly permitted religion to be amply furnished with the latter accessories, and have thus sought to relieve the subject of the trouble, but at the same time also of the capacity, of extending his powers of soul beyond the limits that one arbitrarily sets for him and by means of which one can more easily handle him as merely passive.

This pure, soul-elevating, merely negative presentation of morality, on the contrary, carries no danger of ***fanaticism***, which ***is a delusion of craving to see something past every bound of sensibility***, that is, to dream in accordance with principles (to rave with reason), to ***will*** it; and that, precisely because with the former the presentation remains merely negative. For ***the inscrutability of the idea of freedom*** cuts off entirely every avenue to positive presentation: the moral law, on the other hand, is of itself sufficient in us and determining from the outset, so much so that it does not so much as allow us to cast about for a determining ground lying outside it. If {{ 126 }} enthusiasm is to be compared with ***madness***, then fanaticism is to be compared with ***derangement***, of which the latter is the least compatible of all with the sublime, because it is broodingly ridiculous. In enthusiasm, as an affect, the imagination is unbridled; in fanaticism, as a deep-rooted, brooding passion, it is ruleless. The former is a passing accident that occasionally befalls even the healthiest understanding; the second is a disease that deranges it.

***Simplicity*** (artless purposiveness) is, as it were, nature's style in the sublime, and so too the style of morality, which is a second (supersensible) nature whose laws alone are known to us, while we cannot reach, through intuition, the supersensible faculty within ourselves in which the ground of this legislation resides.

It remains to be observed that, even though the satisfaction in the beautiful, no less than that in the sublime, is not merely marked off recognizably from the other aesthetic judgings by universal ***communicability***, but through this very property also gains an interest with regard to society (within which it admits of being communicated), the ***separation from all society*** is nevertheless likewise looked upon as something sublime, provided it rests on ideas that see past all sensible interest. To suffice for oneself, and thus to stand in no need of society, yet without being unsociable—that is, without fleeing it—is something that draws near to the sublime, as does every raising of oneself above needs. To flee one's fellow human beings, on the other hand, from ***misanthropy***, because one bears them enmity, {{{ 276 }}} or from ***anthropophobia*** (shyness of humankind), because one dreads them as enemies, is in part ugly, in part contemptible. Nonetheless there is a (very improperly so-called) misanthropy, the predisposition to which {{ 127 }} tends to settle with age in the mind of many a right-thinking person, one who, as regards ***benevolence***, is philanthropic enough, but who through a long, sad experience has been drawn far away from ***satisfaction*** in human beings: to which the leaning toward seclusion bears witness, or the fanciful longing for a remote country seat, or else (in young persons) the fancied bliss of passing one's lifetime with a small family upon some isle of which the rest of the world knows nothing, a wish that the novel-writers and poets of Robinsonades know so well how to turn to account. Falsehood, ingratitude, injustice, the childish character of those ends that we ourselves deem weighty and grand—ends in whose pursuit human beings do one another every imaginable harm—stand so at odds with the idea of what they might be were they willing, and run so counter to the keen wish to see them improved, that, so as not to hate them, seeing that one cannot love them, the surrender of all the joys of society seems only a slight sacrifice. This sadness—not over the evils that fate imposes upon other human beings (of which sympathy is the cause), but over those that they inflict upon themselves (which rests on antipathy in principles)—is, because it rests on ideas, sublime, whereas the former can likewise count only as beautiful. — The equally ingenious and thorough ***Saussure*** says, in the description of his Alpine journeys, of ***Bonhomme***, one of the Savoyard mountains: »There prevails there a certain ***insipid sadness***.« He was therefore acquainted, after all, with an ***interesting*** sadness that the sight of a wasteland inspires—into which human beings might well wish to transport themselves in order to hear or learn nothing {{ 128 }} further of the world—which then need not be so entirely inhospitable as to offer human beings only a most toilsome sojourn. — I offer this observation solely with the aim of calling to mind that affliction as well (not a downcast sadness) may be numbered among the ***vigorous*** affects, when it is grounded in moral ideas; whereas, if it rests on sympathy and as such is likewise lovable, it belongs merely among the ***melting*** affects: so as thereby to draw notice to the frame of mind that is ***sublime*** in the former case alone. {{{ 277 }}}

One may now set the transcendental exposition of aesthetic judgments just carried through alongside the physiological one, as a ***Burke*** and many sharp-witted men among us have elaborated it, so as to see where a purely empirical exposition of what is sublime and beautiful would lead. ***Burke***[^**], who merits being named the most eminent author in this manner of treatment, arrives by this route (p. 223 of his work) at the following: »that the feeling of the sublime is founded upon the impulse toward self-preservation and upon ***fear***, that is, upon a pain which, since it does not reach the point of a real disruption of the bodily parts, brings forth movements that—inasmuch as they purge the finer or coarser vessels of perilous and troublesome obstructions—are able to arouse agreeable sensations, not pleasure indeed, but a species of gratifying shudder, a certain repose that is blended with {{ 129 }} terror.« The beautiful, which he grounds on love (from which, however, he wants desire to be kept separate), he traces (pp. 251–252) »to the slackening, relaxation, and enfeeblement of the fibers of the body, and hence a softening, dissolution, exhaustion, a sinking away, a dying away, a melting away with pleasure.« And now he confirms this manner of explanation not only through cases in which the imagination in connection with the understanding, but even in connection with sensation of sense, can arouse in us the feeling of the beautiful as well as of the sublime. — As psychological observations, these dissections of the phenomena of our mind are exceedingly fine and provide rich material for the most favored investigations of empirical anthropology. Nor is it to be denied that all representations within us, be they objectively nothing but sensible or else wholly intellectual, can yet subjectively be tied to gratification or pain, however unnoticeable each may be (for they collectively affect the feeling of life, and none among them, so far as it constitutes a modification of the subject, is capable of being indifferent); indeed, that, as Epicurus contended, ***gratification*** and ***pain*** are at bottom always corporeal in the end, whether they set out from imagining or even from representations of the understanding: for life, deprived of the {{{ 278 }}} feeling of the bodily organ, would be nothing but a consciousness of one's own existence, and no feeling of faring well or ill, that is, of the furtherance or checking of the vital forces; for the mind, taken by itself alone, is wholly life (the life-principle itself), and obstacles or furtherances have to be sought beyond it and yet within the human being himself, and hence in the union with his body.

But if one places the satisfaction in the object entirely and wholly {{ 130 }} in the fact that it gratifies through charm or through emotion, then one may also not expect any ***other*** person to agree with the aesthetic judgment that ***we*** pass; for about this each rightly consults only his own private sense. But then all censorship of taste also entirely ceases; unless one were to make the example that others give through the accidental agreement of their judgments into a ***command*** of approval for us—a principle against which we would nevertheless presumably resist and appeal to the natural right of subjecting the judgment that rests on the immediate feeling of one's own well-being to one's own sense and not to that of others.

If, then, the judgment of taste must of necessity hold not as ***egoistic***, but, by its inner nature—that is, on its own account, and not on account of the examples of their taste that others put forward—as ***pluralistic***, once one accords it the standing of a judgment that may at once require everyone to fall in with it: then there must lie at its ground some principle _a priori_ (whether objective or subjective), one at which no ferreting out of empirical laws of the mind's changes can ever arrive: for such laws only let it be known how judging occurs, but do not prescribe how one ought to judge, and this, moreover, in such a manner that the prescription is ***unconditioned***—which is just what the judgments of taste presuppose, in that they would have the satisfaction bound up ***immediately*** with a representation. The empirical exposition of aesthetic judgments may therefore always be what makes the start, so as to furnish the material for a loftier inquiry; a transcendental treatment of this faculty is nonetheless possible and belongs essentially to the critique of taste. {{ 131 }} For unless it had principles _a priori_, it could not possibly sit in judgment over the judgments of others and pass verdicts of approval or rejection upon them with even a semblance of right.
