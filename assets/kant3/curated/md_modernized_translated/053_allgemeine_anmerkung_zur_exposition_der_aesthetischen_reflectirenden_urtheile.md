## General Remark on the Exposition of Aesthetic Reflective Judgments

In relation to the feeling of pleasure, an object must be classified as either the ***agreeable***, or the ***beautiful***, or the ***sublime***, or the ***good*** (absolutely) (_iucundum_, _pulchrum_, _sublime_, _honestum_).

The ***agreeable***, as a drive of desire, is consistently of one and the same kind, from wherever it may arise and however specifically different the representation (of sense and of sensation, objectively considered) may be. Hence, in the judgment of its influence on the mind, everything depends only on the quantity of stimuli (simultaneously and successively) and, as it were, only on the mass of the agreeable sensation; and this can therefore be made intelligible by nothing other than ***quantity***. It also does not cultivate, but belongs to mere enjoyment. — The ***beautiful***, on the contrary, requires the representation of a certain ***quality*** of the object, which can also be made intelligible and brought to concepts (although in the aesthetic judgment it is not brought to them); and it cultivates, in that it teaches us at the same time to pay attention to purposiveness in the feeling of pleasure. — The ***sublime*** consists merely in the ***relation*** in which {{ 114 }} {{{ 267 }}} the sensible in the representation of nature is judged as suitable for a possible supersensible use of it. — The ***absolutely good***, judged subjectively according to the feeling which it inspires (the object of the moral feeling), as the determinability of the powers of the subject by the representation of an ***absolutely necessitating*** law, is distinguished principally by the ***modality*** of a necessity based on concepts _a priori_, which contains in itself not merely a ***claim***, but also a ***command*** for everyone's approval, and belongs in itself not indeed to the aesthetic, but to the pure intellectual power of judgment; nor is it ascribed in a merely reflective, but in a determining judgment, not to nature, but to freedom. But the ***determinability of the subject*** by this idea, and indeed of a subject that can feel in itself ***obstacles*** in sensibility, but at the same time superiority over it through the overcoming of them as a ***modification of its state***, i.e., the moral feeling, is nevertheless so related to the aesthetic power of judgment and its ***formal conditions*** that it can serve to represent the lawfulness of action from duty at the same time as aesthetic, i.e., as sublime, or also as beautiful, without losing any of its purity: which does not take place if one wished to bring it into a natural connection with the feeling of the agreeable.

If we draw the result from the previous exposition of both kinds of aesthetic judgments, the following brief definitions would result from it:

***Beautiful*** is what pleases in the mere judging (hence not by means of the sensation of sense according to a concept of the understanding). {{ 115 }} From this it follows of itself that it must please without any interest.

***Sublime*** is what pleases immediately through its resistance to the interest of the senses.

Both as definitions of aesthetic universally valid judging refer to subjective grounds, namely, on the one hand, of sensibility as it is purposive in favor of the contemplative understanding, on the other hand, as it is against the same, but rather for the purposes of practical reason, and yet both united in the same subject, in relation to the moral feeling. The beautiful prepares us to love something, even nature, without interest; the sublime, to esteem it highly even against our (sensible) interest.

{{{ 268 }}} One can describe the sublime thus: it is an object (of nature), ***the representation of which determines the mind to think the unattainability of nature as a presentation of ideas***.

Literally taken and logically considered, ideas cannot be presented. But when we expand our empirical power of representation (mathematically or dynamically) for the intuition of nature, reason inevitably steps in as the faculty of the independence of absolute totality, and produces the endeavor, though vain, of the mind to make the representation of the senses adequate to this. This endeavor, and the feeling of the unattainability of the idea by the imagination, is itself a presentation of the subjective purposiveness of our mind in the use of the imagination for its supersensible vocation, and compels us subjectively to ***think*** nature itself in its totality as a presentation of something supersensible, {{ 116 }} without being able to bring this presentation about ***objectively***.

For we soon become aware that nature in space and time entirely lacks the unconditioned, and consequently also absolute magnitude, which is nevertheless demanded by the most common reason. By this very fact we are also reminded that we have to do only with a nature as appearance, and that this itself must still be regarded as a mere presentation of a nature in itself (which reason has in the idea). This idea of the supersensible, however, which we cannot indeed determine further, and consequently cannot ***cognize*** but only ***think*** nature as a presentation of it, is awakened in us by an object whose aesthetic judging strains the imagination to its limit, whether of expansion (mathematically) or of its power over the mind (dynamically), in that it grounds itself on the feeling of a vocation of the mind that entirely transcends the domain of the former (the moral feeling), in regard to which the representation of the object is judged as subjectively purposive.

In fact, a feeling for the sublime of nature cannot well be thought without combining with it a tuning of the mind that is similar to the moral one; and although the immediate pleasure in the beautiful of nature likewise presupposes and cultivates a certain ***liberality*** of the way of thinking, i.e., independence of satisfaction from mere sensible enjoyment, freedom is thereby represented as in ***play*** rather than under a lawful ***business***: which is the {{{ 269 }}} genuine character of the morality of human beings, where reason must exert violence on sensibility, only that in the aesthetic judgment on {{ 117 }} the sublime this violence is represented as being exercised by the imagination itself as an instrument of reason.

The satisfaction in the sublime of nature is therefore also only ***negative*** (whereas that in the beautiful is ***positive***), namely, a feeling of the deprivation of the freedom of imagination by itself, in that it is purposively determined according to a law other than that of empirical use. Through this it acquires an expansion and a power that is greater than that which it sacrifices, the ground of which, however, is hidden from itself, whereas it ***feels*** the sacrifice or the deprivation and at the same time the cause to which it is subjected. The ***astonishment*** that borders on terror, the horror and the sacred shudder that seizes the spectator at the sight of mountain masses towering to the heavens, deep chasms and waters raging therein, deep-shadowed deserts inviting melancholy reflection, etc., is, in the safety in which he knows himself to be, not actual fear, but only an attempt to engage ourselves with the imagination in order to feel the power of this very faculty, to combine the movement of the mind thereby excited with its state of rest, and thus to be superior to nature in ourselves, and consequently also to that outside us, so far as it can have influence on the feeling of our well-being. For the imagination, according to the law of association, makes our state of contentment physically dependent; but the very same faculty, according to principles of the schematism of the power of judgment (consequently so far as it is subordinate to freedom), is an instrument of reason and its ideas, but as such a power to assert our independence against the influences of nature, to degrade what is large according to the former as small, and thus to place the {{ 118 }} absolutely large only in the subject's own vocation. This reflection of the aesthetic power of judgment, to raise itself to adequacy with reason (yet without a determinate concept of it), nevertheless represents the object itself as subjectively purposive through the objective inadequacy of the imagination in its greatest expansion for reason (as the faculty of ideas).

One must here pay attention generally to what has already been recalled above, namely that in the transcendental aesthetic of the power of judgment {{{ 270 }}} we must speak solely of pure aesthetic judgments, consequently the examples must not be taken from such beautiful or sublime objects of nature as presuppose the concept of a purpose; for then it would be either teleological, or based on mere sensations of an object (pleasure or pain), and hence in the former case not aesthetic, in the latter not merely formal purposiveness. If, therefore, one calls the sight of the starry heaven ***sublime***, one must not ground its judging on concepts of worlds inhabited by rational beings, representing the bright points with which we see space above us filled as their suns, moving in orbits very purposively established for them, but rather merely as we see it, as a vast vault that encompasses everything; and solely under this representation must we place the sublimity that a pure aesthetic judgment attributes to this object. Likewise, one must not think of the sight of the ocean as we ***think*** it, enriched with all kinds of knowledge (which is not, however, contained in the immediate intuition); say, as a vast realm of aquatic creatures, as the great reservoir of water for the evaporations that impregnate the {{ 119 }} air with clouds for the benefit of the land, or as an element that indeed separates parts of the world from one another, but nevertheless makes the greatest community among them possible: for that yields nothing but teleological judgments; rather, one must be able to find the ocean sublime merely, as the poets do, according to what the visual appearance shows, for instance, when it is considered in calm, as a clear mirror of water bounded only by the sky, but when it is turbulent, as an abyss threatening to swallow everything. The very same is to be said of the sublime and beautiful in the human figure, where we must not look back to concepts of the purposes ***for which*** all its limbs exist, as determining grounds of the judgment, and must not let the agreement with them ***influence*** our (then no longer pure) aesthetic judgment, although that they do not conflict with those purposes is indeed a necessary condition even of aesthetic satisfaction. Aesthetic purposiveness is the lawfulness of the power of judgment in its ***freedom***. The satisfaction in the object depends on the relation in which we wish to place the imagination: only that it entertain the mind by itself in free occupation. If, on the contrary, something else, be it sense sensation or a concept of the understanding, {{{ 271 }}} determines the judgment: then it is indeed lawful, but not the judgment of a ***free*** power of judgment.

If, therefore, one speaks of intellectual beauty or sublimity, ***firstly***, these expressions are not entirely correct, because they are aesthetic modes of representation which, if we were mere pure intelligences (or in thought placed ourselves in this quality), would not be found in us at all; ***secondly***, although both, as objects of an {{ 120 }} intellectual (moral) satisfaction, are indeed compatible with the aesthetic in so far as they do not ***depend*** on any interest, they are nevertheless in turn difficult to combine with the latter because they are supposed to ***effect*** an interest, which, if the presentation is to agree for satisfaction in the aesthetic judging, could never happen in the latter otherwise than through a sensible interest that one combines with it in the presentation, whereby, however, damage is done to the intellectual purposiveness, and it is contaminated.

The object of a pure and unconditioned intellectual satisfaction is the moral law in its power, which it exercises in us over any and all incentives of the mind ***preceding*** it; and since this power actually makes itself aesthetically cognizable only through sacrifices (which is a deprivation, although for the sake of inner freedom, but on the other hand reveals in us an unfathomable depth of this supersensible faculty with its consequences extending into the immeasurable): the satisfaction from the aesthetic side (in relation to sensibility) is negative, i.e., against this interest, but considered from the intellectual side, positive and combined with an interest. From this it follows: that the intellectual, in itself purposive (moral) good, aesthetically judged, must be represented not so much as beautiful, but rather as sublime, so that it awakens more the feeling of respect (which disdains allure), than of love and familiar affection; because human nature does not agree with that good of itself, but only through violence which reason exerts on sensibility. Conversely, what we call sublime in nature outside us, or even in ourselves (e.g., certain affects), will also be represented only as a {{ 121 }} power of the mind to soar above ***certain*** obstacles of sensibility through moral principles, and thereby become interesting.

I will dwell a little on the latter. The idea of the good with {{{ 272 }}} affect is called ***enthusiasm***. This state of mind seems to be sublime, so much so that it is commonly asserted that without it nothing great can be accomplished. Now every affect[^*] is blind, either in the choice of its purpose, or, even if this has been given by reason, in its execution; for it is that movement of the mind which makes it unable to engage in free deliberation on principles in order to determine itself accordingly. Hence, it can in no way deserve a satisfaction of reason. Aesthetically, nevertheless, enthusiasm is sublime, because it is a tension of forces through ideas, which give the mind a momentum that works far more powerfully and durably than the impulse through sensible representations. But (what seems strange) even the ***affectlessness*** (_Apatheia, Phlegma in significatu bono_) of a mind that vigorously {{ 122 }} pursues its immutable principles is sublime, and indeed in a far more excellent way, because it has at the same time the satisfaction of pure reason on its side. Such a character of mind alone is called noble: which expression is subsequently also applied to things, e.g., buildings, a garment, style of writing, bodily carriage, and the like, when these excite not so much ***astonishment*** (affect in the representation of novelty that exceeds expectation) as ***admiration*** (an astonishment that does not cease with the loss of novelty), which happens when ideas in their presentation unintentionally and without art agree for aesthetic satisfaction.

Every affect of the vigorous kind (which, namely, makes active the consciousness of our powers to overcome every resistance (_animi strenui_)) is ***aesthetically sublime***, e.g., anger, even despair (namely the ***indignant***, but not the ***faint-hearted***). The affect of the melting kind, however (which makes the endeavor to resist itself into an object of displeasure (_animum languidum_)), has nothing ***noble*** {{{ 273 }}} about it, but can be counted as belonging to the beauty of the sensory disposition. Hence the ***emotions***, which can become strong up to the point of affect, are also very different. One has ***courageous***, one has ***tender*** emotions. The latter, if they rise to the point of affect, are of no use at all; the inclination to them is called ***sentimentalism***. A sympathetic pain that will not let itself be comforted, or to which we deliberately surrender ourselves when it concerns fictitious evils, to the point of being deceived by fantasy as if they were real, proves and makes a soft, but at the same time weak soul, which shows a beautiful side and indeed can be called fantastical, but not even enthusiastic. Novels, tearful plays, {{ 123 }} shallow moral precepts that toy with (though falsely) so-called noble sentiments, but in fact make the heart withered and insensible to the strict command of duty, incapable of all respect for the dignity of humanity in our person and the rights of human beings (which is something quite different from their happiness), and indeed of all firm principles; even a religious discourse that recommends crawling, base favor-seeking and ingratiation, which gives up all trust in one's own power to resist the evil in us, instead of the vigorous resolution to try the powers that, despite all our frailty, still remain to us for the overcoming of inclinations; the false humility which places the way in which alone one can become pleasing to the supreme being in self-contempt, in whining, hypocritical repentance, and a merely passive frame of mind: do not agree even with what might be counted as belonging to the beauty, much less to the sublimity of the character of mind.

But even stormy agitations of the mind, whether they be combined under the name of edification with ideas of religion, or, as belonging merely to culture, with ideas containing a social interest, can, however much they may strain the imagination, in no way lay claim to the honor of a ***sublime*** presentation, unless they leave behind a frame of mind which, even if only indirectly, has an influence on the consciousness of one's strength and resolution for that which pure intellectual purposiveness carries with it (the supersensible). For otherwise all these emotions belong only to ***motion***, which one likes to have for the sake of health. The agreeable lassitude which {{ 124 }} follows such a shaking up through the play of the affects is an enjoyment {{{ 274 }}} of well-being from the restored equilibrium of the various vital forces in us: which in the end comes to the same thing as that which the voluptuaries of the Orient find so comfortable when they have their bodies kneaded, as it were, and all their muscles and joints gently pressed and bent; only that there the moving principle is for the most part in us, here on the contrary entirely outside us. Thus many a person believes himself edified by a sermon in which, however, nothing has been built up (no system of good maxims); or improved by a tragedy, who is merely glad to have successfully banished boredom. Hence the sublime must always have relation to the ***way of thinking***, i.e., to maxims of giving the intellectual and ideas of reason supremacy over sensibility.

One need not fear that the feeling of the sublime will lose by such an abstract mode of presentation, which becomes entirely negative in regard to the sensible; for the imagination, although it finds nothing beyond the sensible to which it can hold, nevertheless feels itself unlimited precisely through this removal of its barriers: and that separation is thus a presentation of the infinite, which, although for that very reason it can never be anything but a merely negative presentation, nevertheless expands the soul. Perhaps there is no more sublime passage in the Jewish book of laws than the command: Thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image, or any likeness of anything that is in heaven above, or on the earth beneath, or under the earth, etc. This command alone can explain the enthusiasm that the Jewish people in their civilized epoch felt for their religion when they compared themselves with other peoples, or that pride which Mohammedanism {{ 125 }} inspires. The very same holds also for the representation of the moral law and the predisposition to morality in us. It is an entirely erroneous apprehension that, if we deprive it of everything that can recommend it to the senses, it would then carry with it nothing but cold, lifeless approval and no moving force or emotion. It is exactly the reverse; for where the senses see nothing more before them, and yet the unmistakable and inextinguishable idea of morality remains, it would be more necessary to moderate the momentum of an unlimited imagination, so as not to let it rise to enthusiasm, than to seek help for these ideas in images and childish apparatus out of fear of their powerlessness. Therefore, governments have also {{{ 275 }}} gladly allowed religion to be amply provided with the latter accessories, and have thus sought to relieve the subject of the trouble, but at the same time of the capacity, to extend his mental powers beyond the barriers that one arbitrarily wishes to set for him and by which one can treat him, as merely passive, more easily.

This pure, soul-elevating, merely negative presentation of morality, on the contrary, brings no danger of ***fanaticism***, which is ***a delusion of wishing to see something beyond all bounds of sensibility***, i.e., to dream according to principles (to rave with reason); precisely because the presentation in the former is merely negative. For ***the inscrutability of the idea of freedom*** entirely cuts off the path to any positive presentation: the moral law, however, is in itself sufficient and originally determining in us, so that it does not even permit us to look around for a determining ground outside it. If {{ 126 }} enthusiasm is to be compared with ***delirium***, fanaticism is to be compared with ***dementia***, of which the latter is least of all compatible with the sublime, because it is broodingly ridiculous. In enthusiasm as an affect, the imagination is unbridled; in fanaticism as an ingrained, brooding passion, it is lawless. The former is a transient accident that occasionally affects even the healthiest understanding; the second is a disease that deranges it.

***Simplicity*** (artless purposiveness) is, as it were, the style of nature in the sublime, and so also of morality, which is a second (supersensible) nature, of which we know only the laws, without being able to reach by intuition the supersensible faculty in ourselves which contains the ground of this legislation.

It is still to be noted that, although the satisfaction in the beautiful as well as that in the sublime is distinguishably different from other aesthetic judgments not only by its universal ***communicability***, but also acquires an interest through this property in relation to society (in which it can be communicated), nevertheless ***separation from all society*** is also regarded as something sublime if it rests on ideas which look beyond all sensible interest. To be sufficient to oneself, and consequently not to need society, without, however, being unsociable, i.e., fleeing it, is something approaching the sublime, as is any elevation above needs. On the contrary, to flee human beings out of ***misanthropy***, because one is hostile to them, {{{ 276 }}} or out of ***anthropophobia*** (shyness of humans), because one fears them as one's enemies, is partly ugly, partly contemptible. Nevertheless, there is a (very improperly so-called) misanthropy, the predisposition to which {{ 127 }} tends to find its way with age into the minds of many well-thinking human beings, which is indeed philanthropic enough as far as ***benevolence*** is concerned, but has been far removed from ***satisfaction*** in human beings by a long and sad experience: to which witness is borne by the inclination to retirement, the fantastical wish to spend one's life on a remote country estate, or even (in the case of young persons) the dreamed-of happiness of being able to spend one's lifetime with a small family on an island unknown to the rest of the world, which novelists or poets of Robinsonades know how to use so well. Falsehood, ingratitude, injustice, the childishness in the purposes we ourselves hold to be important and great, in the pursuit of which human beings inflict on one another all conceivable evils, are in such contradiction with the idea of what they could be if they wished, and are so contrary to the lively desire to see them better, that, in order not to hate them, since one cannot love them, the renunciation of all social pleasures seems to be only a small sacrifice. This sadness, not over the evils that fate inflicts on other human beings (of which sympathy is the cause), but over those they inflict on themselves (which rests on antipathy in principles), is, because it rests on ideas, sublime, whereas the former can likewise only count as beautiful. — The equally spirited and thorough ***Saussure*** says, in the description of his Alpine travels, of ***Bonhomme***, one of the Savoyard mountains: "There prevails there a certain ***insipid sadness***." He was therefore also acquainted with an ***interesting*** sadness, which the sight of a desert inspires, into which human beings might well wish to transport themselves in order to hear or know nothing {{ 128 }} more of the world, which must not, after all, be so completely inhospitable that it would offer only a highly troublesome residence for human beings. — I make this remark only with the intention of reminding that even grief (not dejected sadness) can be counted among the ***sturdy*** affects when it has its ground in moral ideas; but if it is grounded on sympathy and, as such, is also amiable, it belongs merely to the ***melting*** affects: in order thereby to draw attention to the frame of mind which is ***sublime*** only in the former case. {{{ 277 }}}

One can now also compare the transcendental exposition of aesthetic judgments just carried out with the physiological one, as worked out by a ***Burke*** and many acute men among us, in order to see where a merely empirical exposition of the sublime and beautiful leads. ***Burke***[^**], who deserves to be named as the most distinguished author in this kind of treatment, finds in this way (p. 223 of his work): "that the feeling of the sublime is grounded on the drive to self-preservation and on ***fear***, i.e., on a pain, which, because it does not go as far as actual derangement of the bodily parts, produces movements which, since they clear the finer or coarser vessels of dangerous and troublesome obstructions, are capable of exciting agreeable sensations, not indeed pleasure, but a kind of delightful horror, a certain tranquility mixed with {{ 129 }} terror." The beautiful, which he grounds on love (from which he nevertheless wishes to have desire separated), he traces (pp. 251–252) "to the relaxation, loosening, and slackening of the fibers of the body, and hence to a softening, dissolution, enervation, a sinking, dying, melting away with pleasure." And now he confirms this mode of explanation not only by cases in which the imagination in combination with the understanding, but even with sense sensation, can excite in us the feeling of the beautiful as well as of the sublime. — As psychological observations, these analyses of the phenomena of our mind are exceedingly beautiful and provide rich material for the favorite investigations of empirical anthropology. It is also not to be denied that all representations in us, whether they be objectively merely sensible or entirely intellectual, can nevertheless be subjectively combined with pleasure or pain, however imperceptible both may be (since they altogether affect the feeling of life, and none of them, in so far as it is a modification of the subject, can be indifferent); indeed, as Epicurus asserted, that ***pleasure*** and ***pain*** are always ultimately bodily, whether they start from the imagination or even from representations of the understanding: because life without the {{{ 278 }}} feeling of the bodily organ is merely consciousness of its existence, but no feeling of well-being or ill-being, i.e., of the promotion or hindrance of the vital forces; because the mind by itself is entirely life (the life-principle itself), and hindrances or promotions must be sought outside it and yet in the human being himself, consequently in combination with his body.

If, however, one places the satisfaction in the object entirely {{ 130 }} in its pleasing through stimulus or through emotion: then one must also not require ***anyone else*** to assent to the aesthetic judgment that ***we*** make; for on this each person with right consults only his private sense. But then all criticism of taste also ceases entirely; unless one were to make the example that others give through the accidental agreement of their judgments into a ***command*** of assent for us, against which principle we would nevertheless presumably struggle and appeal to the natural right of subjecting the judgment, which rests on the immediate feeling of one's own well-being, to one's own sense and not to that of others.

If, therefore, the judgment of taste is to count not as ***egoistic***, but according to its inner nature, i.e., on its own account, and not for the sake of the examples that others give of their taste, necessarily as ***pluralistic***, if it is valued as such a one that may at the same time demand that everyone should assent to it: then it must be grounded on some (be it objective or subjective) principle _a priori_ which one can never reach by the tracking down of empirical laws of mental changes: because these only make known how judging is done, but do not command how it ought to be judged, and indeed in such a way that the command is ***unconditional***; such as judgments of taste presuppose, in that they want to know satisfaction to be ***immediately*** combined with a representation. Thus, the empirical exposition of aesthetic judgments may always make the beginning, in order to procure the material for a higher investigation; a transcendental discussion of this faculty is nevertheless possible and belongs essentially to the critique of taste. {{ 131 }} For without its having principles _a priori_, it could not possibly judge the judgments of others and pass verdicts of approval or rejection upon them even with any appearance of right.
