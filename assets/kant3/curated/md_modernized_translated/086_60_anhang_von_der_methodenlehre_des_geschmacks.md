## {{ 261 }} § 60. Appendix. On the Doctrine of Method of Taste

The division of a critique into a doctrine of elements and a doctrine of method, which precedes science, cannot be applied {{{ 355 }}} to the critique of taste: because there is not, nor can there be, a science of the beautiful, and the judgment of taste is not determinable by principles. For as regards what is scientific in every art, which aims at ***truth*** in the exhibition of its object, this is indeed the indispensable condition (_conditio sine qua non_) of beautiful art, but it is not beautiful art itself. There is therefore for beautiful art only a ***manner*** (_modus_), not a ***method*** (_methodus_). The master must demonstrate what the pupil is to produce and how he is to bring it about; and the general rules under which he ultimately brings his procedure can serve rather to recall its main moments to memory on occasion than to prescribe them to him. In this, regard must nevertheless be had to a certain ideal which art must keep before its eyes, even though it never fully attains it in its execution. Only by exciting the imagination of the pupil to conformity with a given concept, by pointing out the inadequacy of the {{ 262 }} expression for the idea, which the concept itself does not reach because it is aesthetic, and by sharp critique can it be prevented that the examples set before him are not immediately taken by him as archetypes and as models for imitation subject to no still higher norm and to his own judgment, and thus the genius, and with it also the freedom of the imagination itself in its very lawfulness, be stifled—without which no beautiful art, indeed not even a correct taste of one's own for judging it, is possible.

The propaedeutic to all beautiful art, insofar as it is directed toward the highest degree of its perfection, seems to lie not in precepts, but in the culture of the mental powers through those preliminary cognitions which are called _humaniora_: presumably because ***humanity*** means on the one hand the universal ***feeling of sympathy***, and on the other hand the faculty of being able to ***communicate*** oneself intimately and universally; which properties, joined together, constitute the sociability appropriate to mankind, whereby it distinguishes itself from animal limitation. The age as well as the nations in which the active impulse toward ***lawful*** sociability, whereby a people constitutes a lasting community, wrestled with the great difficulties that surround the difficult problem of combining freedom (and thus also equality) with {{ 263 }} a constraint (more of respect and submission out of duty than of fear); such an age and such a nation had to {{{ 356 }}} first invent the art of the reciprocal communication of ideas between the most cultivated part and the cruder, the coordination of the extension and refinement of the former with the natural simplicity and originality of the latter, and in this way first find that middle path between higher culture and compliant nature which constitutes the correct standard, not to be prescribed by any general rules, also for taste as a universal sense of mankind.

A later age will hardly make those models dispensable: because it will be ever less close to nature and finally, without having lasting examples of her, would scarcely be capable of forming a concept of the happy union of the lawful constraint of the highest culture with the force and correctness of free nature feeling its own worth in one and the same people.

But since taste is at bottom a faculty for judging the rendering sensible of moral ideas (by means of a certain analogy of reflection upon both), from which also, and from the greater receptivity for the feeling from the latter (which is called the moral feeling) that is to be grounded thereon, is derived that pleasure which taste declares to be valid for mankind in general, and not merely for each individual's private feeling {{ 264 }}: it is evident that the true propaedeutic for the founding of taste is the development of moral ideas and the culture of moral feeling; since only when sensibility is brought into harmony with this can genuine taste assume a determinate, unalterable form.
