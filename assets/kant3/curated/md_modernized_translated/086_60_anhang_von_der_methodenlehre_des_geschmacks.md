## {{ 261 }} § 60. Appendix. On the doctrine of method of taste

That separation of a critique into a doctrine of elements and a doctrine of method, a separation preceding the science itself, does not admit of application to the critique of taste {{{ 355 }}}: for no science of the beautiful is given or can be given, and the judgment of taste is incapable of determination through principles. For as concerns the scientific side of any art — the side directed toward ***truth*** in the presentation of its object — that side is admittedly the unavoidable condition (_conditio sine qua non_) of fine art, but is not itself that art. There is therefore for fine art only a ***manner*** (_modus_), not a ***mode of teaching*** (_methodus_). The master must demonstrate for the pupil what he is to produce and how; and those general rules beneath which he finally subsumes his own procedure are apt to serve less for prescribing that procedure to him than for occasionally calling its chief moments back to memory. Even so, regard must here be paid to a certain ideal that art must keep before its eyes, though it never fully reaches it in its practice. Only by rousing the pupil's imagination to adequacy with a given concept, by the noted insufficiency of the {{ 262 }} expression for the idea that the concept itself does not reach because that idea is aesthetic, and by keen critique, can one guard against the examples set before him being taken by him at once as archetypes and as patterns for imitation subject to no still higher norm and to no judging of his own, and thus against genius, and with it the very freedom of the imagination in its lawfulness, being stifled — that freedom without which no fine art, indeed not even a correct taste of one's own for judging it, is possible.

The propaedeutic to all fine art, so far as it aims at the utmost degree of its perfection, appears to consist not in prescriptions but in the culture of the mental powers by way of that preliminary knowledge which is called _humaniora_: presumably because ***humanity*** signifies, on the one hand, the universal ***feeling of fellow participation*** and, on the other, the capacity to ***communicate*** oneself most intimately and universally; which properties, joined together, constitute the sociability befitting humankind, whereby it sets itself apart from the confinement of the animal. Both the epoch and the peoples among whom the lively impulse toward ***lawful*** sociability — that by which a nation constitutes a lasting common being — struggled against the great difficulties that hem in the arduous task of joining freedom (and therefore equality as well) to {{ 263 }} a coercion (one grounded more in esteem and in submission out of duty than out of fear); such an epoch and such a people were the first who had to {{{ 356 }}} devise the art whereby the ideas of the most highly developed portion are reciprocally imparted to the ruder portion, the attunement of the widening and refinement of the former to the unaffected plainness and originality of the latter, and thereby that middle term between the loftier culture and an undemanding nature which yields the right measure — one specifiable by no general rules — for taste as well, taken as a universal human sense.

A later epoch will scarcely be able to do without those models: for it will draw ever further from nature, and in the end, possessing no abiding examples drawn from her, could hardly form for itself any concept of that happy marriage — within one selfsame people — between the lawful constraint belonging to the highest culture and the vigor and rectitude of a free nature that feels its own worth.

But since taste is at bottom a faculty for judging the making-sensible of moral ideas (by means of a certain analogy in the reflection upon both), from which faculty, and from the greater receptivity to be grounded thereon for the feeling arising out of those ideas (which is called the moral feeling), there is likewise derived that pleasure which taste pronounces valid for mankind at large, and not for the private feeling of each person alone {{ 264 }}: it becomes plain that the true propaedeutic for grounding taste is the development of moral ideas and the culture of moral feeling; for genuine taste can assume a determinate, unchanging form only where sensibility has been attuned to that feeling.
