## {{ 261 }} § 60. Appendix. On the doctrine of method of taste

The division of a critique into a doctrine of elements and a doctrine of method, which precedes any science, cannot be applied to the critique of taste {{{ 355 }}}: for there neither is nor can be any science of the beautiful, and the judgment of taste is not determinable through principles. For as concerns the scientific side of any art — the side directed toward ***truth*** in the presentation of its object — that side is admittedly the unavoidable condition (_conditio sine qua non_) of fine art, but is not itself that art. There is therefore for fine art only a ***manner*** (_modus_), not a ***mode of teaching*** (_methodus_). The master must show what and how the pupil is to accomplish; and the general rules under which he ultimately brings his procedure can serve rather to recall its principal moments to mind on occasion than to prescribe them to him. Even so, regard must here be paid to a certain ideal that art must keep before its eyes, though it never fully reaches it in its practice. Only by rousing the pupil's imagination to adequacy with a given concept, by the noted insufficiency of the {{ 262 }} expression for the idea that the concept itself does not reach because that idea is aesthetic, and by keen critique, can one guard against the examples set before him being taken by him at once as archetypes and as patterns for imitation subject to no still higher norm and to no judging of his own, and thus against genius, and with it the very freedom of the imagination in its lawfulness, being stifled — that freedom without which no fine art, indeed not even a correct taste of one's own for judging it, is possible.

The propaedeutic to all fine art, so far as it aims at the utmost degree of its perfection, appears to consist not in prescriptions but in the culture of the mental powers by way of that preliminary knowledge which is called _humaniora_: presumably because ***humanity*** signifies, on the one hand, the universal ***feeling of fellow participation*** and, on the other, the capacity to ***communicate*** oneself most intimately and universally; which properties, joined together, constitute the sociability befitting humankind, whereby it sets itself apart from the confinement of the animal. The age as well as the peoples in which the vigorous drive toward ***lawful*** sociability — whereby a people forms an enduring commonwealth — wrestled with the great difficulties surrounding the hard task of uniting freedom (and hence also equality) with {{ 263 }} a constraint (a constraint resting more on respect and dutiful submission than on fear); an age and a people of that kind had first to {{{ 356 }}} invent the art of reciprocal communication of ideas between the most cultivated part and the cruder one, the tuning of the expansion and refinement of the former to the natural simplicity and originality of the latter, and in this way that mean between higher culture and self-contented nature which forms the correct standard, specifiable by no universal rules, for taste too, as a universal human sense.

A later age will hardly render those patterns dispensable: for it will stand ever less close to nature, and in the end, having no lasting examples of it, would scarcely be capable of forming a concept of the fortunate union, within one and the same people, of the lawful constraint of the highest culture with the force and correctness of a free nature that feels its own worth.

But since taste is at bottom a faculty for judging the making-sensible of moral ideas (by means of a certain analogy in the reflection upon both), from which faculty, and from the greater receptivity to be grounded thereon for the feeling arising out of those ideas (which is called the moral feeling), there is likewise derived that pleasure which taste pronounces valid for mankind at large, and not for the private feeling of each person alone {{ 264 }}: it becomes plain that the true propaedeutic for grounding taste is the development of moral ideas and the culture of moral feeling; for genuine taste can assume a determinate, unchanging form only where sensibility has been attuned to that feeling.
