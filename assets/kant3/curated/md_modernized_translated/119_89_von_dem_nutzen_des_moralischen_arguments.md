## § 89. On the use of the moral argument

Confining reason—in respect of all our ideas of the supersensible—to the terms on which it may be put to practical use carries, where {{ 440 }} the idea of God is at stake, an undeniable advantage: it keeps ***theology*** from soaring off into theosophy (into extravagant concepts that throw reason into confusion), and from sinking down into demonology (a way of picturing the highest being on the model of man); it keeps ***religion*** from straying into ***theurgy*** (the fanatical delusion that one might sense other supersensible beings and, reciprocally, work an influence upon them), or into ***idolatry*** (the superstitious delusion that one might become pleasing to the highest being through some means apart from a moral cast of mind)[^*].

For if one grants to the vanity or the overreach of hair-splitting reason the license to settle even the slightest matter theoretically (and so as to enlarge cognition) about what lies past the world of sense; if one allows it to boast of insights into the existence and constitution of the {{ 441 }} divine nature, into its understanding and will, the laws of both, and the attributes that issue from these upon the world: then I would genuinely wish to be shown at which spot, and along what line, one means to fence in the {{{ 460 }}} pretensions of reason; for from whatever quarter those insights were fetched, that same quarter can surely furnish yet more of them (so long as one bent one's thinking to the task, as one imagines one might). The drawing of limits to such claims would surely have to follow from some settled principle, and not simply from our finding that every attempt of the kind has so far come to nothing; for a record of failure up to now settles nothing about whether a happier issue might yet be reached. But here no principle is possible except to assume either: that with regard to the supersensible absolutely nothing at all can be determined theoretically (except merely negatively), or that our reason contains within itself an as yet unmined lode of who-knows-how-great cognition-extending items of knowledge, held in reserve for us and our descendants. — Where religion is concerned, however, i.e., morality taken in relation to God as lawgiver: then, supposing the theoretical cognition of him had to go first, morality would be made to fall in line with theology, and not merely would an outward and arbitrary law-giving by a supreme being supplant an inward and necessary law-giving of reason, but whatever is defective in our insight into {{ 442 }} his nature would also reach through to the moral precept, and so would render religion immoral and turn it upside down.

Touching the hope of a life hereafter, should we—rather than the final end that we are ourselves to accomplish as the prescription of the moral law directs—turn to our theoretical power of cognition for the thread guiding the judgment of reason about our vocation (a judgment, then, held necessary or worthy of acceptance only in a practical bearing), the doctrine of the soul affords, to this end, exactly as theology did above, no more than a negative concept of the being that thinks within us: namely, that not one of its acts, nor any appearance of inner sense, admits of a materialist explanation; and hence that, as to its detached nature and the persistence or non-persistence of its personhood beyond death, no cognition-enlarging, determining judgment on speculative grounds is at all open to us through the whole of our theoretical power of cognition. Since, then, everything here is consigned to a teleological judging of our being, made under a practically necessary consideration, and to the supposition of our lasting on, this being the condition demanded for that final end which reason lays upon us without qualification, there comes to light at the same stroke the advantage (which at first sight does look like a forfeiture): that, just as theology can for us never turn into theosophy {{ 443 }} {{{ 461 }}}, so rational ***psychology*** can never turn into ***pneumatology*** as a science that enlarges cognition, while on the other side it stands equally safeguarded against sliding into any ***materialism***; that it is rather no more than an anthropology of the inner sense, i.e., an acquaintance with our thinking self during ***life***, and, treated as theoretical cognition, likewise stays merely empirical; whereas rational psychology, once the question bears on our eternal existence, is no theoretical science whatever, but hangs upon one solitary inference of moral teleology, just as its whole employment is needed only for the sake of the latter, i.e., of our practical vocation.

[^*]: Idolatry, in the practical sense, is still that religion which conceives of the highest being with properties such that something other than morality could be the condition, sufficient in itself, for conforming to his will in what the human being is capable of doing. For however pure and free of sensible images one may have framed that concept in a theoretical respect, in the practical sphere it is then nevertheless represented as an ***idol***, i.e., anthropomorphically as regards the constitution of his will.
