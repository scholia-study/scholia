## § 89. On the Utility of the Moral Argument

The restriction of reason, in respect of all our ideas of the supersensible, to the conditions of its practical use has, as {{ 440 }} regards the idea of God, the unmistakable utility: that it prevents ***theology*** from losing itself in theosophy (in reason-bewildering, extravagant concepts), or sinking into demonology (an anthropomorphic way of representing the supreme being); and prevents ***religion*** from turning into ***theurgy*** (a fanatical delusion of being able to have a feeling of other supersensible beings and, in turn, an influence upon them), or into ***idolatry*** (a superstitious delusion of being able to make oneself pleasing to the supreme being by means other than a moral disposition)[^*].

For if one allows the vanity or presumption of ratiocination, in respect of what lies beyond the sensible world, to determine even the least thing theoretically (and as extending our cognition); if one permits boasting with insights into the existence and constitution of the {{ 441 }} divine nature, into its understanding and will, the laws of both, and the properties flowing from these onto the world: then I would like to know where and at what point one wants to {{{ 460 }}} limit the pretensions of reason; for from the very same source whence those insights were taken, still more can indeed be expected (if only, as is thought, one exerted one's reflection). The limitation of such claims would have to occur according to a certain principle, not perhaps merely on the ground that we find that all attempts with them have hitherto failed; for that proves nothing against the possibility of a better outcome. But here no principle is possible other than to assume either: that in respect of the supersensible, absolutely nothing can be theoretically determined (except as merely negative), or that our reason contains within itself an as yet untapped treasury of expanding cognitions, reserved for us and our descendants, of who knows what magnitude. — But as regards religion, i.e., morality in relation to God as lawgiver: if the theoretical cognition of God had to precede, then morality would have to conform to theology, and not only would an outer, arbitrary legislation of a supreme being be introduced instead of an inner, necessary legislation of reason, but also everything in the latter that our insight into {{ 442 }} the nature of this being has of deficiency would extend to the moral precept, and thus make religion immoral and pervert it.

In respect of the hope of a future life, if, instead of the final purpose which we ourselves have to accomplish in accordance with the precept of the moral law, we consult our theoretical cognitive faculty as the guide for the rational judgment about our vocation (which is thus regarded as necessary or worthy of acceptance only in a practical relation), the doctrine of the soul in this regard, just as theology above, gives nothing more than a negative concept of our thinking being: namely, that none of its actions and appearances of the inner sense can be explained materialistically; so that of its separate nature and the duration or non-duration of its personality after death, absolutely no extending, determining judgment from speculative grounds is possible for us through our entire theoretical cognitive faculty. Since, therefore, everything here is left to the teleological judgment of our existence in a practically necessary respect, and to the acceptance of our continuation as the condition required for the final purpose absolutely assigned to us by reason, there is shown here at the same time the utility (which indeed at first glance seems to be a loss): that, just as theology can never become theosophy for us {{ 443 }} {{{ 461 }}}, rational ***psychology*** can never become ***pneumatology*** as an expanding science, just as it is also secured, on the other hand, against falling into any ***materialism***; but that it is rather merely an anthropology of the inner sense, i.e., a cognition of our thinking self in ***life***, and as theoretical cognition also remains merely empirical; whereas rational psychology, as far as the question of our eternal existence is concerned, is not a theoretical science at all, but rests on a single inference of moral teleology, just as its entire use is necessary merely for the sake of the latter as our practical vocation.

[^*]: Idolatry in a practical sense is still that religion which conceives of the supreme being with properties according to which something other than morality could be the condition, suitable in itself, for conforming to his will in what human beings are capable of doing. For however pure and free from sensible images one may have conceived that concept in a theoretical respect, in a practical respect it is nevertheless represented then as an ***idol***, i.e., as anthropomorphic according to the constitution of his will.
