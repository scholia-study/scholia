## {{ 147 }} § 36. On the task of a deduction of judgments of taste

With the perception of an object, the concept of an object in general — of which that perception contains the empirical {{{ 288 }}} predicates — can be immediately combined into a cognitive judgment, and thereby a judgment of experience be produced. Underlying this there now are concepts _a priori_ of the synthetic unity belonging to the manifold of intuition, concepts through which that manifold gets thought as an object's determination; and these concepts — the categories — demand a deduction, which was in fact furnished in the Critique of Pure Reason as well, and by means of which the task could then be resolved: How are synthetic cognitive judgments _a priori_ possible? This task, then, bore upon the a priori principles of the pure understanding and its theoretical judgments.

Yet a perception, too, may have immediately combined with it a feeling of pleasure (or displeasure) and a satisfaction, which attends the object's representation and takes the place of a predicate for it, so that an aesthetic judgment — one that is not a cognitive judgment — thereby arises. In the case of such a judgment — provided it is no mere judgment of sensation but rather a formal judgment of reflection, which requires of everyone this satisfaction as necessary — there must lie at the {{ 148 }} basis something serving as a principle _a priori_, something that may perhaps be merely subjective (in case an objective one should prove impossible for judgments of that sort), yet that, even so, stands in need of a deduction, in order that it be grasped how an aesthetic judgment can lay claim to necessity. On this now rests the task with which we are now occupied: How are judgments of taste possible? This task, accordingly, bears upon the principles _a priori_ of the pure power of judgment as they hold in ***aesthetic*** judgments — that is, in judgments of the kind where it is not merely charged (as it is in the theoretical ones) with subsuming things beneath objective concepts of the understanding while standing under a law, but where it is, for itself and subjectively, at once the object and the law.

One may also present this task in the following way: How is there possible a judgment that, drawing solely upon one's ***own*** feeling of pleasure in an object and setting aside any concept of it, would appraise this pleasure _a priori_ — that is, without needing to await the concurrence of anyone else — as something pertaining to the representation of that same object ***in every other subject***?

It is easy to see that judgments of taste are synthetic, for they reach past the object's concept and even past its intuition, appending to that intuition, as a predicate, something that is not even cognition — namely, a feeling of pleasure (or displeasure). But that they are nonetheless — although the predicate here, namely the ***own*** pleasure that the representation carries with it, is empirical {{ 149 }} — judgments _a priori_ as regards the required assent ***of everyone*** {{{ 289 }}}, or wish to be taken for such, this too is already contained in the very terms of their claim; and so this task of the critique of the power of judgment falls under transcendental philosophy's general problem: How are synthetic judgments _a priori_ possible?
