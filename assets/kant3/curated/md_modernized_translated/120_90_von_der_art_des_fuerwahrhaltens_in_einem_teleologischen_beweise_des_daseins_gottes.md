## § 90. On the Kind of Assent in a Teleological Proof of the Existence of God

First, for every proof, whether it be conducted (as in the proof through observation of the object or experiment) through immediate empirical presentation of that which is to be proved, or through reason _a priori_ from principles, it is required: that it should not ***persuade*** but ***convince***, or at least work toward conviction; i.e., that the ground of proof, or the conclusion, should not be a merely subjective {{ 444 }} (aesthetic) determining ground of assent (mere illusion), but objectively valid and a logical ground of cognition: for otherwise the understanding is enticed, but not convinced. Of that kind of illusory proof is the one which is conducted in natural theology, perhaps with good intentions, but yet with deliberate concealment of its weakness: when one adduces the great multitude of testimonies of an origin of natural things according to the principle of ends and makes use of the merely subjective ground of human reason, namely its own inclination, wherever it can be done without contradiction, to think a single principle instead of many, and, where only some or even many requirements for the determination of a concept are met in this principle, to think the rest in addition, in order to complete the concept of the thing by arbitrary supplementation. For indeed, if we find so many products in nature which are for us indications of an intelligent cause, why should we not, instead of many such causes, rather think a single one, and in this one think not merely great understanding, {{{ 462 }}} power, etc., but rather all-wisdom, omnipotence, in a word, think it as such a cause as contains the sufficient ground of such properties for all possible things; and, moreover, attribute to this single, all-powerful original being not merely understanding for the laws and products of nature, {{ 445 }} but also, as a moral cause of the world, the highest moral practical reason; since by this completion of the concept a principle is indicated that is sufficient for both insight into nature and moral wisdom together, and no objection that is even moderately well-founded can be made against the possibility of such an idea? If, at the same time, the moral incentives of the mind are set in motion and a lively interest of the latter is added with rhetorical strength (of which they are indeed well worthy), there arises from this a persuasion of the objective sufficiency of the proof and an illusion which is (in most cases of its use) even beneficial, which entirely dispenses with all examination of its logical rigor and even bears aversion and repugnance against it, as if a blasphemous doubt lay at its foundation. — Now there is indeed nothing to be said against this, as far as one actually takes popular utility into consideration. But since the analysis of this proof into the two heterogeneous pieces that this argument contains, namely into that which belongs to physical and that which belongs to moral teleology, cannot and must not be prevented, because the fusion of the two makes it unrecognizable where the actual nerve of the proof lies, and on which part and how it would have to be worked upon in order to be able to withstand the sharpest scrutiny {{ 446 }} for its validity (even if one should be compelled to confess the weakness of our rational insight on one part): it is a duty for the philosopher (even assuming that he counted the demand for sincerity on him for nothing) to expose the illusion, however beneficial it may still be, which such a mixing can produce, and to separate what belongs merely to persuasion from what leads to conviction (which two are determinations of assent that differ not merely in degree but even in kind), in order to present openly the state of mind in this proof in its entire purity and to be able to subject it freely to the most rigorous scrutiny.

But a proof which is designed for conviction can in turn be of two kinds: either one which is intended to establish what the object is ***in itself***, or what it is ***for us*** (human beings in general) according to the principles of reason {{{ 463 }}} necessary for us for its judging (a proof κατ' αλήθειαν or κατ' άνθρωπον, the latter word taken in its general meaning for human beings in general). In the former case it is grounded on sufficient principles for the determining power of judgment, in the second case merely for the reflecting power of judgment. In the latter case, if it rests on merely theoretical principles, it can never work toward conviction; but if {{ 447 }} it lays a practical principle of reason at its foundation (which consequently holds universally and necessarily), it may indeed claim a conviction that is sufficient in a pure practical respect, i.e., a moral conviction. But a proof ***works toward conviction***, without yet convincing, if it is merely conducted on the way toward it, i.e., contains in itself only objective grounds for it, which, although they are not yet sufficient for certainty, are nevertheless of such a kind that they do not serve merely as subjective grounds of judgment for persuasion.

All theoretical grounds of proof now suffice either: 1) for proof by logically rigorous ***syllogisms***; or, where this is not the case, 2) for ***conclusion*** according to ***analogy***; or, if even this does not take place, still 3) for ***probable opinion***; or finally, what is the least, 4) for the assumption of a merely possible ground of explanation, as a ***hypothesis***. — Now I say: that all grounds of proof in general which work toward theoretical conviction can produce no assent of this kind, from the highest to the lowest degree of it, if the proposition of the existence of an original being, as a God in the meaning appropriate to the entire content of this concept, namely as a ***moral*** author of the world, consequently in such a way that through him the final end of creation is indicated at the same time, is to be proved {{ 448 }}.

1) As regards the ***logically correct*** proof that proceeds from the general to the particular, it has been sufficiently shown in the Critique: that since no intuition possible for us corresponds to the concept of a being which is to be sought beyond nature, and its concept itself, so far as it is to be theoretically determined by synthetic predicates, therefore remains at all times problematic for us, absolutely no cognition of it (whereby the scope of our theoretical knowledge would be in the least expanded) takes place, and the particular concept of a supersensible being cannot be subsumed under the general principles of the nature of things at all, in order to conclude from {{{ 464 }}} the latter to the former; because those principles hold solely for nature as an object of the senses.

2) One can indeed, from two heterogeneous things, precisely in the point of their heterogeneity, ***think*** one of them according to an ***analogy***[^*] with the other; but from that wherein they are heterogeneous, one cannot {{ 449 }} ***conclude*** from one to the other according to analogy, i.e., transfer {{ 450 }} this characteristic of specific difference to the other. Thus, according to the analogy with the law of the equality of action and reaction in the reciprocal attraction and repulsion of {{{ 465 }}} bodies under one another, I can also think the community of the members of a commonwealth according to rules of right; but I cannot transfer those specific determinations (material attraction or repulsion) to the latter and attribute them to the citizens in order to constitute a system which is called a state. — Likewise, we may indeed think the causality of the original being in regard to the things of the world, as natural ends, according to the analogy of an understanding, as the ground of the forms of certain products which we call works of art (for this happens only for the behoof of the theoretical or practical use of our cognitive faculty which we have to make of this concept in regard to natural things in the world according to a certain principle): but we can by no means conclude from the fact that under {{ 451 }} world-beings understanding must be attributed to the cause of an effect which is judged to be artificial, according to an analogy, that the very same causality which we perceive in human beings also belongs to the being which is entirely distinct from nature, in regard to nature itself: because this concerns precisely the point of heterogeneity which is thought in the concept of it between a cause sensibly conditioned in regard to its effects and the supersensible original being itself, and therefore cannot be transferred to the latter. — In this very fact, that I am to think the divine causality only according to the analogy of an understanding (which faculty we know in no other being than the sensibly conditioned human being), lies the prohibition against attributing this to him in its proper meaning[^**].

3) ***Opining*** does not take place in judgments _a priori_ at all; rather, through them one cognizes either something as entirely certain, or nothing at all. But even if the given grounds of proof from which we depart (as here from the ends in the world) are empirical, one can yet {{ 452 }} opine nothing through them beyond the world of sense, nor concede to such daring judgments the least claim to probability. For probability is a part of a certainty possible in a certain series of grounds (the grounds of which are compared therein with the sufficient ground as parts with a whole), to which that insufficient {{{ 466 }}} ground must be capable of being supplemented. But because they must be homogeneous as determining grounds of the certainty of one and the same judgment, since otherwise they would not together constitute a magnitude (such as certainty is): a part of them cannot lie within the limits of possible experience and another part outside of all possible experience. Consequently, since merely empirical grounds of proof lead to nothing supersensible, and the lack in the series of them can also be supplemented by nothing: there takes place in the attempt to arrive through them at the supersensible and a cognition of it not the least approximation, and consequently in a judgment about the latter through arguments taken from experience also no probability takes place.

4) What is to serve as a ***hypothesis*** for the explanation of the possibility of a given appearance, of that at least the possibility must be completely certain. It is enough that in the case of a hypothesis I renounce the cognition of reality (which {{ 453 }} is still asserted in an opinion issued as probable); more I cannot surrender; the possibility of that which I lay at the foundation of an explanation must at least be exposed to no doubt, because otherwise there would be no end to empty chimeras. But to assume the possibility of a supersensible being determined according to certain concepts, since for this none of the required conditions of a cognition according to what rests on intuition in it is given, and thus the mere proposition of contradiction (which can prove nothing but the possibility of thinking and not of the thought object itself) remains as the criterion of this possibility, would be a completely groundless presupposition.

The result of this is: that for the existence of the original being as a deity, or of the soul as an immortal spirit, absolutely no proof in a theoretical respect is possible for human reason, in order to work even the least degree of assent; and this for the very comprehensible reason: because for the determination of the ideas of the supersensible there is for us no material at all, since we would have to take the latter from things in the world of sense, but such material is absolutely not appropriate to that object, so that without all determination of it nothing more remains than the concept of a non-sensible something which contains the ultimate ground of the world of sense, {{ 454 }} which does not yet constitute any cognition (as an expansion of the concept) of its inner constitution.

[^*]: ***Analogy*** (in a qualitative meaning) is the identity of the relation between grounds and consequences (causes and effects), so far as it takes place notwithstanding the specific difference of the things or of those properties in themselves which contain the ground of similar consequences (i.e., considered apart from this relation). Thus, in regard to the artistic actions of animals in comparison with those of human beings, we think the ground of these effects in the former, which we do not know, as the analogue of reason with the ground of similar effects of human beings (reason), which we do know; and we wish thereby at the same time to indicate: that the ground of the animal's artistic faculty, under the name of an instinct, is indeed specifically distinct from reason, but yet has a similar relation to the effect (the building of beavers compared with that of human beings). — But for that reason, because the human being needs ***reason*** for his building, I cannot conclude that the beaver must also have the like, and call it a ***conclusion*** according to analogy. But from the similar mode of operation of animals (of which we cannot immediately perceive the ground) compared with that of human beings (of which we are immediately conscious), we can quite correctly conclude ***according to analogy*** that animals also act according to ***representations*** (are not machines, as Descartes would have it) and, notwithstanding their specific difference, are yet of the same genus (as living beings) with the human being. The principle of authorization to conclude thus lies in the sameness of a ground for counting animals, in regard to the thought determination, in one and the same genus with the human being as human, as far as we compare them with one another externally according to their actions. It is par ratio. Likewise, I can think the causality of the supreme world-cause, in the comparison of its purposive products in the world with the works of art of human beings, according to the analogy of an understanding, but I cannot conclude to these properties in it according to analogy: because here the principle of the possibility of such a mode of conclusion is precisely lacking, namely the _paritas rationis_ for counting the supreme being in one and the same genus with the human being (in regard to their mutual causality). The causality of world-beings, which is always sensibly conditioned (the like of that through understanding), cannot be transferred to a being which has no concept of genus in common with them other than that of a thing in general.
[^**]: One does not thereby miss the least in the representation of the relations of this being to the world, as regards both the theoretical and the practical consequences from this concept. To wish to investigate what it may be in itself is a curiosity just as aimless as it is vain.
