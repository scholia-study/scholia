## § 90. On the mode of holding-to-be-true in a teleological proof of the existence of God

To begin with, for every proof — whether it be conducted (as in proof by observation of the object or by experiment) through immediate empirical exhibition of what is to be proved, or through reason _a priori_ from principles — it is required that it not ***persuade*** but ***convince***, or at least work toward conviction; that is, that the ground of proof, or the inference, be not merely a subjective {{ 444 }} (aesthetic) determining ground of assent (mere semblance), but instead something objectively valid, a logical ground of cognition: since otherwise the understanding gets ensnared rather than genuinely won over. Of that sort of a specious proof is the one that is conducted in natural theology perhaps with good intent, yet with deliberate concealment of its weakness: when one draws in the great mass of evidences for an origin of natural things according to the principle of ends and turns to advantage the merely subjective ground of human reason, namely its peculiar propensity — wherever this can be done without contradiction — to think a single principle in place of many, and, where in this principle only some or even many requisites for the determination of a concept are met with, to think in the rest, so as to complete the concept of the thing by an arbitrary supplement. For, to be sure, when we encounter so many products in nature that are for us indications of an intelligent cause: why should we, in place of many such causes, not rather think a single one, and in it not merely great understanding, {{{ 462 }}} power, and so forth, but far rather all-wisdom, omnipotence — in a word, think it as one that contains the ground sufficient for all such properties in regard to every possible thing? and, beyond this, ascribe to this one all-capable original being understanding not merely for the laws and products of nature {{ 445 }} but also, as a moral cause of the world, the highest ethical practical reason; since by this completion of the concept a principle is furnished that suffices for insight into nature as well as for moral wisdom together, and no even remotely grounded objection can be raised against the possibility of such an idea? If at the same time the moral incentives of the mind are here set in motion and a lively interest of the latter is added with rhetorical force (of which they are indeed well worthy): then there arises from this a persuasion of the objective sufficiency of the proof, and a semblance (in most cases of its use also a salutary one) that raises itself entirely above any examination of its logical rigor and even bears abhorrence and repugnance against such examination, as if a sacrilegious doubt lay at its foundation. — Now against this there is indeed nothing to be said, so far as one really has regard to popular usefulness. Yet since the dissection of it into the two heterogeneous pieces that this argument contains, namely into that which belongs to physical and that which belongs to moral teleology, neither can nor may be prevented — inasmuch as the fusing together of the two makes it unrecognizable where the actual nerve of the proof lies, and on which part and how it would have to be worked out in order, for its validity, before the most rigorous examination {{ 446 }} to be able to hold its ground (even if on one part one should be compelled to concede the weakness of our rational insight): so it is the philosopher's duty (granted that he even counted the demand of sincerity upon him as nothing) to uncover the semblance, however salutary, that such a mingling can produce, and to separate what belongs merely to persuasion from what leads to conviction (both being determinations of assent distinguished not merely in degree but even in kind), in order to present openly the state of mind in this proof in its full purity and to be able to submit it candidly to the strictest examination.

A proof, however, that is directed at conviction can in turn be of two kinds: either such a proof as would make out what the object may be ***in itself***, or else what, ***for us*** (human beings at large), it is according to the principles of reason necessary for us {{{ 463 }}} in the judging of it (a proof κατ' αληθειαν or κατ' ανθρωπον, the latter word taken in the general signification of human beings as such). In the former case it is grounded on principles sufficient for the determining, in the second merely for the reflecting power of judgment. In the latter case, resting on merely theoretical principles, it can never work toward conviction; but if it, on the other hand, {{ 447 }} lays a practical principle of reason at its foundation (which therefore holds universally and necessarily), then it may well lay claim to a conviction sufficient for a pure practical purpose, that is, a moral one. A proof, however, ***works toward conviction*** without yet convincing when it is merely conducted on the way toward it, that is, contains in itself only objective grounds for it which, although not yet sufficient for certainty, are nonetheless of such a kind that they do not serve merely as subjective grounds of the judgment for persuasion.

Now all theoretical grounds of proof suffice either: 1) for proof through logically rigorous ***inferences of reason***; or, where this is not the case, 2) for ***inference*** by ***analogy***; or, should even this perhaps not obtain, still 3) for ***probable opinion***; or finally, which is the least, 4) for the assumption of a merely possible ground of explanation, as ***hypothesis***. — Now I say: that all grounds of proof whatever which work toward theoretical conviction can bring about no holding-to-be-true of this kind, from the highest to the lowest degree of it, if the thesis asserting that an original being exists — a God, taken in the sense that fits the entire content of this concept, namely as a ***moral*** author of the world, and thus in such a way that through it the final end of creation is at the same time specified, is to be {{ 448 }} proved.

1) As regards the ***logically legitimate*** proof that advances from the universal to the particular, the Critique has established well enough that, seeing that the concept of a being which must be looked for outside nature has corresponding to it no intuition available to us — so that this concept, in so far as one would determine it theoretically by means of synthetic predicates, stays for us perpetually problematic — no cognition of such a being can occur at all (a cognition that would enlarge the compass of our theoretical knowing even slightly), and the special concept of a supersensible being admits of no subsumption whatever under the general principles governing the nature of things, in a way that would let us reason from the latter to {{{ 464 }}} it; for those principles are valid only for nature taken as an object of the senses.

2) One can, to be sure, of two heterogeneous things, precisely at the point of their heterogeneity, nonetheless ***think*** one of them by an ***analogy***[^*] with the other; but from that in which they are heterogeneous one cannot {{ 449 }} ***infer*** by analogy from the one to the other, that is, transfer this mark {{ 450 }} of the specific difference to the other. Thus I can, by analogy with the law of the equality of action and reaction in the reciprocal attraction and repulsion of {{{ 465 }}} bodies with one another, think as well the fellowship of the members of a common polity in accordance with rules of right; yet those specific determinations (material attraction or repulsion) I may not carry over to the latter and assign them to the citizens so as to make up a system that goes by the name of a state. — Likewise we may indeed think the causality of the original being in regard to the things of the world, as natural ends, by analogy with an understanding, as the ground of the forms of certain products that we call works of art (for this happens only for the sake of the theoretical or practical use of our faculty of cognition that we have to make of this concept, according to a certain principle, in regard to the natural things in the world): but from the circumstance that, among {{ 451 }} worldly beings, one must credit with understanding the cause of an effect that is appraised as artificial, we can in no way infer by an analogy that this very same causality which we perceive in the human being also belongs, in regard to nature itself, to the being that is wholly distinct from nature: because this concerns precisely the point of heterogeneity that is thought, in the concept of it, between a cause sensibly conditioned in regard to its effects and the supersensible original being itself, and thus cannot be transferred to the latter. — Precisely in this — that I am to think the divine causality only by analogy with an understanding (a faculty we know in no other being than the sensibly conditioned human being) — lies the prohibition against ascribing this to it in the proper signification[^**].

3) ***Opining*** has no place at all in judgments _a priori_; rather, through them one either cognizes something as entirely certain, or nothing at all. But even if the given grounds of proof from which we set out (as here from the ends in the world) are empirical, one still cannot, by means of these, {{ 452 }} opine anything beyond the world of sense, nor grant such hazardous judgments the least claim to probability. For probability is a part of a certainty possible within a certain series of grounds (the grounds of it being therein compared, as parts to a whole, with what is sufficient), to which that insufficient {{{ 466 }}} ground must be capable of being supplemented. But since, as determining grounds for the certainty of one and the same judgment, these grounds have to be of the same kind — for otherwise they would not jointly make up a magnitude, which is what certainty amounts to — it is impossible that some of them should fall inside the limits of a possible experience while others fall beyond every possible experience. And so, given that purely empirical grounds of proof carry us to nothing supersensible, and given that the gap in their series can be filled by nothing whatever: any effort to reach, through them, the supersensible together with a cognition of it makes not the slightest headway, and accordingly a judgment about the latter, resting on considerations borrowed from experience, admits of no probability at all.

4) Of what is to serve as ***hypothesis*** for the explanation of the possibility of a given appearance, at least the possibility must be entirely certain. It is enough that in the case of a hypothesis I renounce cognition of actuality (which in an opinion put forward as probable {{ 453 }} is still asserted); more I cannot surrender; the possibility of that which I lay at the foundation of an explanation must at least not be exposed to any doubt, since otherwise there would be no end to empty figments of the brain. To posit, however, the possibility of a supersensible being determined in accordance with certain concepts, when none of the conditions requisite for a cognition — as regards that in it which rests on intuition — is given for this, and thus the mere principle of contradiction (which can prove nothing but the possibility of the thinking, and not of the thought object itself) remains as the criterion of this possibility, would be a wholly groundless presupposition.

The result of this is: that for the existence of the original being as a divinity, or of the soul as an immortal spirit, there is simply no proof possible for human reason in a theoretical respect, so as to produce even the least degree of holding-to-be-true; and this for the entirely comprehensible reason that, for the determination of the ideas of the supersensible, there is for us no material at all, inasmuch as we would have to take this latter from things in the world of sense, whereas such material is simply not commensurate with that object, so that, once all determination of them is set aside, what is left over is nothing beyond the concept of some non-sensible thing-or-other that would harbor the final ground of the sensible world, {{ 454 }} a concept that still yields no cognition (by way of enlarging it) of that being's inner constitution.

[^*]: ***Analogy*** (in the qualitative signification) is the identity of the relation between grounds and consequences (causes and effects), in so far as it obtains despite the specific diversity of the things, or of those properties in themselves which contain the ground of similar consequences (that is, considered apart from this relation). Thus, regarding the artful operations of animals in comparison with those of the human being, we think the ground of these effects in the former — which we do not know — as an analogue of reason, by comparison with the ground of similar effects of the human being (reason), which we do know; and we mean thereby to indicate at the same time that the ground of the animal capacity for art, under the name of an instinct, is indeed specifically distinct from reason, yet bears a similar relation to the effect (the beaver's construction compared with that of humans). — For that reason, however, I cannot infer from the fact that the human being needs ***reason*** for its building that the beaver must have something of the same, and call this an ***inference*** by analogy. But from the similar mode of operation of animals (whose ground we cannot immediately perceive), compared with that of the human being (of which we are immediately conscious), we can infer quite correctly ***by analogy*** that animals too act according to ***representations*** (and are not, as Descartes would have it, machines), and that, notwithstanding their specific diversity, they are nonetheless of one kind with the human being as regards the genus (as living beings). The principle of the entitlement to infer in this way lies in the sameness of a ground for counting animals, in respect of the said determination, as of one genus with the human being, as a human being, so far as we compare them with one another externally according to their actions. It is par ratio. Likewise I can think the causality of the supreme cause of the world, in comparing its purposive products in the world with the works of art of the human being, by analogy with an understanding, but cannot infer these properties in it by analogy: because here the very principle of the possibility of such a mode of inference is lacking, namely the _paritas rationis_ for counting the highest being together with the human being (in regard to their causality on both sides) as of one and the same genus. The causality belonging to worldly beings, forever sensibly conditioned (as the causality operating through understanding is), cannot be carried over to a being that shares with those no generic concept save that of a thing in general.
[^**]: One forfeits thereby not the slightest thing when representing how this being stands related to the world, whether as concerns the theoretical or the practical inferences that issue from this concept. To want to investigate what it may be in itself is a piece of presumption as pointless as it is futile.
