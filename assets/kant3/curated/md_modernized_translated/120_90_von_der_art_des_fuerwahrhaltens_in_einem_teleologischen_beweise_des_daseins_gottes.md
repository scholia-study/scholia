## § 90. On the mode of holding-to-be-true in a teleological proof of the existence of God

To begin with, for every proof — whether it be conducted (as in proof by observation of the object or by experiment) through immediate empirical exhibition of what is to be proved, or through reason _a priori_ from principles — it is required that it not ***persuade*** but ***convince***, or at least work toward conviction; that is, that the ground of proof, or the inference, be not merely a subjective {{ 444 }} (aesthetic) determining ground of assent (mere semblance), but instead something objectively valid, a logical ground of cognition: since otherwise the understanding gets ensnared rather than genuinely won over. Of that sort of a specious proof is the one that is conducted in natural theology perhaps with good intent, yet with deliberate concealment of its weakness: when one draws in the great mass of evidences for an origin of natural things according to the principle of ends and turns to advantage the merely subjective ground of human reason, namely its peculiar propensity — wherever this can be done without contradiction — to think a single principle in place of many, and, where in this principle only some or even many requisites for the determination of a concept are met with, to think in the rest, so as to complete the concept of the thing by an arbitrary supplement. For indeed, given that we come upon so many products in nature which for us signal an intelligent cause: why, rather than many such causes, should we not sooner think a single one, and attribute to it not merely great understanding, {{{ 462 }}} power, and the like, but rather all-wisdom and omnipotence — in short, conceive it as a being harboring the ground that suffices, for every possible thing, for such properties? and moreover invest this one all-capable original being with understanding not only in respect of nature's laws and products, {{ 445 }} but likewise, as a moral world-cause, with the supreme ethical practical reason; for through such a rounding-out of the concept there is specified a principle adequate at once to insight into nature and to moral wisdom, and not even a remotely well-founded objection can be brought against the possibility of an idea of this kind? If at the same time the moral incentives of the mind are here set in motion and a lively interest of the latter is added with rhetorical force (of which they are indeed well worthy): then there arises from this a persuasion of the objective sufficiency of the proof, and a semblance (in most cases of its use also a salutary one) that raises itself entirely above any examination of its logical rigor and even bears abhorrence and repugnance against such examination, as if a sacrilegious doubt lay at its foundation. — Now against this there is indeed nothing to be said, so far as one really has regard to popular usefulness. Yet since the dissection of it into the two heterogeneous pieces that this argument contains, namely into that which belongs to physical and that which belongs to moral teleology, neither can nor may be prevented — inasmuch as the fusing together of the two makes it unrecognizable where the actual nerve of the proof lies, and on which part and how it would have to be worked out in order, for its validity, before the most rigorous examination {{ 446 }} to be able to hold its ground (even if on one part one should be compelled to concede the weakness of our rational insight): so it is the philosopher's duty (granted that he even counted the demand of sincerity upon him as nothing) to uncover the semblance, however salutary, that such a mingling can produce, and to separate what belongs merely to persuasion from what leads to conviction (both being determinations of assent distinguished not merely in degree but even in kind), in order to present openly the state of mind in this proof in its full purity and to be able to submit it candidly to the strictest examination.

A proof, however, that is directed at conviction can in turn be of two kinds: either such a proof as would make out what the object may be ***in itself***, or else what, ***for us*** (human beings at large), it is according to the principles of reason necessary for us {{{ 463 }}} in the judging of it (a proof κατ' αληθειαν or κατ' ανθρωπον, the latter word taken in the general signification of human beings as such). In the first case its foundation lies in principles adequate for the determining power of judgment; in the second, only for the reflecting power of judgment. In this latter instance, since it rests on merely theoretical principles, it can never operate toward conviction; if, however, {{ 447 }} it makes a practical principle of reason its basis (one that consequently holds universally and necessarily), then it may fairly lay claim to a conviction adequate for a purely practical intent — that is, a moral conviction. A proof, though, ***works toward conviction*** without yet convincing, so long as it is merely led along the road toward it — that is, so long as it holds within itself none but objective grounds for the purpose, grounds which, though not yet adequate to certainty, are for all that of such a sort that they do not, as mere subjective grounds of the judgment, minister to persuasion.

Now all theoretical grounds of proof suffice either: 1) for proof through logically rigorous ***inferences of reason***; or, where this is not the case, 2) for ***inference*** by ***analogy***; or, should even this perhaps not obtain, still 3) for ***probable opinion***; or finally, which is the least, 4) for the assumption of a merely possible ground of explanation, as ***hypothesis***. — Now I assert this: that no grounds of proof whatsoever which work toward theoretical conviction are able to produce a holding-to-be-true of this kind, in any degree of it from the highest down to the lowest, if the proposition affirming the existence of an original being — a God in the signification befitting the whole import of this concept, that is, as a ***moral*** originator of the world, and hence in such fashion that by means of him the final end of creation is thereby specified — is what is to be {{ 448 }} demonstrated.

1) As regards the ***logically legitimate*** proof that advances from the universal to the particular, the Critique has established well enough that, seeing that the concept of a being which must be looked for outside nature has corresponding to it no intuition available to us — so that this concept, in so far as one would determine it theoretically by means of synthetic predicates, stays for us perpetually problematic — no cognition of such a being can occur at all (a cognition that would enlarge the compass of our theoretical knowing even slightly), and the special concept of a supersensible being admits of no subsumption whatever under the general principles governing the nature of things, in a way that would let us reason from the latter to {{{ 464 }}} it; for those principles are valid only for nature taken as an object of the senses.

2) One can, to be sure, of two heterogeneous things, precisely at the point of their heterogeneity, nonetheless ***think*** one of them by an ***analogy***[^*] with the other; but from that in which they are heterogeneous one cannot {{ 449 }} ***infer*** by analogy from the one to the other, that is, transfer this mark {{ 450 }} of the specific difference to the other. Thus I can, by analogy with the law of the equality of action and reaction in the reciprocal attraction and repulsion of {{{ 465 }}} bodies with one another, think as well the fellowship of the members of a common polity in accordance with rules of right; yet those specific determinations (material attraction or repulsion) I may not carry over to the latter and assign them to the citizens so as to make up a system that goes by the name of a state. — In the same way we are surely permitted to conceive the causality of the original being, with respect to the things of the world as natural ends, on the analogy of an understanding, taken as the source of the forms belonging to certain products that we term works of art (for this is undertaken solely on behalf of that theoretical or practical use of our faculty of cognition which we are to make of this concept, following a certain principle, with respect to the natural things in the world): yet from the fact that, among {{ 451 }} beings in the world, understanding must be attributed to the cause of an effect judged to be artificial, we may by no means conclude, on an analogy, that the very causality we observe in the human being belongs likewise, in respect of nature itself, to the being that stands wholly apart from nature: for this touches precisely upon that point of heterogeneity which, in the concept of it, is thought between a cause sensibly conditioned as regards its effects and the supersensible original being itself, and which therefore admits of no transfer to the latter. — It is exactly herein — namely, that I am to conceive the divine causality solely on the analogy of an understanding (a power we are acquainted with in no being other than the sensibly conditioned human being) — that there lies the interdict forbidding me to attribute understanding to it in the literal signification[^**].

3) ***Opining*** has no place at all in judgments _a priori_; rather, through them one either cognizes something as entirely certain, or nothing at all. Yet even granting that the grounds of proof laid down at the outset (here, for instance, the ends in the world) are empirical, one is nonetheless unable, by their means, {{ 452 }} to form any opinion reaching past the world of sense, or to concede to such rash judgments even the slightest claim to probability. For probability is a part of a certainty possible within a certain series of grounds (the grounds of it being therein compared, as parts to a whole, with what is sufficient), to which that insufficient {{{ 466 }}} ground must be capable of being supplemented. But since, as determining grounds for the certainty of one and the same judgment, these grounds have to be of the same kind — for otherwise they would not jointly make up a magnitude, which is what certainty amounts to — it is impossible that some of them should fall inside the limits of a possible experience while others fall beyond every possible experience. And so, given that purely empirical grounds of proof carry us to nothing supersensible, and given that the gap in their series can be filled by nothing whatever: any effort to reach, through them, the supersensible together with a cognition of it makes not the slightest headway, and accordingly a judgment about the latter, resting on considerations borrowed from experience, admits of no probability at all.

4) Whatever is meant to function as ***hypothesis*** in explaining how a given appearance is possible must at the very least have its possibility fully assured. It is enough that in the case of a hypothesis I renounce cognition of actuality (which in an opinion put forward as probable {{ 453 }} is still asserted); more I cannot surrender; the possibility of that which I lay at the foundation of an explanation must at least not be exposed to any doubt, since otherwise empty chimeras of the brain would know no end. Yet to suppose possible a supersensible being fixed according to certain concepts — since for this not one of the conditions required for a cognition, in respect of what within it rests upon intuition, is given, so that there remains, as the criterion of this possibility, nothing but the principle of contradiction (which is able to establish only that the thinking is possible, and not the thought object itself) — would amount to a presupposition utterly without ground.

What follows from this is: that concerning the being of the original being as a deity, or of the soul as a spirit that is immortal, there is, for human reason, absolutely no proof to be had in a theoretical respect that might produce even the smallest measure of holding-to-be-true; and this on a wholly intelligible ground — namely, that for determining the ideas of the supersensible we have no material whatever at hand, since we would be obliged to draw this material from things in the world of sense, though such material is in no way suited to that object, so that, all determination of these ideas being left aside, nothing further remains than the concept of a non-sensible something that is supposed to contain the ultimate ground of the sensible world, {{ 454 }} a concept that as yet furnishes no cognition (as an enlargement of the concept) of that being's inner constitution.

[^*]: ***Analogy*** (in the qualitative signification) consists in the sameness of the relation obtaining between grounds and their consequences (causes and effects), in so far as this holds notwithstanding the specific diversity of the things, or of those properties in themselves that harbor the ground of similar consequences (that is, regarded apart from this relation). Thus, regarding the artful operations of animals in comparison with those of the human being, we think the ground of these effects in the former — which we do not know — as an analogue of reason, by comparison with the ground of similar effects of the human being (reason), which we do know; and we mean thereby to indicate at the same time that the ground of the animal capacity for art, under the name of an instinct, is indeed specifically distinct from reason, yet bears a similar relation to the effect (the beaver's construction compared with that of humans). — For that reason, however, I cannot infer from the fact that the human being needs ***reason*** for its building that the beaver must have something of the same, and call this an ***inference*** by analogy. But when the similar manner of operation in animals (whose ground we cannot perceive immediately) is compared with that of the human being (of which we are immediately conscious), we are entitled to infer, quite rightly ***by analogy***, that animals likewise act in accordance with ***representations*** (rather than being machines, as Descartes would maintain), and that, their specific diversity notwithstanding, they are nevertheless, as regards the genus, one and the same as the human being (namely, as living beings). The principle of the entitlement to infer in this way lies in the sameness of a ground for counting animals, in respect of the said determination, as of one genus with the human being, as a human being, so far as we compare them with one another externally according to their actions. It is par ratio. Likewise I can think the causality of the supreme cause of the world, in comparing its purposive products in the world with the works of art of the human being, by analogy with an understanding, but cannot infer these properties in it by analogy: because here the very principle of the possibility of such a mode of inference is lacking, namely the _paritas rationis_ for counting the highest being together with the human being (in regard to their causality on both sides) as of one and the same genus. The causality belonging to worldly beings, forever sensibly conditioned (as the causality operating through understanding is), cannot be carried over to a being that shares with those no generic concept save that of a thing in general.
[^**]: One forfeits thereby not the slightest thing when representing how this being stands related to the world, whether as concerns the theoretical or the practical inferences that issue from this concept. To want to investigate what it may be in itself is a piece of presumption as pointless as it is futile.
