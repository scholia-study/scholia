## {{{ 425 }}} § 82. On the teleological system in the external relations of organized beings

By external purposiveness I understand that whereby one thing of nature serves another as a means to an end. Now things that have no inner purposiveness, or presuppose none for their possibility, e.g., earths, air, water, etc., can nevertheless be externally, i.e., in relation to other beings, very purposive; but these latter must {{ 380 }} always be organized beings, i.e., natural ends, for otherwise the former could not be judged as means either. Thus water, air, and earths cannot be regarded as means to the accumulation of mountains, because these in themselves contain nothing at all that would require a ground of their possibility according to ends, in relation to which therefore their cause can never be represented under the predicate of a means (that was useful for it).

External purposiveness is an entirely different concept from the concept of inner purposiveness, which is bound up with the possibility of an object regardless of whether its actuality is itself an end or not. Of an organized being one can still ask: What is it there for? but not easily of things in which one recognizes merely the effect of the mechanism of nature. For in the former we already represent to ourselves a causality according to ends for their inner possibility, a creating understanding, and we refer this active faculty to the determining ground of it, the intention. There is only a single external purposiveness that is connected with the inner purposiveness of organization and that, without the question being allowed to be raised to what end this thus-organized being just had to have existed, nevertheless serves in the external relation of a means to an end. This is the organization of both sexes {{ 381 }} in relation to one another for the propagation of their kind; for here one can still always ask, just as in the case of an individual: Why did such a pair have to exist? The answer is: this here in the first place constitutes an ***organizing*** whole, although not an organized one in a single body.

If one now asks what a thing is there for, then the answer is either: Its existence and its generation has no relation at all to a cause acting according to intentions, and then one always understands {{{ 426 }}} an origin of it from the mechanism of nature; or: There is some intentional ground of its existence (as of a contingent natural being), and this thought one can hardly separate from the concept of an organized thing: because, since once we must lay at the basis of its inner possibility a causality of final causes and an idea grounding this, we also cannot think the existence of this product otherwise than as an end. For the represented effect, the representation of which is at the same time the determining ground of the intelligent acting cause for its production, is called an ***end***. In this case, then, one can say either: The end of the existence of such a natural being is in itself, i.e., it is not merely an end, but also a ***final end***; or: This latter is outside it in other natural beings, i.e., it exists purposively not as a final end, but necessarily at the same time {{ 382 }} as a means.

If, however, we go through the whole of nature, we find in it, as nature, no being that could make claim to the prerogative of being the final end of creation; and one can even prove _a priori_: that that which might perhaps still be an ***ultimate end*** for nature could yet never, according to all conceivable determinations and properties with which one might wish to equip it, be a ***final end*** as a natural thing.

If one looks at the vegetable kingdom, one could initially, on account of the immeasurable fertility through which it spreads itself over almost every soil, be led to the thought of holding it to be a mere product of the mechanism of nature that nature displays in the formations of the mineral kingdom. But a closer acquaintance with the indescribably wise organization in it does not let us cling to this thought, but rather occasions the question: What are these creatures there for? If one answers oneself: For the animal kingdom, which is nourished thereby, so that it might be able to spread itself over the earth in so manifold a variety of species, then the question comes again: What then are these plant-consuming animals there for? The answer would be roughly: For the beasts of prey, which can nourish themselves only from that which has life. {{ 383 }} Finally the question is: What are these together with the foregoing kingdoms of nature good for? For the human being, for the manifold use which his understanding teaches him to make of all those creatures; and he is the ultimate end of creation here on earth, because he is the only being on {{{ 427 }}} it that can make for itself a concept of ends and out of an aggregate of purposively formed things can make, by its reason, a system of ends.

One could also, with the knight Linnaeus, go the apparently reverse way and say: The plant-eating animals are there in order to moderate the luxuriant growth of the plant kingdom, by which many species of it would be choked; the beasts of prey, in order to set limits to the voracity of the former; finally the human being, so that, by hunting and diminishing these, a certain equilibrium might be established among the productive and the destructive forces of nature. And thus the human being, however much in a certain relation he might be dignified as an end, would in another have in turn only the rank of a means.

If one makes an objective purposiveness in the manifold of the species of the creatures of the earth and their external relation to one another, as purposively constructed beings, into a principle: then it is in accordance with reason to think, in this relation, in turn a certain {{ 384 }} organization and a system of all the kingdoms of nature according to final causes. But here experience seems loudly to contradict the maxim of reason, chiefly with regard to an ultimate end of nature, which is yet required for the possibility of such a system, and which we can set nowhere else than in the human being: since rather, with regard to him, as one of the many animal species, nature has made the least exception, as little from the destructive as from the generative forces, in subjecting everything to a mechanism of it without an end.

The first thing that would have to be intentionally arranged in an ordering toward a purposive whole of the natural beings on the earth would surely be their dwelling place, the ground and the element on and in which they were to have their subsistence. But a more exact acquaintance with the constitution of this foundation of all organic generation gives indication of no other than entirely unintentionally acting, indeed sooner even devastating than generation-, order-, and purpose-favoring causes. Land and sea contain in themselves not only monuments of ancient mighty devastations that have befallen them and all creatures on and in them; but their whole structure, the strata of the one and the limits of the other, have entirely the appearance of the product of wild, all-mighty forces of a nature laboring in a chaotic state. {{{ 428 }}} However purposively now the figure, the structure, and the slope of the {{ 385 }} lands may seem to be arranged for the reception of the waters from the air, for the springs between strata of earth of manifold kinds (for various products), and for the course of the streams: yet a closer investigation of them proves that they came about merely as the effect partly of fiery, partly of watery eruptions, or also of upheavals of the ocean; as much with regard to the first generation of this figure as chiefly to the subsequent transformation of it together with the perishing of its first organic generations.[^*] If now the dwelling place, the maternal soil (of the land) and the maternal womb (of the sea), give for all these creatures indication of no other than an entirely unintentional mechanism of its generation: how and {{ 386 }} with what right can we demand and assert another origin for these latter products? Even if the human being, as the most exact examination of the remains of those natural devastations (according to Camper's judgment) seems to prove, was not included in these revolutions: he is yet so dependent on the rest of the creatures of the earth that, if a mechanism of nature ruling generally over the others is granted, he must be regarded as included under it; even though his understanding has (for the most part at least) been able to rescue him amid their devastations.

This argument, however, seems to prove more than the intention contained for which it was set up: namely not merely that the human being can be no ultimate end of nature and, for the same reason, that the aggregate of the organized natural things on the earth can be no system of ends; but rather that the natural products previously held to be natural ends have no other origin at all than the mechanism of nature.

{{{ 429 }}} But in the above resolution of the antinomy of the principles of the mechanical and the teleological mode of generation of organic natural beings we have seen: that, since with regard to nature, which forms in accordance with its particular laws (the key to whose systematic connection, however, we lack), they are merely principles of the reflecting power of judgment {{ 387 }} that namely do not determine the origin of these things in themselves, but only say that, according to the constitution of our understanding and our reason, we cannot think it in this kind of being otherwise than according to final causes, the greatest possible endeavor, indeed boldness in attempts to explain them mechanically is not only permitted, but we are also called to it by reason, notwithstanding that we know that we can therewith, on subjective grounds of the particular kind and limitation of our understanding (and not, say, because the mechanism of generation contradicts in itself an origin according to ends), never attain our end; and that finally, in the supersensible principle of nature (both outside us and within us), the compatibility of both ways of representing to oneself the possibility of nature may very well lie, since the manner of representation according to final causes is only a subjective condition of our use of reason, when it does not want the judging of objects undertaken merely as appearances, but rather demands to relate these appearances themselves together with their principles to the supersensible substrate, in order to find possible certain laws of the unity of them that it cannot make for itself representable otherwise than through ends (of which reason too has such as are supersensible).

[^*]: If the once-adopted name ***natural history*** is to remain for the description of nature, then one can call that which the former literally indicates, namely a representation of the former, ***ancient*** state of the earth, about which, even though one may not hope for any certainty, one yet ventures conjectures with good ground, the ***archaeology*** of ***nature***, in contrast with that of art. To the former would belong the petrifactions, just as to the latter the carved stones, etc. For since one really does continually, even if, as is fair, slowly, work at such a thing (under the name of a theory of the earth), this name would after all be given not to a merely imagined inquiry into nature, but to such a one to which nature itself invites and summons us.
