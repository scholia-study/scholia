## {{{ 425 }}} § 82. On the teleological system in the outer relations of organized beings

By external purposiveness I understand the sort in which one natural thing serves another as a means to an end. Now things that possess no inner purposiveness, nor presuppose any for their possibility—earths, air, water, and the like, for instance—can nonetheless be highly purposive externally, that is, in their relation to other beings; yet these latter must {{ 380 }} always be organized beings, that is, natural ends, since otherwise the former could not be judged to be means at all. Thus water, air, and earths cannot be regarded as means for the piling up of mountains, because these contain nothing in themselves that would require a ground of their possibility in terms of ends, in relation to which, accordingly, their cause can never be represented under the predicate of a means (one that would serve for it).

The concept of external purposiveness differs completely from that of inner purposiveness, the latter being bound up with the possibility of an object, no matter whether that object's actuality is itself an end or not. Concerning an organized being one can still raise the question: to what end does it exist? yet this is hardly possible for things in which we recognize nothing more than the working of nature's mechanism. For with the former we already picture to ourselves a causality according to ends as belonging to their inner possibility—a creating understanding—and we relate this active faculty to its determining ground, the intention. Only a single external purposiveness exists which stands in connection with the inner purposiveness of organization and which, although the question may not be posed as to what end a being thus organized had to come into existence, still performs the office, in the external relation, of a means toward an end. This constitutes the organization of both sexes {{ 381 }} in their mutual relation for the reproduction of their kind; for here, just as in the case of a single individual, one may still inquire: why was the existence of such a pair necessary? The answer is: it is precisely this that first constitutes an ***organizing*** whole, though not an organized one within a single body.

If the question is now raised as to what a thing exists for, the reply runs either thus: its being and its production bear no connection at all to a cause that operates by design, in which case its origin is invariably taken {{{ 426 }}} to arise from nature's mechanism; or thus: its being rests on some deliberate ground (as that of a contingent natural entity), a thought that can hardly be detached from the concept of an organized thing—for, seeing that we are once obliged to lay beneath its inner possibility a causality of final causes along with an idea serving as its basis, neither are we able to conceive of this product's existence in any manner other than as an end. For that effect which has been represented, and whose representation is at once the determining ground moving the intelligent efficient cause to produce it, bears the name ***end***. In this instance, accordingly, one may assert either: the end of such a natural being's existence resides within the being itself, so that it counts not solely as an end but likewise as a ***final end***; or: that end lies beyond it, within other natural beings, so that its purposive existence is not that of a final end but rather, of necessity and at the same time, {{ 382 }} that of a means.

If, however, we go through the whole of nature, we come upon no being within it, considered as nature, that could lay claim to the privilege of serving as the final end of creation; and it may even be demonstrated _a priori_ that whatever might perhaps still serve nature as a ***last end***, however it be furnished with every conceivable determination and property one could wish, still, as a natural thing, could never be a ***final end***.

When one looks at the vegetable kingdom, one might at first, on account of the immeasurable fertility by which it spreads over almost every soil, be led to the thought of taking it for a mere product of the mechanism that nature exhibits in the structures of the mineral realm. Yet a closer acquaintance with the indescribably wise organization within it does not let us cling to this thought, and instead prompts the question: for what purpose are these creatures there? If one gives oneself the answer: for the animal kingdom, which draws its nourishment therefrom and could thereby extend itself over the earth in such a diversity of genera, then the question comes back once more: for what, after all, do these plant-devouring animals exist? The answer would run roughly thus: for the beasts of prey, which can nourish themselves only on what has {{ 383 }} life. Finally the question arises: for what are these, together with the preceding kingdoms of nature, good? For the human being, toward the manifold uses which his understanding instructs him to derive from all those creatures; and he is the last end of creation here on earth, since he alone among the beings upon {{{ 427 }}} it is capable of forming for himself a concept of ends and of building, by means of his reason, a system of ends out of an aggregate of things shaped in a purposive fashion.

With the chevalier Linnaeus one might also take the seemingly opposite route and declare: the plant-eating animals exist so as to keep within bounds the rank growth of the vegetable kingdom, through which many of its species would otherwise be smothered; the beasts of prey, so as to place a check upon the greed of the former; and lastly the human being, so that, by hunting and thinning these out, a certain balance may be founded among nature's productive and destructive powers. And so the human being, however greatly he may in one respect be honored as an end, would nonetheless, in yet another respect, possess merely the standing of a means.

Should one adopt as one's principle an objective purposiveness in the manifold of the genera of the earth's creatures and in their external relation to one another; regarded as beings constructed in a purposive way, then it conforms to reason to conceive, within this same relation, once more a certain {{ 384 }} organization, together with a system that comprehends all the realms of nature according to final causes. Yet here experience seems loudly to contradict the maxim of reason, above all as regards a last end of nature, which is nonetheless requisite if such a system is to be possible, and which we are able to posit nowhere save in the human being: since, on the contrary, with respect to him, as one of the many animal species, nature has made not the slightest exception, whether among the destructive or among the generative forces, subjecting everything to a mechanism of its own without any end.

The first thing that, in an arrangement toward a purposive whole of the natural beings upon the earth, would have to be set up intentionally would presumably be their dwelling place, the ground and the element upon and within which they were to have their subsistence. Yet a more exact acquaintance with the constitution of this foundation of all organic generation points to no other causes than wholly unintentionally operating ones—indeed to causes rather more devastating than favorable to generation, order, and ends. Land and sea contain within themselves not merely the monuments of ancient mighty devastations that befell them and all the creatures upon and within them; rather their entire construction—the earth-layers of the one, the boundaries of the other—wholly bears the look of a product of the wild, all-powerful forces of a nature laboring in a chaotic condition. {{{ 428 }}} However purposively the shape, the structure, and the slope of the {{ 385 }} lands may now seem to be arranged for the reception of the waters from the air, for the springs and veins between strata of manifold kinds (for various products), and for the course of the rivers: a closer investigation of them nevertheless proves that they came about merely as the effect partly of fiery, partly of watery eruptions, or even of upheavals of the ocean; this holds both for the first generation of this shape and, above all, for its later transformation together with the perishing of its first organic productions.[^*] If, then, the dwelling place, the mother-soil (of the land) and the mother-womb (of the sea), for all these creatures points to no other than a wholly unintentional mechanism of its generation: by what means and {{ 386 }} with what right may we require and maintain some other origin for these latter products? Even though the human being, as the most exacting examination of the remains of those natural devastations (according to Camper's judgment) seems to prove, was not included in these revolutions: he is nevertheless so dependent on the rest of the earth's creatures that, if a mechanism of nature holding sway over all the others is granted, he too must be regarded as included under it; even if his understanding has been able (at least for the most part) to rescue him amid their devastations.

This argument, however, appears to establish more than lay in the aim for the sake of which it was set forth: namely, not only that the human being can constitute no last end of nature and that, for the very same reason, the assemblage of organized natural things upon the earth is incapable of forming a system of ends; but even, beyond this, that those natural products formerly taken for natural ends have no origin whatsoever besides nature's mechanism.

{{{ 429 }}} But in the foregoing resolution of the antinomy between the principles of the mechanical and the teleological manner of generating organic natural beings, this became clear to us: that, inasmuch as, with regard to nature insofar as it forms according to its own peculiar laws (whose systematic coherence, however, eludes our grasp for want of the key), those principles are nothing but principles of the reflecting power of judgment {{ 387 }}—principles which do not settle the origin of such beings as it is in itself but declare only that, such being the constitution of our understanding and our reason, we can conceive that origin in beings of this sort in no way but according to final causes—the greatest exertion, indeed audacity, in endeavors to explain them mechanically is not alone permitted, but reason itself calls us to it, however well we know that here we can never reach our aim, and this on subjective grounds lying in the special character and the bounds set to our understanding (and not, for instance, because a mechanism of generation would of itself run counter to an origin by ends); and that, at last, in the supersensible principle of nature (as much beyond us as within us) there may quite possibly reside the compatibility of the two ways of representing to ourselves nature's possibility, since representing things by way of final causes is but a subjective condition governing how we employ our reason, whenever it will not have objects judged merely as appearances but insists that these appearances, along with their own principles, be traced back to the supersensible substrate, so as to render attainable certain laws of their unity, which it can bring before itself in no other manner than through ends (among which reason likewise possesses ends that are supersensible).

[^*]: If the once-accepted name ***natural history*** is to remain in use for the description of nature, then one may give the label ***archaeology*** of ***nature***—as opposed to that of art—to what the former term literally indicates, namely a representation of the earth's bygone, ***ancient*** condition, about which, even though no certainty may be hoped for, one may still on good grounds hazard conjectures. The petrifactions would fall under the one, just as the engraved stones and so forth fall under the other. For since one really does work continually at such a thing (under the name of a theory of the earth), though, as is only fitting, slowly, this name would not be bestowed upon a merely fancied study of nature, but rather upon one to which nature herself invites and calls us.
