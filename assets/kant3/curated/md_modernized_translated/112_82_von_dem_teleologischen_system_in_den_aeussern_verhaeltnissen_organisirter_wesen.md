## {{{ 425 }}} § 82. On the teleological system in the outer relations of organized beings

By external purposiveness I understand the sort in which one natural thing serves another as a means to an end. Now things that possess no inner purposiveness, nor presuppose any for their possibility—earths, air, water, and the like, for instance—can nonetheless be highly purposive externally, that is, in their relation to other beings; yet these latter must {{ 380 }} always be organized beings, that is, natural ends, since otherwise the former could not be judged to be means at all. Thus water, air, and earths cannot be regarded as means for the piling up of mountains, because these contain nothing in themselves that would require a ground of their possibility in terms of ends, in relation to which, accordingly, their cause can never be represented under the predicate of a means (one that would serve for it).

External purposiveness is an entirely different concept from the concept of inner purposiveness, a concept that is tied to the possibility of an object, irrespective of whether its actuality is itself an end or not. Of an organized being one may still ask: for what purpose is it there? but not readily of things in which one discerns merely the effect of the mechanism of nature. For in the former we already represent to ourselves a causality in accordance with ends for their inner possibility—a creative understanding—and we relate this active power to what determines it, namely intention. There is only one single external purposiveness that is connected with the inner purposiveness of organization and that, without its being permitted to raise the question toward what end this being so organized had to exist, nonetheless serves, in the outer relation of a means to an end. This is the organization of the two sexes {{ 381 }} in relation to each other for the propagation of their kind; for here one can still ask, just as with an individual: why did such a pair have to exist? The answer is: it is precisely this that first constitutes an ***organizing*** whole, though not an organized one within a single body.

If one now asks for what purpose a thing exists, the answer is either: its existence and its generation stand in no relation whatever to a cause acting according to intentions, and in that case one always understands {{{ 426 }}} its origin to lie in the mechanism of nature; or: there is some intentional ground of its existence (as of a contingent natural being), and this thought can scarcely be separated from the concept of an organized thing: because, since we must once and for all place beneath its inner possibility a causality of final causes together with an idea underlying this causality, we are likewise unable to conceive the existence of this product otherwise than as an end. For the represented effect, whose representation is simultaneously what determines the intelligent efficient cause to bring it forth, is called an ***end***. In this case, then, one can say either: the end for which such a natural being exists lies in the being itself, that is, it is not merely an end but also a ***final end***; or: this end lies outside it, in other natural beings, that is, it exists purposively not as a final end but necessarily, at the same time, {{ 382 }} as a means.

But when we survey nature in its entirety, we discover in it, taken as nature, no being able to lay claim to the distinction of being the final end of creation; and one can even prove _a priori_: that whatever might yet count as a ***last end*** for nature, whatever conceivable determinations and properties one might wish to equip it with, could nevertheless, being a natural thing, never be a ***final end***.

When one looks at the vegetable kingdom, one might at first, on account of the immeasurable fertility by which it spreads over almost every soil, be led to the thought of taking it for a mere product of the mechanism that nature exhibits in the structures of the mineral realm. Yet a closer acquaintance with the indescribably wise organization within it does not let us cling to this thought, and instead prompts the question: for what purpose are these creatures there? If one answers oneself: for the sake of the animal kingdom, which is nourished by it so that it might have spread across the earth in such manifold genera, then the question returns: for what purpose, then, are these plant-consuming animals there? The answer would run roughly thus: for the beasts of prey, which can nourish themselves only on what has {{ 383 }} life. Finally the question arises: for what are these, together with the preceding kingdoms of nature, good? For the human being, toward the manifold uses which his understanding instructs him to derive from all those creatures; and he is the last end of creation here on earth, since he alone among the beings upon {{{ 427 }}} it is capable of forming for himself a concept of ends and of building, by means of his reason, a system of ends out of an aggregate of things shaped in a purposive fashion.

One could also, with the chevalier Linnaeus, take the apparently reverse path and say: the plant-eating animals are there in order to moderate the luxuriant growth of the plant kingdom, by which many of its species would be choked out; the beasts of prey, in order to set limits to the voracity of the former; finally the human being, so that, by pursuing and reducing these, a certain equilibrium among the productive and the destructive forces of nature might be established. And thus the human being, however much he might be dignified as an end in a certain respect, would in another respect again hold only the rank of a means.

If one makes into a principle an objective purposiveness in the manifold of the genera of the earth's creatures and in their outer relation to one another; as beings purposively constructed, then it accords with reason to think, within this relation, once again a certain {{ 384 }} organization and a system of all the kingdoms of nature according to final causes. Yet here experience seems loudly to contradict the maxim of reason, above all as regards a last end of nature, which is nonetheless requisite if such a system is to be possible, and which we are able to posit nowhere save in the human being: since, on the contrary, with respect to him, as one of the many animal species, nature has made not the slightest exception, whether among the destructive or among the generative forces, subjecting everything to a mechanism of its own without any end.

The first thing that, in an arrangement toward a purposive whole of the natural beings upon the earth, would have to be set up intentionally would presumably be their dwelling place, the ground and the element upon and within which they were to have their subsistence. Yet a more exact acquaintance with the constitution of this foundation of all organic generation points to no other causes than wholly unintentionally operating ones—indeed to causes rather more devastating than favorable to generation, order, and ends. Land and sea contain within themselves not merely the monuments of ancient mighty devastations that befell them and all the creatures upon and within them; rather their entire construction—the earth-layers of the one, the boundaries of the other—wholly bears the look of a product of the wild, all-powerful forces of a nature laboring in a chaotic condition. {{{ 428 }}} However purposively the shape, the structure, and the slope of the {{ 385 }} lands may now seem to be arranged for the reception of the waters from the air, for the springs and veins between strata of manifold kinds (for various products), and for the course of the rivers: a closer investigation of them nevertheless proves that they came about merely as the effect partly of fiery, partly of watery eruptions, or even of upheavals of the ocean; this holds both for the first generation of this shape and, above all, for its later transformation together with the perishing of its first organic productions.[^*] If, then, the dwelling place, the mother-soil (of the land) and the mother-womb (of the sea), for all these creatures points to no other than a wholly unintentional mechanism of its generation: by what means and {{ 386 }} with what right may we require and maintain some other origin for these latter products? Even though the human being, as the most exacting examination of the remains of those natural devastations (according to Camper's judgment) seems to prove, was not included in these revolutions: he is nevertheless so dependent on the rest of the earth's creatures that, if a mechanism of nature holding sway over all the others is granted, he too must be regarded as included under it; even if his understanding has been able (at least for the most part) to rescue him amid their devastations.

This argument, however, seems to prove more than the intention for which it was advanced contained: namely, not merely that the human being can be no last end of nature and that, on the same ground, the aggregate of organized natural things upon the earth cannot be a system of ends; but rather that even those natural products previously held to be natural ends possess no origin other than the mechanism of nature.

{{{ 429 }}} Yet in resolving above the antinomy between the principles governing the mechanical and the teleological modes of generation of organic natural beings we have seen: that, since these principles—with respect to nature as it forms according to its particular laws (to whose systematic interconnection, however, we lack the key)—are merely principles of the reflecting power of judgment {{ 387 }} that do not determine the origin of such beings in itself, but say only that, given how our understanding and our reason are constituted, we cannot think that origin in this kind of being otherwise than according to final causes, the utmost endeavor—indeed boldness—in the attempt to give a mechanical explanation of them is not merely permitted; rather, reason itself summons us to it, notwithstanding our knowing that we can thereby—on subjective grounds of the particular character and limitation of our understanding (and not, say, because the mechanism of generation would in itself contradict an origin according to ends)—never suffice; and that, finally, in the supersensible principle of nature (both outside us and within us) there may well lie the reconcilability of both modes of representing to ourselves the possibility of nature, inasmuch as the mode of representation according to final causes is only a subjective condition of our use of reason, when it wants the judging of objects to be undertaken not merely as appearances, but rather demands that these appearances themselves, together with their principles, be referred to the supersensible substrate, in order to find possible certain laws of their unity that it cannot make representable to itself otherwise than through ends (of which reason also has such as are supersensible).

[^*]: Should the name ***natural history***, once it has been adopted, be retained for the description of nature, then one may call what the former literally signifies—namely a representation of the former, ***ancient*** state of the earth, concerning which, though one may hope for no certainty, one may nevertheless with good ground venture conjectures—the ***archaeology*** of ***nature***, in contrast to that of art. To the former would belong the petrifactions, just as to the latter belong the engraved stones and the like. For since one really does work continually at such a thing (under the name of a theory of the earth), though, as is only fitting, slowly, this name would not be bestowed upon a merely fancied study of nature, but rather upon one to which nature herself invites and calls us.
