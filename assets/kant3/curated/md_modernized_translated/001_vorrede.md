## {{{ 167 }}} {{ III }} Preface

The capacity for cognition from _a priori_ principles may be termed ***pure reason***, while the investigation of its possibility and of its boundaries generally may be called the critique of pure reason: even though by this capacity one has in view only reason in its theoretical use, as was also done in the first work under that designation, without yet meaning to bring its capacity as practical reason, governed by its own special principles, under examination. That first inquiry then addresses nothing beyond our power to cognize things _a priori_, occupying itself for that reason with the ***faculty of cognition*** alone, and setting aside both the faculty of desire and the feeling of pleasure and displeasure; and within the cognitive faculties it takes up the ***understanding*** in accordance with its _a priori_ principles, shutting out the ***power of judgment*** together with ***reason*** (as {{ IV }} faculties that belong no less to theoretical cognition), for as one proceeds it turns out that, apart from the understanding, no faculty of cognition is able to furnish constitutive principles of cognition _a priori_. This critique, then, which sifts them all according to the share that each of the others might claim to hold, out of its own root, in the bare possession of cognition, leaves nothing over except what the ***understanding*** lays down _a priori_ as the law for nature, taken as the sum total of appearances (whose form is likewise given _a priori_); yet it consigns all the other pure concepts to the ideas, which, though extravagant for our theoretical faculty of cognition, are nevertheless not on that account useless or dispensable, but serve as regulative principles: partly to hold in check the worrisome pretensions of the understanding, as though it (by being competent to state _a priori_ the conditions under which all the things it can cognize are possible {{{ 168 }}}) had thereby also enclosed the possibility of all things whatever within these bounds, partly to guide it, in its contemplation of nature, by a principle of completeness, even though {{ V }} it can never attain that completeness, and thereby to advance the ultimate aim of all cognition.

It was thus, properly speaking, the ***understanding*** — which has its own domain, and indeed within the ***faculty of cognition***, insofar as it harbors constitutive cognitive principles _a priori_ within itself — that was to be put into secure and exclusive possession against all other claimants by the critique commonly named the critique of pure reason. In just the same way, ***reason***, which nowhere contains constitutive principles _a priori_ except solely with respect to the ***faculty of desire***, was assigned its possession in the critique of practical reason.

Now whether the ***power of judgment***, which, within the ordering of our cognitive faculties, constitutes a middle member between understanding and reason, likewise has principles _a priori_ of its own; whether these are constitutive or merely regulative (and hence prove no domain of its own); and whether, to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure — as the middle member lying between the faculty of cognition, on the one side, and the faculty of desire, on the other (in just the way that understanding prescribes _a priori_ laws for the former and reason for the latter) {{ VI }} — it gives the rule _a priori_: that is what the present critique of the power of judgment concerns itself with.

A critique of pure reason, that is, of our capacity to pass judgment by _a priori_ principles, would stay incomplete were the critique belonging to the power of judgment — which, being itself a cognitive faculty, raises a like claim to such principles — not dealt with as a special part of that whole; even though, within a system of pure philosophy, its principles are not entitled to form any separate part standing between the theoretical and the practical, and may instead, should the need arise, be appended on occasion to either of the two. For should such a system ever come to be established one day under the general name of metaphysics (a thing that is possible to accomplish in wholly complete form, and, for the use of reason in every relation, of the utmost importance): then the critique must first have explored the ground for this edifice down as deep as the first foundation of our capacity for principles independent of experience lies, so that it may not subside at any part, which would inevitably bring on the collapse of the whole. {{{ 169 }}}

Yet from the very nature of the power of judgment (whose proper exercise {{ VII }} is so necessary, and demanded so universally, that under the name of sound understanding nothing other than just this faculty is intended), one can easily infer that finding a principle peculiar to it must be attended by great difficulties (for some principle it has to hold _a priori_ within itself, since without one it would not, as a cognitive faculty of its own kind, lie open even to the commonest criticism), and yet such a principle need not be drawn from _a priori_ concepts; for concepts of that sort are the understanding's own, while the power of judgment bears solely on their application. Judgment is thus itself to put forward a concept through which, strictly speaking, nothing is cognized, one that furnishes a rule for itself alone — though not an objective rule to which it might adjust its judgment, for that would once more call for a further power of judgment, so as to make out whether or not a given case comes under the rule.

This embarrassment over a principle (whether it be a subjective or an objective one) is met with above all in those judgings termed aesthetic, whose object is the beautiful and the sublime, be it in nature or in {{ VIII }} art. And nonetheless it is precisely in these judgings that the critical inquiry into a principle of the power of judgment forms the most important portion of any critique of this faculty. For although, taken on their own, such judgings add nothing whatever to our cognition of things, they belong for all that to the faculty of cognition alone, and they exhibit how this faculty stands in a direct relation, on some _a priori_ principle, to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, without confusing that feeling with anything capable of serving as a determining ground of the faculty of desire, seeing that the latter draws its own _a priori_ principles from concepts of reason. — When it comes, however, to the logical judging of nature — there where experience establishes in things a lawfulness that the general concept of the sensible furnished by the understanding no longer reaches far enough to grasp or to explain, and where the power of judgment can draw from within itself a principle for relating the natural thing to the supersensible that lies beyond cognition, a principle it must, moreover, employ only with reference to itself for the cognition of nature — there such an _a priori_ principle can indeed, and even must, be applied to the ***cognition*** of the world's beings, laying open at the same time {{ IX }} vistas favorable to practical reason; but toward the feeling of pleasure and displeasure it bears no immediate relation — which is exactly the puzzling thing about the principle of the power of judgment, and which is why the critique cannot but set apart a division of its own for this faculty {{{ 170 }}}, since judging logically by concepts (from which a direct consequence for the feeling of pleasure and displeasure can never be drawn) could at most have been attached to philosophy's theoretical part together with a critical restriction of it.

Because the present examination of the faculty of taste — that is, of the aesthetic power of judgment — is conducted here not in order to form and cultivate taste (which will keep to its course henceforth, as it has up to now, quite apart from all such investigations) but with a purely transcendental purpose, it will, or so I flatter myself, be treated indulgently in view of the shortcomings that attach to that former aim. But as concerns the latter aim, it must brace itself for the strictest scrutiny. But there too, as I trust, the sheer difficulty of unraveling a problem that nature has knotted so intricately may excuse a measure of not wholly avoidable obscurity {{ X }} in the solution of it — provided at least that the correct statement of the principle has been made plain enough; even granting that the way of deriving the phenomenon of the power of judgment from it may want all that distinctness which one is entitled to demand elsewhere, namely from cognition proceeding by concepts, a distinctness that I take myself to have attained in the second part of this work as well.

With this, then, I bring my entire critical undertaking to a close. Without delay I shall move on to the doctrinal one, in order, if possible, to wrest from my advancing years what time may still be somewhat favorable to that end. It is self-evident that no special part for the power of judgment is contained therein, seeing that, as regards that faculty, critique does the work of theory; the point is rather that, once philosophy is partitioned into a theoretical and a practical branch, and pure philosophy into just those branches, it is the metaphysics of nature together with that of morals that will constitute the undertaking in question.
