## {{{ 167 }}} {{ III }} Preface

The capacity for cognition from _a priori_ principles may be termed ***pure reason***, while the investigation of its possibility and of its boundaries generally may be called the critique of pure reason: even though by this capacity one has in view only reason in its theoretical use, as was also done in the first work under that designation, without yet meaning to bring its capacity as practical reason, governed by its own special principles, under examination. That inquiry then bears solely on our capacity to cognize things _a priori_, and so it concerns itself only with the ***faculty of cognition***, while leaving aside the feeling of pleasure and displeasure and the faculty of desire; and, among the cognitive faculties, with the ***understanding*** according to its _a priori_ principles, to the exclusion of the ***power of judgment*** and of ***reason*** (as {{ IV }} faculties that likewise belong to theoretical cognition), because it emerges in the course of things that no cognitive faculty save the understanding can place constitutive principles of cognition into our hands _a priori_. This critique, then, which sifts them all according to the share that each of the others might claim to hold, out of its own root, in the bare possession of cognition, leaves nothing over except what the ***understanding*** lays down _a priori_ as the law for nature, taken as the sum total of appearances (whose form is likewise given _a priori_); yet it consigns all the other pure concepts to the ideas, which, though extravagant for our theoretical faculty of cognition, are nevertheless not on that account useless or dispensable, but serve as regulative principles: partly to hold in check the worrisome pretensions of the understanding, as though it (by being competent to state _a priori_ the conditions under which all the things it can cognize are possible {{{ 168 }}}) had thereby also enclosed the possibility of all things whatever within these bounds, partly to guide it, in its contemplation of nature, by a principle of completeness, even though {{ V }} it can never attain that completeness, and thereby to advance the ultimate aim of all cognition.

It was thus, properly speaking, the ***understanding*** — which has its own domain, and indeed within the ***faculty of cognition***, insofar as it harbors constitutive cognitive principles _a priori_ within itself — that was to be put into secure and exclusive possession against all other claimants by the critique commonly named the critique of pure reason. In just the same way, ***reason***, which nowhere contains constitutive principles _a priori_ except solely with respect to the ***faculty of desire***, was assigned its possession in the critique of practical reason.

Now whether the ***power of judgment***, which, within the ordering of our cognitive faculties, constitutes a middle member between understanding and reason, likewise has principles _a priori_ of its own; whether these are constitutive or merely regulative (and hence prove no domain of its own); and whether, to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure — as the middle member lying between the faculty of cognition, on the one side, and the faculty of desire, on the other (in just the way that understanding prescribes _a priori_ laws for the former and reason for the latter) {{ VI }} — it gives the rule _a priori_: that is what the present critique of the power of judgment concerns itself with.

A critique of pure reason, that is, of our capacity to pass judgment by _a priori_ principles, would stay incomplete were the critique belonging to the power of judgment — which, being itself a cognitive faculty, raises a like claim to such principles — not dealt with as a special part of that whole; even though, within a system of pure philosophy, its principles are not entitled to form any separate part standing between the theoretical and the practical, and may instead, should the need arise, be appended on occasion to either of the two. For should such a system ever come to be established one day under the general name of metaphysics (a thing that is possible to accomplish in wholly complete form, and, for the use of reason in every relation, of the utmost importance): then the critique must first have explored the ground for this edifice down as deep as the first foundation of our capacity for principles independent of experience lies, so that it may not subside at any part, which would inevitably bring on the collapse of the whole. {{{ 169 }}}

From the very nature of the power of judgment, however (whose correct use {{ VII }} is so necessary and so universally required that it is precisely this faculty, and no other, that is meant under the name of sound understanding), one may readily gather that great difficulties must attend the discovery of a principle proper to it (for some such principle it must contain _a priori_ within itself, since otherwise it would not, as a distinct cognitive faculty, be exposed even to the most ordinary criticism), a principle which nonetheless need not be derived from concepts _a priori_; for these belong to the understanding, and the power of judgment is concerned only with their application. It is therefore to supply of itself a concept by which no thing is properly cognized, but which serves only itself as a rule — yet not as an objective one, to which it could conform its judgment, since for that a further power of judgment would in turn be required, in order to distinguish whether or not the case falls under the rule.

This perplexity about a principle (be it subjective or objective) shows itself chiefly in those acts of judging that one calls aesthetic, which concern the beautiful and the sublime in nature or in {{ VIII }} art. And yet the critical investigation of a principle of the power of judgment in these very acts is the most important piece of a critique of this faculty. For although such judgings contribute, taken by themselves alone, nothing at all to the cognition of things, they nevertheless belong to the faculty of cognition alone and demonstrate an immediate relation of this faculty to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure according to some principle _a priori_, without conflating it with what can be a determining ground of the faculty of desire, since the latter holds its own _a priori_ principles within concepts of reason. — As regards, however, the logical judging of nature — there where experience sets up a lawfulness in things which the general concept of the sensible, as furnished by the understanding, no longer suffices to grasp or to explain, and where the power of judgment can draw from itself a principle by which the natural thing is related to the supersensible that cannot be cognized, though it must also use this principle only with a view to itself for the cognition of nature — there such an _a priori_ principle can, and indeed must, be brought to bear on the ***cognition*** of the world's beings, and at the same time opens up {{ IX }} prospects that are advantageous for practical reason: but it has no immediate relation to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, which is precisely the enigmatic feature in the principle of the power of judgment that makes a separate division within the critique for this faculty necessary {{{ 170 }}}, seeing that the logical judging by concepts (out of which no immediate inference to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure can ever be drawn) could at most have been appended to the theoretical part of philosophy together with a critical delimitation of the same.

Since the investigation of the faculty of taste, as the aesthetic power of judgment, is undertaken here not for the formation and cultivation of taste (for this will continue on its way hereafter, as it has hitherto, without any such inquiries) but merely with a transcendental aim: it will, I flatter myself, be judged with indulgence in regard to the deficiency of that former purpose. But as concerns the latter aim, it must brace itself for the strictest scrutiny. Yet even there the great difficulty of resolving a problem that nature has entangled so intricately may, I hope, serve as an excuse for a certain not wholly avoidable obscurity {{ X }} in its resolution, provided only that it has been shown clearly enough that the principle has been correctly stated; granting that the manner of deriving the phenomenon of the power of judgment from it may lack all the distinctness that one may rightly demand elsewhere, namely from a cognition according to concepts — a distinctness that I believe I have in fact attained in the second part of this work.

With this, then, I bring my entire critical undertaking to a close. Without delay I shall move on to the doctrinal one, in order, if possible, to wrest from my advancing years what time may still be somewhat favorable to that end. It goes without saying that within it there is no separate part for the power of judgment, since with respect to that faculty critique takes the place of theory; but rather that, following the division of philosophy into the theoretical and the practical, and of pure philosophy into just such parts, the metaphysics of nature and that of morals will make up that undertaking.
