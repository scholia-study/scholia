## {{{ 167 }}} {{ III }} Preface

One can call the faculty of cognition from principles _a priori_ ***pure reason*** and the investigation of the possibility and bounds of this faculty in general the critique of pure reason: even though one understands by this faculty only reason in its theoretical use, as also happened in the first work under that designation, without yet wanting to bring into investigation its faculty as practical reason according to its special principles. That critique then concerns merely our faculty of cognizing things _a priori_, and thus occupies itself only with the ***faculty of cognition*** to the exclusion of the feeling of pleasure and displeasure and of the faculty of desire; and among the faculties of cognition with the ***understanding*** according to its principles _a priori_ to the exclusion of the ***power of judgment*** and ***reason*** (as faculties belonging {{ IV }} likewise to theoretical cognition), because it turns out in the course of the inquiry that no other faculty of cognition than the understanding can furnish constitutive principles of cognition _a priori_. The critique, then, which sifts them all together according to the share that each of the others might claim to have in the bare possession of cognition from its own root, leaves over nothing but what the ***understanding*** prescribes _a priori_ as law for nature, as the sum total of appearances (whose form is likewise given _a priori_); but it consigns all other pure concepts to the ideas, which are transcendent for our theoretical faculty of cognition yet are not on that account useless or dispensable, but rather serve as regulative principles: partly to hold in check the worrisome pretensions of the understanding, as if (by being able to specify _a priori_ the conditions of the possibility of all things that it can cognize) {{{ 168 }}} it had thereby also enclosed within these bounds the possibility of all things in general, partly to guide the understanding itself in the consideration of nature according to a principle of completeness, although {{ V }} it can never attain this, and thereby to promote the final aim of all cognition.

It was thus properly the ***understanding***, which has its own domain, namely in the ***faculty of cognition***, insofar as it contains constitutive principles of cognition _a priori_, that was to be set into secure and sole possession against all other competitors by the critique of pure reason so named in general. In the same way ***reason***, which nowhere contains constitutive principles _a priori_ except solely with respect to the ***faculty of desire***, was assigned its possession in the critique of practical reason.

Whether now the ***power of judgment***, which in the order of our faculties of cognition constitutes a middle term between understanding and reason, also has principles _a priori_ for itself; whether these are constitutive or merely regulative (and so prove no proper domain of their own), and whether it gives the rule _a priori_ to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, as the middle term between the faculty of cognition and the faculty of desire (just as the understanding prescribes laws _a priori_ to the former, but reason to the latter): {{ VI }} that is what the present critique of the power of judgment occupies itself with.

A critique of pure reason, i.e., of our faculty for judging according to principles _a priori_, would be incomplete if that of the power of judgment, which itself lays claim to this as a faculty of cognition, were not treated as a special part of it; although its principles may not constitute a special part in a system of pure philosophy between the theoretical and the practical, but can in case of need be attached occasionally to either of the two. For if such a system is one day to come into being under the general name of metaphysics (which it is possible to accomplish quite completely and which is in every respect highly important for the use of reason): then the critique must previously have explored the ground for this edifice as deeply as the first foundation of the faculty of principles independent of experience lies, so that it may not sink at any part, which would inevitably entail the collapse of the whole. {{{ 169 }}}

But one can readily gather from the nature of the power of judgment (whose correct use {{ VII }} is so necessary and universally required that under the name of sound understanding no other than precisely this faculty is meant) that it must be attended with great difficulties to discover a peculiar principle of it (for some such principle it must contain _a priori_ in itself, since otherwise it would not, as a special faculty of cognition, be exposed even to the commonest critique), which nevertheless must not be derived from concepts _a priori_; for those belong to the understanding, and the power of judgment concerns only the application of them. It is therefore itself to provide a concept through which no thing is properly cognized, but which serves only to itself as a rule, yet not as an objective one to which it can adapt its judgment, because for that yet another power of judgment would be required, in order to be able to distinguish whether it is the case for the rule or not.

This perplexity about a principle (whether it be a subjective or an objective one) is found chiefly in those acts of appraisal that one calls aesthetic, which concern the beautiful and the sublime of nature or of {{ VIII }} art. And nevertheless the critical investigation of a principle of the power of judgment in these is the most important piece of a critique of this faculty. For although for themselves alone they contribute nothing at all to the cognition of things, they yet belong to the faculty of cognition alone and prove an immediate relation of this faculty to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure according to some principle _a priori_, without confounding it with that which can be a determining ground of the faculty of desire, because the latter has its principles _a priori_ in concepts of reason. — But as far as concerns the logical appraisal of nature, there, where experience sets up a lawfulness in things which the universal concept of the understanding regarding the sensible no longer suffices to understand or to explain, and where the power of judgment can take from itself a principle of the relation of the thing of nature to the unknowable supersensible, though it must use this only with regard to itself for the cognition of nature, there such a principle _a priori_ can and must indeed be applied to the ***cognition*** of the beings of the world and at the same time opens up {{ IX }} prospects that are advantageous for practical reason: but it has no immediate relation to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, which is precisely the puzzling thing in the principle of the power of judgment, which makes a special division in the critique necessary {{{ 170 }}} for this faculty, since the logical appraisal according to concepts (from which an immediate inference to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure can never be drawn) could at most have been appended to the theoretical part of philosophy together with a critical restriction of it.

Since the investigation of the faculty of taste, as the aesthetic power of judgment, is here undertaken not for the formation and culture of taste (for this will continue, now as before, to take its course even without all such inquiries), but merely in a transcendental respect: it will, as I flatter myself, also be judged with indulgence in respect of its deficiency for that purpose. But as far as the latter aim is concerned, it must prepare itself for the strictest examination. Yet even there the great difficulty of resolving a problem that nature has made so entangled may, as I hope, serve as an excuse for some not entirely avoidable obscurity {{ X }} in its resolution, if only it has been shown clearly enough that the principle has been correctly stated; granted that the manner of deriving from it the phenomenon of the power of judgment does not have all the distinctness that one can rightly demand elsewhere, namely from a cognition according to concepts, which I also believe to have attained in the second part of this work.

With this, then, I bring my entire critical enterprise to an end. I will proceed without delay to the doctrinal one, in order, if possible, to wrest from my increasing age the time still somewhat favorable for it. It is self-evident that there is no special part for the power of judgment in it, because with respect to it the critique serves instead of theory; rather that, according to the division of philosophy into the theoretical and the practical and of pure philosophy into just such parts, the metaphysics of nature and that of morals will constitute that enterprise.
