## § 22. The necessity of the universal assent that is thought in a judgment of taste is a subjective necessity that is represented as objective under the presupposition of a common sense

In all judgments by which we declare something to be beautiful we allow no one to be of another opinion; without, however, grounding our judgment on {{ 67 }} concepts, but only on our feeling: which we therefore lay at the ground not as a private feeling, but as a communal one. Now this common sense cannot, for this purpose, be grounded on experience; for it would justify judgments that contain an ought: it does not say that everyone ***will*** agree with our judgment, but that everyone ***ought*** to agree with it. Thus the common sense, of whose judgment I here give my judgment of taste as an example and on account of which I ascribe ***exemplary*** validity to it, is a mere ideal norm, under the presupposition of which one could rightly make a judgment that agreed with it, and the satisfaction in an object expressed in it, into a rule for everyone: because the principle, although only subjective, yet, assumed as subjectively universal (an idea necessary for everyone), could, as concerns the unanimity of different judging persons, demand universal assent like an objective one; if only one were certain of having correctly subsumed under it.

This indeterminate norm of a common sense is actually presupposed by us: this is proved by our presumption in making judgments of taste. {{{ 240 }}} Whether there in fact is such a common sense as a constitutive principle of the possibility of experience, or whether a still higher principle of reason makes it only a regulative principle for us, first to bring forth a common sense {{ 68 }} in us for higher ends; whether, therefore, taste is an original and natural faculty, or only the idea of a faculty still to be acquired and artificial, so that a judgment of taste, with its expectation of a universal assent, is in fact only a demand of reason to bring forth such a unanimity of the manner of sensing, and the ought, i.e., the objective necessity of the confluence of the feeling of everyone with that of each one in particular, signifies only the possibility of becoming unanimous in this, and the judgment of taste only sets up an example of the application of this principle: this we neither will nor can investigate here, but for now we have only to resolve the faculty of taste into its elements and at the end to unite them in the idea of a common sense.

Explanation of the beautiful inferred from the fourth moment.

***Beautiful*** is that which is cognized without a concept as the object of a ***necessary*** satisfaction.
