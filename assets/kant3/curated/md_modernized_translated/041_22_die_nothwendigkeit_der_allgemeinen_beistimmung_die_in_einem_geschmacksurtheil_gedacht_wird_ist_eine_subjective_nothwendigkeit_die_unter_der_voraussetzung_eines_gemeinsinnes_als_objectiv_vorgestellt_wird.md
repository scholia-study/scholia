## § 22. The necessity of the universal assent that is thought in a judgment of taste is a subjective necessity that, under the presupposition of a common sense, is represented as objective

In all judgments by which we declare something to be beautiful, we permit no one to hold a differing opinion; yet without basing our judgment upon {{ 67 }} concepts, resting it solely on our feeling: which we therefore take as a ground not as a private feeling but as a communal one. Now for this purpose this common sense admits of no grounding in experience; for it aims to justify judgments that carry an ought within them: it asserts not that everyone ***will*** concur with our judgment, but that everyone ***ought*** to fall in with it. Thus common sense, of whose judgment I here present my judgment of taste as an example, and by reason of which I attach ***exemplary*** validity to it, is a merely ideal norm, under whose presupposition one could rightly make a judgment that accorded with it, together with the satisfaction in an object expressed therein, into a rule for everyone: because the principle, though only subjective, yet, taken as subjectively universal (an idea necessary for everyone), could, as regards the unanimity of different persons who judge, demand universal assent just as an objective one would; provided only that one were assured of having subsumed correctly beneath it.

This indeterminate norm of a common sense is indeed presupposed by us: this is attested by our presumption in pronouncing judgments of taste. {{{ 240 }}} Whether there genuinely exists such a common sense, serving as a constitutive principle for the possibility of experience, or whether instead some yet higher principle of reason renders it for us merely a regulative principle, so as first to bring forth in us a common sense {{ 68 }} for higher ends; whether taste, accordingly, is an original and natural capacity, or only the idea of one still to be acquired and artificial, such that a judgment of taste, with its expectation of a universal assent, is really nothing but a demand of reason to generate such a unanimity in the mode of sensing, where the ought, i.e., the objective necessity that everyone's feeling flow together with that particular to each, signifies merely the possibility of arriving at accord herein, and the judgment of taste merely provides an instance in which this principle is applied: this we can and will not yet inquire into here, but have for now only to break the faculty of taste down into its elements and ultimately to unite them within the idea of a common sense.

The beautiful, explained by inference from the fourth moment.

***Beautiful*** is what is cognized, without a concept, as the object of a ***necessary*** satisfaction.
