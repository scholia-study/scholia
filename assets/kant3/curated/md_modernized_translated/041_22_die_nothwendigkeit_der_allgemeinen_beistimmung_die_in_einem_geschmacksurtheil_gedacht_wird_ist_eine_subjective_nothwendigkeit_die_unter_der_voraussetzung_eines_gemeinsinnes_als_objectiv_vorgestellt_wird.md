## § 22. The necessity of the universal agreement that a judgment of taste entails is a subjective necessity which, where a common sense is presupposed, is represented as objective

Whenever we pronounce a thing to be beautiful, we grant to no one the right to be of a different mind; yet without thereby grounding our judgment upon {{ 67 }} concepts, but solely upon our feeling: which we accordingly take as our basis not as a private feeling but as one held in common. Now, to this end, this common sense cannot be founded upon experience; for it seeks to authorize judgments that contain an ought: it does not say that each person ***will*** agree with our judgment, but rather that each ***ought*** to fall into accord with it. Common sense, therefore—the sense from whose judgment I here offer my judgment of taste by way of example, and on whose account I ascribe ***exemplary*** validity to that judgment—is a mere ideal norm; presupposing it, one could with right make a judgment that agreed with it, and the satisfaction in an object expressed in that judgment, into a rule for everyone: since the principle, subjective though it is, would nevertheless, taken for subjectively universal (an idea necessary to everyone), be entitled, so far as concerns unanimity among different judging subjects, to demand universal assent in the manner of an objective principle; provided one were only certain of having subsumed under it correctly.

We really do presuppose this indeterminate norm of a common sense: the proof lies in our presumption to pass judgments of taste. {{{ 240 }}} Whether a common sense of this kind actually exists as a constitutive principle rendering experience possible, or whether a yet higher principle of reason turns it for us into a merely regulative one, whose task would be first of all to bring forth a common sense {{ 68 }} in us for higher purposes; whether taste is accordingly an original and natural faculty, or only the idea of an artificial one that has still to be acquired, so that a judgment of taste, with its claim to a universal assent, would in fact be only a demand made by reason to bring forth such unanimity in the way of sensing, and the ought—the objective necessity, namely, of everyone's feeling flowing together with each person's particular feeling—would mean no more than the possibility of reaching accord in this respect, and the judgment of taste would supply merely one example of how this principle is applied: all this we neither wish nor are yet able to investigate here; for the present we have only to resolve into its elements the faculty of taste, and at last to unite these in the idea of a common sense.

The beautiful, explained by inference from the fourth moment.

***Beautiful*** is that which, without a concept, is cognized to be the object of a ***necessary*** satisfaction.
