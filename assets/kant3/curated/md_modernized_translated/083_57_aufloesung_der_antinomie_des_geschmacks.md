## § 57. Resolution of the Antinomy of Taste

There is no possibility of resolving the conflict of those principles underlying every judgment of taste (which are nothing other than the two peculiarities of the judgment of taste presented above in the Analytic), except by showing: that the concept to which one refers the object in this kind of judgment is not taken in the same sense in both maxims of the aesthetic power of judgment; that this dual sense or point of view of judging is necessary for our transcendental power of judgment; but also that the illusion in the mixing of the one with the other, as a natural illusion, is unavoidable.

The judgment of taste must refer to some concept; for otherwise it could absolutely not claim necessary validity for {{ 235 }} everyone. But it must not be provable ***from*** a concept on that account, because a concept can be either determinable, or else in itself undetermined and at the same time undeterminable. Of the former kind is the concept of the understanding, which is determinable through predicates of sensible intuition that can correspond to it; of the second kind, however, is the transcendental concept of reason of the supersensible, which lies at the basis of all that intuition, and which therefore cannot be further determined theoretically.

Now the judgment of taste does indeed go to objects of the senses, but not in order to determine a ***concept*** of them for the understanding; for it is not a judgment of cognition. It is therefore, as an intuitive singular representation related to the feeling of pleasure, only a private judgment: and to that extent it would, as regards its validity, be limited to the judging individual alone: the object is ***for me*** an object of liking, for others it may be otherwise; — everyone has his own taste.

Nevertheless, there is without doubt contained in the judgment of taste an extended relation of the representation of the object (and at the same time of the subject), on which we ground an extension of this kind of judgment as necessary for everyone: at the basis of which, therefore, some concept must {{{ 340 }}} necessarily lie; but a concept that cannot be determined by intuition at all, {{ 236 }} through which nothing can be cognized, and hence through which ***no proof*** for the judgment of taste ***can be conducted***. A concept of this kind, however, is the mere pure concept of reason of the supersensible, which lies at the basis of the object (and also of the judging subject) as an object of sense, hence as an appearance. For if one did not assume such a regard, the claim of the judgment of taste to universal validity could not be saved; if the concept on which it is grounded were merely a confused concept of the understanding, such as of perfection, to which one could add the sensible intuition of the beautiful as corresponding to it, then it would at least in itself be possible to ground the judgment of taste on proofs, which contradicts the thesis.

But now all contradiction disappears if I say: the judgment of taste is grounded on a concept (of a ground in general of the subjective purposiveness of nature for the power of judgment), from which, however, nothing can be cognized and proven in respect of the object, because it is in itself undeterminable and unfit for cognition; yet through this very same concept it at the same time obtains validity for everyone (in each person, to be sure, as a singular judgment immediately accompanying the intuition): because its determining ground lies perhaps in the concept of that which can be regarded as the supersensible substrate of humanity. {{ 237 }}

In the resolution of an antinomy, everything depends only on the possibility that two propositions that conflict in appearance do not in fact contradict each other but can coexist, even though the explanation of the possibility of their concept exceeds our cognitive faculty. That this illusion is also natural and unavoidable for human reason, and likewise why it is and remains so, although after the resolution of the apparent contradiction it no longer deceives, can also be made comprehensible from this.

For we take the concept on which the universal validity of a judgment must be grounded in the same meaning in both conflicting judgments, and yet assert of it two opposite predicates. In the thesis it should therefore say: The judgment of taste is not grounded on ***determinate*** concepts; but in the antithesis: The judgment of taste is indeed grounded on a concept, {{{ 341 }}} although an ***indeterminate*** one (namely, of the supersensible substrate of appearances); and then there would be no conflict between them.

More than to resolve this conflict in the claims and counterclaims of taste we cannot achieve. To give a determinate objective principle of taste, according to which its judgments could be guided, {{ 238 }} tested, and proven, is absolutely impossible; for then it would not be a judgment of taste. The subjective principle, namely the indeterminate idea of the supersensible in us, can only be indicated as the sole key to the deciphering of this faculty, which is hidden from us as to its sources, but cannot be made further comprehensible by anything.

The correct concept of taste, namely as a merely reflecting aesthetic power of judgment, lies at the basis of the antinomy set up and reconciled here; and there both principles, which in appearance conflicted, were united with each other, in that ***both can be true***, which is also sufficient. If, on the contrary, the determining ground of taste were assumed to be ***agreeableness*** (because of the singularity of the representation that lies at the basis of the judgment of taste), as is done by some, or the principle of ***perfection***, as others wish (because of its universal validity), and the definition of taste adjusted accordingly: then there arises from this an antinomy that is absolutely impossible to reconcile, except by showing that ***both*** of the ***propositions***, which stand opposed to each other (and not merely contradictorily), ***are false***: which then proves that the concept on which each is grounded contradicts itself. One sees, therefore, that {{ 239 }} the resolution of the antinomy of the aesthetic power of judgment takes a similar path to that which the Critique followed in the resolution of the antinomies of pure theoretical reason; and that likewise here, and also in the Critique of Practical Reason, the antinomies force us against our will to look beyond the sensible and to seek in the supersensible the point of union of all our faculties _a priori_: because no other way out remains to make reason consistent with itself.

Remark I.

Since in transcendental philosophy we so often find occasion to distinguish ideas from concepts of the understanding, it can be of {{{ 342 }}} use to introduce technical terms suited to their difference. I believe that no one will have any objection if I propose a few. — Ideas in the most general meaning are representations referred to an object according to a certain (subjective or objective) principle, insofar as they can yet never become a cognition of it. They are referred to an intuition either according to a merely subjective principle of the agreement of the cognitive faculties with one another (of the imagination and of the understanding): and are then called ***aesthetic***; or they are referred to a concept according to an objective principle, but yet can never yield a cognition of the object: and are called ideas of reason; in which case the concept is a ***transcendent*** concept, which is distinguished from the concept of the understanding, to which an {{ 240 }} adequately corresponding experience can at all times be underlaid, and which on that account is called ***immanent***.

An ***aesthetic idea*** cannot become a cognition, because it is an ***intuition*** (of the imagination) for which a concept can never be found adequate. An ***idea of reason*** can never become a cognition, because it contains a ***concept*** (of the supersensible) to which an intuition can never be given adequately.

Now I believe one could call the aesthetic idea an ***inexponible*** representation of the imagination, but the idea of reason an ***indemonstrable*** concept of reason. Of both it is presupposed that they are not perhaps entirely groundless, but are rather generated in accordance with certain principles of the cognitive faculties to which they belong (the former to subjective, the latter to objective principles), in accordance with the above explanation of an idea in general.

***Concepts of the understanding*** must as such at all times be demonstrable (if by demonstrating, as in anatomy, is understood merely ***exhibiting***); i.e., the object corresponding to them must at all times be able to be given in intuition (pure or empirical): for through this alone can they become cognitions. The concept of ***magnitude*** can be given in the intuition of space _a priori_, e.g., of a straight line, etc.; the concept of ***cause*** in impenetrability, the impact of bodies, etc. Consequently, both can be verified by an empirical intuition, i.e., the thought of them can be shown (demonstrated, pointed out) in an example; and this must be {{{ 343 }}} possible: otherwise one is not certain whether the thought is not empty, i.e., without any object.

In logic, one commonly uses the expressions of the demonstrable or {{ 241 }} indemonstrable only in respect of ***propositions***: since the former could be better designated by the name of only mediately, the latter by that of ***immediately certain*** propositions; for pure philosophy also has propositions of both kinds, if by these are understood true propositions capable or incapable of proof. Yet from grounds _a priori_ she, as philosophy, can to be sure prove, but not demonstrate; unless one wants to depart entirely from the meaning of the word, according to which to demonstrate (_ostendere_, _exhibere_) means as much as to exhibit its concept at the same time in intuition (be it in proving or even merely in defining): which, if it is intuition ***a priori***, is called constructing the concept, but if it is empirical, nonetheless remains the exhibition of the object, through which objective reality is secured for the concept. Thus one says of an anatomist: he demonstrates the human eye, when he makes intuitive the concept that he has previously presented discursively, by means of the dissection of this organ.

According to this, the concept of reason of the supersensible substrate of all appearances in general, or also of that which must be laid at the basis of our free choice in relation to moral laws, namely transcendental freedom, is already as regards its species an indemonstrable concept and an idea of reason, whereas virtue is this as regards degree: because to the former, in itself, nothing corresponding to it as regards quality can be given in experience, while in the latter, no product of experience of that causality reaches the degree that the idea of reason prescribes as a rule.

Just as in an idea of reason the ***imagination*** with its {{ 242 }} intuitions does not reach the given concept: so in an aesthetic idea the ***understanding*** through its concepts never reaches the entire inner intuition of the imagination, which it connects with a given representation. Since, now, bringing a representation of the imagination to concepts is called ***exponing*** it: the aesthetic idea can be called an ***inexponible*** representation of the same (in its free play). I shall have opportunity in the sequel to elaborate further on this kind of ideas; now I only remark: that both kinds {{{ 344 }}} of ideas, the ideas of reason as well as the aesthetic ones, must have their principles; and indeed both in reason, the former in objective, the latter in subjective principles of their use.

One can, according to this, also define genius through the faculty of ***aesthetic ideas***: whereby at the same time the ground is indicated why in products of genius nature (of the subject), not a deliberate end of art (of the production of the beautiful), gives the rule. For since the beautiful must not be judged according to concepts, but according to the purposive attunement of the imagination for agreement with the faculty of concepts in general: not rule and precept, but only that which is mere nature in the subject, but cannot be grasped under rules or concepts, i.e., the supersensible substrate of all its faculties (which no concept of the understanding reaches), consequently that in relation to which it is the ultimate end given by the intelligible of our nature to make all our cognitive faculties agree, can serve as the subjective standard of that aesthetic but unconditional purposiveness in beautiful art, which is to make a legitimate {{ 243 }} claim to having to please everyone. Thus alone is it possible that a subjective and yet universally valid principle _a priori_ should lie at the basis of that for which one cannot prescribe any objective principle.

Remark II.

The following important remark offers itself here of its own accord: namely, that there are ***three kinds of antinomy*** of pure reason, which, however, all agree in this, that they compel it to depart from the otherwise very natural assumption of taking the objects of the senses for things in themselves, and instead to hold them to be mere appearances and to underlay them with an intelligible substrate (something supersensible, of which the concept is only an idea and admits of no proper cognition). Without such an antinomy, reason would never be able to resolve to adopt such a principle, which so greatly narrows the field of its speculation, and to make sacrifices in which so many otherwise highly glittering hopes must entirely vanish; for even now, when a so much greater use in practical respect opens up to it as a compensation for this loss, it seems {{{ 345 }}} unable without pain to separate itself from those hopes and to untie itself from its old attachment.

That there are three kinds of antinomy has its ground in this, that there are three cognitive faculties: understanding, power of judgment, and reason, each of which (as a higher cognitive faculty) must have its principles _a priori_; since then reason, insofar as it judges of these principles themselves and their use, relentlessly demands for the given {{ 244 }} conditioned in respect of them all the unconditioned, which can yet never be found if one considers the sensible as belonging to things in themselves and does not rather underlay it, as mere appearance, with something supersensible (the intelligible substrate of nature outside us and within us) as a thing in itself. There exists then 1) an antinomy of reason in respect of the theoretical use of the understanding up to the unconditioned ***for the cognitive faculty***; 2) an antinomy of reason in respect of the aesthetic use of the power of judgment ***for the feeling of pleasure and displeasure***; 3) an antinomy in respect of the practical use of reason, which is of itself legislative, ***for the faculty of desire***: insofar as all these faculties have their higher principles _a priori_ and, in accordance with an unavoidable demand of reason, must also be able to judge ***unconditionally*** according to these principles and determine their object.

In respect of two antinomies, that of the theoretical and that of the practical use of those higher cognitive faculties, we have already shown elsewhere their ***unavoidability*** if such judgments do not look back to a supersensible substrate of the given objects as appearances, but on the other hand also their ***resolvability*** as soon as the latter occurs. As concerns now the antinomy in the use of the power of judgment in accordance with the demand of reason, and its resolution given here: there is no other means of escaping it than ***either*** to deny that any principle _a priori_ lies at the basis of the aesthetic judgment of taste, so that all claim to the necessity of universal assent is a groundless, empty delusion, and a judgment of taste deserves to be held correct only insofar as {{ 245 }} ***it happens*** that many agree in respect of it, and even this actually not because one ***presupposes*** a principle _a priori_ behind this agreement, but rather (as in taste of the palate) because the subjects happen by chance to be uniformly {{{ 346 }}} organized; ***or*** one would have to assume that the judgment of taste is actually a disguised judgment of reason concerning the perfection discovered in a thing and the relation of the manifold in it to an end, and hence is called aesthetic only because of the confusion that clings to this reflection of ours, although it is at bottom teleological: in which case one could declare the resolution of the antinomy by means of transcendental ideas to be unnecessary and null, and thus unite those laws of taste with the objects of the senses not as mere appearances but also as things in themselves. How little, however, either the one or the other evasion avails, has been shown in several places in the exposition of judgments of taste.

But if one concedes to our deduction at least so much, that it takes place on the right path, even if it has not yet been made clear enough in all parts, three ideas show themselves: ***first***, of the supersensible in general without further determination as the substrate of nature; ***second***, of the very same as the principle of the subjective purposiveness of nature for our cognitive faculty; ***third***, of the very same as the principle of the ends of freedom and the principle of the agreement of the latter with the former in morality.
