## § 57. Resolution of the antinomy of taste

The conflict of those principles that underlie every judgment of taste (they are simply the two peculiarities of such judgments set out earlier in the Analytic) admits of no removal save by making the following clear: that the concept by which, in judgments of this kind, one relates to the object is not employed in a single sense across both maxims of the aesthetic power of judgment; that this doubled sense, or standpoint of judging, is required by our transcendental power of judgment; but that the semblance arising when the one is confounded with the other, being a natural illusion, cannot be avoided either.

Some concept or other the judgment of taste must indeed involve; for without one it could lay no claim whatever to a necessary validity holding for {{ 235 }} everyone. Yet just for that reason it must not be demonstrable ***from*** a concept, for a concept may be either determinable, or else in itself indeterminate and, along with that, indeterminable. The concept of the understanding belongs to the first sort, being determinable by way of predicates drawn from a sensible intuition able to answer to it; to the second belongs the transcendental concept that reason forms of the supersensible, which underlies all such intuition and hence permits no further theoretical determination.

Now, the judgment of taste does bear upon objects of sense, though not so as to fix for the understanding any ***concept*** of them; for it is no judgment of cognition. Being an intuitive, singular representation tied to the feeling of pleasure, it is on that account only a private judgment: and to that extent its validity would reach no further than the individual doing the judging: the object is ***for me*** one in which I take satisfaction, whereas with others it may stand quite differently; — each has a taste of his own.

And yet the judgment of taste unquestionably harbors a wider relation of the object's representation (and, along with it, of the subject's), on which we rest the claim that judgments of this kind extend as necessary to everyone: a claim that must accordingly rest on some concept or other as its {{{ 340 }}} ground; but on a concept that no intuition can ever determine {{ 236 }}, by which nothing gets cognized, and from which, consequently, ***no proof*** for the judgment of taste ***can be conducted***. A concept of that description is nothing but the pure rational concept of the supersensible, which underlies the object (and the judging subject as well) taken as an object of sense, and thus as appearance. For were such a regard left out of account, the judgment of taste's pretension to universal validity could not be rescued; and if the concept it rests upon were merely some confused concept of the understanding, of perfection perhaps, to which the sensible intuition of the beautiful might be joined as its correlate, then it would at least be intrinsically possible to base the judgment of taste on proofs — which runs counter to the thesis.

But now every contradiction disappears once I put it thus: the judgment of taste rests on a concept (that of a ground, generally, for nature's subjective purposiveness with respect to the power of judgment), from which, however, nothing about the object can be cognized or proved, since it is in itself indeterminable and useless for cognition; yet by this very concept the judgment at the same time gains validity for everyone (in each case, admittedly, as a singular judgment attending the intuition directly): because what determines it may well lie in the concept of that which one can take to be the supersensible substrate of humanity {{ 237 }}.

Resolving an antinomy hinges on nothing more than the possibility that two propositions, seemingly at odds, in truth fail to contradict each other and can stand together, even where explaining how their concept is possible outstrips our power of cognition. That this semblance is, moreover, natural and, for human reason, inescapable — and likewise why it is so and remains so, though after the apparent contradiction is dissolved it deceives no longer — can be rendered intelligible from the same considerations.

For in both of the conflicting judgments we take the concept, on which a judgment's universal validity has to rest, in one identical meaning, and nonetheless ascribe to it two contrary predicates. Hence the thesis ought properly to run: the judgment of taste has no basis in ***determinate*** concepts; and the antithesis: the judgment of taste does rest on a concept, albeit an ***indeterminate*** {{{ 341 }}} one (that, namely, of the supersensible substrate of appearances); whereupon no conflict remains between them.

Beyond settling this conflict among taste's claims and counterclaims, nothing further lies within our power. It is flatly impossible to furnish some determinate, objective principle of taste by which its judgments might be steered, {{ 238 }} examined, and proved; for the judgment would then cease to be one of taste. The subjective principle — that indeterminate idea of the supersensible within us — can be pointed to only as the one key for unriddling this power, whose sources stay concealed even from ourselves, and beyond that cannot be made intelligible at all.

What underlies the antinomy here erected and reconciled is the right concept of taste, that is, of taste as a merely reflecting aesthetic power of judgment; and on that basis the two ostensibly conflicting principles were brought together, since ***both can be true***, which is enough. Were one instead to take, as what determines taste (on account of the singularity of the representation underlying the judgment of taste), either ***agreeableness***, as some do, or, as others prefer (on account of its universal validity), the principle of ***perfection***, and to shape the definition of taste to suit: then an antinomy springs up that admits of no reconciliation whatever, save by showing that ***both*** of the opposed (yet not merely contradictory) ***propositions are false***: which then proves that the concept underpinning each is at odds with itself. One sees, then, that {{ 239 }} disposing of the antinomy belonging to the aesthetic power of judgment follows much the same route the Critique took when resolving the antinomies of pure theoretical reason; and that, here as likewise in the Critique of practical reason, the antinomies force us, despite ourselves, to look past the sensible and to search within the supersensible for the point where all our faculties come together _a priori_: since no other road is left for bringing reason into accord with itself.

Remark I.

Since in transcendental philosophy occasion arises so frequently to set ideas apart from the understanding's concepts, there may be {{{ 342 }}} some advantage in bringing in technical terms fitted to their distinction. I trust there will be no objection should I put a few forward. — Ideas, in the widest sense, are representations that relate to an object under a certain principle (subjective or objective), while nonetheless never coming to constitute a cognition of that object. Either they bear on an intuition under a principle that is merely subjective — the mutual accord of the cognitive powers (imagination and understanding) — and then bear the name ***aesthetic***; or they bear on a concept under an objective principle while still never delivering any cognition of the object, and are named ideas of reason; and here the concept is a ***transcendent*** one, marked off from the concept of the understanding, beneath which an {{ 240 }} experience adequately answering to it may always be placed, and which is therefore styled ***immanent***.

An ***aesthetic idea*** can never turn into a cognition, for it is an ***intuition*** (of the imagination) for which no adequate concept is ever to be had. An ***idea of reason*** can never turn into a cognition, for it holds a ***concept*** (of the supersensible) to which no commensurate intuition can ever be supplied.

I hold, accordingly, that one may term the aesthetic idea an ***inexponible*** representation of the imagination, and the idea of reason an ***indemonstrable*** concept of reason. Of each it is taken for granted that it is not simply baseless, but rather (following the account of an idea in general given above) produced in keeping with certain principles of the cognitive powers it answers to (for the one the subjective, for the other the objective principles).

***Concepts of the understanding***, precisely as such, have at every point to be demonstrable (taking demonstration, as in anatomy, to mean simply ***exhibiting***); that is, whatever object answers to them has always to admit of presentation in intuition (pure or empirical): for it is by this route alone that they turn into cognitions. The concept of ***magnitude*** admits of being given _a priori_ in the intuition of space — say, that of a straight line, and so on; the concept of ***cause*** in impenetrability, in the collision of bodies, and so on. Both, then, can be borne out by an empirical intuition — that is, the thought of them exhibited in an instance (demonstrated, displayed); and such exhibition must be able to {{{ 343 }}} occur: failing which one has no assurance that the thought is not vacant, that is, wanting any object whatever.

Logic ordinarily reserves the terms demonstrable and {{ 241 }} indemonstrable for ***propositions*** alone: though the first would be better named propositions certain only mediately, the second ***immediately certain*** ones; for pure philosophy likewise possesses propositions of each sort, understanding thereby true propositions that either do or do not admit of proof. From grounds _a priori_, however, philosophy as such can indeed prove but not demonstrate — unless one wholly abandons the word's sense, on which demonstrating (_ostendere_, _exhibere_) amounts to setting one's concept, in the very act (be it of proving or merely of defining), before intuition as well: an intuition that, where it is ***a priori***, is termed the construction of the concept, but where it is empirical still counts as the display of the object, whereby objective reality is made secure for the concept. So it is said of an anatomist that he demonstrates the human eye when, through dissecting this organ, he renders intuitive the concept he had earlier laid out discursively.

On this footing the rational concept of the supersensible substrate underlying all appearances whatever — or of that which has to be laid at the base of our power of choice in respect of moral laws, namely transcendental freedom — counts already by its very species as an indemonstrable concept and idea of reason, while virtue counts as such only by degree: for to the former nothing whatsoever answering to it in quality can be given in experience at all, whereas in the latter no experiential product of that causality reaches the degree that the idea of reason lays down as its rule.

As, with an idea of reason, the ***imagination*** and its {{ 242 }} intuitions fall short of the concept that is given: so, with an aesthetic idea, the ***understanding*** by its concepts never reaches the entire inner intuition that the imagination binds up with a given representation. And since bringing a representation of the imagination under concepts is nothing else than to ***expound*** it: the aesthetic idea may fairly be called an ***inexponible*** representation of that faculty (at play freely). Further on I shall find room to say more concerning ideas of this sort; for the present I note only this: that both kinds {{{ 344 }}} of idea, the rational no less than the aesthetic, must have their principles, and both in reason at that — the one in the objective, the other in the subjective principles governing its use.

On this basis genius, too, may be explained by way of the faculty of ***aesthetic ideas***: which at once discloses why, in what genius produces, it is nature (the subject's), and not any deliberate end of art (the bringing-forth of the beautiful), that furnishes the rule. For since the beautiful is not to be appraised by concepts, but by the purposive tuning of the imagination toward accord with the faculty of concepts as such: it follows that neither rule nor prescription, but solely what is sheer nature in the subject — yet cannot be caught under rules or concepts, namely the supersensible substrate of all its powers (which no concept of the understanding reaches), and hence that in relation to which the final end bestowed by the intelligible in our nature is to bring all our cognitive powers into concord — can serve as the subjective measure for that aesthetic yet unconditioned purposiveness in fine art which is meant to lay a rightful {{ 243 }} claim to having necessarily to please everyone. It is on this footing alone, moreover, that a subjective and yet universally valid principle _a priori_ can underlie this power, to which no objective principle admits of being prescribed.

Remark II.

A weighty remark presents itself here unbidden: namely, that pure reason has ***three kinds of antinomy***, all of which nevertheless concur in forcing it to quit the otherwise entirely natural assumption that takes objects of sense for things in themselves, letting them pass instead as mere appearances and setting beneath them an intelligible substrate — something supersensible whose concept is a mere idea and yields no cognition properly so called. Lacking such an antinomy, reason could never resolve upon adopting a principle that so severely contracts the field of its speculation, nor upon sacrifices in which so many otherwise glittering hopes are bound to fade away entirely; for even now, when in recompense for this forfeiture a far larger employment opens to it on the practical side, it seems it cannot {{{ 345 }}}, without pain, take leave of those hopes and loosen itself from the old attachment.

The reason there are three kinds of antinomy is that there are three cognitive powers — understanding, power of judgment, and reason — each of which, as a higher cognitive power, must possess its own principles _a priori_; whereupon reason, insofar as it passes judgment on those principles themselves and their employment, insistently requires, for all of them, the unconditioned answering to whatever {{ 244 }} conditioned is given — an unconditioned never to be met with so long as one counts the sensible among things in themselves rather than laying beneath it, as mere appearance, something supersensible (nature's intelligible substrate outside and inside us) taken as a thing in itself. Thus we get 1) an antinomy of reason regarding the theoretical employment of the understanding, carried up to the unconditioned, ***for the faculty of cognition***; 2) an antinomy of reason regarding the aesthetic employment of the power of judgment ***for the feeling of pleasure and displeasure***; 3) an antinomy regarding the practical employment of reason as legislating of itself ***for the faculty of desire***: inasmuch as every one of these powers holds its higher principles _a priori_ and, answering to an inescapable requirement of reason, must be capable as well of judging ***unconditionally*** by those principles and of determining its object.

As for two of these antinomies — that of the theoretical and that of the practical employment of those higher cognitive powers — we have already made plain elsewhere both their ***unavoidability***, where judgments of the kind decline to trace back to a supersensible substrate of the given objects as appearances, and, on the other hand, their ***resolvability*** the moment such a tracing-back takes place. Turning now to the antinomy in the employment of the power of judgment as reason's demand raises it, together with the resolution offered here: there is no way of sidestepping it other than ***either*** to deny that any principle _a priori_ underlies the aesthetic judgment of taste — so that every pretension to a necessary universal agreement would be a hollow, baseless fancy, and a judgment of taste would rank as correct merely because {{ 245 }} ***it turns out*** that many concur about it, and even that not really on the score that one ***suspects*** a principle _a priori_ behind such concurrence, but (just as with the palate's taste) because the subjects chance to be alike {{{ 346 }}} in their make-up; ***or*** one would have to grant that the judgment of taste is at bottom a disguised judgment of reason upon a perfection detected in a thing and upon the reference of the manifold within it to an end, and gets called aesthetic solely because of the confusion clinging to this reflection of ours, though in truth it is teleological: on which supposition one might pronounce the resolution of the antinomy through transcendental ideas needless and void, and so reconcile those laws of taste with objects of sense not as bare appearances but as things in themselves as well. But just how little either shift accomplishes has been demonstrated at various points in the exposition of judgments of taste.

Yet if our deduction be allowed at least this much, that it moves along the correct path, however far it may still fall short of full clarity in every particular, then three ideas come to light: ***first***, the supersensible taken at large, with no closer determination, as nature's substrate; ***second***, that same supersensible as the principle of nature's subjective purposiveness for our cognitive power; ***third***, that same supersensible again as the principle of freedom's ends and of the concord of those ends with that purposiveness in the sphere of morals.
