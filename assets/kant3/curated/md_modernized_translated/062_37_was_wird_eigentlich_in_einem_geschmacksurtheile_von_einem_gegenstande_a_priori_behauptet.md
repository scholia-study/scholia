## § 37. What is Actually Asserted _a priori_ of an Object in a Judgment of Taste?

That the representation of an object is immediately combined with a pleasure can only be internally perceived and, if one wished to indicate nothing more than this, would yield a merely empirical judgment. For _a priori_ I cannot combine any representation with a definite feeling (of pleasure or displeasure), except where an _a priori_ principle determining the will lies at the basis in reason; since then the pleasure (in the moral feeling) is the consequence of this, but for that very reason cannot be compared at all with the pleasure in taste, because it requires a determinate concept of a law, whereas the latter, by contrast, is supposed to be immediately combined with the mere judging prior to all concepts. Hence all judgments of taste are also singular judgments, {{ 150 }} because they combine their predicate of satisfaction not with a concept, but with a given singular empirical representation.

Thus it is not the pleasure, but the ***universal validity of this pleasure***, which is perceived in the mind as combined with the mere judging of an object, that is represented _a priori_ in a judgment of taste as a universal rule for the power of judgment, valid for everyone. It is an empirical judgment: that I perceive and judge an object with pleasure. But it is an _a priori_ judgment: that I find it beautiful, i.e., that I may attribute that satisfaction to everyone as necessary.
