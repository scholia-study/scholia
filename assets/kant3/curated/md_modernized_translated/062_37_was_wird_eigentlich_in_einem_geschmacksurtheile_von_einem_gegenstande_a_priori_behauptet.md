## § 37. What is actually asserted _a priori_ of an object in a judgment of taste?

The fact that the representation of an object stands in immediate connection with a pleasure is something that can be noticed only inwardly, and, if one meant to point to nothing beyond this, it would furnish no more than an empirical judgment. For there is no representation with which I could combine _a priori_ a determinate feeling (of pleasure or displeasure), save where some principle determining the will _a priori_ underlies it in reason; and in that case the pleasure (in moral feeling) follows from that principle, yet precisely on that account admits of no comparison whatever with the pleasure of taste, since it requires a determinate concept of a law: the latter, by contrast, is meant to attach itself immediately to the bare act of judging, in advance of any concept. For this reason, too, every judgment of taste is a singular judgment, {{ 150 }} since it ties its predicate of satisfaction not to a concept but to a single empirical representation that has been given.

It is thus not the pleasure, but rather the ***universal validity of this pleasure***—perceived in the mind as tied to the mere judging of an object—that a judgment of taste sets forth _a priori_, as a universal rule for the power of judgment, holding good for everyone. This is an empirical judgment: that I perceive and judge an object as accompanied by pleasure. This, however, is a judgment _a priori_: that I regard it as beautiful, which is to say, that I am entitled to require of everyone that same satisfaction as something necessary.
