## § 6. The beautiful is what, without a concept, is represented as an object of universal satisfaction

This definition of the beautiful can be inferred from the preceding definition of it as an object of satisfaction without any interest. For that of which someone is conscious that the satisfaction in it is, for him himself, without any interest, he cannot but judge that it must contain a ground of satisfaction for everyone. For since it rests on no inclination of the subject (nor on any other deliberate interest), but rather since the person judging feels himself wholly ***free*** with respect to the satisfaction that he devotes to the object, he can locate no private conditions, tied to his own self alone, as grounds of the satisfaction, and must accordingly regard it as grounded in what he can presuppose in everyone else as well; consequently he must take himself to have reason to expect a like satisfaction of everyone. Accordingly he will talk about the beautiful as though beauty {{ 18 }} were a property of the object and the judgment logical (amounting, through concepts of the object, to a cognition of it), even though it is only aesthetic and holds merely a relation of the object's representation to the subject; the reason being that it does share with the logical the resemblance that its validity for everyone can be presupposed in it. Yet this universality cannot arise out of concepts either. For from concepts there is no passage to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure (save in pure practical laws, which, however, carry an interest along with them, such as is not tied to the pure judgment of taste {{{ 212 }}} ). Hence to the judgment of taste, along with the consciousness that within it all interest has been detached, there must attach a claim to validity for everyone without a universality directed at objects, i.e., with it there must be conjoined a claim to subjective universality.
