## § 6. The beautiful is what, without a concept, is represented as an object of universal satisfaction

This definition of the beautiful can be derived from the one given earlier, where it was characterized as an object of satisfaction wholly devoid of interest. For whenever someone is aware that his own satisfaction in a thing is free of all interest, he can appraise it in no other way than as necessarily holding a ground of satisfaction valid for everyone. For it rests upon no inclination belonging to the subject (nor upon any other reflective interest), while the one who judges feels himself utterly ***free*** in regard to the satisfaction he bestows on the object; and so he can discover no private conditions, such as his subject alone might cling to, to serve as grounds of the satisfaction, and he must therefore take it to be founded in something presupposable in every other person as well; hence he must suppose himself to have reason for expecting a like satisfaction from everyone. Accordingly he will talk about the beautiful as though beauty {{ 18 }} were a property of the object and the judgment logical (amounting, through concepts of the object, to a cognition of it), even though it is only aesthetic and holds merely a relation of the object's representation to the subject; the reason being that it does share with the logical the resemblance that its validity for everyone can be presupposed in it. Yet this universality cannot arise out of concepts either. For from concepts there is no passage to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure (save in pure practical laws, which, however, carry an interest along with them, such as is not tied to the pure judgment of taste {{{ 212 }}} ). Hence to the judgment of taste, along with the consciousness that within it all interest has been detached, there must attach a claim to validity for everyone without a universality directed at objects, i.e., with it there must be conjoined a claim to subjective universality.
