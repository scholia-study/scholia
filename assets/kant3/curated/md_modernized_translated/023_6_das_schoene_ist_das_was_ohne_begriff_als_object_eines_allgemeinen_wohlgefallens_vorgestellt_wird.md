## § 6. The Beautiful is That which is Represented without Concepts as the Object of a Universal Satisfaction

This explanation of the beautiful can be derived from the previous explanation of it as an object of satisfaction without any interest. For that of which someone is conscious that the satisfaction in it is for himself without any interest, that he cannot judge otherwise than that it must contain a ground of satisfaction for everyone. For since it is not grounded on any inclination of the subject (nor on any other deliberate interest), but since the person making the judgment feels himself completely ***free*** with regard to the satisfaction that he devotes to the object: he can find no private conditions as grounds of the satisfaction, to which his subject alone would be attached, and must therefore regard it as grounded in that which he can presuppose in everyone else as well; consequently he must believe himself to have ground for expecting a similar satisfaction of everyone. He will therefore speak of the beautiful as if beauty {{ 18 }} were a property of the object and the judgment were logical (constituting a cognition of the object through concepts of it); even though it is only aesthetic and contains merely a relation of the representation of the object to the subject: for this reason, because it nevertheless has a similarity with the logical, in that one can presuppose its validity for everyone in it. But this universality cannot arise from concepts either. For from concepts there is no transition to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure (except in pure practical laws, which, however, carry an interest with them, such as is not combined with the pure judgment of taste {{{ 212 }}}). Consequently there must be attached to the judgment of taste, together with the consciousness of the separation in it from all interest, a claim to validity for everyone without a universality attached to objects, i.e., there must be combined with it a claim to subjective universality.
