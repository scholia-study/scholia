## § 88. Restriction of the validity of the moral proof

Pure reason, taken as a practical faculty — namely as the power to govern, through ideas (pure concepts of reason), how our causality is freely employed — does more than harbor within the moral law a merely regulative principle of our conduct: by that same law it furnishes us at once with a subjectively constitutive one, seated in the concept of an object thinkable by reason alone, an object that our deeds are meant to render actual in the world according to that law. Accordingly, when freedom is exercised under moral laws, the idea of a final end belonging to it carries a reality that is subjectively ***practical***. Reason determines us _a priori_ to promote with every power at our command the world's highest good, a good lying in the conjunction of the greatest well-being of the world's rational beings with the highest condition of goodness in them — namely, universal happiness joined to a morality maximally conformable to law. In this final end the possibility of the one part, namely happiness, is empirically conditioned, that is, dependent on the constitution of nature (on whether it accords with this end or not) and in theoretical respect {{ 430 }} problematic; whereas the other part, namely morality, with respect to which we are free from the cooperation of nature, is, as to its possibility, firmly settled _a priori_ and dogmatically certain. For the concept of a final end of the world's rational beings to attain objective theoretical reality, then, two things are demanded: that we ourselves have a final end prescribed to us _a priori_, and further that creation, that is, the world itself, possess a final end in respect of its existence — something which, could it be established _a priori_, would supply the final end's subjective reality with an objective one besides. For should creation have any final end whatever, no other conception of it is open to us than that it has to {{{ 454 }}} agree with the moral one (which by itself first makes the concept of an end possible). Now we do indeed find ends in the world: and physical teleology exhibits them to such a degree that, if we judge in conformity with reason, we ultimately have grounds for adopting, as a principle for the investigation of nature, that nothing whatever in nature is without an end; yet the final end of nature we seek in vain within nature itself. This end therefore can and must, just as the idea of it lies only in reason, be sought, even as regards its objective possibility, solely in rational beings. But the practical reason of the latter not only specifies this final end, it also determines this concept with respect to the conditions under which a final end of the creation can be thought by {{ 431 }} us at all.

It now becomes a question whether the objective reality of the concept of a final end of creation might not likewise be exhibited adequately for the theoretical requirements of pure reason — not, admittedly, with apodictic force for the determining power of judgment, yet well enough for the maxims by which the theoretically reflecting power of judgment proceeds. That is the very least one may demand of speculative philosophy, which pledges itself to link the ethical end to the ends of nature through the idea of one sole end; and yet even so slight a demand still far outstrips anything it can ever deliver.

Following the principle of the theoretically reflecting power of judgment, our statement would run thus: Should we have grounds for positing behind nature's purposive products a supreme cause of nature — one whose causality, as regards the actuality of those products (creation), must be thought as of another kind than the mechanism of nature calls for, that is, as the causality of an understanding — then we shall equally have grounds enough to attribute to this primordial being not merely ends dispersed everywhere in nature but a final end besides; not so as to demonstrate that such a being exists, but at all events (as was done in physical teleology) to convince ourselves that the possibility {{ 432 }} of such a world becomes intelligible to us not through ends alone, but solely by our laying a final end at the basis of its existence.

Yet a final end is merely a concept of our practical reason, one that can neither be inferred from any data of experience for the sake of judging nature theoretically nor be brought into connection with any cognition of nature. This concept admits of no employment whatever, save purely for practical {{{ 455 }}} reason acting under moral laws; and creation's final end is that character of the world which agrees with the one thing we can lay down determinately by laws, namely the final end of our pure practical reason, precisely insofar as it is meant to be practical. — Now the moral law, in laying this latter end upon us, gives us for practical purposes — that is, so that we may direct our powers toward realizing it — a ground for supposing its possibility and feasibility, and hence too (for its realization would be impossible were nature not to concur in a condition of it that lies beyond our control) a constitution of things attuned to that end. We thus have a moral ground for thinking, for a world, a final end of the creation as well.

Here we do not yet have the step that leads from moral teleology {{ 433 }} over to a theology — that is, to a moral world-author's existing — but only to a final end of creation as determined along these lines. That for this creation, then — that is, for the existence of things in keeping with a ***final end*** — one must posit, in the first place, an intelligent being, and, in the second, not merely (as with the possibility of nature's things, which we found ourselves obliged to judge as ***ends***) an intelligent one, but a being who is at once ***moral*** as author of the world, and thus a ***God***: this constitutes a second inference, and one so constituted that one sees it to be passed solely for the power of judgment under concepts of practical reason, and, being such a verdict, for the reflecting and not the determining power of judgment. We cannot, after all, arrogate to ourselves the insight that, even though within us moral-practical reason differs essentially in its principles from the technical-practical, matters must stand the same way in the world's supreme cause once it is taken as an intelligence, so that a peculiar and separate kind of causality would be needed in it for a final end beyond what mere ends of nature demand; and hence that in our final end we possess not only a ***moral ground*** for positing a final end of creation (as its effect), but likewise a ***moral being*** as creation's primordial ground. We can, however, indeed say: that ***in accordance with the constitution of our rational faculty*** {{ 434 }} we cannot in any way make comprehensible to ourselves the possibility of such a purposiveness, related ***to the moral law*** and its object, as is present in this final end, without a world-author and ruler who is at the same time a moral legislator.

{{{ 456 }}} That a supreme, morally legislating author is real has thus been sufficiently shown only ***for the practical use*** of our reason, without anything being theoretically determined regarding his existence. For reason, if its end is to be possible — an end laid upon us in any case by its own legislation — stands in need of an idea whereby the hindrance stemming from our inability to fulfill that end under the mere natural concept of the world is removed (which suffices for the reflecting power of judgment); and by this the idea takes on practical reality, notwithstanding that every means of securing it such reality in a theoretical respect, toward explaining nature and determining the supreme cause, is altogether lacking to it as regards speculative cognition. In the eyes of the theoretically reflecting power of judgment, physical teleology gave adequate proof, out of nature's ends, of an intelligent cause of the world; for the practical, moral teleology brings this about by means of the concept of a final end that it finds itself constrained to assign to creation in a practical respect. The {{ 435 }} objective reality of the idea of God, as moral world-author, admittedly cannot be established by physical ends ***alone***; and yet, once the cognition of these is joined to that of the moral, the former — in accordance with pure reason's maxim of pursuing unity of principles so far as this can be carried out — become highly significant for lending support to that idea's practical reality by way of the reality it already possesses, in a theoretical respect, for the power of judgment.

Here it is now, for the prevention of a readily arising misunderstanding, most necessary to remark that we can, first, ***think*** these properties of the highest being only by analogy. For how should we investigate its nature, of which experience can show us nothing similar? Secondly, that by this analogy we also merely think it, and cannot ***cognize*** it thereby and, say, ascribe those properties to it theoretically; for that would be a task for the determining power of judgment, in the speculative aim of our reason, of gaining insight into what the supreme cause of the world is ***in itself***. Here, however, it is solely a matter of what concept we are to form of it in keeping with how our cognitive faculties are constituted, and whether its existence has to be assumed by us, in order likewise to procure merely practical reality for an end that pure practical reason, apart from any such presupposition, lays upon us _a priori_ to accomplish with all our powers, {{ 436 }} that is, in order to be able to think an intended effect as possible. That concept may well be extravagant for speculative reason {{{ 457 }}}; the properties, too, that we ascribe to the being thereby thought may, used objectively, conceal an anthropomorphism within themselves: yet the aim of their use is not to wish to determine its nature, which is unattainable for us, but rather to give determination to ourselves and to our own will in keeping with it. In the same way that we designate a cause by the concept we hold of its effect (though only as concerns its relation to that effect), without thereby aiming to settle its inner constitution from within by means of the properties that alone can make such causes known to us and that experience must supply; and just as, say, we attribute to the soul, among other powers, a _vim locomotivam_, since bodily movements genuinely arise whose source lies in its representations, without on that account wishing to credit it with the sole manner in which moving forces are familiar to us (that is, through attraction, pressure, and impact, and so through motion, each of which invariably presupposes an extended being): — just so shall we be obliged to posit ***something*** harboring the ground of the possibility, and of the practical reality (that is, the feasibility), of a necessary moral final end; this being, however, we may — going by the character of the effect looked for from it — conceive as a wise being governing the world by moral {{ 437 }} laws, and, as befits the makeup of our cognitive faculties, we must conceive it as a cause of things set apart from nature, purely in order to articulate the ***relation*** in which this being, surpassing every one of our cognitive faculties, stands to the object of ***our*** practical reason: yet without meaning, on that ground, to ascribe to it theoretically the one causality of this kind known to us, namely an understanding and a will, nor even to draw within this being itself an objective distinction between the causality thought in it with regard to what is ***for us*** the final end and the causality bearing on nature (and its determinations of ends generally), but able rather to admit this distinction only as subjectively necessary for how our faculty of cognition is constituted and as valid for the reflecting, not for the objectively determining, power of judgment. Where the practical is at stake, however, a ***regulative*** principle of this kind (bearing on prudence or wisdom) — enjoining us to act as befits ends toward whatever, by the constitution of our cognitive faculties, we can think possible only in one determinate way — is simultaneously ***constitutive***, that is, practically determining; whereas that very same principle, employed to judge the objective possibility of things, is in no way theoretically determining (as if {{{ 458 }}} the object too were to admit only that one kind of possibility which our {{ 438 }} faculty of thought admits), figuring instead as a purely ***regulative*** principle that serves the reflecting power of judgment.

Remark.

The present moral proof is by no means a newly devised one; at most it is a ground of proof newly set forth, for it lay within the rational faculty of man already before that faculty's first stirrings, and with the ongoing cultivation of that faculty it merely unfolds ever further. The moment human beings first set about reflecting on right and wrong — in an age when they still passed over the purposiveness of nature with indifference, exploiting it while imagining nothing in it beyond nature's habitual course — a judgment was bound to force itself upon them: that in the end it could surely never come to the same thing whether a man had conducted himself uprightly or falsely, equitably or by violence, even should he, clear to the close of his life and at least outwardly, have met with no fortune rewarding his virtues nor any penalty for his crimes. It is as though they perceived within themselves a voice declaring that things must turn out otherwise; and hence there must also have lain hidden the representation, however obscure, of something that they felt bound to strive after, with which such an outcome could not at all be reconciled, or with which, once they regarded the course of the world as the sole order of things, they were again unable to unite that inner determination of ends of their mind. Now, in whatever manner and however still so {{ 439 }} crudely they might represent to themselves how such an irregularity (which to the human mind is bound to be far more offensive than the blind chance that one might perhaps wish to lay at the basis of the judging of nature as its principle) could be redressed, they could nevertheless never conceive of any other principle of the possibility of nature's being united with their inner moral law than a highest cause that rules the world in accordance with moral laws: for a final end laid upon them as a duty within them, and a nature devoid of any final end outside them, in which that end is nonetheless to become actual, stand in mutual contradiction. About the inner constitution of that cause of the world they might now hatch much nonsense; that moral relation within the governance of the world remained ever the same, one universally graspable by the least cultivated reason, insofar as it regards itself as practical, but with which, by contrast, speculative reason cannot by {{{ 459 }}} far keep equal pace. — Very probably, moreover, it was this moral interest that first stirred attention toward the beauty and the ends of nature; and though such attention then served splendidly to fortify that idea, it could neither establish it nor, still less, do without it, for even the study of nature's ends takes on that immediate interest — the interest displayed so abundantly in our admiration of nature, apart from any benefit to be gained from it — only through its bearing on the final end.
