## § 88. Restriction of the validity of the moral proof

Pure reason, as a practical faculty, i.e., as a faculty of determining the free use of our causality through ideas (pure rational concepts), contains not only in the moral law a regulative principle of our actions, but thereby at the same time also provides a subjectively constitutive one in the concept of an object that only reason can think and that is to be made actual through our actions in the world in accordance with that law. The idea of a final end in the use of freedom according to moral laws therefore has subjectively ***practical*** reality. We are determined _a priori_ by reason to promote with all our powers the worldly best, which consists in the combination of the greatest well-being of the rational world-beings with the highest condition of the good in them, i.e., of universal happiness with the most lawful morality. In this final end the possibility of the one part, namely happiness, is empirically conditioned, i.e., dependent on the constitution of nature (whether it agrees with this end or not) and in a theoretical respect {{ 430 }} problematic; while the other part, namely morality, with regard to which we are free from the cooperation of nature, stands fixed as to its possibility _a priori_ and is dogmatically certain. For the objective theoretical reality of the concept of the final end of rational world-beings it is therefore required that not only we have a final end set for us _a priori_, but also that creation, i.e., the world itself, have, according to its existence, a final end: which, if it could be proved _a priori_, would add the objective reality to the subjective reality of the final end. For if creation has a final end at all, we cannot think it otherwise than so that it must agree with the moral (which alone makes the concept of an end possible). {{{ 454 }}} Now we do indeed find ends in the world: and physical teleology presents them in such measure that, if we judge in accordance with reason, we have ground to assume in the end, as the principle of the investigation of nature, that in nature there is nothing at all without an end; but the final end of nature we seek in nature itself in vain. This can and must therefore, just as the idea of it lies only in reason, itself, even as to its objective possibility, be sought only in rational beings. But the practical reason of the latter not only provides this final end, it also determines this concept with regard to the conditions under which a final end of creation can alone be thought by {{ 431 }} us.

The question now is: whether the objective reality of the concept of a final end of creation cannot be shown to be sufficient also for the theoretical demands of pure reason, if not apodictically for the determining power of judgment, yet sufficiently for the maxims of the theoretically reflecting power of judgment. This is the least that one can require of speculative philosophy, which undertakes to connect the moral end with the natural ends by means of the idea of a single end; but even this little is yet far more than it is ever capable of accomplishing.

According to the principle of the theoretically reflecting power of judgment we would say: If we have ground to assume, for the purposive products of nature, a supreme cause of nature whose causality, with regard to the actuality of the latter (creation), must be thought as of another kind than is required for the mechanism of nature, namely as that of an understanding: then we shall also have ground sufficient to think, in this original being, not merely ends everywhere in nature, but also a final end, if not in order to demonstrate the existence of such a being, yet at least (just as happened in physical teleology) in order to convince ourselves that we can make comprehensible to ourselves the possibility {{ 432 }} of such a world not merely according to ends, but also only by laying a final end at the basis of its existence.

But a final end is merely a concept of our practical reason and can neither be inferred from any data of experience for the theoretical judging of nature, nor be related to a cognition of it. No use of this concept is possible except solely for the practical {{{ 455 }}} reason according to moral laws; and the final end of creation is that constitution of the world which agrees with that which we can alone determinately specify according to laws, namely the final end of our pure practical reason, and indeed insofar as it is to be practical. — Now through the moral law, which lays this latter upon us, we have, in a practical respect, namely in order to apply our powers to the effecting of it, a ground to assume its possibility, its feasibility, hence also (because without the accession of nature to a condition of it that does not stand in our power the effecting of it would be impossible) a nature of things that agrees with it. We thus have a moral ground to think also a final end of creation in a world.

This is now not yet the inference from moral teleology {{ 433 }} to a theology, i.e., to the existence of a moral author of the world, but only to a final end of creation that is determined in this way. That now, for this creation, i.e., the existence of things in accordance with a ***final end***, there must be assumed, firstly, an intelligent being, but secondly not merely (as for the possibility of the things of nature, which we were compelled to judge as ***ends***) an intelligent, but at the same time a ***moral*** being as author of the world, hence a ***God***: is a second inference, which is so constituted that one sees it is rendered merely for the power of judgment according to concepts of practical reason, and as such for the reflecting, not the determining power of judgment. For we cannot presume to see: that, although in us moral-practical reason is essentially distinguished according to its principles from technical-practical reason, it must be so also in the supreme world-cause, if it is assumed as an intelligence, and that a special and different kind of causality of it is required for the final end than merely for the ends of nature; that we therefore have in our final end not merely a ***moral ground*** for assuming a final end of creation (as effect), but also a ***moral being*** as the original ground of creation. But we can well say: that ***according to the constitution of our faculty of reason*** {{ 434 }} we cannot at all make comprehensible to ourselves the possibility of such a purposiveness, related ***to the moral law*** and its object, as is in this final end, without an author and ruler of the world who is at the same time a moral legislator.

{{{ 456 }}} The actuality of a highest morally legislative author is therefore sufficiently demonstrated merely ***for the practical use*** of our reason, without determining anything theoretically with regard to the existence of it. For this reason needs, for the possibility of its end, which is in any case set for us through its own legislation, an idea by which the hindrance from the incapacity for its observance, according to the mere concept of nature about the world, is removed (sufficiently for the reflecting power of judgment); and this idea thereby gets practical reality, even if all means of procuring for it such a reality in a theoretical respect, for the explanation of nature and the determination of the supreme cause, are entirely lacking to it for speculative cognition. For the theoretically reflecting power of judgment, physical teleology sufficiently proved, from the ends of nature, an intelligent world-cause; for the practical, the moral teleology effects this through the concept of a final end which it is compelled, in a practical respect, to ascribe to creation. The {{ 435 }} objective reality of the idea of God, as moral author of the world, cannot indeed be demonstrated through physical ends ***alone***; nevertheless, however, if the cognition of it is connected with that of the moral, the former are, by virtue of the maxim of pure reason to follow unity of principles as far as can be done, of great significance, in order to come to the aid of the practical reality of that idea through that which it already has, in a theoretical respect, for the power of judgment.

Here, now, in order to prevent an easily occurring misunderstanding, it is most necessary to remark that we can, firstly, ***think*** these properties of the highest being only by analogy. For how should we investigate its nature, of which experience can show us nothing similar? Secondly, that we thereby also only think it, do not ***cognize*** it accordingly, and cannot, say, theoretically ascribe these properties to it; for that would be for the determining power of judgment, in the speculative aim of our reason, in order to see into what the supreme world-cause is ***in itself***. Here, however, it is only a matter of what concept we have to make for ourselves of it according to the constitution of our cognitive faculties and whether we have to assume its existence, in order likewise to procure only practical reality for an end which pure practical reason, without any such presupposition, lays upon us _a priori_ to effect with all our powers, {{ 436 }} i.e., in order to be able to think an intended effect as possible. That concept may well be transcendent for the speculative reason; {{{ 457 }}} the properties too, which we ascribe to the being thereby thought, may, used objectively, conceal an anthropomorphism in themselves: the aim of their use is also not to want to determine its nature, which is unattainable for us, but rather to determine ourselves and our will accordingly. Just as we name a cause according to the concept that we have of the effect (but only with regard to its relation to this), without on that account wanting to determine inwardly the inner constitution of it through the properties that must be known to us solely and alone of such causes and given through experience; just as we, e.g., ascribe to the soul, among other things, also a _vis locomotiva_, because movements of the body really arise whose cause lies in its representations, without on that account wanting to ascribe to it the only way in which we know moving forces (namely through attraction, pressure, impact, hence motion, which always presuppose an extended being): — so too we shall have to assume ***something*** that contains the ground of the possibility and the practical reality, i.e., the feasibility, of a necessary moral final end; this, however, according to the constitution of the effect expected from it, we can think as a wise being governing the world according to moral {{ 437 }} laws and, in accordance with the constitution of our cognitive faculties, must think as a cause of things distinct from nature, only in order to express the ***relation*** of this being, which exceeds all our cognitive faculties, to the object of ***our*** practical reason: yet without thereby wanting, on that account, to ascribe to it theoretically the only causality of this kind known to us, namely an understanding and a will, indeed without even wanting to distinguish objectively the causality thought in it, with regard to that which is the final end ***for us***, as in this being itself, from the causality with regard to nature (and its determinations of ends in general), but able to assume this distinction only as subjectively necessary for the constitution of our cognitive faculty and as valid for the reflecting, not for the objectively determining power of judgment. But when it comes to the practical, then such a ***regulative*** principle (for prudence or wisdom): to act in accordance with that which, according to the constitution of our cognitive faculties, can alone be thought as possible by us in a certain way, as ends, is at the same time ***constitutive***, i.e., practically determining; while the very same principle, as a principle for judging the objective possibility of things, is in no way theoretically determining (namely, that {{{ 458 }}} to the object too the only kind of possibility pertains that pertains to our {{ 438 }} capacity to think), but is a merely ***regulative*** principle for the reflecting power of judgment.

Remark.

This moral proof is not, say, a newly invented one, but at most only a newly elaborated ground of proof; for it has already lain in the human faculty of reason before its earliest budding and is only ever more developed with the ongoing culture of it. As soon as human beings began to reflect on right and wrong, at a time when they still looked indifferently past the purposiveness of nature, used it without thinking thereby of anything other than the accustomed course of nature, the judgment must have unavoidably presented itself: that it could never in the outcome be all the same whether a human being had conducted himself honestly or falsely, fairly or violently, even if up to the end of his life, at least visibly, he had encountered no fortune for his virtues, or no punishment for his crimes. It is: as if they perceived in themselves a voice, that it must go otherwise; hence there must also have lain hidden the, although obscure, representation of something to which they felt themselves obligated to strive, with which such an outcome cannot be reconciled at all, or with which, if they once regarded the course of the world as the only order of things, they in turn did not know how to unite that inner determination of ends of their mind. Now they might represent to themselves the way in which such an irregularity (which must be far more revolting to the human mind than the blind chance that one might perhaps want to lay at the basis of the judging of nature as a principle) could be balanced out, in all sorts of however {{ 439 }} crude a manner; yet they could never conceive any other principle of the possibility of the union of nature with their inner moral law than a supreme cause governing the world according to moral laws: because a final end set as a duty in them, and a nature without any final end outside them, in which nevertheless that end is to become actual, stand in contradiction. About the inner constitution of that world-cause they might now hatch much nonsense; that moral relation in the world-government always remained the same, which is universally graspable for the most uncultivated reason, insofar as it considers itself as practical, with which, on the contrary, the speculative by {{{ 459 }}} far cannot keep equal step. — Also, in all probability it was through this moral interest that attention was first aroused to the beauty and ends of nature, which then served excellently to strengthen that idea, but yet could not ground it, still less dispense with the former, because even the investigation of the ends of nature gets that immediate interest, which shows itself in so great a measure in the admiration of them without regard to any advantage to be drawn from it, only in relation to the final end.
