## § 88. Restriction of the validity of the moral proof

Reason in its purity, considered as a practical faculty — that is, as the capacity to determine the free exercise of our causality by means of ideas (pure concepts of reason) — not only holds within the moral law a regulative principle for our actions, but through that very law simultaneously places in our hands a subjectively constitutive one, lodged in the concept of an object that reason alone is able to think and that ought to be made actual in the world through our actions in conformity with that law. The idea of a final end in the use of freedom under moral laws therefore possesses subjectively ***practical*** reality. We are determined _a priori_ by reason to further, with all our powers, the best for the world, which consists in the union of the greatest welfare of the rational beings of the world with the supreme condition of the good within them, that is, of universal happiness with morality in fullest accord with law. In this final end the possibility of the one part, namely happiness, is empirically conditioned, that is, dependent on the constitution of nature (on whether it accords with this end or not) and in theoretical respect {{ 430 }} problematic; whereas the other part, namely morality, with respect to which we are free from the cooperation of nature, is, as to its possibility, firmly settled _a priori_ and dogmatically certain. In order, then, for the concept of a final end of the world's rational beings to possess objective theoretical reality, it is required not only that we have a final end set before us _a priori_, but also that the creation, that is, the world itself, have a final end as to its existence: which, were it capable of being proved _a priori_, would add objective reality to the subjective reality of the final end. For if the creation has a final end at all, we can think it in no other way than as one that must {{{ 454 }}} accord with the moral end (which alone makes the concept of an end possible). Now we do indeed find ends in the world: and physical teleology exhibits them to such a degree that, if we judge in conformity with reason, we ultimately have grounds for adopting, as a principle for the investigation of nature, that nothing whatever in nature is without an end; yet the final end of nature we seek in vain within nature itself. This end therefore can and must, just as the idea of it lies only in reason, be sought, even as regards its objective possibility, solely in rational beings. But the practical reason of the latter not only specifies this final end, it also determines this concept with respect to the conditions under which a final end of the creation can be thought by {{ 431 }} us at all.

The question that now arises is whether the concept of a final end of the creation cannot also have its objective reality established sufficiently for the theoretical demands of pure reason — if not apodictically for the determining power of judgment, yet sufficiently for the maxims of the theoretically reflecting power of judgment. This is the least that one can require of speculative philosophy, which undertakes to connect the moral end with the natural ends by means of the idea of a single end; yet even this little is still far more than it is ever able to accomplish.

According to the principle of the theoretically reflecting power of judgment we would say: If we have reason to posit, for nature's purposive products, a highest cause of nature whose causality, in regard to the actuality of those products (the creation), has to be conceived as differing in kind from what the mechanism of nature demands, namely as the causality of an understanding, then we shall likewise have sufficient reason to think, in this primordial being, not only ends distributed throughout nature but a final end as well — not, indeed, in order to establish the existence of such a being, but at least (as happened in physical teleology) to satisfy ourselves that the possibility {{ 432 }} of such a world can be made comprehensible to us not merely in terms of ends, but also only by attributing a final end to its existence.

But a final end is nothing other than a concept belonging to our practical reason, and it can neither be derived from any data of experience for the theoretical judging of nature nor be referred to any cognition of nature. No use of this concept is possible except solely for practical {{{ 455 }}} reason under moral laws; and the final end of the creation is that constitution of the world which accords with what alone we can determinately specify according to laws, namely with the final end of our pure practical reason, and indeed insofar as it is to be practical. — Now, through the moral law, which imposes this final end upon us, we have, in a practical respect — namely for the purpose of applying our powers to bringing it about — a ground for assuming the possibility, the achievability, of that end, and consequently also (since, without the concurrence of nature in a condition of it not standing within our power, the bringing about of it would be impossible) a nature of things that accords with it. We thus have a moral ground for thinking, for a world, a final end of the creation as well.

This is not yet the inference from moral teleology {{ 433 }} to a theology, that is, to the existence of a moral author of the world, but merely to a final end of the creation, determined in the manner described. That now, for this creation — that is, for the being of things conformed to a ***final end*** — there must be assumed, first, an intelligent being, and, second, not merely (as for the possibility of the things of nature, which we were compelled to judge as ***ends***) an intelligent one, but rather one that is at the same time a ***moral*** being as author of the world, and hence a ***God***: this is a second inference, of such a character that one sees it is rendered solely for the power of judgment under concepts of practical reason, and, as such a verdict, for the reflecting rather than the determining power of judgment. For we cannot presume to have insight that, although in us moral-practical reason is essentially distinct in its principles from technical-practical reason, it must be so as well in the supreme cause of the world, if it is assumed as an intelligence, and that a special and distinct kind of its causality is required for the final end, over and above what is required merely for ends of nature; and hence that in our final end we have not merely a ***moral ground*** for assuming a final end of the creation (as effect), but also a ***moral being*** as the original ground of the creation. We can, however, indeed say: that ***in accordance with the constitution of our rational faculty*** {{ 434 }} we cannot in any way make comprehensible to ourselves the possibility of such a purposiveness, related ***to the moral law*** and its object, as is present in this final end, without a world-author and ruler who is at the same time a moral legislator.

{{{ 456 }}} That a supreme, morally legislating author is real has thus been sufficiently shown only ***for the practical use*** of our reason, without anything being theoretically determined regarding his existence. For reason, in order that its end be possible — an end that is in any case set for us through its own legislation — requires an idea by which the obstacle arising from the incapacity to comply with it under the mere concept of nature as world is cleared away (sufficiently for the reflecting power of judgment); and this idea thereby acquires practical reality, even though all means of furnishing it such reality in a theoretical respect, for the explanation of nature and the determination of the supreme cause, are entirely wanting to it for speculative cognition. For the theoretically reflecting power of judgment, physical teleology proved sufficiently, from the ends of nature, an intelligent cause of the world; for the practical, moral teleology accomplishes this through the concept of a final end, which it is compelled to ascribe to the creation in a practical respect. The {{ 435 }} idea of God, as moral world-author, cannot, to be sure, have its objective reality demonstrated through physical ends ***alone***; nevertheless, when the cognition of these is combined with that of the moral, the former, by virtue of the maxim of pure reason to pursue unity of principles as far as can be done, are of great importance for coming to the aid of the practical reality of that idea by means of the reality that it already has, in a theoretical respect, for the power of judgment.

Here it is now, for the prevention of a readily arising misunderstanding, most necessary to remark that we can, first, ***think*** these properties of the highest being only by analogy. For how should we investigate its nature, of which experience can show us nothing similar? Secondly, that by this analogy we also merely think it, and cannot ***cognize*** it thereby and, say, ascribe those properties to it theoretically; for that would be a task for the determining power of judgment, in the speculative aim of our reason, of gaining insight into what the supreme cause of the world is ***in itself***. Here, however, it is solely a matter of what concept we are to form of it in keeping with how our cognitive faculties are constituted, and whether its existence has to be assumed by us, in order likewise to procure merely practical reality for an end that pure practical reason, apart from any such presupposition, lays upon us _a priori_ to accomplish with all our powers, {{ 436 }} that is, in order to be able to think an intended effect as possible. That concept may well be extravagant for speculative reason {{{ 457 }}}; the properties, too, that we ascribe to the being thereby thought may, used objectively, conceal an anthropomorphism within themselves: yet the aim of their use is not to wish to determine its nature, which is unattainable for us, but rather to give determination to ourselves and to our own will in keeping with it. Just as we name a cause according to the concept we have of the effect (but only with regard to its relation to the latter), without thereby meaning to fix internally its inner nature through the properties by which such causes can be known to us solely and alone and which must be furnished through experience; just as, for example, we ascribe to the soul, among other things, a _vim locomotivam_, because movements of the body really do arise whose cause lies in its representations, without thereby wishing to ascribe to it the only mode in which we are acquainted with moving forces (namely through attraction, pressure, impact, and hence motion, all of which always presuppose an extended being): — so likewise we shall have to assume ***something*** that contains the ground of the possibility and of the practical reality, that is, the achievability, of a necessary moral final end; yet, in keeping with the character of the effect expected from it, we can think this as a wise being that rules the world in keeping with moral {{ 437 }} laws, and, in accordance with the constitution of our cognitive faculties, we must think it as a cause of things distinct from nature, merely so as to give expression to the ***relation*** that this being, exceeding all our cognitive faculties, bears to the object of ***our*** practical reason: yet without meaning, on that account, to attribute to it in theory the sole causality of this sort that we know, namely an understanding and a will, or indeed even to distinguish objectively, within this being itself, the causality thought in it with respect to what is ***for us*** the final end from the causality with respect to nature (and its determinations of ends in general), but rather being able to assume this distinction only as subjectively necessary for the constitution of our cognitive faculty and valid for the reflecting, not for the objectively determining, power of judgment. But when it comes to the practical, then such a ***regulative*** principle (for prudence or wisdom) — to act, toward that which, given the constitution of our cognitive faculties, can be thought by us as possible in only a certain way, as befits ends — is at the same time ***constitutive***, that is, practically determining; whereas the very same thing, as a principle for judging the objective possibility of things, is by no means theoretically determining (as though {{{ 458 }}} the sole kind of possibility that belongs to our {{ 438 }} faculty of thought belonged to the object as well), but is a merely ***regulative*** principle for the reflecting power of judgment.

Remark.

This moral proof is not exactly a newly invented one, but at most only a newly expounded ground of proof; for it had already lain within the human rational faculty even before the earliest budding of that faculty, and with the continuing cultivation of it is only ever more developed. As soon as human beings began to reflect on right and wrong — at a time when they still looked with indifference upon the purposiveness of nature, making use of it without thinking anything in it other than the accustomed course of nature — the judgment inevitably had to present itself that in the outcome it can never be a matter of indifference whether a person has behaved honestly or falsely, fairly or violently, even if right up to the end of his life he has, at least visibly, met with no happiness for his virtues nor any punishment for his crimes. It is as though they perceived within themselves a voice declaring that things must turn out otherwise; and hence there must also have lain hidden the representation, however obscure, of something that they felt bound to strive after, with which such an outcome could not at all be reconciled, or with which, once they regarded the course of the world as the sole order of things, they were again unable to unite that inner determination of ends of their mind. Now, in whatever manner and however still so {{ 439 }} crudely they might represent to themselves how such an irregularity (which to the human mind is bound to be far more offensive than the blind chance that one might perhaps wish to lay at the basis of the judging of nature as its principle) could be redressed, they could nevertheless never conceive of any other principle of the possibility of nature's being united with their inner moral law than a highest cause that rules the world in accordance with moral laws: for a final end laid upon them as a duty within them, and a nature devoid of any final end outside them, in which that end is nonetheless to become actual, stand in mutual contradiction. About the inner constitution of that cause of the world they might now hatch much nonsense; that moral relation within the governance of the world remained ever the same, one universally graspable by the least cultivated reason, insofar as it regards itself as practical, but with which, by contrast, speculative reason cannot by {{{ 459 }}} far keep equal pace. — In all probability, too, it was through this moral interest that attention was first awakened to the beauty and the ends of nature, which then served admirably to reinforce that idea, yet could not ground it, still less dispense with it, because even the investigation of the ends of nature acquires that immediate interest — which shows itself in such great measure in the admiration of nature without regard to any advantage to be drawn from it — only in relation to the final end.
