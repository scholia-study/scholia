## {{{ 311 }}} § 48. On the relation of genius to taste

The ***judging*** of beautiful objects as such calls for ***taste***; fine art itself, however — namely the ***bringing-forth*** of such objects — calls for ***genius***.

If genius is taken to be a talent for fine art (this being the sense that properly belongs to the word), and if, on that understanding, one wants to break it down into the faculties whose combination is required to make up such a talent, then one must first fix precisely the difference between beauty in nature — for judging which taste alone is enough — and beauty in art, whose very possibility (a matter that judging {{ 188 }} such an object must likewise keep in view) presupposes genius.

Beauty in nature is a ***beautiful thing***; beauty in art is a ***beautiful representation*** of a thing.

To judge something in nature as beautiful, I do not first require a concept of what kind of thing the object is supposed to be; in other words, I need not be acquainted with its material purposiveness (its end), for the bare form, even where the end is unknown, is what pleases on its own account in the judging. If, on the other hand, the object is presented as something produced by art and is to be pronounced beautiful in that capacity, then — since art always assumes an end in the cause (and in its causality) — one must first lay down a concept of what the thing is supposed to be; and because the perfection of a thing consists in the agreement of its manifold with an inner determination of it as an end, the judging of beauty in art will have to reckon with that perfection as well, something about which the judging of a beauty in nature (as such) raises no question whatever. — Admittedly, in judging above all the living objects of nature — say, a human being or a horse — one usually also takes objective purposiveness into account in order to {{ 189 }} pass verdict on their beauty; but in that case the judgment is no longer purely aesthetic, that is, a mere judgment of taste. Nature is then judged not as it seems to be art, but insofar as it genuinely ***is*** art (even if of a superhuman kind); and the teleological judgment supplies the aesthetic one with its {{{ 312 }}} ground and condition, on which the latter has to rely. Here too, when someone remarks, for instance, "that is a beautiful woman," what is actually being thought is nothing but this: that nature, in this shape, gives a beautiful presentation of the ends of the female frame; for one has to look past the bare form toward a concept, so that the object comes to be thought in this manner by way of a logically conditioned aesthetic judgment.

It is exactly here that fine art displays its excellence: it renders in beautiful description things that, in nature itself, would be ugly or displeasing. The Furies, diseases, the ravages of war, and suchlike can, taken as harmful things, be given a very beautiful description, and may even be depicted in a painting; there is but one species of ugliness that cannot be rendered true to nature without wrecking all aesthetic satisfaction, and with it beauty in art: namely, the kind that stirs up ***disgust***. For in that strange sensation, which rests on sheer imagination, the object is presented as if it were pressing itself upon us to be enjoyed, a thing against which {{ 190 }} we nevertheless strive with all our force; and so, within our sensation, the artistic representation of the object ceases to be told apart from the nature of the object itself, whereupon the former can no longer possibly count as beautiful. The art of sculpture, likewise, because in its works art is nearly mistaken for nature, has barred from its figures any direct representation of ugly objects, allowing them instead — death, say (in the guise of a beautiful genius), or the courage of war (in the figure of Mars) — to be presented only through an allegory or through attributes that make a pleasing impression, and thus only indirectly, by way of an interpretation supplied by reason and not for a merely aesthetic power of judgment.

This is what may be said about the beautiful representation of an object — a representation that is, strictly, nothing more than the form of the presentation of a concept, the manner by which that concept comes to be communicated to everyone. — Yet the giving of this form to a product of fine art demands taste and nothing more; against taste the artist gauges his work, once he has trained and refined it on all sorts of examples drawn from art or from nature, and through repeated, frequently toilsome efforts to meet its demand he arrives at the form that satisfies him: so the form is not, so to speak, a matter of inspiration or of some free surge of the mental faculties, but of a slow and downright vexing correction, whose aim is to render it fit for the thought {{ 191 }} {{{ 313 }}} while still doing no harm to the freedom in the play of those faculties.

Taste, however, is only a power of judging, not a productive power; and something's being in accordance with it does not on that account make it a work of fine art: it might be a product that belongs to useful and mechanical art, or indeed to science, turned out in keeping with settled rules — rules that admit of being learned and have to be observed to the letter. The pleasing form one confers on it merely serves as a vehicle for communication and, so to say, a mode of presentation, in regard to which a certain latitude still remains, even where the thing is in other respects tied to a fixed end. So we require of a table service, or of a moral treatise, or even of a sermon, that it carry this form of fine art in itself, without on that account appearing ***affected***; and yet we shall not for all that name such things works of fine art. Under that heading, by contrast, we count a poem, a musical composition, a gallery of pictures, and the like; and in a putative work of fine art of this sort one may frequently observe genius without taste in the one case, and taste without genius in the other.
