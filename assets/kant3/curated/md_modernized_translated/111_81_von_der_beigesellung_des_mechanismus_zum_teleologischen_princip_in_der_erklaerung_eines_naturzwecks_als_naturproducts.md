## {{ 374 }} § 81. On the coupling of mechanism with the teleological principle in the explanation of a natural end as a product of nature

Just as, according to the preceding §, the mechanism of nature cannot on its own suffice for us to conceive by its means the possibility of an organized {{{ 422 }}} being, but must rather (at least given the constitution of our cognitive faculty) be subordinated from the outset to a cause acting intentionally, so too the merely teleological ground of such a being is equally insufficient for regarding and judging it at the same time as a product of nature, unless the mechanism of the latter is coupled with the former, as though it were the instrument of an intentionally acting cause to whose ends nature is nonetheless subordinated in its mechanical laws. Our reason cannot comprehend the possibility of such a union of two wholly different kinds of causality — that of nature in its universal lawfulness with an idea that restricts nature to a particular form for which it contains no ground of its own; this possibility lies in the supersensible substrate of nature, about which we can determine nothing affirmatively except that it is the being in itself of which we know merely the appearance. Yet the principle — that everything we take to belong to this nature (_Phaenomenon_) {{ 375 }} and to be a product of it must also be thought as connected with it according to mechanical laws — nonetheless retains its force: for without this kind of causality organized beings, though ends of nature, would still not be products of nature.

Now if the teleological principle of the generation of these beings is adopted (as indeed it cannot be otherwise), then one can ground the cause of their inwardly purposive form on either ***occasionalism*** or ***prestabilism***. On the former, the supreme cause of the world would, in accordance with its idea, on the occasion of every mating directly impart the organic formation to the matter that mingles therein; on the latter, it would have placed into the initial products of this its wisdom only the predisposition, by means of which an organic being brings forth its own kind and the species continually maintains itself, and likewise the loss of individuals is continuously made good by a nature that is at the same time working toward their destruction. If one adopts occasionalism for the production of organized beings, then all of nature is thereby entirely lost, and with it every use of reason for judging the possibility of such a kind of product; one may therefore presume that no one who has any concern for philosophy will adopt this system.

***Prestabilism***, in turn, can proceed in two ways. {{ 376 }} For it regards every organic {{{ 423 }}} being generated by its own kind either as the ***educt*** or as the ***product*** of the former. The system of generations as mere educts is called that of ***individual preformation***, or also the ***theory of evolution***; that of generations as products is called the system of ***epigenesis***. This latter can also be called the system ***of generic preformation***: because the productive capacity of those that generate operates in accordance with the inner purposive predispositions allotted to their lineage, so that the specific form was _virtualiter_ preformed. Accordingly, the opposing theory of individual preformation could also better be called the ***theory of involution*** (or that of encasement).

The champions of the ***theory of evolution***, who exempt every individual from the formative power of nature so as to let it come directly from the hand of the creator, were nevertheless unwilling to venture to have this happen according to the hypothesis of occasionalism, whereby mating would be a mere formality, under which a supreme intelligent cause of the world would have resolved on each occasion to form a fruit with its own immediate hand and to leave to the mother only its unfolding and nourishment. They declared themselves for preformation; {{ 377 }} just as if it made no difference whether such forms are let to arise supernaturally at the beginning or in the course of the world, and as if a great many supernatural arrangements were not rather spared by occasional creation — arrangements that would be required so that the embryo formed at the world's beginning should not, throughout the long stretch until its development, suffer from the destructive forces of nature and should preserve itself unharmed, and likewise an immeasurably greater number of such preformed beings than would ever be brought to development, and with them just as many creations, would thereby be rendered needless and purposeless. Yet they did at least want to leave something here to nature, so as not to lapse entirely into a complete hyperphysics that can dispense with all explanation of nature. They did indeed still hold fast to their hyperphysics, even when they found in malformations (which one surely cannot possibly take to be ends of nature) an admirable purposiveness, though it might be aimed at nothing more than that an anatomist should one day take offense at it, as at a purposeless purposiveness, and feel a dispiriting admiration. But the generation of hybrids they simply could not fit into the system of preformation, {{{ 424 }}} and instead had to grant to the seed of the male creatures — to which they had otherwise conceded nothing but the mechanical property of serving as the embryo's first nourishment — on top of all this a {{ 378 }} purposively formative power: which, however, with regard to the whole product of a generation from two creatures of the same kind, they were unwilling to allow to either of the two.

If, by contrast, one did not even recognize in the defender of ***epigenesis*** the great advantage he has over the former with respect to the empirical grounds for the proof of his theory, reason would nevertheless already be disposed in advance with marked favor toward his manner of explanation, because it regards nature, with respect to those things one can originally represent as possible only according to the causality of ends, as itself productive — at least so far as reproduction is concerned — and not merely as unfolding, and thus, with the smallest possible expenditure of the supernatural, leaves to nature everything that follows from the first beginning onward (without, however, determining anything about this first beginning, at which physics altogether founders, whatever chain of causes it may try to work with).

With regard to this theory of epigenesis, no one has done more, both toward proving it and toward establishing the genuine principles of its application — in part by setting bounds to an overly presumptuous use of it — than the court councillor ***Blumenbach***. It is from organized matter that he begins all physical modes of explaining these formations {{ 379 }}. For that raw matter should originally have formed itself according to mechanical laws, that life should have been able to spring from the nature of the lifeless, and that matter should have been able of its own accord to settle into the form of a self-maintaining purposiveness — this he rightly declares to be contrary to reason; yet at the same time he leaves to the mechanism of nature, under this ***principle*** — inscrutable to us — of an original ***organization***, an indeterminable yet also unmistakable share, in respect of which the capacity of matter (as distinct from the merely mechanical ***formative power*** that generally belongs to it) is called by him, in an organized body, a ***formative drive*** (standing, as it were, under the higher direction and guidance of the former).
