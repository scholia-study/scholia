## {{ 374 }} § 81. On adjoining mechanism to the teleological principle in explaining a natural end as a natural product

Just as the mechanism of nature, as the foregoing § established, cannot of itself suffice for thinking, on that basis, the possibility of an organized {{{ 422 }}} being, but must instead be subordinated at the outset (at least given how our faculty of cognition is constituted) to a cause that acts by design, so likewise the bare teleological ground of such a being does not suffice for us to consider and judge it at once as a product that nature yields, so long as the mechanism proper to the latter is not conjoined with the former as the instrument, so to speak, of a designedly acting cause, to whose purposes nature in its mechanical laws is subordinated all the same. How two entirely distinct sorts of causality could be united in this way — namely nature under its general conformity to law together with an idea that confines nature to a specific form for which nature by itself holds no ground whatever — is something our reason cannot grasp; that unification resides in nature's supersensible substrate, concerning which we can affirmatively establish nothing beyond the point that it constitutes the being in itself whose mere appearance is all we are acquainted with. The principle, however — that whatever we assume to pertain to this nature (_Phaenomenon_) {{ 375 }} and to count as its product we must likewise conceive as bound up with it by mechanical laws — retains its validity none the less: for lacking this species of causality, organized beings, as ends of nature, would after all be no products of nature.

Supposing, then, that one accepts the teleological principle governing how these beings are generated (as indeed nothing else is possible), one may lay at the base of the cause of their internally purposive form either ***occasionalism*** or ***prestabilism***. On the former, the supreme cause of the world would, in accordance with its idea, on the occasion of every mating directly impart the organic formation to the matter that mingles therein; on the latter, it would have placed into the initial products of this its wisdom only the predisposition, by means of which an organic being brings forth its own kind and the species continually maintains itself, and likewise the loss of individuals is continuously made good by a nature that is at the same time working toward their destruction. Should one accept occasionalism for the bringing-forth of organized beings, the whole of nature is in that case utterly forfeited, and along with it every exercise of reason in appraising whether products of such a kind are possible; hence one may take it for granted that anyone who cares in the least about philosophy will never embrace this system.

***Prestabilism***, in turn, can proceed in two ways. {{ 376 }} For it regards every organic {{{ 423 }}} being generated by its own kind either as the ***educt*** or as the ***product*** of the former. Where generations are regarded as mere educts, the system bears the name of ***individual preformation***, or else the ***theory of evolution***; where they are regarded as products, it is termed the system of ***epigenesis***. One may equally designate this last as the system ***of generic preformation***: for the generative capacity of the parents does proceed according to the inner purposive predispositions apportioned to their stock, so that the specific form stood _virtualiter_ preformed. In keeping with this, the rival theory of individual preformation might with more propriety be given the name ***theory of involution*** (or of encasement).

The champions of the ***theory of evolution***, who exempt every individual from the formative power of nature so as to let it come directly from the hand of the creator, were nevertheless unwilling to venture to have this happen according to the hypothesis of occasionalism, whereby mating would be a mere formality, under which a supreme intelligent cause of the world would have resolved on each occasion to form a fruit with its own immediate hand and to leave to the mother only its unfolding and nourishment. They declared themselves for preformation; {{ 377 }} just as if it made no difference whether such forms are let to arise supernaturally at the beginning or in the course of the world, and as if a great many supernatural arrangements were not rather spared by occasional creation — arrangements that would be required so that the embryo formed at the world's beginning should not, throughout the long stretch until its development, suffer from the destructive forces of nature and should preserve itself unharmed, and likewise an immeasurably greater number of such preformed beings than would ever be brought to development, and with them just as many creations, would thereby be rendered needless and purposeless. Yet they did at least want to leave something here to nature, so as not to lapse entirely into a complete hyperphysics that can dispense with all explanation of nature. They did indeed still hold fast to their hyperphysics, even when they found in malformations (which one surely cannot possibly take to be ends of nature) an admirable purposiveness, though it might be aimed at nothing more than that an anatomist should one day take offense at it, as at a purposeless purposiveness, and feel a dispiriting admiration. But the generation of hybrids they simply could not fit into the system of preformation, {{{ 424 }}} and instead had to grant to the seed of the male creatures — to which they had otherwise conceded nothing but the mechanical property of serving as the embryo's first nourishment — on top of all this a {{ 378 }} purposively formative power: which, however, with regard to the whole product of a generation from two creatures of the same kind, they were unwilling to allow to either of the two.

If, by contrast, one did not even recognize in the defender of ***epigenesis*** the great advantage he has over the former with respect to the empirical grounds for the proof of his theory, reason would nevertheless already be disposed in advance with marked favor toward his manner of explanation, because it regards nature, with respect to those things one can originally represent as possible only according to the causality of ends, as itself productive — at least so far as reproduction is concerned — and not merely as unfolding, and thus, with the smallest possible expenditure of the supernatural, leaves to nature everything that follows from the first beginning onward (without, however, determining anything about this first beginning, at which physics altogether founders, whatever chain of causes it may try to work with).

With regard to this theory of epigenesis, no one has done more, both toward proving it and toward establishing the genuine principles of its application — in part by setting bounds to an overly presumptuous use of it — than the court councillor ***Blumenbach***. It is from organized matter that he begins all physical modes of explaining these formations {{ 379 }}. For the claim that crude matter shaped itself originally in conformity with mechanical laws, that life could have arisen out of the nature of what is lifeless, and that matter, of its own accord, could have fitted itself into the form of a purposiveness that sustains itself — this he justly pronounces repugnant to reason; and yet he simultaneously grants to the natural mechanism, beneath this ***principle*** of an original ***organization*** that is unfathomable to us, a share both indeterminable and yet unmistakable, in view of which he names the capacity of matter (as opposed to the merely mechanical ***formative power*** that everywhere inheres in it), within an organized body, a ***formative drive*** (standing, so to speak, under the loftier direction and instruction of the former).
