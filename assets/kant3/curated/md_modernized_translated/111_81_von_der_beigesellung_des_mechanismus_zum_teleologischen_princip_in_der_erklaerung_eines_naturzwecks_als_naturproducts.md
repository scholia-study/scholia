## {{ 374 }} § 81. On the association of mechanism with the teleological principle in the explanation of a natural end as a natural product

Just as the mechanism of nature, according to the preceding §, cannot suffice on its own to make conceivable thereby the possibility of an organized {{{ 422 }}} being, but (at least according to the constitution of our cognitive faculty) must originally be subordinated to an intentionally acting cause: so too the mere teleological ground of such a being suffices just as little to consider and judge it at the same time as a product of nature, if the mechanism of the latter is not associated with the former, as it were as the instrument of an intentionally acting cause, to whose ends nature is nevertheless subordinated in its mechanical laws. The possibility of such a union of two entirely different kinds of causality, of nature in its universal lawfulness with an idea that restricts the former to a particular form, for which it of itself contains no ground at all, our reason does not comprehend; it lies in the supersensible substrate of nature, of which we can affirmatively determine nothing except that it is the being in itself of which we merely know the appearance. But the principle: that everything we assume as belonging to this nature (_Phaenomenon_) {{ 375 }} and as a product of it, must be thought of as connected with it also according to mechanical laws, nonetheless remains in its force: because without this kind of causality organized beings, as ends of nature, would yet not be natural products.

If now the teleological principle of the generation of these beings is assumed (as indeed it cannot be otherwise): then one can lay at the basis of the cause of their internally purposive form either ***occasionalism*** or ***prestabilism***. According to the former the supreme world-cause would, in accordance with its idea, on the occasion of every mating impart immediately the organic formation to the matter mingling in it; according to the second it would have placed into the initial products of this its wisdom only the predisposition, by means of which an organic being brings forth its like and the species continually preserves itself, just as the loss of the individuals is continuously replaced by their nature, which at the same time labors at their destruction. If one assumes occasionalism of the production of organized beings, then all nature is herein entirely lost, and with it also all use of reason for judging about the possibility of such a kind of products; hence one can presuppose that no one will assume this system to whom anything of philosophy is of concern.

***Prestabilism*** can now in turn proceed in a twofold manner. {{ 376 }} It namely considers each organic being generated by its like {{{ 423 }}} either as the ***educt*** or as the ***product*** of the former. The system of generations as mere educts is called that of ***individual preformation***, or also the ***theory of evolution***; that of generations as products is called the system of ***epigenesis***. This latter can also be called the system ***of generic preformation***: because the productive faculty of the generators was nevertheless preformed _virtualiter_ in accordance with the inner purposive predispositions that fell to their stem, hence as to the specific form. In accordance with this one could also better call the opposing theory of individual preformation the ***theory of involution*** (or that of encasement).

The defenders of the ***theory of evolution***, who exempt every individual from the formative power of nature in order to let it come immediately from the hand of the creator, did not yet, however, want to venture to let this happen according to the hypothesis of occasionalism, so that the mating would be a mere formality, under which a supreme intelligent world-cause had resolved each time to form a fetus with its immediate hand and to leave to the mother only its unfolding and nourishment. They declared themselves for preformation; {{ 377 }} as if it were not all the same to let such forms arise in a supernatural way at the beginning or in the course of the world, and as if a great multitude of supernatural arrangements were not rather spared by occasional creation, which would be required so that the embryo formed at the beginning of the world should not, throughout the long time until its development, suffer from the destructive forces of nature and should preserve itself unharmed, and as if an immeasurably greater number of such preformed beings than were ever to be developed, and with them just as many creations, were not thereby rendered unnecessary and purposeless. Yet they wanted at least to leave something herein to nature, in order not to fall into complete hyperphysics, which can dispense with all explanation of nature. They indeed still held fast to their hyperphysics, even when they found in monstrous births (which one surely cannot possibly hold to be ends of nature) an admirable purposiveness, even if it should be aimed only at this, that an anatomist should some day take offense at it, as a purposeless purposiveness, and feel a depressing admiration. But the generation of hybrids they could absolutely not fit into the system of preformation, {{{ 424 }}} but had to concede, on top of everything else, to the seed of the male creatures, to which they had otherwise granted nothing but the mechanical property of serving as the first nutriment of the embryo, yet a {{ 378 }} purposively formative power: which, however, with regard to the whole product of a generation from two creatures of the same species, they were unwilling to grant to either of the two.

If, on the other hand, one did not even recognize in the defender of ***epigenesis*** the great advantage that he has over the former with respect to the grounds of experience for the proof of his theory: then reason would yet already in advance be disposed with preeminent favor toward his manner of explanation, because, with regard to the things that one can originally represent to oneself as possible only according to the causality of ends, it considers nature, at least as far as propagation is concerned, as itself bringing forth, not merely as developing, and so, with the smallest possible expenditure of the supernatural, leaves everything that follows, from the first beginning on, to nature (without however determining anything about this first beginning, at which physics altogether founders, attempt it with what chain of causes it will).

With regard to this theory of epigenesis no one has achieved more both for the proof of it and for the grounding of the genuine principles of its application, partly through the restriction of a too presumptuous use of it, than Privy Councilor ***Blumenbach***. From organized matter he sets out all physical manner of explanation of these formations. {{ 379 }} For that raw matter should originally have formed itself according to mechanical laws, that life should have been able to arise out of the nature of the lifeless, and that matter should have been able of itself to fit into the form of a self-preserving purposiveness, he rightly declares to be contrary to reason; but at the same time he leaves to natural mechanism, under this for us unfathomable ***principle*** of an original ***organization***, an indeterminable, yet also unmistakable share, for which the faculty of matter (in distinction from the merely mechanical ***formative power*** universally inhering in it) is called by him, in an organized body, a (as it were, standing under the higher guidance and direction of the former) ***formative drive***.
