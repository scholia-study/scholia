## {{ 53 }} § 17. On the ideal of beauty

No objective rule of taste can exist that would fix through concepts what counts as beautiful. For any judgment arising from this source is aesthetic; that is to say, what determines it is the subject's feeling and not some concept of an object. To hunt for a principle of taste that would deliver, through determinate concepts, the universal criterion of the beautiful is labor spent in vain, since the thing sought is at once impossible and self-contradictory in itself. The universal communicability of the sensation (of satisfaction or dissatisfaction)—and indeed a communicability that comes about without any concept—together with the unanimity, so far as it may be reached, of every age and {{{ 232 }}} people with respect to this feeling when certain objects are represented: this is the empirical criterion, feeble though it be and hardly enough to warrant a conjecture, of the descent of a taste thus attested by examples from that deeply concealed basis, shared by all humankind, of unanimity in their appraisal of the forms whereby objects are given to them.

This is why we treat certain products of taste as ***exemplary***: not in the sense that taste might be picked up by copying what others do. For taste has to be a power proper to oneself; and someone who copies a model does display dexterity to the extent that he succeeds, {{ 54 }} but he displays taste only to the extent that he is able to appraise that model on his own.[^*] What follows from this, though, is that the supreme model—the archetype of taste—amounts to a mere idea, one that each person has to generate within himself, and by reference to which he is to appraise whatever is an object of taste, whatever serves as an instance of judging by taste, and indeed the very taste of anyone at all. ***Idea***, strictly speaking, denotes a concept of reason, whereas ***ideal*** denotes the representation of a single being taken as commensurate with an idea. For this reason that archetype of taste—resting, to be sure, upon the indeterminate idea reason holds of a maximum, yet admitting of representation not by way of concepts but solely in a particular presentation—is more aptly termed the ideal of the beautiful, something we endeavor to produce in ourselves even while we do not actually possess it. It can, however, be nothing more than an ideal of the imagination, and this precisely because its ground lies not in concepts but in presentation; and the power {{ 55 }} of presentation is nothing other than the imagination. — By what route, then, do we reach an ideal of beauty of this sort? _A priori_ or empirically? And equally: which sort of the beautiful admits of an ideal at all?

To begin with, it is worth observing that the beauty for which one would seek an ideal cannot be a ***vague*** one, but must be a beauty rendered ***fixed*** through a concept of objective purposiveness, and hence one that pertains not to the object of an entirely pure judgment of taste but to that of a judgment of taste which is in part intellectualized {{{ 233 }}}. That is to say: in whatever type of grounds of judging an ideal is supposed to occur, there must lie at its foundation some idea of reason governed by determinate concepts, an idea that settles _a priori_ the end on which the object's inner possibility depends. One cannot so much as conceive an ideal of beautiful flowers, of a handsome piece of furniture, or of a beautiful vista. But no ideal can be represented even of a beauty that hangs upon determinate ends—for instance, a beautiful dwelling-house, or a beautiful tree, or a beautiful garden, and the like; presumably because such ends are not pinned down and made determinate enough by their concept, so that the purposiveness turns out to be nearly as unconstrained as with ***vague*** beauty. The sole thing that carries the end of its own existence within itself is the ***human being***—who is able to set his own ends by means of reason, or, in cases where he must take them from outer perception, is still able to bring them into connection with essential and universal ends and then {{ 56 }} to judge aesthetically how they harmonize with these: this ***human being***, accordingly, is the one object in all the world capable of an ideal of ***beauty***, precisely as humanity in his person, considered as an intelligence, is the one thing capable of the ideal of ***perfection***.

Two ingredients, however, are required for this: ***first***, the aesthetic ***normal idea***, a singular intuition (belonging to the imagination) that supplies the measure by which he is judged as a thing that falls under a particular animal species; ***second***, the ***idea of reason***, which takes the ends of humanity—so far as these cannot be given a sensible representation—as the principle by which his shape is judged, a shape through which, as the effect of those ends in appearance, they are made manifest. For the shape of an animal belonging to a particular species the normal idea has to draw its elements from experience; but that highest purposiveness in how the shape is put together—the purposiveness that could serve as the general measure for aesthetically judging every single member of the species, the image that lay, so to speak, intentionally at the root of nature's craft, matched only by the species in its entirety and by no member taken in isolation—resides, all the same, solely in the idea of the one doing the judging, an idea that nonetheless, together with its proportions, can be presented fully _in concreto_, as an aesthetic idea, in an exemplary image. So as to render somewhat intelligible {{ 57 }} how all this comes about (for who can wrest from nature the entirety of her secret?), we shall try our hand at a psychological explanation.

It deserves notice that, by some means altogether beyond our comprehension, the {{{ 234 }}} imagination is able not merely to summon up, as occasion demands and even after a long lapse of time, the signs that stand for concepts; but also to reconstruct the image and shape of an object out of a number—past all telling—of objects of assorted sorts, or indeed of a single sort; and further, once the mind is bent on comparisons, in all likelihood really (though not with enough clarity to reach consciousness) to let one image settle, as it were, over another, and by the coincidence of many of the same sort to extract a middle figure that answers to all of them as a shared standard. Suppose a person has laid eyes on a thousand fully grown men. If he now sets out to judge the normal size that is to be estimated by comparison, then—so I would suggest—the imagination drops a large quantity of the images (perhaps that whole thousand) one upon the next; and, if I may here borrow the analogy of an optical projection, within that region where the greater part of them coincide, inside the outline where the spot glows with the most densely applied color, the ***average size*** becomes recognizable, standing, in height as much as in breadth, at an equal remove from the utmost boundaries of the tallest and the shortest builds; and this is the {{ 58 }} build of a beautiful man. (The very same result could be reached mechanically, were one to measure all thousand of them, add up their heights on their own and their widths (and depths) on their own, and then divide the total by a thousand. The imagination, however, accomplishes exactly this by way of a dynamic effect that springs from the repeated taking-in of such shapes upon the organ of the inner sense.) If in like fashion one now seeks the average head for this average man, and for that head the average nose, and so on, then this shape underlies the normal idea of the beautiful man in whatever country the comparison is undertaken; and thus, under these empirical conditions, a Negro must necessarily form a normal idea of bodily beauty different from a white person's, the Chinese one different from the European's. Things would proceed in just the same way with the ***model*** for a handsome horse or hound (of some particular breed). — This ***normal idea*** is not something abstracted from proportions gathered out of experience and treated ***as determinate rules,*** rather, it is only in light of it that rules for judging first come to be possible. It is that image for the whole species which hovers amid all the individual intuitions—diverse in manifold ways—of separate persons, the image nature placed beneath her offspring in this same species as their archetype, {{{ 235 }}} though one she appears to have realized completely in no single specimen. In no way is it {{ 59 }} the complete ***archetype*** of ***beauty*** for this species; it is merely the form that makes up the indispensable precondition of all beauty, and so no more than ***correctness*** in presenting the species. It amounts, as people termed ***Polyclitus's*** renowned ***Doryphorus***, to the ***rule*** (for just this office ***Myron's*** cow could likewise serve within its own kind). On this very account it can also harbor nothing that is specifically characteristic, since otherwise it would fail to be a ***normal idea*** for the species. What its presentation pleases by is not beauty at all, but simply the fact that it runs afoul of none of the conditions on which alone something of this species is able to be beautiful. Such a presentation is no more than academically proper.[^**]

Still to be set apart from the ***normal idea*** of the beautiful is its ***ideal***, which—on the grounds already brought forward—one is entitled to look for only in the ***human figure***. In that figure the ideal comes down to the outward showing of the ***moral***, and lacking this the object {{ 60 }} would give no pleasure that is universal and, over and above that, positive (and not just the negative pleasure of a presentation that keeps to the rules). The perceptible showing of moral ideas that inwardly rule the human being can, to be sure, be drawn only from experience; but to render their bond with everything that our reason ties to the morally good under the idea of the supreme purposiveness—goodness of soul, or purity, or strength, or serenity, and the like—as if visibly, in a bodily manifestation (as the outward effect of the inner), calls for pure ideas of reason joined to a mighty power of imagination in whoever would so much as judge them, and still more in whoever would present them. That such an ideal {{{ 236 }}} of beauty is correct is borne out by the following: it lets no charm of sense insinuate itself into the satisfaction taken in its object, and yet it makes room for a great interest in that object; which then goes to show that appraisal by such a yardstick can never be purely aesthetic, and that appraisal by an ideal of beauty is not a bare judgment of {{ 61 }} taste.

Definition of the beautiful drawn from this third moment.

***Beauty*** is a thing's form of ***purposiveness***, insofar as that form is registered upon the object ***without the representation of an end***.[^***]

[^*]: Exemplars of taste with respect to the arts of discourse have to be drawn up in a language both dead and learned: the former, so as to be spared the alteration that unavoidably overtakes living languages, whereby dignified expressions grow stale, ordinary ones fall out of use, and freshly minted ones enjoy only a short-lived currency; the latter, so that such a language may possess a grammar answerable to no wanton turn of fashion but holding instead to its own unalterable rule.
[^**]: One discovers that a wholly regular face—the sort a painter might wish to have sit for him as his model—usually has nothing to say: for it holds nothing characteristic and thereby conveys the idea of the species rather than what is peculiar to a given person. When the characteristic of this kind is carried to excess—that is, when it encroaches upon the normal idea itself (upon the species' purposiveness)—it goes by the name of ***caricature***. Experience likewise shows that such perfectly regular faces commonly betray, within, no more than a mediocre person as well; presumably (granting that nature does express in the outward the proportions of the inward) for this reason: that where none of the mental endowments juts out beyond the proportion needed to constitute a merely faultless human being, one has no warrant to expect anything of what is termed ***genius***, in whom nature seems to swerve from her customary ratios among the powers of the mind to the benefit of a single one.
[^***]: Against this definition one might cite, as a counter-case, that there exist things in which a purposive form is discernible although no end in them is recognized—for instance, the stone tools frequently unearthed from old burial mounds, fitted with a hole as though for a handle, which, even though their shape plainly betrays a purposiveness whose end goes unknown, are nonetheless not on that account pronounced beautiful. Yet the mere fact that one views them as a work of art already suffices to force the concession that one relates their shape to some intention and a determinate end. And for that reason there is no immediate satisfaction whatever in beholding them. A flower, by contrast—say a tulip—is deemed beautiful because there is met with, in the perceiving of it, a certain purposiveness that, judged as we judge it, is referred to no end whatever.
