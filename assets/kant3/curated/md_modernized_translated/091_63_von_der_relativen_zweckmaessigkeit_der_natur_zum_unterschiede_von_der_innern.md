## § 63. On the relative purposiveness of nature as distinguished from inner purposiveness

Only where a relation of cause to effect stands to be judged[^*] does experience direct our power of judgment toward the concept of an objective and material purposiveness—that is, toward the concept of an end of nature; and such a relation we are able to recognize as law-governed solely by {{{ 367 }}} placing the idea of the effect beneath the causality of its cause, taken as the condition that, grounding that causality itself, makes the former possible. But this may occur in two different manners: we regard the effect either directly as a product of art, or else merely as material for the art of other possible natural beings—hence either as an end, or as a means to the purposive employment of other causes. This second purposiveness goes by the name of usefulness (for human beings), or likewise beneficialness (for any other {{ 280 }} creature), and it remains merely relative, while the first is an inner purposiveness belonging to the natural being.

Rivers, for example, carry along with them all sorts of soil serviceable to the growth of plants, which they deposit sometimes in the midst of the land, often also at their mouths. The tide bears this silt over the land along many a coast, or lays it down on its shore; and when human beings above all lend a hand so that the ebb does not carry it off again, the fertile land increases, and the vegetable kingdom gains ground where formerly fish and shellfish had their abode. Most enlargements of land of this kind have probably been accomplished by nature itself, and it still carries on with them, though slowly. — Now the question arises whether this is to be judged as an end of nature, because it contains a usefulness for human beings; for the usefulness for the vegetable kingdom itself cannot be brought into the reckoning, since just as much is withdrawn from the sea creatures on the other side as advantage accrues to the land.

Or, to offer an instance of how certain natural things prove beneficial as means to other creatures (supposing these are posited as ends): for spruces no ground thrives better than sandy ground. Before the primeval sea drew back from the land, it had deposited in our northern territories such broad tracts of sand that upon this ground, otherwise wholly unfit for any cultivation, sprawling spruce forests could {{ 281 }} establish themselves—forests for whose irrational eradication we often accuse our ancestors; and one may then ask whether that most ancient laying-down of the sand-strata served as an end of nature on behalf of the spruce forests made possible upon them. So much is evident: that whoever admits these as an end of nature must concede sand as well, though only as a relative end, whose means was in turn the old seacoast together with its receding; for within a chain whose links stand subordinated one to another in a nexus of ends, each connecting link has to count as an {{{ 368 }}} end (though not, of course, as a final end), the link for which its immediately preceding cause serves as the means. Likewise, were cattle, sheep, horses, and the like ever to be present in the world, grass had of necessity to spring up on the earth; but salt-herbs, too, had to grow in the sandy wastes if camels were to flourish, just as these and other grass-feeding species had to be met with in plenty if wolves, tigers, and lions were ever to exist. Hence the objective purposiveness founded upon beneficialness is no objective purposiveness inhering in the things as they are in themselves, as if the sand, on its own, could not be comprehended as an effect arising out of its cause, the sea, without ascribing an end to that sea and viewing the effect—namely the sand—as a work of art. Such purposiveness is merely relative, and merely accidental to the very thing to which it is ascribed; {{ 282 }} and although, among the examples cited, the kinds of grass are on their own to be judged as organized products of nature, and hence as full of art, still, in relation to the animals that feed on them, they are regarded as mere raw matter.

But when the human being goes still further and, by the freedom of his causality, turns natural things to his frequently foolish purposes (the bright bird-feathers to adorn his garments, tinted earths or plant-saps for cosmetic paint)—and at times, from a rational purpose, the horse to ride, the ox, and in Minorca even the ass and the swine to plow with—then not even a relative natural end (for such use) may be assumed here. For his reason knows how to endow things with a fitness to his capricious whims—a fitness for which nature itself had by no means predestined him. Only ***if*** it be granted that human beings were meant to dwell upon the earth can it follow that, at the very least, the means cannot be lacking whereby they were able to persist as animals—and indeed as rational animals (to however humble a degree); and in that event those natural things that are indispensable for this end would likewise have to be counted as natural ends.

It is easy to gather from all this that external purposiveness (one thing's beneficialness for others) admits of being taken as an external natural end solely provided that the {{ 283 }} existence of whatever it benefits, be it nearby or at some remove, is in its own right an end of nature. But since the latter can never be made out by mere observation of nature, the consequence is that relative purposiveness, {{{ 369 }}} though it points only hypothetically to natural ends, still authorizes no absolute teleological judgment.

Snow protects the seed-crops in cold countries against the frost; it eases the intercourse of human beings (by means of sledges); the Laplander finds there animals that bring about this intercourse (reindeer), which find ample nourishment in a dry moss that they must themselves scratch out from under the snow, and which nonetheless let themselves be tamed with ease and be willingly robbed of the freedom in which they could perfectly well have sustained themselves. For other peoples in the same frigid zone the sea holds a rich store of animals which, besides the food and clothing they supply, and the wood which the sea as it were floats down to them for their dwellings, furnish them further with fuel for warming their huts. Here, then, one meets with an admirable convergence of so many of nature's relations upon a single end; and that end is the Greenlander, the Lapp, the Samoyed, the Yakut, and so forth. Yet why human beings should have to live there in the first place is not something one perceives. To say, therefore: that ***for this reason*** vapors from the air in the form of snow {{ 284 }} fall down, that the sea has its currents which float thither the wood grown in warmer countries, and that great sea-animals filled with oil are present, ***because*** the idea of an advantage for certain wretched creatures lies at the basis of the cause that procures all these natural products, would be a very daring and arbitrary judgment. For even if all this natural usefulness were absent, we should miss nothing in the sufficiency of the natural causes for this constitution; rather, so much as to demand such a disposition, and to impute such an end to nature (since, apart from this, it was only the utmost incompatibility of human beings among themselves that could have scattered them into such inhospitable regions), would strike us as being itself presumptuous and thoughtless.

[^*]: Since in pure mathematics the question is never one of the existence of things, but solely of their possibility, that is, of an intuition answering to their concept, and therefore not at all one of cause and effect, it follows that every purposiveness remarked there must be counted as merely formal, never as a natural end.
