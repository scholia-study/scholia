## § 63. On the relative purposiveness of nature as distinguished from inner purposiveness

Experience leads our power of judgment to the concept of an objective and material purposiveness, that is, to the concept of an end of nature, only when a relation of cause to effect is to be judged[^*], a relation that we find ourselves able to grasp as law-governed only through {{{ 367 }}} subordinating the idea of the effect to the causality of its cause, as the condition, lying at the very ground of that causality itself, of the possibility of the former. This, however, can come about in two ways: either by taking the effect immediately as a product of art, or else merely as material serving the art of other possible natural beings, and thus either as an end or as a means toward the purposive use of other causes. The latter purposiveness is called usefulness (for human beings), or also beneficialness (for every other {{ 280 }} creature), and is merely relative, whereas the former is an inner purposiveness of the natural being.

Rivers, for example, carry along with them all sorts of soil serviceable to the growth of plants, which they deposit sometimes in the midst of the land, often also at their mouths. The tide bears this silt over the land along many a coast, or lays it down on its shore; and when human beings above all lend a hand so that the ebb does not carry it off again, the fertile land increases, and the vegetable kingdom gains ground where formerly fish and shellfish had their abode. Most enlargements of land of this kind have probably been accomplished by nature itself, and it still carries on with them, though slowly. — Now the question arises whether this is to be judged as an end of nature, because it contains a usefulness for human beings; for the usefulness for the vegetable kingdom itself cannot be brought into the reckoning, since just as much is withdrawn from the sea creatures on the other side as advantage accrues to the land.

Or, to give an example of the beneficialness of certain natural things as means for other creatures (if one presupposes these as ends): no soil is more conducive to spruces than a sandy soil. Now the ancient sea, before it withdrew from the land, left behind so many stretches of sand in our northern regions that upon this soil, otherwise so useless for all cultivation, extensive spruce forests were able to {{ 281 }} spring up, for whose senseless extirpation we frequently reproach our forebears; and here one may ask whether this primeval deposition of the layers of sand was an end of nature for the sake of the spruce forests possible upon it. This much is clear: that if one takes these to be an end of nature, one must also grant that sand, yet only as a relative end, for which in turn the ancient seashore and its withdrawal was the means; for in the series of mutually subordinated members of a connection of ends, every intermediate member must be regarded as an {{{ 368 }}} end (though precisely not as a final end), one for which its nearest cause is the means. In the same way, if cattle, sheep, horses, etc., were once to exist in the world, then grass had to grow on the earth, but salt-plants had also to grow in sandy deserts if camels were to thrive, or else these and other grass-eating kinds of animal had to be encountered in abundance if there were to be wolves, tigers, and lions. Accordingly, the objective purposiveness that rests upon beneficialness is not an objective purposiveness of the things in themselves, as though the sand, taken by itself, could not be understood as an effect issuing from its cause, the sea, unless one placed an end beneath that sea and treated the effect, that is, the sand, as a work of art. Such purposiveness is merely relative, and merely accidental to the very thing to which it is ascribed; {{ 282 }} and although, among the examples cited, the kinds of grass are on their own to be judged as organized products of nature, and hence as full of art, still, in relation to the animals that feed on them, they are regarded as mere raw matter.

But when, beyond all this, the human being, through the freedom of his causality, finds natural things beneficial to his often foolish aims (the gaudy bird feathers for trimming his attire, colored earths or plant juices for face-paint), sometimes also, out of rational intent, the horse for riding, the bull, and in Minorca even the donkey and the pig, for plowing: then here one cannot even assume a relative natural end (with a view to this use). For his reason knows how to lend things an agreement with his arbitrary fancies, for which he himself was not even predestined by nature. Only ***if*** one assumes that human beings were to live on earth do at least the means become indispensable, without which they could not subsist as animals, and even as rational animals (however low the degree may be); but in that case those natural things which are indispensable to this purpose would also have to be regarded as natural ends.

From this one readily discerns that outer purposiveness (the beneficialness of one thing for others) can be regarded as an outer natural end only on the condition that the {{ 283 }} existence of that for which it is beneficial, whether proximately or in a remote fashion, is itself an end of nature. But since the latter can never be made out by mere observation of nature, the consequence is that relative purposiveness, {{{ 369 }}} though it points only hypothetically to natural ends, still authorizes no absolute teleological judgment.

Snow protects the seed-crops in cold countries against the frost; it eases the intercourse of human beings (by means of sledges); the Laplander finds there animals that bring about this intercourse (reindeer), which find ample nourishment in a dry moss that they must themselves scratch out from under the snow, and which nonetheless let themselves be tamed with ease and be willingly robbed of the freedom in which they could perfectly well have sustained themselves. For other peoples in the same frigid zone the sea holds a rich store of animals which, besides the food and clothing they supply, and the wood which the sea as it were floats down to them for their dwellings, furnish them further with fuel for warming their huts. Here, then, is an admirable concurrence of so many relations of nature to a single end; and this end is the Greenlander, the Lapp, the Samoyed, the Yakut, etc. But one does not see why human beings must live there at all. To say, therefore: that ***for this reason*** vapors from the air in the form of snow {{ 284 }} fall down, that the sea has its currents which float thither the wood grown in warmer countries, and that great sea-animals filled with oil are present, ***because*** the idea of an advantage for certain wretched creatures lies at the basis of the cause that procures all these natural products, would be a very daring and arbitrary judgment. For even if all this natural usefulness were absent, we should miss nothing in the sufficiency of the natural causes for this constitution; rather, so much as to demand such a disposition, and to impute such an end to nature (since, apart from this, it was only the utmost incompatibility of human beings among themselves that could have scattered them into such inhospitable regions), would strike us as being itself presumptuous and thoughtless.

[^*]: Since in pure mathematics the question is never one of the existence of things, but solely of their possibility, that is, of an intuition answering to their concept, and therefore not at all one of cause and effect, it follows that every purposiveness remarked there must be counted as merely formal, never as a natural end.
