## § 63. On the Relative Purposiveness of Nature as Distinguished from the Inner

Experience directs our power of judgment to the concept of an objective and material purposiveness, i.e., to the concept of a purpose of nature, only when a relation of cause to effect is to be judged[^*], which we find ourselves capable of understanding as law-governed only thereby, {{{ 367 }}} that we place the idea of the effect underneath the causality of its cause, as the condition of the possibility of the former lying at the basis of this causality itself. But this can happen in a twofold way: either by our viewing the effect directly as a product of art, or only as material for the art of other possible natural beings, and thus either as a purpose, or as a means for the purposive use of other causes. The latter purposiveness is called utility (for human beings), or else conduciveness (for any other {{ 280 }} creature), and is merely relative, whereas the former is an inner purposiveness of the natural being.

Rivers, for example, carry with them all kinds of earth serviceable for the growth of plants, which they deposit sometimes in the midst of the land, often also at their mouths. The flood-tide carries this silt over the land on many coasts, or deposits it on its shores; and if human beings especially assist so that the ebb-tide does not carry it away again, the fertile land increases, and the vegetable kingdom gains a place where previously fish and shellfish had had their abode. Nature itself has probably accomplished most extensions of land in this way, and still continues to do so, although slowly. — Now the question is whether this is to be judged as a purpose of nature because it contains a utility for human beings; for that which is for the vegetable kingdom itself cannot be taken into account, because just as much is withdrawn from the sea creatures as advantage accrues to the land.

Or, to give an example of the conduciveness of certain natural things as means for other creatures (if we presuppose them as purposes): no soil is more conducive to spruces than a sandy soil. Now, before it receded from the land, the ancient sea left behind so many sandy tracts in our northern regions that on this soil, otherwise so useless for all cultivation, extensive spruce forests have been able {{ 281 }} to grow, for whose irrational destruction we frequently accuse our ancestors; and there one can ask whether this primeval deposition of sandy layers was a purpose of nature for the sake of the spruce forests made possible on it. This much is clear: that if one assumes these as a purpose of nature, one must also admit that sand, but only as a relative purpose, for which in turn the ancient seashore and its receding were the means; for in the series of mutually subordinated members of a purposive connection, each intermediate member must be regarded as a {{{ 368 }}} purpose (although not indeed as a final purpose), for which its proximate cause is the means. Likewise, if cattle, sheep, horses, etc. were to exist in the world, then grass had to grow on earth, but salt-herbs also had to grow in sandy deserts if camels were to thrive, or these and other species of herbivorous animals had to be found in abundance if there were to be wolves, tigers, and lions. Hence the objective purposiveness that is grounded on conduciveness is not an objective purposiveness of things in themselves, as if the sand for itself, as an effect from its cause, the sea, could not be conceived without attributing a purpose to the latter and without regarding the effect, namely the sand, as a work of art. It is a merely relative purposiveness, merely accidental to the thing itself to which it is attributed; {{ 282 }} and although among the adduced examples the species of grass are to be judged in themselves as organized products of nature, and consequently as ingenious, they are nevertheless regarded, in relation to the animals that feed on them, as mere raw matter.

But when, moreover, human beings, through the freedom of their causality, find natural things conducive to their often foolish aims (colorful bird feathers for the adornment of their clothing, colored earths or plant juices for makeup), sometimes also from a rational aim find the horse conducive for riding, the bull and in Minorca even the donkey and the pig for plowing: one cannot here assume even a relative purpose of nature (with respect to this use). For his reason knows how to give things an agreement with his arbitrary whims, to which he himself was not even predestined by nature. Only ***if*** one assumes that human beings were meant to live on earth, then at least the means without which they could not exist as animals, and even as rational animals (in however low a degree it may be), must not be lacking either; but then those natural things that are indispensable for this purpose would also have to be regarded as natural purposes.

One easily sees from this that outer purposiveness (the conduciveness of one thing for others) can be regarded as an outer purpose of nature only under the condition that the {{ 283 }} existence of that to which it is immediately or remotely conducive is in itself a purpose of nature. But since the latter can never be determined by mere contemplation of nature: it follows that relative purposiveness, {{{ 369 }}} although it hypothetically gives indication of natural purposes, nevertheless does not justify any absolute teleological judgment.

Snow protects the crops in cold countries against the frost; it facilitates the communication of human beings (by sleighs); the Laplander finds there animals that bring about this communication (reindeer), which find sufficient nourishment in a dry moss which they themselves must scrape out from under the snow, and yet allow themselves to be easily tamed and willingly deprived of the freedom in which they could very well preserve themselves. For other peoples in the same icy zone, the sea contains a rich supply of animals that, besides the food and clothing they provide, and the wood which the sea floats to them as it were for dwellings, also supply them with combustible materials for heating their huts. Here is now an admirable convergence of so many relations of nature toward one purpose; and this is the Greenlander, the Laplander, the Samoyed, the Yakut, etc. But one does not see why human beings must live there at all. To say, therefore, that vapor falls down from the air in the form of snow {{ 284 }} ***for this reason***, that the sea has its currents which wash thither the wood grown in warmer countries, and that large sea animals filled with oil are there, ***because*** the idea of an advantage for certain wretched creatures lies at the basis of the cause that brings all these natural products together, would be a very hazardous and arbitrary judgment. For even if all this natural utility were not there, we would find nothing lacking in the adequacy of natural causes for this constitution; indeed, even to demand such an arrangement and to expect such a purpose from nature (since, in any case, only the greatest incompatibility of human beings with one another could have dispersed them into such inhospitable regions) would seem to us ourselves presumptuous and thoughtless.

[^*]: Because in pure mathematics there can be no question of the existence of things, but only of their possibility, namely, of an intuition corresponding to their concept, and consequently not of cause and effect at all: all purposiveness noted there must consequently be regarded merely as formal, and never as a purpose of nature.
