## § 42. On the intellectual interest in the beautiful

It was out of a well-meaning intention that those who would gladly direct all the pursuits of human beings, toward which their inner natural disposition drives them, to the ultimate end of humanity—namely the morally good—took it to be a sign of a good moral character to take an interest in the beautiful as such. They have, however, been contradicted, and not without ground, by others who appeal to experience, to the effect that virtuosos of taste, not merely often but indeed as a rule, are vain, obstinate, and given over to ruinous passions, and could perhaps lay even less claim than others to the distinction of an attachment to ethical principles; and so it appears that the feeling for the beautiful is not merely specifically distinct from moral feeling (as indeed it really is), but that the interest which one can bind up with it can be reconciled with the moral {{ 166 }} one only with much difficulty, and by no means through any inner affinity.

I now readily grant that the interest in the ***beauty of art*** (under which I also count the artificial use of natural beauties for adornment, and hence for vanity) affords no proof whatsoever of a cast of mind attached to the morally good, or even merely inclined toward it. Against this, however, I hold that to take an ***immediate interest*** in ***nature***'s beauty (and not merely to possess taste for judging it) is at all times a mark of a good soul; and that, {{{ 299 }}} where this interest is habitual, it indicates at least a frame of mind favorable to moral feeling, if it gladly joins itself to the ***contemplation of nature***. One must, however, keep well in mind that here I really mean the beautiful ***forms*** of nature, whereas the ***charms*** that nature is wont to bind up so abundantly with those forms I still set aside, because the interest in them, though likewise immediate, is nonetheless empirical.

Whoever, in solitude (and without any intention of wishing to communicate his observations to others), contemplates the beautiful shape of a wild flower, of a bird, of an insect, and so on, in order to marvel at it, to love it, and to be unwilling to see it absent from nature altogether—even though some harm might thereby come to him, and far less any advantage shine forth from it for him—takes an immediate and indeed intellectual interest {{ 167 }} in the beauty of nature. That is, not only its product as regards form, but also the existence of that product pleases him, without any sensory charm having a part in it, or his connecting any end with it.

It is, however, remarkable here that, if one had secretly deceived this lover of the beautiful and had stuck artificial flowers (which can be manufactured wholly like natural ones) into the ground, or set artificially carved birds upon the branches of trees, and he then discovered the deception, the immediate interest that he previously took in it would at once vanish, though perhaps another—namely the interest of vanity, to deck out his room with them for the eyes of others—would install itself in its place. That nature has brought forth this beauty: this thought must accompany the intuition and the reflection; and on this alone is grounded the immediate interest that one takes in it. Otherwise there remains either a mere judgment of taste devoid of all interest, or only one bound up with a mediate interest, namely one referred to society: which latter yields no sure indication of a morally good cast of mind.

This precedence of natural beauty over artistic beauty—that, even where the latter should in respect of form actually surpass the former, it nonetheless alone {{ 168 }} arouses an immediate interest—agrees with the purified and thorough cast of mind of all human beings who have cultivated their ethical feeling. When a man who has taste enough to {{{ 300 }}} judge the products of fine art with the greatest correctness and refinement gladly leaves the room in which those beauties are to be found that sustain vanity and at best social pleasures, and turns instead to nature's beauty, so as to find here, as it were, a sensuous delight for his mind along a course of thinking that he is never able to unfold completely: then we shall regard this very choice of his with esteem, and presuppose in him a beautiful soul, to which no art-connoisseur or amateur can pretend by virtue of the interest that his objects arouse in him. — What, then, is the difference in the so very different estimation of two kinds of object which, in the judgment of mere taste, would scarcely dispute precedence with one another?

We have a faculty of merely aesthetic power of judgment, for judging forms without concepts and for finding, in the mere judging of them, a satisfaction which at the same time we make into a rule for everyone, without this judgment being grounded on an interest or producing one. — On the other hand, we also have a faculty of an intellectual power of judgment, for mere forms of practical maxims (insofar as they qualify of themselves for universal legislation) {{ 169 }} to determine _a priori_ a satisfaction, which we make into a law for everyone, without our judgment being grounded on any interest, ***but which nonetheless produces one***. The pleasure or displeasure in the first judgment is called that of taste, the second that of moral feeling.

But since reason is also interested that the ideas (for which, within moral feeling, reason brings about an immediate interest) should have objective reality as well, that is, that nature should at the very least display a trace, or offer a hint, that it contains within itself some ground for assuming a lawful accord of its products with our satisfaction that is independent of all interest (a satisfaction which we recognize _a priori_ as a law for everyone, without being able to ground this on proofs): reason must therefore take an interest in every manifestation on nature's part of an accord similar to this one; and consequently the mind cannot reflect upon the beauty of ***nature*** without at the same time finding itself interested therein. This interest, however, is by its kinship moral; and whoever takes it in the beauty of nature can take it therein only insofar as he has already beforehand well grounded his interest in the ethically good. Thus in one whom the beauty of nature {{{ 301 }}} immediately interests, one has cause to presume at least a predisposition {{ 170 }} to a good moral disposition.

It will be said: this construal of aesthetic judgments as akin to moral feeling looks far too studied to pass for the true decipherment of the cipher-script through which nature, in her beautiful forms, speaks to us in figures. Yet, to begin with, this immediate interest in the beauty of nature is really not at all common, but proper only to those whose cast of mind is either already cultivated toward the good or eminently receptive to such cultivation; and, in the next place, the analogy between the pure judgment of taste—which, without depending on any interest, lets a satisfaction be felt and at the same time represents it _a priori_ as befitting humanity in general—and the moral judgment, which does the very same thing from concepts, leads, even without clear, subtle, and deliberate reflection, to an equal immediate interest in the object of the former as in that of the latter: only that the one is a free interest, the other one grounded on objective laws. To this is added, moreover, the admiration of nature, which displays itself in her beautiful products as art, not merely by accident but as it were intentionally, according to a lawful arrangement and as purposiveness without an end: which latter, since we nowhere encounter it externally, we naturally seek within ourselves, and indeed in that {{ 171 }} which constitutes the ultimate end of our existence, namely the moral vocation (though the inquiry after the ground of the possibility of such a natural purposiveness will only first be taken up in teleology).

That the satisfaction in fine art is not, in the pure judgment of taste, bound up with an immediate interest in the same way as that in beautiful nature, is also easy to explain. For the former is either an imitation of the latter that goes as far as deception: and in that case it produces its effect as natural beauty (taken to be such); or it is an art visibly directed intentionally toward our satisfaction: but in that case the satisfaction in this product would indeed take place immediately through taste, yet would arouse no interest other than a mediate one in the underlying cause, namely an art that can interest only through its end, never in itself. One will perhaps say that this is likewise the case when an object of nature interests through its beauty only insofar {{{ 302 }}} as a moral idea is associated with it; but it is not this, rather the constitution of the object in itself, that it qualifies for such an association—which therefore belongs to it inwardly—that interests immediately.

The charms in beautiful nature, which are so frequently encountered as if fused together with beautiful form, belong either {{ 172 }} to the modifications of light (in coloration) or of sound (in tones). For these are the only sensations that permit not only a sense-feeling but, beyond that, reflection on the form of such sensory modifications, and thus contain within themselves, as it were, a language that nature conveys to us and that seems to bear a higher meaning. Thus the white color of the lily seems to attune the mind to ideas of innocence, and, following the order of the seven colors from red on up to violet, 1) to the idea of sublimity, 2) of boldness, 3) of candor, 4) of amiableness, 5) of modesty, 6) of steadfastness, and 7) of tenderness. The song of birds proclaims joyfulness and contentment with its existence. At least so we construe nature, whether or not anything of the kind be its intention. But this interest that we here take in beauty absolutely requires that it be beauty of nature; and it vanishes entirely as soon as one notices that one has been deceived and that it is only art: so much so that even taste can then find nothing beautiful in it, nor sight anything charming any longer. What is more highly prized by poets than the enchantingly beautiful song of the nightingale in a solitary thicket on a still summer evening by the gentle light of the moon? Yet there are examples where, no such songster being found {{ 173 }}, some merry innkeeper deceived his guests—who had come to him to enjoy the country air—to their utmost satisfaction, by hiding in a thicket a mischievous lad who knew how to imitate this song (with a reed or rush in his mouth) quite like nature. But as soon as one becomes aware that it is a deception, no one will long endure listening to this song previously held to be so charming; and it is the same with every other songbird. It must be nature, or be taken by us for nature, if we are to be able to take, in the beautiful as something of that kind, an immediate ***interest***; still more so, however, if we are even to require of others that they should take it therein: which does indeed happen, {{{ 303 }}} in that we regard as coarse and ignoble the cast of mind of those who have no ***feeling*** for beautiful nature (for so we name the receptivity to an interest in its contemplation) and who, at the meal or over the bottle, hold to the enjoyment of mere sense-impressions.
