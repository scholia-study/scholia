## § 42. On the intellectual interest in the beautiful

With kindly intent it came about that those who liked to steer every occupation of humankind—occupations to which the inner disposition of our nature impels us—toward mankind's final end, that is, the morally good, regarded a concern for the beautiful in general as betokening a good moral character. Yet this view has met with well-founded objection from others, who invoke experience: that the virtuosos of taste are, not just frequently but as a matter of course, conceited, willful, and given to corrupting passions, and might lay claim to the merit of an attachment to ethical principles even less readily than the rest; whence it seems that the feeling for beauty is not only specifically different from moral feeling—as in truth it is—but that whatever interest can be linked with it may be reconciled with the moral {{ 166 }} one only with difficulty, and assuredly not by way of any inner affinity.

I now readily grant that the interest in the ***beauty of art*** (under which I also count the artificial use of natural beauties for adornment, and hence for vanity) affords no proof whatsoever of a cast of mind attached to the morally good, or even merely inclined toward it. Against this, however, I hold that to take an ***immediate interest*** in ***nature***'s beauty (and not merely to possess taste for judging it) is at all times a mark of a good soul; and that, {{{ 299 }}} where this interest is habitual, it indicates at least a frame of mind favorable to moral feeling, if it gladly joins itself to the ***contemplation of nature***. One must, however, keep well in mind that here I really mean the beautiful ***forms*** of nature, whereas the ***charms*** that nature is wont to bind up so abundantly with those forms I still set aside, because the interest in them, though likewise immediate, is nonetheless empirical.

Someone who, alone (and with no aim of sharing his remarks with anyone else), gazes at the lovely form of a wild flower, a bird, an insect, and so on, so as to admire and cherish it, and would not willingly do without it in nature at large—even should this bring him a measure of harm, still less yield him any visible profit—thereby takes an immediate, indeed intellectual, interest {{ 167 }} in nature's beauty. That is, not only its product as regards form, but also the existence of that product pleases him, without any sensory charm having a part in it, or his connecting any end with it.

What is noteworthy in this connection, however, is that were someone to hoodwink this admirer of the beautiful behind his back—planting in the soil artificial blossoms (which can be fashioned to resemble real ones exactly) or perching artificially carved birds on the boughs of trees—and were he thereupon to detect the fraud, the immediate interest he had earlier felt in them would promptly fade, while in its stead there might well arise a different one, namely the interest of vanity in adorning his room with such things for others to see. That such beauty is nature's own production—this thought must attend the intuition together with the reflection; and it is upon this alone that the immediate interest one takes in the thing rests. Otherwise there remains either a mere judgment of taste devoid of all interest, or only one bound up with a mediate interest, namely one referred to society: which latter yields no sure indication of a morally good cast of mind.

This precedence of natural beauty over artistic beauty—that, even where the latter should in respect of form actually surpass the former, it nonetheless alone {{ 168 }} arouses an immediate interest—agrees with the purified and thorough cast of mind of all human beings who have cultivated their ethical feeling. Should a man possessing taste enough to appraise the products of fine art with the utmost accuracy and finesse {{{ 300 }}} readily quit the room where one comes upon those beauties that nourish vanity and, at best, social amusements, and turn himself toward the beauty of nature so as to find there, as it were, a rapture for his spirit within a train of thought that he can never fully develop: then we ourselves shall view this choice of his with high regard, and presume in him a beautiful soul, upon which no connoisseur or lover of art can stake a claim on account of the interest that his objects awaken in him. — What, then, is the difference in the so very different estimation of two kinds of object which, in the judgment of mere taste, would scarcely dispute precedence with one another?

We possess a capacity of the merely aesthetic power of judgment for appraising forms apart from concepts and for finding, in the bare appraisal of them, a satisfaction that we simultaneously erect as a rule binding on everyone, without that judgment either resting on an interest or generating one. — On the other side, we likewise possess a capacity of an intellectual power of judgment, able, for the mere forms of practical maxims (so far as these of their own accord qualify for universal legislation), {{ 169 }} to fix _a priori_ a satisfaction that we lay down as a law for everyone, our judgment grounded on no interest whatsoever, ***and yet bringing one forth***. In the former judgment the pleasure or displeasure goes by the name of taste, in the latter by that of moral feeling.

But because it also matters to reason that the ideas (in which, within moral feeling, reason effects an immediate interest) should possess objective reality too—that is, that nature should at least give a trace or drop a hint of harboring in herself some ground for us to assume a law-governed correspondence of her products to that satisfaction of ours which stands independent of every interest (the very satisfaction that we acknowledge _a priori_ as valid law for all, though we cannot rest it upon any proofs): reason is bound to take an interest in each manifestation by nature of a correspondence akin to this; and hence the mind is unable to ponder the beauty of ***nature*** without discovering itself, in that very act, to be interested. This interest, however, is by its kinship moral; and whoever takes it in the beauty of nature can take it therein only insofar as he has already beforehand well grounded his interest in the ethically good. Thus in one whom the beauty of nature {{{ 301 }}} immediately interests, one has cause to presume at least a predisposition {{ 170 }} to a good moral disposition.

It will be said: this construal of aesthetic judgments as akin to moral feeling looks far too studied to pass for the true decipherment of the cipher-script through which nature, in her beautiful forms, speaks to us in figures. But in the first place, such an immediate interest in nature's beauty is genuinely far from widespread, belonging solely to those whose way of thinking is either already schooled toward the good or exceptionally open to such schooling; and, secondly, the kinship linking the pure judgment of taste—which, beholden to no interest at all, allows a satisfaction to be felt while presenting it _a priori_ as fitting for humanity at large—with the moral judgment, which accomplishes exactly this out of concepts, gives rise, even absent any clear, refined, and intentional deliberation, to an interest, equally immediate, in the object of the one just as in that of the other: save that the first interest is free, whereas the second rests upon objective laws. There is joined to this, besides, our admiration for nature, which manifests itself in her beautiful products as art—arising not by mere chance but, so to speak, on purpose, in accordance with an orderly arrangement conforming to law and as a purposiveness devoid of purpose: and this last, given that we come upon it nowhere in the outer world, we look for as a matter of course within our own selves, specifically in what {{ 171 }} makes up the final end of our being, that is, our moral determination (although the question concerning the ground of the possibility of such a purposiveness in nature will not be broached until teleology).

It is likewise readily explained why the satisfaction in fine art, taken in the pure judgment of taste, is not tied to an immediate interest in the manner that the satisfaction in beautiful nature is. For fine art is either an imitation of nature carried to the point of illusion: in which event it works upon us as natural beauty (or what passes for it); or else it is an art openly aimed with intent at our satisfaction: yet in that event, though the satisfaction in the product would arise at once through taste, it would kindle no more than a mediate interest in the cause lying beneath it—namely, an art capable of engaging us solely by way of its end, and never on its own account. Perhaps someone will object that the same holds when a natural object arouses interest by its beauty merely to the extent {{{ 302 }}} that a moral idea is linked with it; but what interests immediately is not this, rather the object's own inner make-up, whereby it lends itself to such a linkage—a make-up that thus pertains to it intrinsically.

Those charms in beautiful nature that one so often meets seemingly melted into one with beautiful form pertain either {{ 172 }} to the modifications of light (in the giving of color) or to those of sound (in tones). For these are the only sensations that permit not only a sense-feeling but, beyond that, reflection on the form of such sensory modifications, and thus contain within themselves, as it were, a language that nature conveys to us and that seems to bear a higher meaning. In the same way the lily's white hue appears to tune the mind toward ideas of innocence, and—proceeding through the sequence of the seven colors from red all the way to violet—toward the idea 1) of sublimity, 2) of boldness, 3) of frankness, 4) of friendliness, 5) of unassumingness, 6) of constancy, and 7) of affection. A bird's singing announces cheerfulness and a contentment with its own existence. At least so we construe nature, whether or not anything of the kind be its intention. This interest, however, which we here bestow on beauty, positively demands that the beauty be nature's own; and the moment one perceives oneself deceived, that nothing but art is at hand, it fades away altogether—to such a degree that thereafter taste itself can discover nothing beautiful in it, nor the eye anything charming. What is more highly prized by poets than the enchantingly beautiful song of the nightingale in a solitary thicket on a still summer evening by the gentle light of the moon? There are recorded cases, nevertheless, in which—where no such singer was to be had {{ 173 }}—some jovial host had, to their greatest delight, tricked the guests who had stopped in with him to relish the country air, by concealing in a bush a playful young fellow who could reproduce this call (a reed or rush held in his mouth) so as to resemble nature perfectly. The instant it dawns on someone, though, that a fraud is afoot, nobody will bear to keep listening for long to a song deemed so delightful before; and matters stand no differently with any other songbird. The thing must be nature, or be held by us to be such, if in the beautiful, as beauty of that sort, we are to take an immediate ***interest***; and all the more if we may even expect of others that they too should take one in it: which indeed comes to pass, {{{ 303 }}} inasmuch as we count the mentality coarse and base of those in whom there is no ***feeling*** for beautiful nature (for by that name we designate the susceptibility to an interest in contemplating her) and who, at table or over the bottle, cling to the enjoyment of mere sensations of sense.
