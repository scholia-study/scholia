## § 42. On the Intellectual Interest in the Beautiful

It was with a well-meaning intention that those who wanted to direct all activities of human beings, to which their inner natural predisposition impels them, toward the ultimate end of humanity, namely the morally good, regarded taking an interest in the beautiful in general as a sign of a good moral character. But they have been contradicted, not without reason, by others who appeal to experience that virtuosi of taste are not only frequently, but indeed even commonly, vain, obstinate, and given to pernicious passions, and could perhaps even less than others claim the privilege of attachment to moral principles; and so it seems that the feeling for the beautiful is not only (as it indeed actually is) specifically distinct from moral feeling, but also that the interest which can be connected with it is compatible with the moral {{ 166 }} only with difficulty, and by no means through inner affinity.

I do, to be sure, gladly concede that interest in the ***beautiful in art*** (in which I also include the artificial use of natural beauties for decoration, hence for vanity) provides no proof at all of a way of thinking that is attached to the morally good, or even only inclined to it. On the other hand, however, I maintain that to take an ***immediate interest*** in the beauty of ***nature*** (not merely to have taste in order to judge it) is always a mark of a good soul; and that, {{{ 299 }}} if this interest is habitual, it indicates at least a frame of mind favorable to moral feeling, if it gladly connects itself with the ***contemplation of nature***. One must, however, remember well that I here actually mean the beautiful ***forms*** of nature, while still setting aside the ***charms*** that she is accustomed so richly to combine with them, because the interest in them, although also immediate, is yet empirical.

He who, in solitude (and without any intention of wishing to communicate his observations to others), contemplates the beautiful shape of a wild flower, a bird, an insect, etc., in order to admire it, to love it, and not to wish to find it missing from nature in general, even though some harm might happen to him thereby, much less any benefit shine forth from it for him, takes an immediate and indeed intellectual interest {{ 167 }} in the beauty of nature. That is to say, not only does its product please him as regards its form, but its existence also pleases him, without any sensory charm having a share in this, or his connecting any end whatsoever with it.

But it is remarkable in this regard that, if one had secretly deceived this lover of the beautiful and stuck artificial flowers (which can be manufactured entirely similar to natural ones) into the ground, or set artificially carved birds on the branches of trees, and he thereafter discovered the deception, the immediate interest that he previously took in them would immediately vanish, while perhaps another interest, namely that of vanity in decorating his room with them for foreign eyes, would find a place in its stead. That nature has produced that beauty: this thought must accompany the intuition and reflection; and on this alone is grounded the immediate interest that one takes in it. Otherwise, there remains either a mere judgment of taste without any interest at all, or only one connected with an indirect interest, namely one related to society; which latter provides no reliable indication of a morally good way of thinking.

This preeminence of natural beauty over artistic beauty, in still being alone in {{ 168 }} arousing an immediate interest, even if the former were even surpassed by the latter as regards its form, agrees with the refined and thorough way of thinking of all human beings who have cultivated their moral feeling. If a man who has taste enough to judge of the products of beautiful art with the greatest correctness and refinement gladly {{{ 300 }}} leaves the room in which those beauties that entertain vanity and perhaps social pleasures are to be found, and turns to the beautiful in nature in order here, as it were, to find voluptuousness for his mind in a train of thought that he can never fully develop, then we will view this choice of his itself with respect and presuppose in him a beautiful soul, to which no art connoisseur and lover can make a claim in virtue of the interest that he takes in his objects. — What, then, is the difference between these so diverse estimations of two kinds of objects, which in the judgment of mere taste would hardly dispute each other's preeminence?

We have a faculty of merely aesthetic power of judgment to judge of forms without concepts and to find a liking in the mere judging of them, which we at the same time make a rule for everyone, without this judgment being grounded on any interest, nor producing such an interest. — On the other hand, we also have a faculty of an intellectual power of judgment to determine _a priori_ a liking for the mere forms of practical maxims (so far as they qualify of themselves for universal legislation), {{ 169 }} which we make a law for everyone, without our judgment being grounded on any interest whatsoever, ***but yet producing such an interest***. The pleasure or displeasure in the former judgment is called that of taste, the second that of moral feeling.

But since it also interests reason that the ideas (for which it effects an immediate interest in moral feeling) also have objective reality, i.e., that nature should at least show a trace or give a hint that it contains in itself some ground for assuming a lawful agreement of its products with our liking that is independent of all interest (which we cognize _a priori_ as a law for everyone, without being able to ground this on proofs): reason must take an interest in every manifestation of nature of an agreement similar to this; consequently, the mind cannot reflect on the beauty of ***nature*** without at the same time finding itself interested in it. This interest is, as regards its affinity, moral; and he who takes it in the beautiful in nature can take it in the same only insofar as he has already well-founded his interest in the morally good. Thus, in him whom the beauty of nature {{{ 301 }}} immediately interests, one has reason to presuppose at least a predisposition {{ 170 }} to a good moral cast of mind.

It will be said: this interpretation of aesthetic judgments as having affinity with moral feeling looks far too studied to be held to be the true deciphering of the cipher-code whereby nature figuratively speaks to us in its beautiful forms. Yet, in the first place, this immediate interest in the beautiful in nature is indeed not common, but peculiar only to those whose way of thinking is either already trained to the good or preeminently receptive to this training; and further, the analogy between the pure judgment of taste, which, without depending on any interest, lets a liking be felt and at the same time represents it _a priori_ as proper for humanity in general, and the moral judgment, which does just the same from concepts, leads, even without distinct, subtle, and intentional reflection, to a similar immediate interest in the object of the former, just as in that of the latter: only that the former is a free interest, the latter one grounded on objective laws. To this is also added the admiration of nature, which shows itself in its beautiful products as art, not merely by chance, but as it were intentionally, according to lawful arrangement and as a purposiveness without an end: which latter, since we find it externally nowhere, we naturally seek in ourselves, and indeed in that {{ 171 }} which constitutes the ultimate end of our existence, namely, our moral vocation (concerning which inquiry into the ground of the possibility of such a purposiveness of nature, however, we shall speak for the first time in Teleology).

That liking for beautiful art in a pure judgment of taste is not connected with an immediate interest in the same way as is that for beautiful nature, is also easy to explain. For the former is either such an imitation of the latter as goes to the point of deception: and then it produces its effect as natural beauty (taken as such); or it is an art visibly directed intentionally toward our liking: but then the liking for this product would, to be sure, take place immediately through taste, but it would arouse no other than a mediate interest in the underlying cause, namely an art that can never interest in itself, but only through its end. It will perhaps be said that this is also the case when an object of nature interests through its beauty only insofar {{{ 302 }}} as a moral idea is associated with it; but it is not this idea, but rather the constitution of that object in itself, namely that it qualifies for such an association, which therefore belongs to it internally, that interests immediately.

The charms in beautiful nature, which are so frequently found melting, as it were, into beautiful form, belong either {{ 172 }} to the modifications of light (in coloration) or of sound (in tones). For these are the only sensations that allow not merely sensory feeling, but also reflection on the form of these modifications of the senses, and thus contain in themselves, as it were, a language that nature addresses to us, and which seems to have a higher meaning. Thus the white color of the lily seems to attune the mind to ideas of innocence, and, according to the order of the seven colors, from the red to the violet, 1) to the idea of sublimity, 2) of audacity, 3) of frankness, 4) of friendliness, 5) of modesty, 6) of constancy, and 7) of tenderness. The song of birds heralds joyfulness and contentment with its existence. At least we interpret nature in this way, whether such be its intention or not. But this interest, which we here take in beauty, absolutely requires that it be beauty of nature; and it vanishes entirely as soon as one notices that one has been deceived and that it is only art: so much so, indeed, that even taste can then no longer find anything beautiful in it, nor the eye anything charming. What is more highly praised by poets than the enchantingly beautiful song of the nightingale in lonely copses on a still summer evening in the soft light of the moon? Yet there are examples where, in the absence of such a singer, {{ 173 }} some jovial innkeeper had deceived his guests, who had stopped at his place to enjoy the country air, to their greatest satisfaction, by hiding in a copse a mischievous lad who knew how to copy this song entirely similar to nature (with a reed or tube in his mouth). But as soon as one becomes aware that it is a deception, no one will endure for long to listen to this singing, which was previously held to be so charming; and the same is the case with every other songbird. It must be nature, or taken by us to be so, in order for us to be able to take an immediate ***interest*** in the beautiful as such; but still more is this so, if we are actually to have a right to expect of others that they should take such an interest in it: which in fact happens, {{{ 303 }}} in that we regard the way of thinking of those as coarse and ignoble who have no ***feeling*** for beautiful nature (for thus we call the receptivity for an interest in its contemplation) and stick, at mealtime or with the bottle, to the enjoyment of mere sensory sensations.
