## § 16. The judgment of taste by which an object is declared beautiful under the condition of a determinate concept is not pure

Beauty is of two kinds: free beauty (_pulchritudo vaga_), and beauty that is merely dependent (_pulchritudo adhaerens_). The former presupposes no concept of what the object is meant to be; the second does presuppose such a concept, along with the perfection of the object in accordance with it. Beauties of the former sort are called (self-subsisting) beauties of this or that thing; the other kind, as attaching to a concept {{ 49 }} (conditioned beauty), is ascribed to objects that fall under the concept of a particular end.

Flowers are free beauties of nature. Hardly anyone besides a botanist knows what sort of thing a flower is meant to be; and even he, who discerns in it the plant's organ of fructification, takes no account of this natural end when he judges it by taste. Hence no perfection of any kind, no inner purposiveness to which the composition of the manifold might refer, is laid at the ground of this judgment. Many birds (the parrot, the hummingbird, the bird of paradise) and a host of marine crustaceans are beauties in themselves, belonging to no object determined by concepts with regard to its end, but pleasing freely and on their own account. Thus the designs _à la grecque_, the foliage for borders or on wallpaper, and so forth, signify nothing in themselves: they represent nothing, no object under a determinate concept, and are free beauties. One may also count as belonging to the same kind what in music is called fantasias (without a theme), indeed all music without a text.

In judging a free beauty (in respect of mere form) the judgment of taste is pure. No concept is presupposed of any end for which the manifold should serve the given object, and which {{{ 230 }}} the latter is therefore meant to represent, whereby the freedom of the imagination, {{ 50 }} which as it were plays in observing the shape, would only be restricted.

But the beauty of a human being (and within this kind that of a man or a woman or a child), the beauty of a horse, of a building (such as a church, a palace, an arsenal, or a garden pavilion) presupposes a concept of the end that determines what the thing is meant to be, hence a concept of its perfection, and is therefore merely adherent beauty. Now just as the combination of the agreeable (of sensation) with beauty, which properly concerns only the form, hindered the purity of the judgment of taste: so does the combination of the good (namely that for which the manifold is good for the thing itself according to its end) with beauty do damage to the purity of that judgment.

One could apply to a building much that pleases immediately in intuition, if only it were not meant to be a church; one could adorn a figure with every kind of flourish and with light though regular strokes—as the New Zealanders do in their tattooing—provided only it were not a human being; and this human being could have far finer features and a more pleasing, gentler outline of facial structure, if only he were not meant to represent a man, or even a warlike one.

Now the satisfaction in the manifold in a thing {{ 51 }} in relation to the inner end that determines its possibility is a satisfaction grounded on a concept; but that in beauty is one that presupposes no concept, being instead directly connected with the representation by means of which the object is given to us (not that by which it is thought). When the judgment of taste, then, in respect of the latter is made dependent on the end in the former, as a judgment of reason, and is thereby restricted, then it is no longer a free and pure judgment of taste.

To be sure, taste gains through this combination of aesthetic with intellectual satisfaction in that it becomes fixed and, though it is not universal, rules can nevertheless be prescribed to it with regard to certain purposively determined objects. But these are then also no rules of taste, but merely of the reconciliation of taste with reason, that is, of the beautiful with the good, through which the former becomes usable as an instrument of the aim with regard to the latter, in order to subordinate that disposition of mind which sustains itself and is of {{{ 231 }}} subjective universal validity to that way of thinking which can be attained only through laborious resolve but is objectively universally valid. Properly speaking, however, neither does perfection gain through beauty, nor beauty through perfection; {{ 52 }} rather, since it cannot be avoided, when we compare the representation through which an object is given to us with the object (with regard to what it is meant to be) by means of a concept, to hold it together at the same time with the sensation in the subject, the ***entire faculty*** of the power of representation gains when both states of mind are in accord.

A judgment of taste with regard to an object of determinate inner end would be pure only if the one judging either had no concept of this end or abstracted from it in his judgment. But then this person, even though he passed a correct judgment of taste in judging the object as free beauty, would nevertheless be censured by the other, who regards the beauty in it only as a dependent property (looks to the end of the object), and accused of a false taste, although both judge correctly in their own way: the one according to what he has before his senses, the other according to what he has in his thoughts. By means of this distinction one can settle many a dispute among the judges of taste about beauty, by showing them that the one is holding to free beauty, the other to dependent beauty, the former passing a pure, the second an applied judgment of taste.
