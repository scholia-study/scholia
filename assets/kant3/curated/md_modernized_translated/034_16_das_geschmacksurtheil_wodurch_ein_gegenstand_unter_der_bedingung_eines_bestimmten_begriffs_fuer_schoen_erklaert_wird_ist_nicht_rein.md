## § 16. The judgment of taste by which an object is declared beautiful under the condition of a determinate concept is not pure

There are two kinds of beauty: free beauty (_pulchritudo vaga_), or merely adherent beauty (_pulchritudo adhaerens_). The former presupposes no concept of what the object is supposed to be; the second presupposes such a concept and the perfection of the object in accordance with it. The kinds of the former are called (self-subsisting) beauties of this or that thing; the other, as adhering to a concept {{ 49 }} (conditioned beauty), is attributed to objects that stand under the concept of a particular end.

Flowers are free beauties of nature. What sort of thing a flower is supposed to be is hardly known to anyone except the botanist; and even he, who recognizes in it the organ of fructification of the plant, takes no account of this natural end when he judges about it through taste. Thus no perfection of any kind, no inner purposiveness to which the composition of the manifold might refer, is laid at the ground of this judgment. Many birds (the parrot, the hummingbird, the bird of paradise), a host of crustaceans of the sea are beauties in themselves, which belong to no object determined as to its end according to concepts, but please freely and in themselves. Thus the drawings _à la grecque_, the foliage for borders or on wallpapers, etc., signify nothing in themselves: they represent nothing, no object under a determinate concept, and are free beauties. One can also count what is called in music fantasias (without a theme), indeed all music without a text, as of the same kind.

In the judging of a free beauty (according to mere form) the judgment of taste is pure. No concept of any end for which the manifold is to serve the given object, and what {{{ 230 }}} the latter is thus supposed to represent, is presupposed, by which the freedom of the imagination, {{ 50 }} which as it were plays in the observation of the shape, would only be restricted.

But the beauty of a human being (and within this kind that of a man or a woman or a child), the beauty of a horse, of a building (such as a church, palace, arsenal, or summerhouse) presupposes a concept of the end that determines what the thing is supposed to be, hence a concept of its perfection, and is thus merely adherent beauty. Now just as the combination of the agreeable (of sensation) with beauty, which properly concerns only the form, hindered the purity of the judgment of taste: so the combination of the good (namely that for which the manifold is good for the thing itself according to its end) with beauty does detriment to the purity of the latter.

One could attach to a building much that pleases immediately in intuition, if only it were not supposed to be a church; one could beautify a figure with all sorts of flourishes and light yet regular lines, as the New Zealanders do with their tattooing, if only it were not a human being; and this one could have much finer features and a more pleasing, gentler outline of facial formation, if only it were not supposed to represent a man, or even a warlike one.

Now the satisfaction in the manifold in a thing {{ 51 }} in relation to the inner end that determines its possibility is a satisfaction grounded on a concept; but that in beauty is one which presupposes no concept, but is immediately connected with the representation through which the object is given (not through which it is thought). If now the judgment of taste with respect to the latter is made dependent on the end in the former, as a judgment of reason, and is thereby restricted, then the former is no longer a free and pure judgment of taste.

To be sure, taste gains by this combination of aesthetic satisfaction with the intellectual in that it is fixed and, although it is not universal, rules can nevertheless be prescribed to it with respect to certain purposively determined objects. But these are then also not rules of taste, but merely of the reconciliation of taste with reason, i.e., of the beautiful with the good, through which the former becomes usable as an instrument of the intention with respect to the latter, in order to subordinate that disposition of mind which sustains itself and is of {{{ 231 }}} subjective universal validity to that way of thinking which can be sustained only by laborious resolve but is objectively universally valid. Properly, however, neither does perfection gain through beauty, nor beauty through perfection; {{ 52 }} but because it cannot be avoided, when we compare the representation through which an object is given to us with the object (with respect to what it is supposed to be) through a concept, to hold it together at the same time with the sensation in the subject, the ***entire faculty*** of the power of representation gains when both states of mind agree.

A judgment of taste with respect to an object of a determinate inner end would be pure only if the one judging either had no concept of this end, or abstracted from it in his judgment. But then this one, although he made a correct judgment of taste in judging the object as a free beauty, would nevertheless be censured by the other, who regards the beauty in it only as an adherent property (looks to the end of the object), and accused of false taste, although both judge correctly in their own way: the one according to what he has before his senses, the other according to what he has in his thoughts. By means of this distinction one can settle many a dispute of the judges of taste about beauty, by showing them that the one holds to free, the other to adherent beauty, the former making a pure, the latter an applied judgment of taste.
