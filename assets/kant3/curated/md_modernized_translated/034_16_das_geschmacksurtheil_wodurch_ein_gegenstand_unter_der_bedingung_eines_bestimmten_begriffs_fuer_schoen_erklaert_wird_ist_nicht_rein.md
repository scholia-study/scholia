## § 16. The judgment of taste by which an object is declared beautiful under the condition of a determinate concept is not pure

Beauty is of two kinds: free beauty (_pulchritudo vaga_), and beauty that is merely dependent (_pulchritudo adhaerens_). The former does not presuppose any concept of what the object ought to be; the second presupposes such a concept, together with the object's perfection as measured against it. The varieties of the former are termed (self-subsisting) beauties belonging to this or that thing; the other sort, since it clings to a concept {{ 49 }} (conditioned beauty), is attributed to objects that stand under the concept of a special end.

Flowers are free beauties of nature. Hardly anyone besides a botanist knows what sort of thing a flower is meant to be; and even he, who discerns in it the plant's organ of fructification, takes no account of this natural end when he judges it by taste. Hence no perfection of any kind, no inner purposiveness to which the composition of the manifold might refer, is laid at the ground of this judgment. Birds of many kinds (the parrot, the hummingbird, the bird of paradise), together with numerous shellfish of the sea, are beauties on their own, ones that pertain to no object fixed by concepts as regards its end, but rather please freely and for themselves. In the same way the drawings _à la grecque_, the foliage used for framing or upon paper hangings, and the like, mean nothing on their own account: they exhibit nothing at all, present no object falling under a fixed concept, and count as free beauties. To the very same class one may likewise reckon what in music goes by the name of fantasias (without a theme), indeed the whole of music unaccompanied by any text.

When we appraise a free beauty (according to its mere form alone), the judgment of taste is pure. Here one presupposes no concept of some end that the manifold would have to serve in the given object, and which {{{ 230 }}} the object is accordingly supposed to present—an end through which the freedom of the imagination, {{ 50 }} playing as it were in its contemplation of the shape, would merely be curtailed.

The beauty of a human being, however (and within this kind that of a man, of a woman, or of a child), the beauty likewise of a horse or of a building (be it church, palace, arsenal, or garden-house), presupposes a concept of the end which lays down what the thing ought to be, and hence a concept of its perfection, so that it is nothing but adherent beauty. Just as, then, joining the agreeable (of sensation) to beauty—which strictly speaking has to do only with the form—stood in the way of the purity of the judgment of taste, so too joining the good (namely, the good that the manifold has for the thing itself, in view of its end) to beauty impairs that same purity.

One could apply to a building much that pleases immediately in intuition, if only it were not meant to be a church; one could adorn a figure with every kind of flourish and with light though regular strokes—as the New Zealanders do in their tattooing—provided only it were not a human being; and this human being could have far finer features and a more pleasing, gentler outline of facial structure, if only he were not meant to represent a man, or even a warlike one.

Now the satisfaction taken in the manifold within a thing {{ 51 }} with reference to the inner end that settles its possibility is a satisfaction resting upon a concept; the satisfaction in beauty, by contrast, is one that requires no concept, being bound up immediately with the representation whereby the object is given (rather than that whereby it is thought). If, then, in respect of the latter the judgment of taste is made dependent on, and thereby limited by, the end that lies in the former, taken as a judgment of reason, it ceases to be a free and pure judgment of taste.

Admittedly, through this linkage of the aesthetic satisfaction with the intellectual one, taste does gain something, in that it becomes fixed—and although it is not universal, still rules can be laid down for it as regards certain objects determined in a purposive manner. Yet these are then not rules of taste either, but only rules for harmonizing taste with reason, that is, the beautiful with the good, whereby the former is rendered serviceable as an instrument of the intention regarding the latter, so as to place that frame of mind which maintains itself and possesses {{{ 231 }}} subjective universal validity beneath the way of thinking that can be secured only through toilsome resolve yet holds objectively universal validity. Strictly, however, perfection is not enhanced by beauty, nor beauty by perfection; {{ 52 }} rather, since it is unavoidable that, when we compare the representation whereby a given object comes to us with the object (in view of what it ought to be) through a concept, we hold it together at once with the sensation in the subject, the ***entire faculty*** of the power of representation is enhanced whenever the two states of mind harmonize.

A judgment of taste concerning an object that has a determinate inner end would be pure only in the case where the one judging either possessed no concept of that end or set it aside in his judging. But in that case this person, although he would be pronouncing a correct judgment of taste in appraising the object as a free beauty, would still be reproached by the other—who takes the beauty in it to be merely a dependent quality (attending to the object's end)—and charged with a false taste, even though each judges rightly after his own fashion: the one by what lies before his senses, the other by what he holds in thought. Through this differentiation many a quarrel among the arbiters of taste concerning beauty can be resolved, by pointing out to them that the one adheres to free beauty while the other adheres to dependent beauty, the former delivering a pure and the second an applied judgment of taste.
