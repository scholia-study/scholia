## § 69. What an antinomy of the power of judgment is

Taken by itself, the ***determining*** power of judgment has no principles that ground ***concepts of objects***. It has no autonomy; for it does no more than ***subsume*** under given laws, or concepts, which serve as its principles. For just this reason it too runs no risk of an antinomy of its own, nor of any conflict among its principles. In the same way the transcendental power of judgment, whose office was to subsume the conditions under categories, was of itself not ***nomothetic***; it did no more than state the conditions of sensible intuition under which a given concept, functioning as a law of the understanding, can be furnished with reality (that is, application): and on this point it could never come into discord with itself (at least so far as its principles are concerned).

The ***reflecting*** power of judgment, however, is required to subsume under a law {{ 312 }} that has not yet been given, and which is therefore really nothing more than a principle for reflecting on objects for which, from the objective side, we are wholly without a law, or without any concept of the object adequate to serve as a principle for the cases as they occur. Since, however, the cognitive faculties are never to be employed without some principle, the reflecting power of judgment will in such cases have to serve as a principle to itself: and because this principle is not objective, and can lay down no ground of cognition of the object adequate to the aim, it is to serve as a merely subjective principle for the purposive employment of the cognitive faculties, that is, for reflecting on objects of a given sort. Hence, in reference to such cases, the reflecting power of judgment possesses maxims of its own, and necessary ones at that, for the sake of the cognition of the laws of nature {{{ 386 }}} within experience, so as through them to reach concepts, be these even concepts of reason, if it absolutely needs such concepts merely to become acquainted with nature according to its empirical laws. — Now among these necessary maxims of the reflecting power of judgment there can occur a conflict, and thus an antinomy, on which a dialectic rests; and this dialectic, provided that each of two conflicting maxims is rooted in the nature of the cognitive faculties, can be called a natural one, together with an {{ 313 }} inevitable semblance that criticism has to lay bare and resolve, so that it does not deceive us.
