## § 80. On the Necessary Subordination of the Principle of Mechanism to the Teleological in the Explanation of a Thing as a Natural End

The ***authorization*** to ***aim*** at a merely mechanical mode of explanation of all natural products is in itself completely unrestricted; but the ***ability*** to ***get by*** with it alone is, according to the constitution of our understanding in so far as it has to do with things as natural ends, not only very restricted, but also clearly limited: namely, so that {{ 367 }} according to a principle of the power of judgment, nothing at all can be accomplished toward the explanation of the latter by the former procedure alone, and consequently the estimation of such products must at all times be subordinated by us to a teleological principle as well.

{{{ 418 }}} It is therefore reasonable, indeed meritorious, to pursue the mechanism of nature for the behoof of an explanation of natural products as far as can be done with probability, and not to abandon this attempt because it is ***in itself*** impossible to meet with the purposiveness of nature along its path, but only because it is ***for us*** as humans impossible; since this would require an intuition other than sensible and a determinate cognition of the intelligible substrate of nature, from which ground could be given even for the mechanism of appearances according to particular laws, all of which completely exceeds our faculty.

So that the investigator of nature does not work at a total loss, he must, in the estimation of things whose concept as natural ends is indubitably established (organized beings), always lay at the basis some original organization which makes use of that mechanism itself in order to produce other organized forms, or to develop its own into new shapes (which, however, always result from that end and in accordance with {{ 368 }} it).

It is praiseworthy, by means of comparative anatomy, to go through the great creation of organized natures in order to see whether there is not to be found in it something similar to a system, and indeed according to the principle of generation; without our needing to remain standing at the mere principle of estimation (which provides no disclosure for the insight into their generation) and despondently to abandon all claim to ***insight into nature*** in this field. The agreement of so many genera of animals in a certain common schema, which seems to lie at the basis not only of their bone structure but also of the arrangement of the other parts, where an admirable simplicity of the ground plan has been able to produce so great a manifoldness of species through the shortening of one part and elongation of another, through the folding up of this one and the unfolding of that one, lets a ray of hope, albeit weak, fall into the mind that here indeed something might be accomplished with the principle of the mechanism of nature, without which there can be no natural science at all. This analogy of forms, in so far as they seem to be generated according to a common prototype despite all their diversity, strengthens the suspicion of a real relationship among them in their generation from a common mother, through the step-by-step approximation of one animal genus {{ 369 }} {{{ 419 }}} to another, from that one in which the principle of ends seems to be most confirmed, namely man, down to the polyp, and from this even to mosses and lichens, and finally to the lowest stage of nature noticeable to us, to raw matter: from which and its forces, according to mechanical laws (like those by which it acts in crystal generations), the whole technique of nature, which is so incomprehensible to us in organized beings that we believe ourselves compelled to think of another principle for it, seems to descend.

Here it is free to the ***archaeologist*** of nature to let that great family of creatures (for so one would have to represent them if the said continuously connected relationship is to have a ground) arise from the remaining traces of its oldest revolutions according to all mechanism of the same known or conjectured by him. He can make the mother-womb of the earth, which had just emerged from its chaotic state (as if it were a large animal), initially give birth to creatures of less purposeful form, and these in turn to others which developed in a manner more appropriate to their place of generation and their relations to one another; until this womb itself, hardened and ossified, had restricted its births to definite, henceforth non-degenerating species, and the manifoldness remained {{ 370 }} as it had turned out at the end of the operation of that fruitful formative power. — But he must nevertheless, to that end, attribute to this universal mother an organization purposely set for all these creatures, otherwise the purposive form of the products of the animal and vegetable kingdoms is not to be thought of at all as regards their possibility.[^*] But in that case {{{ 420 }}} he has only postponed the ground of explanation further, and cannot presume to have made the generation of those two kingdoms independent of the condition of {{ 371 }} final causes.

Even as regards the alteration to which certain individuals of organized genera are accidentally subjected, if one finds that their thus altered character is hereditary and received into the generative power, it cannot fitly be estimated otherwise than as an occasional development of a purposive disposition originally present in the species for the self-preservation of the kind: because the generating of its like is so closely connected, in the thoroughgoing internal purposiveness of an organized being, with the condition of admitting nothing into the generative power which does not also belong, in such a system of ends, to one of the undeveloped original dispositions. For if one departs from this principle, one cannot know with certainty whether several parts of the form now to be found in a species might not be of equally accidental, purposeless origin; and the principle of teleology: to estimate nothing in an organized being as unpurposive of that which maintains itself in its reproduction, would thereby have to become very unreliable in its application and be valid solely for the original stock (which, however, we no longer know).

***Hume*** makes, against those who find it necessary to assume for all such natural ends {{ 372 }} a teleological principle of estimation, i.e., an architectural understanding, the objection: that one could with equal right ask how such an understanding is possible, i.e., how the manifold faculties and properties which constitute the possibility of an understanding that has at the same time executive power, could have found themselves together so purposely in one being. But this objection is void. For the whole difficulty which surrounds the question concerning the first generation of a thing containing ends in itself and comprehensible through them alone rests on the inquiry after the unity of the ground of the connection of the manifold ***outside one another*** in this product; since then, if this ground is placed in {{{ 421 }}} the understanding of a producing cause as a simple substance, that question, in so far as it is teleological, is sufficiently answered, whereas if the cause is sought merely in matter as an aggregate of many substances outside one another, the unity of the principle for the internally purposive form of its formation is entirely lacking; and the ***autocracy*** of matter in generations which can be comprehended by our understanding only as ends is a word without meaning.

Hence it is that those who seek a supreme ground of the possibility of the objectively purposive forms of matter, {{ 373 }} without granting it an understanding, yet gladly make the world-whole into a single, all-embracing substance (pantheism), or (which is only a more definite explanation of the former) into a sum total of many determinations inhering in a single ***simple substance*** (Spinozism), merely in order to obtain that condition of all purposiveness, the ***unity*** of the ground; whereby they indeed satisfy ***one*** condition of the task, namely unity in the relation of ends, by means of the merely ontological concept of a simple substance, but for the ***other*** condition, namely the relation of the same to its consequence as an ***end***, whereby that ontological ground for the question is to be more closely determined, they adduce nothing, and consequently do not answer ***the whole*** question at all. Moreover, it remains absolutely unanswerable (for our reason) unless we represent to ourselves that original ground of things as a simple ***substance***, and its property of the specific constitution of the natural forms founded upon it, namely the unity of end, as that of an intelligent substance, and the relation of the same to the latter (on account of the contingency which we find in everything that we think of as possible only as an end) as the relation of a ***causality***.

[^*]: An hypothesis of this kind may be called a daring adventure of reason; and there may be few, even of the most acute naturalists, through whose heads it has not sometimes passed. For it is not absurd like _generatio aequivoca_, by which is understood the generation of an organized being through the mechanics of raw, unorganized matter. It would still be _generatio univoca_ in the most general sense of the word, provided only that something organic were generated from another organic being, albeit specifically distinct from it under this kind of being; e.g., if certain aquatic animals developed by degrees into amphibious animals, and from these, after some generations, into land animals. _A priori_, in the judgment of mere reason, this does not contradict itself. But experience shows no example of this, according to which, rather, all generation that we know is _generatio homonyma_, not merely _univoca_ in contrast to generation from unorganized matter, but also brings forth a product similar in its organization itself to the generator, and _generatio heteronyma_, as far as our empirical cognition of nature extends, is nowhere met with.
