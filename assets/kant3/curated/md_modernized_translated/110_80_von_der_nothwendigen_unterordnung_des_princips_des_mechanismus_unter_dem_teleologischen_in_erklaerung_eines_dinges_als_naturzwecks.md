## § 80. On the necessary subordination of the principle of mechanism under the teleological one in explaining a thing as a natural end

The ***warrant*** to ***set out*** upon a purely mechanical manner of explaining every natural product is, taken by itself, wholly without limit; but the ***capability*** to ***make do*** with that manner alone is, given how our understanding is constituted, so far as it has dealings with things as natural ends, not merely much confined but also distinctly bounded: namely in such fashion that {{ 367 }} on a principle of the power of judgment nothing whatever can be achieved toward the explanation of the latter through that first procedure taken alone, so that the judging of such products must always, on our part, be brought at the same time under a teleological principle.

{{{ 418 }}} It is therefore rational, and even to one's credit, to trace natural mechanism, for the purpose of explaining the products of nature, as far as one plausibly can, and not to give this endeavor up on the ground that meeting with nature's purposiveness along such a route is ***in itself*** impossible, but only because it is so ***for us*** as human beings; for it would call for an intuition of a kind other than the sensible, together with a determinate cognition of nature's intelligible substrate, out of which the very ground of the mechanism of the appearances under particular laws could be furnished — something that lies wholly past our power.

In order, then, that the student of nature not toil to no avail, he must, when appraising those things whose concept as natural ends stands beyond doubt (namely organized beings), invariably presuppose some primordial organization that presses that very mechanism into service, whether for generating further organized forms or for unfolding its own into fresh shapes (which, all the same, arise ever out of that purpose and answer to it {{ 368 }} ).

It is a praiseworthy thing to survey the vast creation of organized natures through a comparative anatomy, so as to see whether something resembling a system, and this too as regards the principle by which they are generated, is not to be met with in it; without our being obliged to remain stuck at the bare principle of judging (which yields no disclosure about how they are generated) and, losing heart, to renounce every claim to ***insight into nature*** in this domain. That so many kinds of animal coincide in one certain shared pattern, which seems to underlie not merely their bony framework but the disposition of the other parts as well, where a strikingly simple ground-plan has managed, by cutting one part short and drawing another out, by folding these parts in and unfolding those, to yield so vast a variety of species, lets a beam of hope, faint though it be, fall upon the mind, that here, perhaps, something may be achieved by that principle of nature's mechanism apart from which no science of nature whatever is possible. This analogy of forms, in so far as, for all their variety, they appear to have been produced after one common prototype, reinforces the surmise that they are genuinely related in their descent from a single common ancestral mother, by way of a step-by-step gradation of one animal kind {{ 369 }} {{{ 419 }}} toward the next, starting from the one in which the principle of ends stands most confirmed, that is, the human being, and running down to the polyp, and from the polyp on to mosses and lichens, and at the last to the lowest grade of nature that we can still notice, namely brute matter: out of which, and out of its powers operating under mechanical laws (of the same sort as those governing it when crystals form), the entire technic of nature, so far past our grasp in organized beings that we take ourselves obliged to conceive another principle for it, appears to take its origin.

At this point the ***archaeologist*** of nature stands at liberty to derive that vast family of creatures from the surviving vestiges of nature's most ancient upheavals, following every mechanism of hers that he knows or conjectures (for thus one would be obliged to conceive them, should the pervasive, interconnected kinship already spoken of be meant to rest on a ground). He may let the earth's maternal womb, freshly issued from its chaotic condition (a great animal, as it were), bring forth at the outset creatures of a form less suited to purpose, and these again yet others that shaped themselves in closer keeping with their birthplace and their mutual relations; until that womb, grown stiff and turned to bone, had confined its offspring to fixed species degenerating no further, and the manifold thereafter stayed {{ 370 }} such as it had turned out once the working of that fertile formative power reached its close. — Yet even so he must, to that end, ascribe to this universal mother an organization purposively set up with regard to all these creatures, since otherwise the end-directed shape of what the animal and vegetable kingdoms bring forth cannot be thought as possible at all.[^*] But then {{{ 420 }}} he has merely pushed the ground of explanation back a stage, and may not lay claim to having rendered the origination of those two realms free of any dependence on {{ 371 }} final causes.

So far even as it concerns the alteration to which certain individuals of the organized kinds are subjected by chance, once one finds that a character thus altered passes by inheritance and is absorbed into the procreative power, one cannot suitably judge it as anything but the occasional unfolding of a purposive predisposition, present in the species from the start, serving the kind's self-preservation: for, given the thoroughgoing inner purposiveness of an organized being, the begetting of its like is bound up so intimately with the requirement that nothing be absorbed into the procreative power which does not likewise belong, within such a system of ends, to one of its as-yet-undeveloped original predispositions. For should one abandon this principle, one could no longer be sure whether various pieces of the form now met with in a species might not be of a like accidental, purposeless origin; and the principle of teleology, namely to count nothing in an organized being, among all that keeps itself going through that being's reproduction, as contrary to purpose, would thereby grow highly untrustworthy in its use and hold good only for the primal stem (which, however, is no longer known to us).

***Hume*** brings, against those who deem it needful to posit, for all such natural ends, {{ 372 }} a teleological principle of judging, that is, an architectonic understanding, the objection that one might with just as much right ask how such an understanding is itself possible, that is, how the several powers and characteristics that constitute the possibility of an understanding possessed at once of executive might could have gathered so purposively into a single being. Yet this objection comes to nothing. For the entire difficulty besetting the question of how a thing that harbors ends within itself, and is intelligible through them alone, first comes to be, rests on the demand for a single ground of the linkage of the manifold that lies ***external to one another*** in this product; and thus, once this ground is placed in {{{ 421 }}} the understanding of a productive cause taken as a simple substance, that question, so far as it is teleological, receives adequate answer, whereas if the cause be looked for in matter alone, taken as an assemblage of numerous substances lying apart from one another, then all unity of principle for the inwardly purposive form of matter's shaping is wanting; and the ***autocracy*** of matter in products that our understanding can grasp only as ends is a word without meaning.

It is from this that those who look for a highest ground of the possibility of matter's objectively purposive forms {{ 373 }} while refusing to allow that ground an understanding, are nevertheless ready to turn the sum of the world into one solitary, all-encompassing substance (pantheism), or else (which is merely a more determinate account of the former) into an aggregate of many determinations that inhere in one solitary ***simple substance*** (Spinozism), simply so as to secure that prerequisite of all purposiveness, the ***unity*** of the ground; and here, while they do meet ***one*** condition of the task, namely unity in the reference to ends, through the bare ontological concept of a simple substance, yet toward the ***other*** condition, namely the bearing of that substance upon its result as an ***end***, the very thing by which the ontological ground was to be given closer determination for the question, they bring forward nothing, and so leave ***the whole*** question entirely unanswered. It stays, moreover, wholly past answering (for our reason) unless we picture to ourselves that primordial ground of things as a simple ***substance***, and its property, as concerns the specific make-up of the natural forms that rest upon it, that is, their unity of ends, as the property of an intelligent substance, while picturing the bearing of that substance on those forms (owing to the contingency we discover in everything we are able to think as possible only as an end) as the bearing of a ***causality***.

[^*]: A hypothesis of this kind one might well style a bold adventure on reason's part; and there can hardly be many, even among the keenest students of nature, through whose heads it has not now and then passed. For there is nothing so incongruous about it as there is about _generatio aequivoca_, whereby one means the bringing-forth of an organized being out of the mechanics of raw matter that is wholly unorganized. Even so it would remain _generatio univoca_ taken in the word's widest acceptation, provided only that something organic were brought forth from another organic thing, albeit one specifically different from it within this class of being; for instance, were water-creatures to develop by degrees into marsh-dwellers and, from these, after several generations, into animals of the land. Regarded _a priori_, before reason's unaided judgment, the matter carries no contradiction within itself. But experience furnishes no instance of this; on the contrary, every reproduction with which we are acquainted is _generatio homonyma_, not just _univoca_ as opposed to reproduction from unorganized stuff, but yielding a product that, in its very organization, is of the same kind as what produced it, and _generatio heteronyma_ is met with nowhere, so far as our empirical cognition of nature extends.
