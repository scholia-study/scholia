## VII. On the Aesthetic Representation of the Purposiveness of Nature

That in the representation of an object which is merely subjective, i.e., which constitutes its relation to the subject and not to the object, is the aesthetic constitution of it; but that in it which serves or can be used for the determination {{{ 189 }}} of the object (for cognition) is its logical validity. In the cognition of an object of the senses both relations occur together. In the sensory representation of the things outside me the quality of the space in which we intuit them is the merely subjective in my representation of them (through which what they may be as objects in themselves remains undecided), on account of which relation the object is also thereby thought of merely as appearance; but space, notwithstanding its merely subjective quality, is nevertheless still a constituent of the cognition of things as appearances. ***Sensation*** (here the outer) likewise expresses the merely subjective in our representations of the things outside us, but properly {{ XLIII }} the material (real) element of them (through which something existing is given), just as space expresses the mere form _a priori_ of the possibility of their intuition; and yet the former is also used for the cognition of the objects outside us.

But that subjective element in a representation ***which cannot become any constituent of cognition at all*** is the ***pleasure*** or ***displeasure*** bound up with it; for through it I cognize nothing in the object of the representation, although it may well be the effect of some cognition. Now the purposiveness of a thing, insofar as it is represented in perception, is likewise no constitution of the object itself (for such a thing cannot be perceived), even though it can be inferred from a cognition of things. The purposiveness, therefore, that precedes the cognition of an object, indeed that is even immediately bound up with it without wanting to use the representation of the object for a cognition, is the subjective element of it which cannot become any constituent of cognition at all. The object is therefore on that occasion called purposive only because its representation is immediately bound up with the feeling of pleasure; and this representation itself is an aesthetic representation of purposiveness. — It is only a question whether {{ XLIV }} there is in general any such representation of purposiveness.

If pleasure is bound up with the mere apprehension (_apprehensio_) of the form of an object of intuition without a relation of it to a concept for a determinate cognition: then the representation is thereby related not to the object but solely to the subject; and the pleasure can express nothing other than its suitability to the faculties of cognition which are in play in the reflecting power of judgment, {{{ 190 }}} and insofar as they are in it, hence merely a subjective formal purposiveness of the object. For that apprehension of the forms into the imagination can never take place without the reflecting power of judgment, even unintentionally, comparing them at least with its faculty of relating intuitions to concepts. If now in this comparison the imagination (as the faculty of intuitions _a priori_) is unintentionally put into accord with the understanding (as the faculty of concepts) through a given representation, and a feeling of pleasure is thereby aroused, then the object must on that occasion be regarded as purposive for the reflecting power of judgment. Such a judgment is an aesthetic judgment about the purposiveness of the object, which is grounded on no existing concept of the object and furnishes none of it. That object whose form (not the material element of its representation, as sensation) is judged in the mere {{ XLV }} reflection upon it (without any intention regarding a concept to be acquired of it) to be the ground of a pleasure in the representation of such an object: with the representation of this object this pleasure is also judged to be necessarily bound up, consequently as being not merely for the subject which apprehends this form but for everyone who judges in general. The object is then called beautiful; and the faculty for judging through such a pleasure (consequently also with universal validity) is called taste. For since the ground of the pleasure is placed merely in the form of the object for reflection in general, hence in no sensation of the object and also without relation to a concept that contained any intention: it is then solely the lawfulness in the empirical use of the power of judgment in general (unity of the imagination with the understanding) in the subject with which the representation of the object in reflection, whose conditions are valid _a priori_ universally, agrees; and since this agreement of the object with the faculties of the subject is contingent, it brings about the representation of a purposiveness of the object with regard to the faculties of cognition of the subject.

Here, now, is a pleasure which, like all pleasure or displeasure that is not effected through the concept of freedom (i.e., through the prior determination of the higher faculty of desire by pure reason), can never {{ XLVI }} be seen from concepts to be necessarily bound up with the representation of an object, but must always be cognized only through {{{ 191 }}} reflected perception as connected with this, consequently, like all empirical judgments, can announce no objective necessity and lay claim to validity _a priori_. But the judgment of taste, too, lays claim, like every other empirical judgment, only to being valid for everyone, which is always possible notwithstanding its inner contingency. The strange and deviant thing lies only in this: that it is not an empirical concept but a feeling of pleasure (consequently no concept at all) which nevertheless, through the judgment of taste, just as if it were a predicate bound up with the cognition of the object, is to be required of everyone and connected with the representation of it.

A single judgment of experience, e.g., that of one who perceives a movable drop of water in a rock crystal, rightly demands that everyone else must find it likewise, because he has made this judgment according to the universal conditions of the determining power of judgment under the laws of a possible experience in general. Likewise, the one who feels pleasure in the mere reflection upon the form of an object without regard to a concept, although this judgment is empirical and a single judgment, rightly lays {{ XLVII }} claim to everyone's assent: because the ground for this pleasure is met with in the universal, although subjective, condition of reflecting judgments, namely the purposive correspondence of an object (whether it be a product of nature or of art) with the relation of the faculties of cognition among themselves which are required for every empirical cognition (the imagination and the understanding). The pleasure is therefore in the judgment of taste indeed dependent on an empirical representation and cannot be bound up _a priori_ with any concept (one cannot determine _a priori_ which object will be in accord with taste or not, one must try it out); but it is the determining ground of this judgment only by one's being conscious that it rests merely on the reflection and the universal, although only subjective, conditions of its agreement with the cognition of objects in general, for which the form of the object is purposive.

That is the reason why the judgments of taste, in respect of their possibility, since this presupposes a principle _a priori_, are also subject to a critique, although this principle is neither a principle of cognition {{{ 192 }}} for the understanding nor a practical one for the will, and is thus _a priori_ not at all determining.

The receptivity to a pleasure from reflection upon the forms {{ XLVIII }} of things (of nature as well as of art) designates, however, not only a purposiveness of the objects in relation to the reflecting power of judgment, in accordance with the concept of nature, in the subject, but also, conversely, of the subject with regard to the objects in respect of their form, indeed even of their formlessness, in accordance with the concept of freedom; and through this it comes about that the aesthetic judgment is related not merely as a judgment of taste to the beautiful, but also, as one arisen from a feeling of spirit, to the ***sublime***, and thus that critique of the aesthetic power of judgment must be divided into two principal parts corresponding to these.
