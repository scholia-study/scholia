## VII. On the aesthetic representation of the purposiveness of nature

In the representation of an object, that which is merely subjective — that is, which makes up its bearing on the subject and not on the thing — constitutes the representation's aesthetic character; but that in it which serves, or admits of being used, toward determining {{{ 189 }}} the object (toward cognition) constitutes its logical validity. Where an object of the senses is cognized, both of these relations are present together. In the sensory representation of things external to me, the quality of the space wherein we intuit them makes up what is merely subjective in my representation of those things (so that what they may be as objects in themselves stays unsettled), and it is because of this relation that the object, on this account too, is thought only as appearance; space, however, its merely subjective quality notwithstanding, still remains an ingredient in the cognition of things as appearances. ***Sensation*** (here the outer sort) expresses, in the same way, what is merely subjective in our representations of things beyond us, but strictly {{ XLIII }} their material (real) ingredient (through which something existent is given), whereas space expresses the mere _a priori_ form of the possibility of intuiting them; and yet sensation too is put to use for cognizing objects beyond us.

Yet the subjective feature of a representation ***which can never become any component of cognition*** is the ***pleasure*** or ***displeasure*** attached to it; for by means of it I cognize nothing about the object represented, even though it may well spring from some cognition as that cognition's effect. The purposiveness of a thing, for its part, insofar as perception represents it, likewise amounts to no property belonging to the object itself (since no such property could be perceived), even if it admits of being inferred from our cognition of things. Purposiveness of this kind, then — which runs ahead of the cognition of an object, indeed which becomes immediately tied to the representation even where one has no wish to employ that representation for cognition — is the representation's subjective side, incapable of ever becoming a component of cognition. The object, accordingly, gets called purposive on this ground alone: that its representation stands in immediate connection with the feeling of pleasure; and this very representation counts as an aesthetic representation of purposiveness. — It remains only to ask whether {{ XLIV }} such a representation of purposiveness so much as exists.

Suppose that pleasure is connected with the bare grasping (_apprehensio_) of the form belonging to an object of intuition, where that form is not referred to any concept for the sake of a determinate cognition: the representation is then referred, not to the object, but purely to the subject; and the pleasure can amount to nothing beyond the object's fittingness to those cognitive faculties which are at play {{{ 190 }}} within the reflecting power of judgment, and so far as they are at play there, hence to nothing beyond a merely subjective and formal purposiveness of the object. For that grasping of forms into the imagination could never occur unless the reflecting power of judgment, even without meaning to, set them side by side at least with its own capacity for referring intuitions to concepts. Now if, in the course of this comparison, a given representation should set the imagination (taken as our faculty for _a priori_ intuitions) unintentionally into agreement with the understanding (taken as our faculty for concepts), and if a feeling of pleasure is thereby awakened, then the object must, in that case, be viewed as purposive for the reflecting power of judgment. A judgment of that sort is an aesthetic judgment upon the purposiveness of the object — one grounded in no already available concept of the thing, and yielding no concept of it either. When the form of an object (not the material of its representation, as sensation) is appraised, in the mere {{ XLV }} reflection upon it (with no view to a concept to be won from it), as the basis of a pleasure taken in representing such an object: then that pleasure is judged to be necessarily bound to the representation of it as well, and hence to hold not just for the subject who takes in this form, but for every judging being whatever. In that case the object is termed beautiful; and taste is our capacity for judging through a pleasure of this kind (and therefore with universal validity). For since the ground of the pleasure is laid solely in the object's form as it stands for reflection generally, thus in no sensation belonging to the object and equally without any bearing on a concept that would carry some aim: what alone is then at issue is the conformity to law in the empirical exercise of the power of judgment as such (the imagination and the understanding brought into unity) within the subject, a conformity with which the representation of the object accords in reflection, whose conditions are universally valid _a priori_; and since this concord between the object and the subject's faculties is contingent, it gives rise to the representation of a purposiveness of the object in regard to the subject's cognitive faculties.

Here, then, we have a pleasure which — like every pleasure or displeasure that is not produced through the concept of freedom (that is, through a prior determination of the higher faculty of desire by pure reason) — can never {{ XLVI }} be seen, out of concepts, to attach necessarily to the representation of an object, but has instead to be recognized, on every occasion, as linked with that representation only by way of {{{ 191 }}} a reflected perception, and which therefore, as with all empirical judgments, can proclaim no objective necessity nor stake any claim to validity _a priori_. But the judgment of taste, exactly like any other empirical judgment, sets up only the claim to hold good for everyone, a claim that remains ever possible despite the judgment's inner contingency. The puzzling and exceptional thing lies solely here: that what the judgment of taste demands of everyone, and asks to be attached to the representation of the object — treating it as though it were a predicate joined to the cognition of the object — is not an empirical concept at all, but a feeling of pleasure (and so no concept whatever).

A single empirical judgment — say, that of a person who notices a mobile droplet of water enclosed within a rock crystal — rightly requires that anyone else must find the matter just as he does, since he has passed this judgment according to the general conditions of the determining power of judgment, beneath the laws governing any possible experience whatever. In precisely the same way, someone who takes pleasure in merely reflecting on an object's form, leaving any concept aside — although his judgment is empirical and a single one — rightly puts forward {{ XLVII }} a claim to the agreement of everyone: for the ground of this pleasure is discovered in the general, albeit subjective, condition governing reflecting judgments, namely in the purposive accord of an object (be it nature's own product or a work of art) with that reciprocal relation among the cognitive faculties (imagination and understanding) demanded by every empirical cognition. Within the judgment of taste, therefore, the pleasure does depend upon an empirical representation and admits of no _a priori_ tie to any concept (one is unable to settle _a priori_ whether a given object will suit taste or fail to; it has to be tried out); yet it serves as the determining ground of this judgment only insofar as one is aware that it rests upon nothing but reflection, together with the general — though merely subjective — conditions under which such reflection accords with the cognition of objects at large, an accord for which the object's form is purposive.

This is why the judgments of taste, in respect of their very possibility, stand subject to a critique as well — since that possibility presupposes a principle _a priori_ — even though the principle in question is neither a cognitive principle {{{ 192 }}} for the understanding nor a practical one for the will, and hence determines nothing whatever _a priori_.

But our susceptibility to a pleasure that springs from reflecting on the forms {{ XLVIII }} of things (those of nature no less than those of art) marks out not merely a purposiveness of the objects, relative to the reflecting power of judgment and answering to the concept of nature, as lodged in the subject, but conversely a purposiveness of the subject too, with respect to the objects — as regards their form, indeed even their want of form — answering to the concept of freedom; and by this means it comes to pass: that the aesthetic judgment is referred not only, qua judgment of taste, to the beautiful, but also, as sprung from a feeling of spirit, to the ***sublime***, so that the critique of the aesthetic power of judgment has to break apart into two principal divisions matching these two.
