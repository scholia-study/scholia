## {{ 246 }} § 58. On the idealism of the purposiveness of nature, as well as of art, as the sole principle of the aesthetic power of judgment

One may, first of all, locate the principle of taste either in the claim that it always judges according to empirical determining grounds, and hence according to grounds given only _a posteriori_ through the senses, or one may grant that it judges from a ground _a priori_. The first of these would constitute the ***empiricism*** in the critique of taste, the second its ***rationalism***. On the ***first***, the object of our satisfaction would not be distinguished from the ***agreeable***; on the second, if the judgment rested on determinate concepts, it would not be distinguished from the ***good***; and thus all ***beauty*** would be denied out of the world, and in its place there would remain only a special name, perhaps for a certain mixture of the two aforementioned kinds of satisfaction. Yet {{{ 347 }}} we have shown that there are also grounds of satisfaction _a priori_, which can therefore coexist with the principle of rationalism, even though they cannot be grasped in ***determinate concepts***.

The rationalism of the principle of taste, by contrast, is either that of the ***realism*** of purposiveness or that of its ***idealism***. Now since a judgment of taste is not a cognitive judgment, and beauty {{ 247 }}, regarded on its own, is not a quality belonging to the object, the rationalism of taste's principle can never be placed in the view that the purposiveness in this judgment is thought as objective—that is, that the judgment bears theoretically, and hence also logically (albeit only in a confused judging), upon the perfection of the object, but only ***aesthetically***, upon the accord of its representation in the imagination with those principles essential to the power of judgment as such, within the subject. It follows that, even on the principle of rationalism, the judgment of taste and the difference between its realism and its idealism admit of being placed only in this: that either, in the former case, that subjective purposiveness is taken to accord with our power of judgment as an actual (intentional) ***end*** of nature (or of art), or, in the latter case, only as a purposive agreement—one arising of itself and by chance, without any end—with the need of the power of judgment in regard to nature and its forms generated according to particular laws.

The realism of the aesthetic purposiveness of nature—where, namely, one would like to assume that at the basis of the production of the beautiful there lay an idea of it in the producing cause, that is, an ***end*** in favor of our imagination—is very much advocated by the {{ 248 }} beautiful formations in the realm of organized nature. Flowers, blossoms, indeed the shapes of whole plants; the daintiness of animal formations of all sorts of kinds, unnecessary for their own use but as if selected for our taste; above all the variety and harmonious composition of colors so pleasing and charming to our eyes (in the pheasant, in shellfish, insects, down to the commonest flowers), which, since they concern merely the surface—and even on this not the figure of the creatures, which could yet be requisite for their inner ends—and thus seem to be aimed entirely at outer contemplation: give {{{ 348 }}} great weight to the manner of explanation through the assumption of actual ends of nature for our aesthetic power of judgment.

Against this, however, it is not only reason that resists such an assumption, through its maxims of everywhere preventing, so far as possible, the unnecessary multiplication of principles; but nature also shows, in its free formations, everywhere so much mechanical propensity toward the generation of forms that seem, as it were, made for the aesthetic use of our power of judgment, without giving the slightest ground for the conjecture that this calls for anything more than its mechanism—nature simply as such—in accordance with which they can be purposive for our judging even without any idea underlying them. By a ***free formation*** of nature, however, I understand {{ 249 }} ***that one*** in which, through the volatilization or separation of a part of a ***fluid at rest*** (sometimes merely of the heat-material), the remainder, on becoming solid, assumes a determinate shape or fabric (figure or texture) that differs according to the specific difference of the materials, but in one and the same material is exactly the same. For this, however, what one always understands by a true fluidity is presupposed—namely, that the matter in it is completely dissolved, that is, not to be regarded as a mere mixture of solid parts merely suspended within it.

The formation then comes about through ***shooting together***, that is, through a sudden solidifying—not by a gradual passage out of the fluid into the solid condition, but as it were by a leap, a transition that also goes by the name of ***crystallization***. The most ordinary instance of formation of this sort is freezing water, in which straight little ice-spicules first form, joining together at angles of 60 degrees, while others attach themselves in the same way at each point of them, until everything has turned to ice: so that during this time the water between the ice-spicules does not gradually grow more viscous, but is as perfectly fluid as it would be at a far greater warmth, and yet has the complete cold of ice. The matter that separates out, which slips away all at once in the instant {{ 250 }} of solidifying, is a sizable quantity of heat-stuff, whose departure—since it was required merely for being fluid—leaves this ice, now formed, no colder in the slightest than the water that had been fluid within it just before.

Many salts, and likewise stones that have a crystalline figure, are generated in the same way from an earth-kind dissolved in water by who knows what mediation {{{ 349 }}}. In the same way the druse-like configurations of many minerals—of cubical galena, of ruby silver ore, and the like—are formed, in all likelihood also in water and through the shooting together of the parts: in that they are compelled by some cause to leave this vehicle and to unite with one another into determinate outer shapes.

But internally, too, all materials that were fluid merely through heat and have taken on solidity through cooling display a determinate texture in their fracture, and allow the inference that, had their own weight or contact with the air not hindered it, they would have displayed their specifically peculiar shape outwardly as well: something that has been observed in certain metals which, after melting, had hardened on the outside but were still fluid within, by drawing off the inner, still-fluid part and letting the remainder left behind inside shoot together quietly. Many of those mineral {{ 251 }} crystallizations, such as the spar-druses, hematite, and iron-flowers, often give exceedingly beautiful shapes such as art might ever devise; and the Glory in the grotto of Antiparos is merely the product of water seeping through layers of gypsum.

To all appearances the fluid is generally older than the solid, and both plants and animal bodies are formed from fluid nutritive matter insofar as it takes shape at rest: admittedly, in the latter, first of all according to a certain original disposition directed toward ends (which, as will be shown in the second part, must be judged not aesthetically but teleologically, following the realist principle); but incidentally perhaps also shooting together in conformity with the general law of the affinity of materials and forming itself in freedom. Now, just as the aqueous fluids, dissolved within an atmosphere that constitutes a mixture of several kinds of air, generate—once the former part company with the latter through the departure of warmth—snow-figures whose form, varying with the momentary composition of the air, is frequently of an exceedingly beautiful and often quite artificial-looking shape: so it can well be thought, without withdrawing anything from the teleological principle of judging organization: that as regards the beauty of flowers, of bird-feathers, of shells—both in their shape and in their color—this {{ 252 }} could be ascribed to nature and to its capacity to form itself, in its freedom, without particular ends directed at this, according to chemical laws, through the deposition of the matter requisite for {{{ 350 }}} organization, also aesthetically-purposively.

But what directly proves the principle of the ***ideality*** of purposiveness in the beautiful of nature—as that which we always lay at the basis in the aesthetic judgment itself, and which permits us to make use of no realism of an end of nature for our power of representation as a ground of explanation—is this: that in judging beauty as such we look for the standard of it _a priori_ within our own selves, and that the aesthetic power of judgment is, with respect to the judgment whether a thing be beautiful or not, itself the lawgiver—something that could not occur were the realism of nature's purposiveness assumed, since we should then have to learn from nature what we ought to find beautiful, and the judgment of taste would fall under empirical principles. For in such a judging it does not depend on what nature is, or even is for us as an end, but on how we receive it. It would always be an objective purposiveness of nature had it fashioned its forms for the sake of our satisfaction; and not a subjective purposiveness resting upon the free play of the imagination {{ 253 }}, a case in which the favor is one we extend to nature, not one that nature grants us. The property of nature that it affords us an occasion to become aware of the inner purposiveness in the relationship among our mental powers in judging certain of its products—and indeed as one that is to be declared necessary and universally valid on a supersensible ground—cannot be a natural end, or rather be judged by us as such: because otherwise the judgment that would be determined thereby would be based on heteronomy and not, as befits a judgment of taste, be free and have autonomy as its basis.

In fine art the principle of the idealism of purposiveness stands out even more clearly. For that no aesthetic realism of it through sensations can be assumed here (in which case it would be, instead of fine art, merely agreeable art): this it has in common with beautiful nature. But that the satisfaction through aesthetic ideas need not depend on the attainment of determinate ends (as mechanically intentional art), and hence that even in the rationalism of the principle the ideality of ends, not their reality, underlies it: is evident already from the fact that fine art as such must be regarded not as a {{{ 351 }}} product of the understanding and of science, but of genius, and thus receives its rule through ***aesthetic*** ideas, which are essentially distinct from ideas of reason of determinate ends. {{ 254 }}

Just as the ***ideality*** of objects of sense, taken as appearances, is the sole means of accounting for the possibility that their forms admit of _a priori_ determination: so likewise the ***idealism*** of purposiveness, in the judging of the beautiful in nature and in art, is the one presupposition under which alone the critique can account for the possibility of a judgment of taste that demands _a priori_ validity for everyone (without, for all that, basing upon concepts the purposiveness that gets represented in the object).
