## {{ 246 }} § 58. On the Idealism of the Purposiveness of Nature as Well as Art, as the Sole Principle of the Aesthetic Power of Judgment

One can, first of all, place the principle of taste either in this, that the latter always judges according to empirical determining grounds, and thus according to such as are given only _a posteriori_ through the senses, or one can admit that it judges from a ground _a priori_. The former would be the ***empiricism*** of the critique of taste, the second its ***rationalism***. According to the ***first***, the object of our liking would not be distinguished from the ***agreeable***, according to the second, if the judgment rested on definite concepts, it would not be distinguished from the ***good***; and thus all ***beauty*** would be denied out of the world, and only a particular name, perhaps for a certain mixture of both of the aforementioned kinds of liking, would remain in its stead. However, {{{ 347 }}} we have shown that there are also grounds of liking _a priori_ that can therefore coexist with the principle of rationalism, even though they cannot be grasped in ***definite concepts***.

The rationalism of the principle of taste, on the contrary, is either that of the ***realism*** of purposiveness, or that of the ***idealism*** of the same. Now, because a judgment of taste is not a judgment of cognition, and beauty {{ 247 }} is not a property of the object considered for itself, the rationalism of the principle of taste can never be placed in this, that the purposiveness in this judgment is conceived of as objective, i.e., that the judgment refers theoretically, and hence also logically (even if only in a confused estimation), to the perfection of the object, but rather that it refers only ***aesthetically*** to the agreement of its representation in the imagination with the essential principles of the power of judgment in general, in the subject. Consequently, even according to the principle of rationalism, the judgment of taste and the difference between its realism and its idealism can be placed only in this, that either that subjective purposiveness is assumed in the former case to agree with our power of judgment as a real (intentional) ***purpose*** of nature (or of art), or in the second case is assumed to be only a purposive agreement emerging of itself and in an accidental way without purpose, in conformity with the need of the power of judgment with respect to nature and its forms generated according to particular laws.

In favor of the realism of the aesthetic purposiveness of nature—since one might namely assume that at the basis of the production of the beautiful there lay an idea of it in its producing cause, namely a ***purpose*** for the benefit of our imagination—the {{ 248 }} beautiful formations in the realm of organized nature speak very strongly. The flowers, blossoms, indeed the shapes of entire plants, the elegance of animal formations of all kinds which is unnecessary for their own use but selected, as it were, for our taste; above all, the variety and harmonious combination of colors so pleasing and charming to our eyes (on the pheasant, on shellfish, insects, down to the commonest flowers), which, since they concern merely the surface, and on this not even the figure of the creatures, which might still be required for their internal purposes, seem to be aimed entirely at external contemplation: {{{ 348 }}} give great weight to the mode of explanation by the assumption of real purposes of nature for our aesthetic power of judgment.

On the contrary, this assumption is opposed not only by reason through its maxims of everywhere preventing the unnecessary multiplication of principles as far as possible; but nature also shows in its free formations everywhere so much mechanical propensity to the generation of forms that seem, as it were, made for the aesthetic use of our power of judgment, without offering the least ground for the suspicion that there is required for this anything more than its mechanism, merely as nature, according to which they can be purposive for our estimation even without any idea lying at their basis. But I understand {{ 249 }} by a ***free formation*** of nature ***that*** whereby, from a ***fluid in repose***, through the volatilization or separation of a part of it (sometimes merely of the matter of heat), the remainder upon solidifying assumes a definite shape or tissue (figure or texture) which is different according to the specific difference of the materials, but in one and the same material is precisely the same. For this, however, what one always understands by a true fluid is presupposed, namely, that the matter in it is completely dissolved, i.e., is not to be regarded as a mere mixture of solid parts merely suspended in it.

The formation then occurs through ***shooting***, i.e., through a sudden solidification, not through a gradual transition from the fluid to the solid state, but as it were through a leap, which transition is also called ***crystallizing***. The commonest example of this kind of formation is freezing water, in which straight ice-spicules are first generated, which join together at angles of 60 degrees, while others attach themselves in the same way at every point of them, until everything has become ice: so that during this time the water between the ice-spicules does not gradually become more viscous, but is as completely fluid as it would be at a far greater temperature, and yet has the complete coldness of ice. The matter that separates, which suddenly escapes at the moment {{ 250 }} of solidification, is a considerable quantum of caloric, the departure of which, since it was required merely for being fluid, leaves this now solidified ice not in the least colder than the water that was fluid in it shortly before.

Many salts, likewise stones that have a crystalline figure, are generated in the same way from an earthy matter dissolved in water, who knows by what sort of mediation {{{ 349 }}}. In the same way, the drusy configurations of many mineral specimens—of cubic galena, of red silver ore, and the like—are formed, in all probability, also in water and through the shooting of the parts: in that they are forced by some cause to leave this vehicle and to unite with one another in definite external shapes.

But also internally, all materials that were fluid merely through heat and have assumed solidity through cooling show a definite texture in their fracture, and allow us to judge therefrom that if their own weight or contact with the air had not prevented it, they would have exhibited their specifically peculiar shape externally as well: the like of which has been observed in some metals which, after melting, had hardened externally but were still fluid internally, by tapping off the internal, still fluid part and then letting the remaining part left inside shoot in repose. Many of those mineral {{ 251 }} crystallizations, such as spar-druses, hematite, and flos ferri, often present extremely beautiful shapes, such as art could ever devise; and the "grotto" in the cave of Antiparos is merely the product of water trickling through gypsum strata.

The fluid is, to all appearance, in general older than the solid, and both plants and animal bodies are formed from fluid nutritional matter, insofar as this forms itself in repose: to be sure, in the latter, first of all, according to a certain original predisposition directed toward purposes (which, as will be shown in the second part, must be judged not aesthetically, but teleologically, according to the principle of realism); but along with this, perhaps also as shooting and forming itself in freedom, in accordance with the general law of the affinity of materials. Now, just as the aqueous fluids dissolved in an atmosphere that is a mixture of different kinds of air—when the latter separate from it through the departure of heat—generate snow-figures, which, according to the variation of the current air mixture, are often of highly artistic-seeming and extremely beautiful figure: so it can well be thought, without detracting anything from the teleological principle of the assessment of organization, that, as regards the beauty of flowers, of birds' feathers, of shells, in their shape as well as color, this {{ 252 }} can be ascribed to nature and its capacity to form itself also aesthetically-purposively in its freedom, without special purposes directed thereto, according to chemical laws through the deposition of the matter required for {{{ 350 }}} organization.

But what directly proves the principle of the ***ideality*** of the purposiveness in the beautiful of nature—as that which we ourselves always lay at the basis of the aesthetic judgment, and which does not permit us to use any realism of a purpose of nature as the ground of explanation for our power of representation—is that in the estimation of beauty in general we seek its standard _a priori_ in ourselves, and the aesthetic power of judgment is itself legislative with respect to the judgment of whether something is beautiful or not, which cannot take place upon the assumption of the realism of the purposiveness of nature, because we would then have to learn from nature what we ought to find beautiful, and the judgment of taste would be subjected to empirical principles. For in such an estimation, what matters is not what nature is, or even what it is for us as a purpose, but how we receive it. It would always be an objective purposiveness of nature if it had formed its shapes for our liking; and not a subjective purposiveness which rested on the play of the imagination in its freedom {{ 253 }}, where it is favor with which we receive nature, not favor that it shows to us. The property of nature, that it contains for us the opportunity to perceive the inner purposiveness in the relation of our mental powers in the estimation of certain of its products—and indeed as such a purposiveness as, from a supersensible ground, is to be declared necessary and universally valid—cannot be a purpose of nature, or rather cannot be judged by us as such: because otherwise the judgment that would be determined thereby would have heteronomy, but would not, as is fitting for a judgment of taste, be free and have autonomy at its basis.

In beautiful art, the principle of the idealism of purposiveness is even more clearly to be recognized. For that an aesthetic realism of the same through sensations (whereby it would be merely agreeable instead of beautiful art) cannot be assumed here is something it has in common with beautiful nature. But that the liking through aesthetic ideas must not depend on the attainment of definite purposes (as mechanically intentional art)—consequently that, even in the rationalism of the principle, the ideality of the purposes, not their reality, lies at the basis—is also already evident from the fact that beautiful art as such must be regarded not as a {{{ 351 }}} product of the understanding and of science, but of genius, and thus receives its rule through ***aesthetic*** ideas, which are essentially distinguished from ideas of reason of definite purposes. {{ 254 }}

Just as the ***ideality*** of the objects of the senses as appearances is the only way to explain the possibility that their forms can be determined _a priori_: so too the ***idealism*** of the purposiveness in the estimation of the beautiful of nature and of art is the sole assumption under which alone the critique can explain the possibility of a judgment of taste that demands _a priori_ validity for everyone (without, however, grounding on concepts the purposiveness that is represented in the object).
