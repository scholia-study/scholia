## {{ 246 }} § 58. On the idealism of the purposiveness of nature, as well as of art, as the sole principle of the aesthetic power of judgment

One may, first of all, locate the principle of taste either in the claim that it always judges according to empirical determining grounds, and hence according to grounds given only _a posteriori_ through the senses, or one may grant that it judges from a ground _a priori_. The former would amount to the ***empiricism*** of the critique of taste, the latter to its ***rationalism***. On the ***first*** view, the object of our satisfaction would not differ from the ***agreeable***; on the second, were the judgment to rest upon determinate concepts, it would not differ from the ***good***; and so all ***beauty*** would be argued away out of the world, with nothing surviving in its place but a special designation, given perhaps to some blend of the two kinds of satisfaction just named. Yet {{{ 347 }}} we have demonstrated that grounds of satisfaction _a priori_ likewise exist, grounds that may accordingly stand together with the principle of rationalism, notwithstanding that they admit of no capture in ***determinate concepts***.

The rationalism of taste's principle, on the other hand, amounts either to the ***realism*** of purposiveness or to the ***idealism*** thereof. Since, now, a judgment of taste amounts to no cognitive judgment, and beauty {{ 247 }}, taken by itself, is no property of the object, the rationalism of taste's principle can never be located in supposing the purposiveness within this judgment to be conceived as objective—in other words, in supposing that the judgment refers theoretically, and hence logically as well (albeit only through a confused appraisal), to the perfection of the object, rather than referring merely ***aesthetically***, within the subject, to the agreement of its representation in the imagination with those principles that are essential to the power of judgment as such. Consequently, even under the principle of rationalism, taste's judgment—together with the distinction between its realism and its idealism—can be located solely in this: that the subjective purposiveness in question is assumed, in the former case, to harmonize with our power of judgment as a genuine (deliberate) ***end*** of nature (or of art), or, in the latter case, merely as a purposive concordance that emerges spontaneously and fortuitously, devoid of any end, answering to the requirement of the power of judgment as regards nature and the forms it produces under particular laws.

The realism of the aesthetic purposiveness of nature—where, namely, one would like to assume that at the basis of the production of the beautiful there lay an idea of it in the producing cause, that is, an ***end*** in favor of our imagination—is very much advocated by the {{ 248 }} beautiful formations in the realm of organized nature. Flowers, blossoms, indeed the forms of entire plants; the gracefulness of animal formations of every sort of kind—superfluous for their own use, yet as though chosen for our taste; above all the variety, so agreeable and alluring to our eyes, and the harmonious arrangement of colors (on the pheasant, on shellfish, insects, down to the most common blossoms), which, inasmuch as they touch merely the surface—and not even, on that surface, the figure of the creatures, a figure that might still be requisite for their inner ends—seem to be directed wholly toward outward viewing: lend {{{ 348 }}} great weight to that mode of explanation which assumes actual ends of nature on behalf of our aesthetic power of judgment.

Opposed to this assumption, by contrast, stands not reason alone—with its maxims of forestalling everywhere, as far as may be, any needless multiplying of principles—but nature too displays throughout its free formations such an abundance of mechanical inclination toward producing forms that appear, so to speak, fashioned for the aesthetic use of our power of judgment, yet furnish not the least ground for conjecturing that anything beyond its mechanism, taken simply as nature, is needed for this, in keeping with which they can prove purposive for our judging even in the absence of any idea lying at their basis. By a ***free formation*** of nature, though, I mean {{ 249 }} ***that one*** whereby, once a portion of a ***fluid at rest*** has been driven off or separated out (at times merely the heat-matter), what remains takes on, in solidifying, a determinate shape or web (figure or texture)—one that varies with the specific diversity of the materials, yet within one and the same material proves precisely identical. For this, however, what one always understands by a true fluidity is presupposed—namely, that the matter in it is completely dissolved, that is, not to be regarded as a mere mixture of solid parts merely suspended within it.

The formation then comes about through ***shooting together***, that is, through a sudden solidifying—not by a gradual passage out of the fluid into the solid condition, but as it were by a leap, a transition that also goes by the name of ***crystallization***. The commonest example of this kind of formation is water in the act of freezing, wherein straight little ice-spikes are first generated, which conjoin at angles of 60 degrees while still others fasten on likewise at every point of them, until the whole has become ice: such that throughout this interval the water lying among the ice-spikes does not thicken by degrees, but stays as thoroughly fluid as it would be in far greater warmth, and nonetheless carries the utter coldness of ice. The matter that separates out, which slips away all at once in the instant {{ 250 }} of solidifying, is a sizable quantity of heat-stuff, whose departure—since it was required merely for being fluid—leaves this ice, now formed, no colder in the slightest than the water that had been fluid within it just before.

Numerous salts, and equally stones possessing a crystalline figure, arise in just the same manner out of an earthy substance dissolved in water through who knows what agency {{{ 349 }}}. In the same way the druse-like configurations of many minerals—of cubical galena, of ruby silver ore, and the like—are formed, in all likelihood also in water and through the shooting together of the parts: in that they are compelled by some cause to leave this vehicle and to unite with one another into determinate outer shapes.

But internally, too, all materials that were fluid merely through heat and have taken on solidity through cooling display a determinate texture in their fracture, and allow the inference that, had their own weight or contact with the air not hindered it, they would have displayed their specifically peculiar shape outwardly as well: something that has been observed in certain metals which, after melting, had hardened on the outside but were still fluid within, by drawing off the inner, still-fluid part and letting the remainder left behind inside shoot together quietly. Many of those mineral {{ 251 }} crystallizations, such as the spar-druses, hematite, and iron-flowers, often give exceedingly beautiful shapes such as art might ever devise; and the Glory in the grotto of Antiparos is merely the product of water seeping through layers of gypsum.

By all appearances the fluid is on the whole older than the solid, and plants no less than animal bodies are formed out of fluid nutritive matter insofar as it shapes itself at rest: in the latter, to be sure, primarily in accordance with a certain original predisposition oriented toward ends (which, as the second part will show, has to be appraised not aesthetically but teleologically, under the realist principle); yet, alongside this, perhaps also crystallizing in conformity with the universal law governing the affinity of materials, and giving itself form in freedom. Now, just as the aqueous fluids, dissolved within an atmosphere that constitutes a mixture of several kinds of air, generate—once the former part company with the latter through the departure of warmth—snow-figures whose form, varying with the momentary composition of the air, is frequently of an exceedingly beautiful and often quite artificial-looking shape: so it can well be thought, without withdrawing anything from the teleological principle of judging organization: that as regards the beauty of flowers, of bird-feathers, of shells—both in their shape and in their color—this {{ 252 }} could be ascribed to nature and to its capacity to form itself, in its freedom, without particular ends directed at this, according to chemical laws, through the deposition of the matter requisite for {{{ 350 }}} organization, also aesthetically-purposively.

But what directly proves the principle of the ***ideality*** of purposiveness in the beautiful of nature—as that which we always lay at the basis in the aesthetic judgment itself, and which permits us to make use of no realism of an end of nature for our power of representation as a ground of explanation—is this: that in judging beauty as such we look for the standard of it _a priori_ within our own selves, and that the aesthetic power of judgment is, with respect to the judgment whether a thing be beautiful or not, itself the lawgiver—something that could not occur were the realism of nature's purposiveness assumed, since we should then have to learn from nature what we ought to find beautiful, and the judgment of taste would fall under empirical principles. For in such a judging it does not depend on what nature is, or even is for us as an end, but on how we receive it. It would always be an objective purposiveness of nature had it fashioned its forms for the sake of our satisfaction; and not a subjective purposiveness resting upon the free play of the imagination {{ 253 }}, a case in which the favor is one we extend to nature, not one that nature grants us. That characteristic of nature—namely, that it holds for us an opportunity to perceive the inner purposiveness in the relation of our mental powers when we appraise certain of its products, and to perceive it, moreover, as something that is to be pronounced necessary and universally valid on a supersensible ground—cannot be a natural end, or rather be judged by us to be one: for otherwise the judgment thereby determined would rest on heteronomy instead of being free and grounded in autonomy, as suits a judgment of taste.

In fine art the principle of the idealism of purposiveness stands out even more clearly. For the fact that no aesthetic realism of it by way of sensations may be assumed here (in which event it would amount, in place of fine art, to merely agreeable art): this is what it shares with beautiful nature. Yet the point that satisfaction through aesthetic ideas need not hinge upon the achievement of determinate ends (in the manner of mechanically deliberate art), so that within the rationalism of the principle itself it is the ideality of ends, not their reality, that lies at the basis: becomes plain already from this, that fine art as such has to be viewed not as a {{{ 351 }}} product of understanding and of science, but rather as one of genius, thereby acquiring its rule through ***aesthetic*** ideas, which differ essentially from ideas of reason concerned with determinate ends. {{ 254 }}

In just the way that the ***ideality*** of objects of sense, considered as appearances, alone makes it explicable that their forms should admit of being determined _a priori_: so, equally, the ***idealism*** of purposiveness in judging what is beautiful in nature and in art is the single presupposition under which alone the critique can render intelligible the possibility of a judgment of taste demanding _a priori_ validity for everyone (without, however, resting on concepts the purposiveness that is represented in the object).
