## § 44. On fine art

There is neither a science of the beautiful, but only a critique of it, nor is there any fine science, but only fine art. For as regards the first, {{ 177 }} it would have to be established within it scientifically, that is, through grounds of proof, {{{ 305 }}} whether or not something is to be counted as beautiful; hence the judgment about beauty, were it to belong to a science, would be no judgment of taste. As for the second, a science that is supposed to be beautiful as a science is an absurdity. For if, treating it as a science, one asked after grounds and proofs, one would be put off with tasteful sayings (bons mots). — What has given rise to the common expression ***fine sciences*** is doubtless nothing other than the quite correct observation that fine art in its full perfection requires much science — for instance, acquaintance with ancient languages, wide reading in the authors reckoned as classics, history, knowledge of antiquities, and so forth — and that these historical sciences, because they constitute the necessary preparation and foundation for fine art, and partly too because the knowledge of the products of fine art (oratory and poetry) has itself been included under them, have by a confusion of words themselves come to be called fine sciences.

When art, in conformity with the ***cognition*** of a possible object, merely carries out the actions required to make it actual, it is ***mechanical***; but if it has the feeling of pleasure as its immediate aim, it is called ***aesthetic*** art. The latter is either {{ 178 }} ***agreeable*** or ***fine*** art. It is the former when its end is that pleasure should accompany the representations as mere ***sensations***, the latter when it should accompany them as ***modes of cognition***.

Agreeable arts are those directed merely toward enjoyment; of this kind are all those charms that can delight a company at table: narrating in an entertaining way, drawing the company into frank and lively conversation, tuning it by jest and laughter to a certain tone of merriment, where, as the saying goes, much may be chattered on into the revelry with no one wanting to be held responsible for what he says, because it is aimed only at momentary entertainment and not at any lasting matter for later reflection or repetition. (To this also belongs the manner in which the table is furnished for enjoyment, or indeed, at great banquets, the table music: a curious thing, which is meant only as an agreeable noise to sustain the mood of the minds toward cheerfulness and which, without anyone devoting the slightest attention to its composition, fosters the free conversation of one neighbor with the other {{{ 306 }}}.) To this belong, further, all games that carry with them no further interest than to make the time pass unnoticed.

Fine art, by contrast, is a mode of representation that is in itself {{ 179 }} purposive and that, although without an end, nevertheless furthers the cultivation of the mental powers for sociable communication.

The universal communicability of a pleasure already carries with it in its very concept that this pleasure must be one not of enjoyment arising from mere sensation, but of reflection; and thus aesthetic art, as fine art, is of a kind that takes the reflecting power of judgment, and not sensory sensation, as its standard.
