## § 44. On fine art

There is neither a science of the beautiful, but only a critique of it, nor is there any fine science, but only fine art. For as regards the first, {{ 177 }} it would have to be established within it scientifically, that is, through grounds of proof, {{{ 305 }}} whether or not something is to be counted as beautiful; hence the judgment about beauty, were it to belong to a science, would be no judgment of taste. As for the second, a science that is supposed to be beautiful as a science is an absurdity. For if, treating it as a science, one asked after grounds and proofs, one would be put off with tasteful sayings (bons mots). — The ordinary phrase ***fine sciences*** has surely arisen from nothing but the perfectly correct remark that fine art, brought to its full perfection, calls for a great deal of science — such as familiarity with the ancient tongues, wide reading in the authors held to be classic, history, an acquaintance with antiquity, and so forth — so that these historical branches of learning, since they furnish fine art with its indispensable groundwork and preparation, and partly, too, since they themselves take in the knowledge of the products of fine art (eloquence and poetry), have through a slippage of words come to be styled fine sciences in their own right.

When art, suited to the ***cognition*** of a possible object, does no more than perform the operations needed to bring that object into being, it is ***mechanical***; should it, however, take the feeling of pleasure directly for its purpose, it is termed ***aesthetic*** art. The latter is either {{ 178 }} ***agreeable*** or ***fine*** art. It is the former when its end is that pleasure should accompany the representations as mere ***sensations***, the latter when it should accompany them as ***modes of cognition***.

Agreeable arts are ones whose sole aim is enjoyment; to them belong all the charms that can amuse a company gathered at table: telling stories in a diverting manner, prompting the company to open and animated talk, and, through joking and laughter, setting it in a certain merry key, where, as people say, all sorts of things may be prattled into the carousing while nobody means to answer for what he utters, since the whole is calculated only on the entertainment of the moment and not on any abiding material for later thought or retelling. (To this also belongs the manner in which the table is furnished for enjoyment, or indeed, at great banquets, the table music: a curious thing, which is meant only as an agreeable noise to sustain the mood of the minds toward cheerfulness and which, without anyone devoting the slightest attention to its composition, fosters the free conversation of one neighbor with the other {{{ 306 }}}.) To this belong, further, all games that carry with them no further interest than to make the time pass unnoticed.

Fine art, by contrast, is a mode of representation that is in itself {{ 179 }} purposive and that, although without an end, nevertheless furthers the cultivation of the mental powers for sociable communication.

The universal communicability of a pleasure already carries with it in its very concept that this pleasure must be one not of enjoyment arising from mere sensation, but of reflection; and thus aesthetic art, as fine art, is of a kind that takes the reflecting power of judgment, and not sensory sensation, as its standard.
