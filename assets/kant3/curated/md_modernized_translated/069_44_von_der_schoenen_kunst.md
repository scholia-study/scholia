## § 44. On Beautiful Art

There is neither a science of the beautiful, but only a critique, nor a beautiful science, but only beautiful art. For as concerns the former {{ 177 }}, it would have to be determined in it scientifically, i.e., by grounds of proof, {{{ 305 }}} whether or not something is to be held as beautiful; the judgment on beauty, therefore, if it belonged to science, would not be a judgment of taste. As regards the second, a science that is supposed to be beautiful as such is an absurdity. For if, in it as science, one were to ask for grounds and proofs, one would be put off with tasteful utterances (witticisms). — What has given rise to the common expression ***beautiful sciences*** is without doubt nothing else than that it has been quite correctly observed that for beautiful art in its entire perfection much science is required, such as, e.g., knowledge of ancient languages, reading of authors who are considered classics, history, knowledge of antiquities, etc., and therefore these historical sciences, because they constitute the necessary preparation and foundation for beautiful art, and partly also because the knowledge of the products of beautiful art (eloquence and poetry) has itself been included under them, have themselves, through a confusion of words, been called beautiful sciences.

If art, adequate to the ***cognition*** of a possible object, merely performs the actions required to make it actual, it is ***mechanical***; but if it has the feeling of pleasure as its immediate aim, it is called ***aesthetic*** art. This is either {{ 178 }} ***agreeable*** or ***beautiful*** art. It is the first when its end is that pleasure should accompany the representations as mere ***sensations***; the second, when it is to accompany them as ***modes of cognition***.

Agreeable arts are those whose only aim is enjoyment; of this kind are all those charms that can delight society at a table: such as telling stories entertainingly, putting the company into a free and lively talkativeness, and by jest and laughter tuning them to a certain tone of gaiety, where, as the saying goes, much can be babbled at random, and no one wants to be held responsible for what he says, because it is calculated only for momentary entertainment, not for any lasting matter for reflection or repetition. (To this belongs also the way the table is equipped for enjoyment, or even, at large banquets, table music: a strange thing, which is intended only as an agreeable noise to maintain the mood of the minds in cheerfulness, and which, without anyone paying the least attention to its composition, favors the free conversation of one neighbor with another {{{ 306 }}}.) To this belong furthermore all games that bring with them no other interest than to make time pass imperceptibly.

Beautiful art, on the contrary, is a mode of representation which is purposive for itself {{ 179 }} and, although without an end, nevertheless promotes the culture of the mental powers for social communication.

The universal communicability of a pleasure already carries with it in its concept that this must not be a pleasure of enjoyment from mere sensation, but one of reflection; and thus aesthetic art, as beautiful art, is one that has the reflecting power of judgment, and not sensation of the senses, as its standard.
