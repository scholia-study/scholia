## {{{ 174 }}} II. On the domain of philosophy in general

As far as concepts _a priori_ have their application, just so far does the use of our faculty of cognition according to principles extend, and with it philosophy.

The whole aggregate of objects, however, upon which those concepts are brought to bear, so as if possible to yield a cognition of them, can be divided in keeping with the differing sufficiency or insufficiency of our faculties for this purpose.

Concepts, to the extent that they are related to objects—regardless of whether any cognition of these is attainable or not—possess their field, one fixed solely through the relation in which their object stands to our faculty of cognition generally. — That portion of this field in which cognition is possible for us constitutes a ground (_territorium_) for these concepts and for the faculty of cognition they require. That stretch of the ground over which these exercise legislation is the domain (_ditio_) belonging to these concepts and to the faculties of cognition that are theirs by right. Empirical concepts thus have their ground, to be sure, in nature as the totality {{ XVII }} of all objects of the senses, but no domain (only their place of residence, _domicilium_): for although they are produced in a lawful manner, they are not legislative, but rather the rules grounded upon them are empirical and therefore contingent.

Our whole faculty of cognition possesses two domains—the one belonging to the concepts of nature, the other to the concept of freedom—for through both alike it legislates _a priori_. Accordingly, philosophy too now divides into the theoretical and the practical. But the ground on which its domain is erected and its legislation ***exercised*** is always still only the totality of the objects of all possible experience, insofar as they count as nothing more than mere appearances; for without this no legislation of the understanding with respect to them could be conceived.

Legislation by means of concepts of nature is effected by the understanding and is theoretical. Legislation by means of the concept of freedom is effected by reason and is merely practical. In the practical sphere alone can reason be legislative; as regards theoretical cognition (of nature) it is able merely (being acquainted with the law by way of the understanding) {{{ 175 }}} to derive inferences from given laws through syllogisms, inferences that in the end always stop short at nature. In the reverse case, however, where rules are of a practical kind, reason does not for that reason become at once ***legislative***, seeing that they {{ XVIII }} can likewise be technically-practical.

Understanding and reason accordingly exercise two different legislations on a single and self-same ground of experience, yet without either being allowed to do the other any prejudice. For just as the concept of nature exerts no influence on the legislation carried out through the concept of freedom, so too does the latter in no way trouble nature's legislation. — That one can at the very least think, without falling into contradiction, the joint subsistence of both legislations, along with the faculties that pertain to them, within a single subject—this was established by the Critique of Pure Reason, which demolished the objections brought against it by exposing the dialectical semblance harbored in them.

But the reason why these two different domains—which, while not in their legislation, do nevertheless in their effects within the sensible world continually restrict one another—do not amount to ***One*** lies in this: that the concept of nature, though it presents its objects in intuition, offers them not as things in themselves but as mere appearances, while the concept of freedom, for its part, presents within its object something that is a thing in itself, though never within intuition, and thus neither of them is able to supply any theoretical cognition of its object (and indeed of the thinking subject itself) as things in themselves—which would be the supersensible, an idea that one must, to be sure, lay beneath the possibility of all those objects of experience {{ XIX }}, though one can never elevate or broaden it into a cognition.

Hence there exists, for our entire faculty of cognition, an unbounded yet likewise inaccessible field—that of the supersensible—wherein we come upon no ground of our own, so that upon it we can possess no domain for theoretical cognition, be it for the understanding's concepts or for reason's; a field that we are indeed obliged to fill with ideas on behalf of reason's theoretical as well as practical use, though to these ideas, with respect to the laws arising from the concept of freedom, we can impart no reality save a practical one, so that our theoretical cognition is thereby not enlarged in the slightest toward the supersensible.

Now although a chasm beyond all survey stands fixed, sundering the concept of nature's domain, as the sensible, from that of the concept of freedom, {{{ 176 }}} as the supersensible, such that no crossing from the one to the other (that is, by way of reason's theoretical employment) is possible—as though they amounted to a plurality of distinct worlds, of which the first can exert no influence upon the second: the latter is nonetheless ***meant to*** bear an influence upon the former, that is, the concept of freedom is to render actual within the sensible world the end laid down by its laws; and nature must therefore admit of being conceived in such a manner that the conformity to law {{ XX }} of its form accords, at the very least, with the possibility of those ends which are to be brought about in it according to laws of freedom. — There must, then, after all exist some ground for the ***unity*** of the supersensible that lies at the basis of nature with what the concept of freedom practically comprises within it—a ground the concept of which, although it attains a cognition of that ground neither theoretically nor practically, and consequently possesses no proper domain of its own, still renders possible the passage from a way of thinking that proceeds according to the principles of the one to a way of thinking according to the principles of the other.
