## {{{ 174 }}} II. On the Domain of Philosophy in General

As far as concepts _a priori_ have their application, so far does the use of our cognitive faculty according to principles extend, and with it philosophy.

But the sum total of all objects to which those concepts are referred, in order to bring about, if possible, a cognition of them, can be divided according to the different adequacy or inadequacy of our faculties for this purpose.

Concepts, in so far as they are referred to objects, regardless of whether a cognition of them is possible or not, have their field, which is determined solely according to the relation that their object has to our cognitive faculty in general. — The part of this field in which cognition is possible for us is a territory (_territorium_) for these concepts and the cognitive faculty required for it. The part of the territory over which these are legislative is the domain (_ditio_) of these concepts and of the cognitive faculties belonging to them. Empirical concepts thus indeed have their territory in nature, as the sum total {{ XVII }} of all objects of the senses, but no domain (rather only their residence, _domicilium_): because although they are produced according to law, they are not legislative, but the rules founded on them are empirical, and therefore contingent.

Our entire cognitive faculty has two domains, that of the concepts of nature and that of the concept of freedom; for through both it is legislative _a priori_. Philosophy also divides itself accordingly into theoretical and practical. But the territory upon which its domain is established and its legislation is ***exercised*** is always still only the sum total of the objects of all possible experience, in so far as they are taken for nothing more than mere appearances; for without this, no legislation of the understanding in regard to them could be thought.

Legislation through concepts of nature takes place through the understanding and is theoretical. Legislation through the concept of freedom takes place from reason and is merely practical. Only in the practical alone can reason be legislative; in regard to theoretical cognition (of nature) it can only (as versed in law by means of the understanding) {{{ 175 }}} draw consequences from given laws through inferences, which nevertheless always remain confined to nature. Conversely, however, where rules are practical, reason is not therefore immediately ***legislative***, because they {{ XVIII }} can also be technical-practical.

Understanding and reason thus have two distinct legislations on one and the same territory of experience, without either being allowed to interfere with the other. For just as little as the concept of nature has an influence on the legislation through the concept of freedom, just as little does the latter disrupt the legislation of nature. — The possibility of thinking at least without contradiction the coexistence of both legislations and of the faculties belonging thereto in one and the same subject was proved by the Critique of Pure Reason, in that it annihilated the objections against it by exposing the dialectical illusion in them.

But that these two different domains, which to be sure do not continuously restrict each other in their legislation but yet do so in their effects in the world of sense, do not constitute ***one*** domain, comes from this: that while the concept of nature indeed represents its objects in intuition, but not as things in themselves, rather as mere appearances, the concept of freedom on the contrary represents in its object a thing in itself, but not in intuition, and consequently neither of the two can procure a theoretical cognition of its object (and even of the thinking subject) as a thing in itself, which would be the supersensible, of which one must indeed underlay the idea for the possibility of all those objects of experience {{ XIX }} but can never raise and extend it to a cognition.

There is therefore an unlimited, but also inaccessible field for our entire cognitive faculty, namely the field of the supersensible, wherein we find no territory for ourselves, and thus can have on it no domain for theoretical cognition, whether for concepts of understanding or of reason; a field which we must indeed occupy with ideas for the behoof of both the theoretical and practical use of reason, but to which we can procure no other than practical reality in relation to the laws from the concept of freedom, whereby consequently our theoretical cognition is not in the least extended to the supersensible.

Although an incalculable gulf is fixed between the domain of the concept of nature, as the sensible, and the domain of the concept of freedom, {{{ 176 }}} as the supersensible, so that no transition is possible from the former to the latter (thus by means of the theoretical use of reason), just as if they were so many different worlds, the first of which can have no influence on the second: yet the latter ***ought*** to have an influence on the former, namely, the concept of freedom is to make the purpose given by its laws real in the world of sense; and nature must consequently also be able to be thought in such a way that the lawfulness {{ XX }} of its form at least harmonizes with the possibility of the purposes to be effected in it according to laws of freedom. — Thus there must still be a ground of the ***unity*** of the supersensible, which lies at the basis of nature, with that which the concept of freedom contains practically, of which the concept, even if it does not attain to a cognition of it either theoretically or practically, and therefore has no peculiar domain, nevertheless makes possible the transition from the way of thinking according to the principles of the one to that according to the principles of the other.
