## {{{ 174 }}} II. On the domain of philosophy in general

As far as concepts _a priori_ have their application, just so far does the use of our faculty of cognition according to principles extend, and with it philosophy.

The totality of all objects, however, to which those concepts are referred so as, where possible, to bring about a cognition of them, can be divided according to the differing adequacy or inadequacy of our faculties to this aim.

Concepts, insofar as they are referred to objects irrespective of whether a cognition of them is possible or not, have their field, which is determined merely by the relation that their object bears to our faculty of cognition as such. — The part of this field within which cognition is possible for us is a ground (_territorium_) for these concepts and the faculty of cognition required for it. The part of the ground upon which these are legislative is the domain (_ditio_) of these concepts and of the faculties of cognition proper to them. Empirical concepts thus have their ground, to be sure, in nature as the totality {{ XVII }} of all objects of the senses, but no domain (only their place of residence, _domicilium_): for although they are produced in a lawful manner, they are not legislative, but rather the rules grounded upon them are empirical and therefore contingent.

Our entire faculty of cognition has two domains, that of the concepts of nature and that of the concept of freedom, since by means of both it is legislative _a priori_. Accordingly, philosophy too now divides into the theoretical and the practical. But the ground on which its domain is erected and its legislation ***exercised*** is always still only the totality of the objects of all possible experience, insofar as they count as nothing more than mere appearances; for without this no legislation of the understanding with respect to them could be conceived.

Legislation by means of concepts of nature is effected by the understanding and is theoretical. Legislation by means of the concept of freedom is effected by reason and is merely practical. It is in the practical alone that reason can be legislative; with respect to theoretical cognition (of nature) it can only (being versed in the law by means of the understanding) {{{ 175 }}} draw conclusions from given laws by way of syllogisms, conclusions that nonetheless always remain confined to nature. Conversely, however, where rules are practical, reason is not on that account immediately ***legislative***, since they {{ XVIII }} may also be technically-practical.

Understanding and reason thus have two distinct legislations upon one and the same ground of experience, without either being permitted to interfere with the other. For as little as the concept of nature has any influence on the legislation through the concept of freedom, just as little does the latter disturb the legislation of nature. — The possibility of at least conceiving without contradiction the coexistence of both legislations, together with the faculties belonging to them, in one and the same subject was proved by the Critique of Pure Reason, in that it annihilated the objections raised against this by uncovering the dialectical illusion contained in them.

But that these two distinct domains, which, though not in their legislation, yet in their effects in the sensible world do ceaselessly limit one another, do not constitute ***One***, arises from this: that the concept of nature can indeed represent its objects in intuition, yet not as things in themselves, but rather as mere appearances, whereas the concept of freedom can represent in its object a thing in itself, yet not in intuition, so that neither of the two can furnish a theoretical cognition of its object (and even of the thinking subject) as things in themselves, which would be the supersensible—an idea that one must indeed lay at the basis of the possibility of all those objects of experience {{ XIX }}, though one can never raise it to, or expand it into, a cognition.

There is thus a boundless, but also inaccessible field for our entire faculty of cognition, namely the field of the supersensible, in which we find no ground for ourselves and hence can have upon it no domain for theoretical cognition, whether for the concepts of the understanding or those of reason; a field that we must indeed occupy with ideas for the sake of both the theoretical and the practical use of reason, but to which, in relation to the laws that stem from the concept of freedom, we can furnish no reality other than a practical one, whereby accordingly our theoretical cognition is not in the least expanded toward the supersensible.

Now although an unsurveyable gulf is fixed between the domain of the concept of nature, as the sensible, and the domain of the concept of freedom, {{{ 176 }}} as the supersensible, so that no passage from the former to the latter (hence by means of the theoretical use of reason) is possible, just as if they were so many different worlds, the first of which can have no influence on the second: still the latter ***is to*** have an influence on the former, namely the concept of freedom is to make actual in the sensible world the end enjoined by its laws; and nature must consequently also be capable of being thought in such a way that the lawfulness {{ XX }} of its form at least harmonizes with the possibility of the ends that are to be realized within it in conformity with laws of freedom. — There must accordingly, then, be a ground for the ***unity*** of the supersensible that underlies nature with that which the concept of freedom contains practically, the concept of which, even though it attains to a cognition of it neither theoretically nor practically, and hence has no domain of its own, nevertheless makes possible the transition from a manner of thinking governed by the principles of the one to a manner of thinking governed by those of the other.
