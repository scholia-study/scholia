## § 55.

A power of judgment that is to be dialectical must first of all be ratiocinating; i.e., its judgments must lay claim to universality, and indeed a priori[^*]: for the dialectic consists in the opposition of such judgments. Hence the incompatibility of aesthetic judgments of sense (about the agreeable and the disagreeable) is not dialectical. Likewise the conflict of judgments of taste, insofar as each appeals merely to his own taste, does not constitute a dialectic of taste: because {{ 232 }} no one intends to make his judgment into a universal rule. There thus remains no concept of a dialectic that could concern taste except that of a dialectic of the ***critique*** of taste (not of taste itself) with regard to its ***principles***: namely, in that concepts that conflict with one another arise naturally and unavoidably concerning the ground of the possibility of judgments of taste in general. A transcendental critique of taste will therefore contain a part that can bear the name of a dialectic of the aesthetic power of judgment only insofar as an antinomy of the principles of this faculty is found, which makes its lawfulness, hence also its inner possibility, doubtful.

[^*]: A ratiocinating judgment (_iudicium ratiocinans_) can be any one that announces itself as universal; for to that extent it can serve as the major premise in an inference of reason. A judgment of reason (_iudicium ratiocinatum_), by contrast, can be called only such a one as is thought as the conclusion of an inference of reason, consequently as grounded _a priori_.
