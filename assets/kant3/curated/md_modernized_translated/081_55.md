## § 55.

A power of judgment that is to be dialectical must first of all be ratiocinating; that is, its judgments must lay claim to universality, and indeed a priori[^*]: for the dialectic consists in the opposition of such judgments. Hence the incompatibility of aesthetic judgments of sense (concerning the agreeable and the disagreeable) is not dialectical. The conflict of judgments of taste, too, insofar as everyone appeals merely to his own taste, constitutes no dialectic of taste: because {{ 232 }} no one intends to turn his own judgment into a universal rule. There thus remains no concept of any dialectic that could bear on taste except one belonging to a dialectic of the ***critique*** of taste (and not to taste itself) in regard to its ***principles***: for with respect to the ground on which judgments of taste are possible at all, mutually opposed concepts arise in a natural and unavoidable manner. A transcendental critique of taste, then, will include a part that can bear the name of a dialectic of the aesthetic power of judgment only if an antinomy is met with among the principles of this faculty—an antinomy that casts doubt on its lawfulness and consequently also on its inner possibility.

[^*]: A ratiocinating judgment (_iudicium ratiocinans_) may be the name for any judgment that gives itself out as universal; for to that extent it can serve as the major premise in a syllogism of reason. A judgment of reason (_iudicium ratiocinatum_), by contrast, can be called only such a one as is thought of as the conclusion of a syllogism of reason, and consequently as grounded _a priori_.
