## VI. On the connection of the feeling of pleasure with the concept of nature's purposiveness

The agreement of nature we have in mind, in the manifold of its particular laws, with our need to discover a generality of principles for them, must, so far as any of our insight reaches, be judged contingent, and yet at the same time indispensable for the requirement of our understanding, and hence a purposiveness through which nature accords with our aim, though an aim bent solely on cognition. — Those universal laws of the understanding that are simultaneously laws of nature belong to it every bit as necessarily (even though they arise from spontaneity) as do the laws governing the motion of matter; and their generation presupposes no design upon our cognitive faculties, since it is only by their means that we first {{ XXXIX }} {{{ 187 }}} arrive at any concept of what it is to have cognition of things (of nature), and since they attach to nature necessarily as the object of our cognition in general. That nature's order under its particular laws should nonetheless be truly commensurate to that capacity — in spite of a manifoldness and heterogeneity that at least possibly outstrips our power to take it in — is, as far as we can discern, contingent; and the tracing of that order is a task of the understanding, undertaken with a view to a necessary end of its own, namely to instill unity of principles into nature: an end that the power of judgment must then impute to nature, since the understanding can lay down no law to it in this regard.

The attainment of any aim is tied to the feeling of pleasure; and where what conditions the former is an _a priori_ representation — as with the present principle for the reflecting power of judgment in general — the feeling of pleasure, too, stands determined on an _a priori_ ground and as valid for everyone: and indeed purely through the object's relation to the faculty of cognition, without the concept of purposiveness here paying the least regard to the faculty of desire, so that it thereby marks itself off wholly from every practical purposiveness of nature.

Indeed, since in the coincidence of perceptions with the laws that flow from universal concepts of nature (the categories) we find not the least effect on the feeling of pleasure in us, {{ XL }} and cannot find any, because in this the understanding operates unintentionally and by the necessity of its own nature: so, on the other side, the discovered concurrence of two or more heterogeneous empirical laws of nature under one principle that comprehends them both is the source of a quite palpable pleasure, and often even of wonder, indeed of a wonder that does not cease though one already knows its object well enough. To be sure, in the graspability of nature, as well as in the unity of its partition into genera and species — through which alone empirical concepts become possible, and by which we cognize nature under its particular laws — we sense no marked pleasure any longer: yet it was assuredly present in its day, and only because the most ordinary experience would be impossible without it has it little by little fused with bare cognition and dropped out of special notice. — Something is accordingly called for that, in the judging of nature, alerts us to its purposiveness for our understanding — an effort to bring its dissimilar laws, wherever possible, under higher though still empirical ones — {{{ 188 }}} so as, when it succeeds, to take pleasure in this attunement of them to our faculty of cognition, an attunement we look upon as merely contingent. A representation of nature, on the contrary, would be thoroughly repugnant to us were it to announce in advance that, on the slightest probing {{ XLI }} past the most ordinary experience, we would run into a heterogeneity of its laws such as to render the unification of its particular laws under universal empirical ones impossible for our understanding: for this runs counter to the principle governing the subjectively purposive specification of nature into its genera, and counter to our reflecting power of judgment with respect to the aim of the latter.

This assumption on the part of the power of judgment nonetheless remains so undetermined regarding how far that ideal purposiveness of nature is to be stretched for our faculty of cognition, that, should we be told that a deeper or wider acquaintance with nature through observation must in the end run up against a multiplicity of laws which no human understanding can reduce to a single principle, we rest content with that too, though we would sooner hear it when others hold out to us the hope: that the more inwardly we came to know nature, or the more we could set it beside outer members for now unknown to us, the more simply, in its principles, and the more in unison, beneath the seeming heterogeneity of its empirical laws, we should find it, the farther our experience were to advance. For our power of judgment bids us operate on the principle of nature's adequacy to our faculty of cognition, as far as that extends, without settling — since it is no determining power of judgment that furnishes us this rule — {{ XLII }} whether somewhere it meets its limits or not: because, though with respect to the rational employment of our cognitive faculties we can indeed set limits, in the empirical field no fixing of limits is possible.
