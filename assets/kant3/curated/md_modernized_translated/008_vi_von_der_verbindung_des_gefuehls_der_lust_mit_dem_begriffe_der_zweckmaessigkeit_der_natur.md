## VI. On the Combination of the Feeling of Pleasure with the Concept of the Purposiveness of Nature

The aforementioned agreement of nature in the manifoldness of its particular laws with our need to find universality of principles for it must, according to all our insight, be appraised as contingent, yet nevertheless as indispensable for our need of the understanding, hence as purposiveness through which nature agrees with our aim, but one directed only toward cognition. — The universal laws of the understanding, which are at the same time laws of nature, are just as necessary for it (although arisen from spontaneity) as the laws of motion of matter; and their generation presupposes no aim with our faculties of cognition, because only through them do we first {{ XXXIX }} {{{ 187 }}} obtain a concept of what cognition of things (of nature) is, and they belong necessarily to nature as object of our cognition in general. But that the order of nature according to its particular laws, in all its at least possible manifoldness and heterogeneity surpassing our faculty of comprehension, should yet really be commensurate with this faculty, is, as far as we can see, contingent; and the finding of it is a business of the understanding which is conducted with an aim to a necessary end of the understanding, namely to bring unity of principles into it: which end the power of judgment must then attribute to nature, because the understanding can prescribe no law to it about this.

The attainment of every aim is bound up with the feeling of pleasure; and if the condition of the former is a representation _a priori_, as here a principle for the reflecting power of judgment in general, then the feeling of pleasure is also determined through a ground _a priori_ and valid for everyone: and indeed merely through the relation of the object to the faculty of cognition, without the concept of purposiveness here taking the least regard of the faculty of desire and thus distinguishing itself entirely from all practical purposiveness of nature.

In fact, since from the coincidence of perceptions with the laws according to universal concepts of nature (the categories) we encounter not the least effect on the feeling of pleasure in us, {{ XL }} nor can we encounter any, because the understanding therein proceeds unintentionally according to its nature by necessity: so on the other hand the discovered compatibility of two or more empirical heterogeneous laws of nature under a principle comprehending them both is the ground of a very noticeable pleasure, often even of an admiration, even of one that does not cease although one is already sufficiently acquainted with its object. To be sure, we no longer feel any noticeable pleasure at the comprehensibility of nature and at its unity of division into genera and species, through which alone empirical concepts are possible by means of which we cognize it according to its particular laws: but it certainly was there in its time, and only because the commonest experience would not be possible without it has it gradually become mixed with mere cognition and is no longer particularly noticed. — There thus belongs something which, in the appraisal of nature, makes us attentive to the purposiveness of it for our understanding, a study of bringing its heterogeneous laws where possible under higher, though still always empirical, ones, to this end, {{{ 188 }}} that, when it succeeds, we may feel pleasure at this concurrence of them for our faculty of cognition, which we regard as merely contingent. By contrast, a representation of nature would thoroughly displease us through which one told us in advance that at the least inquiry {{ XLI }} beyond the commonest experience we would encounter a heterogeneity of its laws which would make the unification of its particular laws under universal empirical ones impossible for our understanding: because this conflicts with the principle of the subjectively-purposive specification of nature in its genera and with our reflecting power of judgment in regard to the latter.

This presupposition of the power of judgment is nevertheless so undetermined about how far that ideal purposiveness of nature for our faculty of cognition is to be extended, that if one tells us that a deeper or more extended acquaintance with nature through observation must in the end encounter a manifoldness of laws that no human understanding can reduce to a principle, we are content with it too, even though we would rather hear, when others give us hope: that the more we come to know nature in its inner constitution, or could compare it with external members for now unknown to us, the simpler we would find it in its principles and the more concordant amid the apparent heterogeneity of its empirical laws, the further our experience advanced. For it is a behest of our power of judgment to proceed according to the principle of the suitability of nature to our faculty of cognition, as far as it reaches, without (because it is not a determining power of judgment that gives us this rule) making out {{ XLII }} whether it has its bounds anywhere or not: because we can indeed determine bounds with regard to the rational use of our faculties of cognition, but in the empirical field no determination of bounds is possible.
