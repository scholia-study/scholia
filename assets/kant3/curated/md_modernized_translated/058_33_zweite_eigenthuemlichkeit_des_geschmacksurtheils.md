## {{{ 284 }}} {{ 140 }} § 33. Second peculiarity of the judgment of taste

The judgment of taste is not determinable at all by grounds of proof, exactly as though it were merely ***subjective***.

If someone does not find a building, a view, a poem beautiful, then, ***in the first place***, he will not let approval be inwardly forced upon him by a hundred voices that all praise it highly. He may indeed pretend that it pleases him too, so as not to be taken for someone lacking taste; he may even fall to doubting whether his taste has been adequately formed through familiarity with a large enough range of objects of some given sort (much as a person who thinks he can make out something far off as a forest, while everyone else takes it for a town, comes to distrust the judgment of his own eyes). Yet this he does see clearly: that the approval of others yields no proof that would be valid for judging beauty; that others may at most see and observe on his behalf, and that what many have perceived in one and the same way may serve, as a sufficient ground of proof for him who believes he has perceived it otherwise, toward a theoretical and hence logical [judgment], but that what has pleased others can never serve as the ground of an aesthetic judgment. The judgment of others that is unfavorable to us may indeed rightly make us hesitant with regard to {{ 141 }} our own, yet it can never convince us of the latter's incorrectness. Hence there is no empirical ***ground of proof*** by which to compel a judgment of taste from anyone.

***Secondly***, still less can a proof _a priori_ according to determinate rules determine the judgment about beauty. If someone reads me his poem, or takes me to a play that in the end refuses to suit my taste, then he may cite ***Batteux*** or ***Lessing***, or critics of taste still older and more famous, and all the rules they have laid down, as proof that his poem is beautiful; certain passages that happen to displease me may even accord perfectly well with rules of beauty (as they are given there and universally acknowledged): I plug up my ears, will hear no reasons and no ratiocination, and would sooner grant that the critics' rules are mistaken, or at any rate inapplicable in the present instance, than let my judgment be determined by grounds of proof _a priori_ {{{ 285 }}}, since it is meant to be a judgment of taste and not of understanding or of reason.

This appears to be one of the principal reasons why the name of taste, of all names, has been fastened upon this aesthetic capacity for judging. For a person may list off for me every ingredient {{ 142 }} in a dish and observe of each that each of them is otherwise agreeable to me, and may over and above this rightly praise the wholesomeness of this fare; yet I stay deaf to all such grounds, I put the dish to the test of ***my*** own tongue and palate: and it is on that basis (not on universal principles) that I pass my judgment.

In point of fact, the judgment of taste is throughout always passed as a singular judgment about the object. The understanding can, by comparing the object with the judgment of others in the point of what pleases, form a universal judgment: e.g., all tulips are beautiful; but that is then no judgment of taste, but a logical one, which turns the relation of an object to taste into a predicate of things of a certain kind in general; whereas that by which I deem a single given tulip beautiful, i.e., take my satisfaction in it to hold with universal validity, is the judgment of taste and nothing else. Its peculiarity, however, consists in this: that, even though it has merely subjective validity, it nonetheless lays claim to ***all*** subjects just as thoroughly as could ever be the case if it were an objective judgment resting on grounds of cognition and capable of being enforced through a proof.
