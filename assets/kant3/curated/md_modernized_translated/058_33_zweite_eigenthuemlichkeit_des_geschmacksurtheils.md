## {{{ 284 }}} {{ 140 }} § 33. Second Peculiarity of the Judgment of Taste

The judgment of taste is not at all determinable by grounds of proof, just as if it were merely ***subjective***.

If someone does not find a building, a view, a poem beautiful, then, ***firstly***, he does not allow approval to be internally imposed upon him by a hundred voices that all praise it highly. He may indeed pretend as if it also pleases him, so as not to be regarded as lacking in taste; he can even begin to doubt whether he has sufficiently cultivated his taste through acquaintance with a sufficient quantity of objects of a certain kind (just as someone who, in the distance, believes he recognizes something as a forest which all others regard as a town, doubts the judgment of his own sight). But this he nevertheless clearly sees: that the approval of others yields no proof valid at all for the estimation of beauty; that others may at most see and observe for him, and that what many have seen in the same way can serve as a sufficient ground of proof for him, who believes he has seen it otherwise, for a theoretical, hence logical judgment, but never can that which has pleased others serve as the ground of an aesthetic judgment. The judgment of others which is unfavorable to us can indeed rightly make us doubtful with regard to {{ 141 }} our own, but can never convince us of its incorrectness. Thus there is no empirical ***ground of proof*** to force the judgment of taste upon anyone.

***Secondly***, even less can a proof _a priori_ according to definite rules determine the judgment about beauty. If someone reads his poem to me, or takes me to a play that in the end does not please my taste, then he may cite ***Batteux*** or ***Lessing***, or even older and more famous critics of taste, and all the rules established by them as proof that his poem is beautiful; also, certain passages that happen to displease me may well agree with the rules of beauty (just as they are given there and generally recognized): I stop my ears, want to hear no reasons and no rationalizing, and will rather assume that those rules of the critics are false, or at least that this is not the case of their application, than that I should let my judgment be determined by grounds of proof _a priori_ {{{ 285 }}}, since it is supposed to be a judgment of taste and not of the understanding or of reason.

It seems that this is one of the chief causes why this aesthetic power of judgment has been designated precisely with the name of taste. For someone may enumerate to me all the ingredients {{ 142 }} of a dish and note of each that it is otherwise pleasing to me, and moreover rightly praise the wholesomeness of this food; yet I am deaf to all these reasons, I try the dish on ***my*** tongue and my palate: and according to that (not according to general principles) I pass my judgment.

In fact, the judgment of taste is absolutely always passed as a singular judgment about the object. The understanding can, through the comparison of the object with respect to what is pleasing with the judgment of others, make a general judgment: e.g., all tulips are beautiful; but that is then not a judgment of taste, but a logical judgment, which makes the relation of an object to taste into a predicate of things of a certain kind in general; but that whereby I find a single given tulip beautiful, i.e., find my liking for it to be universally valid, is alone the judgment of taste. Its peculiarity, however, consists in this: that, although it has merely subjective validity, it nevertheless lays claim to ***all*** subjects just as could ever happen if it were an objective judgment that rests on grounds of cognition and could be compelled by a proof.
