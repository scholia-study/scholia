## {{{ 284 }}} {{ 140 }} § 33. Second peculiarity of the judgment of taste

The judgment of taste cannot be settled by any grounds of proof whatever, as though it were merely something ***subjective***.

Should someone judge a building, a prospect, or a poem to be without beauty, then, ***in the first place***, he refuses to let a hundred voices, however loudly they all extol it, press their approval inwardly upon him. He may indeed pretend that it pleases him too, so as not to be taken for someone lacking taste; he may even fall to doubting whether his taste has been adequately formed through familiarity with a large enough range of objects of some given sort (much as a person who thinks he can make out something far off as a forest, while everyone else takes it for a town, comes to distrust the judgment of his own eyes). This much, however, he grasps quite clearly: that the applause of others furnishes no proof holding good for the estimation of beauty; that at most they may look and take note in his stead, and that whatever a crowd has seen in one and the same manner can, for him who supposes he has seen it differently, count as a sufficient ground of proof toward a theoretical and therefore logical [judgment], yet that what has given others pleasure is never able to furnish the basis of an aesthetic judgment. The unfavorable judgment others pass on us may, to be sure, quite justifiably give us pause about {{ 141 }} our own, though it can never bring us to see it as mistaken. Hence there is no empirical ***ground of proof*** by which to compel a judgment of taste from anyone.

***Secondly***, still less can a proof _a priori_ according to determinate rules determine the judgment about beauty. Suppose someone recites his poem to me, or brings me along to a drama that, in the end, simply refuses to sit well with my taste: he is welcome to summon ***Batteux*** or ***Lessing***, or critics of taste older and more celebrated still, along with every rule these have set up, as proof that his poem is beautiful; and even should particular passages, the very ones that displease me, chime perfectly with rules of beauty (in the shape there laid down and generally recognized): I stop up my ears, refuse to hear any reasons or subtle reasoning, and will sooner suppose those critics' rules to be false, or at least inapplicable to the case at hand, than let my judgment be fixed by grounds of proof _a priori_ {{{ 285 }}}, seeing that what is at issue is to be a judgment of taste, not one of understanding or of reason.

This appears to be one of the principal reasons why the name of taste, of all names, has been fastened upon this aesthetic capacity for judging. For a person may list off for me every ingredient {{ 142 }} in a dish and observe of each that each of them is otherwise agreeable to me, and may over and above this rightly praise the wholesomeness of this fare; yet I stay deaf to all such grounds, I put the dish to the test of ***my*** own tongue and palate: and it is on that basis (not on universal principles) that I pass my judgment.

Indeed, a judgment of taste is invariably rendered, in each instance, as a singular judgment concerning the object. By setting the object, in respect of its pleasing quality, alongside the judgment of others, the understanding is able to arrive at a universal judgment: for instance, all tulips are beautiful; that, however, is then no judgment of taste but a logical one, which makes the bearing of an object upon taste into a predicate belonging to things of a certain kind as such; whereas what alone constitutes the judgment of taste is that through which I find a single given tulip beautiful, that is, find my satisfaction in it to be universally valid. Its peculiarity, however, consists in this: that, even though it has merely subjective validity, it nonetheless lays claim to ***all*** subjects just as thoroughly as could ever be the case if it were an objective judgment resting on grounds of cognition and capable of being enforced through a proof.
