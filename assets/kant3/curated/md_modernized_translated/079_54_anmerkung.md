## § 54. Remark

There is, as we have repeatedly demonstrated, an essential difference between that ***which pleases merely in the judging of it*** and that which ***gratifies*** (pleases in sensation). This latter kind cannot be expected of everyone as the former kind can. Gratification (its cause may well lie even in ideas) appears in every instance to rest in {{{ 331 }}} a feeling that the whole of the human being's life is being promoted, and therewith his physical welfare too, that is, his health; {{ 223 }} and thus ***Epicurus***, who declared all gratification to be fundamentally a bodily sensation, was to this degree perhaps not mistaken, erring only about himself when he counted intellectual and indeed practical satisfaction as gratifications. Once one keeps the latter distinction before one's eyes, one can explain how a gratification can itself displease the person who feels it (like the joy of a needy but right-minded person over the inheritance from his loving but stingy father), or how a deep pain can nonetheless please the person who suffers it (the sorrow of a widow over the death of her deserving husband), or how a gratification can please us over and above (like our gratification in the sciences we pursue), or a pain (e.g., hatred, envy, and vindictiveness) can moreover displease us. Here the satisfaction or dissatisfaction rests on reason and is one and the same as ***approval*** or ***disapproval***; but gratification and pain can rest only on feeling, or on the prospect of a possible ***well-being*** or ***ill-being*** (on whatever ground it may be).

Because it promotes the feeling of health, every shifting free play of sensations (which has no purpose at its basis) gratifies: whether or not, in judging by reason, its object and even this very gratification meet with our satisfaction; and such gratification may climb as high as an affect, although the object as such holds no interest for us — at any rate none commensurate with the degree of that affect. We can divide it into the ***game of chance***, the ***game of tone***, and the ***game of thought***. The ***first*** calls for an ***interest*** — of vanity or of self-interest — which is nevertheless nowhere near so great as our interest in the way we set about procuring it for ourselves; {{ 224 }} the ***second*** calls merely for the changing of the ***sensations***, every one of which bears a relation to affect, though without reaching the degree of an affect, and rouses aesthetic ideas; the ***third*** arises solely from the changing of representations within the power of judgment, whereby no thought bearing any interest is produced, and yet the mind is thereby quickened.

How gratifying the games must be, without any need to lay an interested aim as their basis, is shown by all our evening companies; for without a game hardly any of them can keep itself going. Yet the affects of hope, fear, joy, anger, and scorn {{{ 332 }}} come into play here, since at every moment they exchange their roles, and are so vivid that through them, as an inner motion, the body's whole business of life seems to be furthered — as the resulting cheerfulness of mind attests — although nothing at all has been either gained or learned. But since the game of chance is not a beautiful game, we shall set it aside here. Music and matter for laughter, on the other hand, are two sorts of play with aesthetic ideas — or with representations of the understanding as well — by means of which nothing is ultimately thought, and which can nonetheless afford a lively gratification through their variation alone; and by this they make it tolerably clear that in both cases the enlivening is purely bodily, however much it be stirred up by ideas of the mind, and that it is the feeling of health, brought about by a movement of the viscera answering to that play, which makes up the entire gratification — so esteemed as refined and full of spirit — of a merry gathering. What makes up the gratification one takes in the fact that the body can be reached through the soul as well, so that the soul may serve as the body's physician, is not the judging of harmonies in tones or in flashes of wit — which, with its beauty, serves merely as an indispensable vehicle — but rather the promoted business of life in the body, the affect that sets the {{ 225 }} viscera and the diaphragm in motion, in short the feeling of health (which apart from some such prompting cannot otherwise be felt).

In music, this play proceeds from the body's sensation to aesthetic ideas (of the objects for affects), and from these it then returns once more, though with concentrated force upon the body. In jesting (which, no less than the former, deserves to be counted among the agreeable arts rather than the fine ones) the play begins from thoughts that, one and all, in so far as they wish to give themselves sensible expression, engage the body too; and inasmuch as the understanding suddenly slackens in this presentation, wherein it fails to find what it had expected, one feels the effect of that slackening in the body through the vibrating of the organs, which promotes the recovery of their equilibrium and exerts a salutary influence upon health.

There must be something absurd in anything meant to provoke a hearty, convulsive laughter (something, therefore, in which the understanding by itself can take no satisfaction). ***Laughter is an affect that springs from the abrupt conversion of a tensed expectation into nothing.*** This very conversion, which is certainly not delightful for the understanding, does nonetheless indirectly delight very vividly for a moment. The {{{ 333 }}} cause, then, must reside in the effect that the representation has upon the body, together with the body's reciprocal action upon the mind; and this not because the representation, objectively taken, is an object of gratification (for how could a deceived expectation gratify?), but purely because, being a {{ 226 }} mere play of representations, it produces within the body an equilibrium of the vital forces.

If someone recounts that an Indian who, at the table of an Englishman in Surat, saw a bottle of ale being opened and all this beer, changed into foam, rushing out, expressed his great astonishment with many exclamations, and, upon the Englishman's question: what is there here to be so astonished at? answered: I am not astonished either that it comes out, but rather at how you managed to get it in, then we laugh, and it gives us a hearty delight: not because we perhaps find ourselves cleverer than this ignorant person, or on account of anything else pleasing that the understanding lets us notice in it; but rather our expectation was strained and suddenly vanishes into nothing. Or take the heir of a wealthy kinsman who means to give him a thoroughly solemn burial, but laments that he simply cannot succeed at it; for, as he puts it, the more I pay my hired mourners to wear a grieving face, the more cheerful they come to look — at which we laugh aloud, and the reason lies in this: that an expectation is all at once turned into nothing. One must indeed observe: that it must convert itself not into the positive opposite of an expected object — for that is always something and can often grieve us, — but rather into nothing. For if someone stirs up great expectation in us with the telling of a tale, and at the close we at once see through its untruth, then it gives us displeasure; as, e.g., the tale of people who are supposed to have gotten gray hair in a single night from great grief. If, by contrast, another wag replies to a tale of that kind by relating in great detail the sorrow of a merchant who, on his way home from India to Europe with his entire fortune {{ 227 }} in goods, found himself forced during a violent storm to cast the whole cargo overboard, and who grieved to such a degree that his ***wig*** went gray in that very night: then we burst out laughing, and it affords us gratification, because for a time we go on knocking our own misstep — over an object that is anyway a matter of indifference to us, or rather the idea we keep chasing — to and fro like a ball, though all we intend is to catch hold of it and keep it. It is {{{ 334 }}} not here the dismissal of a liar or a blockhead that awakens the gratification: for even on its own the latter tale, told with assumed seriousness, would throw a company into bright laughter; and the former would ordinarily not even be worth the attention.

It is remarkable that, in every case of this sort, the jest must harbor within itself something capable of deceiving us for an instant; and so, once the semblance dissolves into nothing, the mind turns back to it to make the attempt yet again, and in this way, through a tensing and slackening that follow one another in quick succession, is flung back and forth and thrown into vacillation: which — since the recoil from what had, so to speak, drawn the string tight came about suddenly (and not by a gradual easing) — must generate a motion of the mind together with an inward bodily motion harmonizing with it, a motion that continues involuntarily and produces weariness, yet therewith also a cheering-up (the effects of an agitation that redounds to health).

For suppose that, along with all our thoughts, some movement or other in the organs of the body stands harmoniously conjoined: one will then more or less comprehend how that abrupt shifting of the mind — now to one standpoint, now to another, so as to regard its object {{ 228 }} — can be matched by an alternating tautening and slackening of the elastic portions of our viscera, which imparts itself to the diaphragm (just like what ticklish people feel): whereupon the lungs drive the air out in bursts following rapidly one on another, and thus produce a movement conducive to health — a movement that by itself, and not what goes on in the mind, is itself the real ground of the gratification afforded by a thought that at bottom represents nothing. — Voltaire remarked that, as a counterweight against the many toils of life, heaven has bestowed on us two things: ***hope*** and ***sleep***. He could have added ***laughter*** to these; if only the means of arousing it in rational beings were as readily at hand, and the wit or the originality of temper required for it were not just as rare as the talent is frequent for composing in a ***head-breaking*** way like mystical brooders, in a ***neck-breaking*** way like geniuses, or in a ***heart-breaking*** way like sentimental novelists (and probably moralists of the same stripe).

One may therefore, as it appears to me, readily concede to Epicurus the following: that all gratification, even when brought on by concepts that {{{ 335 }}} awaken aesthetic ideas, is ***animal*** — that is, bodily — sensation; without on that account doing the least injury to the ***spiritual*** feeling of reverence toward moral ideas — a feeling that is no gratification but rather an esteem for oneself (for the humanity within us), one that lifts us above any need of it — nor even to the less noble feeling of ***taste***.

A thing compounded of the two shows itself in ***naïveté***, which is the breaking-forth of that sincerity, originally natural to humankind, against the art of dissimulation that has turned into a second nature. One {{ 229 }} laughs at the simplicity that does not yet understand how to dissemble; and yet one also delights in the simplicity of nature, which here draws a line through that art. One expected the everyday convention of the affected utterance carefully laid out for beautiful semblance; and behold! it is uncorrupted, guiltless nature that one was not at all prepared to meet with, and which the one who let it show through had no intention of laying bare. That the beautiful but false semblance, which ordinarily counts for a great deal in our judgment, is here suddenly converted into nothing, that the rogue in ourselves is, as it were, exposed, brings forth the movement of the mind in two opposed directions one after the other, which at the same time gives the body a wholesome shaking. But the fact that something infinitely better than all assumed convention — the purity of one's cast of thought (or at any rate the predisposition to it) — has, for all that, not been wholly extinguished in human nature, mingles into this play of the power of judgment a strain of seriousness and high esteem. Yet since it is only an appearance that shows itself for a brief while, and the veil of the art of dissimulation is soon drawn across once more, there mingles into it at the same time a certain regret — a stir of tenderness which, treated as play, blends very readily with such good-natured laughter, and in fact is commonly so blended with it, while it also serves to make good, to the person who supplies the occasion for it, his embarrassment at not yet having been made worldly-wise in the human manner. — An art of being ***naïve*** is for that reason a contradiction; to portray naïveté in a fictitious character, however, is indeed possible, and is a fine art, albeit likewise a rare one. Naïveté ought not to be confounded with that frank simplicity which refrains from artificializing nature merely because it fails to grasp {{ 230 }} what the art of social intercourse consists in.

To that which is heartening, closely akin to the gratification from laughter, and belonging to the originality of spirit but precisely not to the talent of fine art, one may also count the ***humorous*** manner {{{ 336 }}}. ***Humor*** in the good sense, that is, means the talent of being able to put oneself voluntarily into a certain disposition of mind in which all things are judged quite otherwise than usual (indeed inverted) and yet in conformity with certain principles of reason within such a frame of mind. Anyone who is subject to such alterations against his will is ***moody***; but the one who can assume them at will and to a purpose (with a view to a vivid presentation by way of a contrast that provokes laughter), he together with his manner of delivery is termed ***humorous***. This manner, though, has its place among the agreeable arts rather than the fine ones, since the object of the latter must invariably exhibit a certain dignity in itself, and for that reason calls for a measure of seriousness in the presentation, just as taste calls for it in the judging.
