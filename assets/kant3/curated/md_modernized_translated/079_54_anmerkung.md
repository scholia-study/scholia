## § 54. Remark

Between that ***which pleases merely in the judging*** and that which ***gratifies*** (pleases in sensation) there is, as we have often shown, an essential difference. The latter is something that one cannot expect of everyone in the way one can the former. Gratification (the cause of which may, after all, also lie in ideas) seems always to consist {{{ 331 }}} in a feeling of the promotion of the entire life of the human being, and consequently also of bodily well-being, i.e., health; {{ 223 }} so that ***Epicurus***, who took all gratification to be at bottom bodily sensation, may perhaps not have been wrong to that extent, and only misunderstood himself when he counted intellectual and even practical liking among gratifications. If one has the latter difference before one's eyes, then one can explain how a gratification can itself displease the one who feels it (such as the joy of a needy but right-thinking person over the inheritance from his loving but parsimonious father), or how a deep pain can nonetheless please the one who suffers it (the sadness of a widow over the death of her meritorious husband), or how a gratification can moreover still please (such as that in sciences which we pursue), or a pain (e.g., hatred, envy, and greed for revenge) can additionally displease us. Liking or disliking here rests on reason and is one and the same with ***approval*** or ***disapproval***; gratification and pain, however, can rest only on the feeling or the prospect of a possible ***well-being*** or ***ill-being*** (from whatever ground it may be).

Every changing free play of sensations (which have no intention at their basis) gratifies because it promotes the feeling of health: whether or not we have a liking for its object, or even for this gratification itself, in the judgment of reason; and this gratification can rise to an affect, although we take no interest, or at least no such interest, in the object itself as would be proportioned to the degree of the latter. We can divide them into ***games of chance***, ***games of tones***, and ***games of thought***. The ***first*** requires an ***interest***, whether of vanity or of self-interest, which however is by no means as great as the interest in the way we seek to procure it; {{ 224 }} the ***second*** merely the change of ***sensations***, each of which has its relation to affect, but without the degree of an affect, and stirs up aesthetic ideas; the ***third*** arises merely from the change of representations in the power of judgment, by which, although no thought that carries some interest with it is produced, the mind is nonetheless enlivened.

How gratifying games must be, without its being necessary to lay an interested intention at their basis, is shown by all our evening parties; for almost none can entertain itself without a game. But the affects of hope, of fear, of joy, of anger, of derision {{{ 332 }}} play along, in that they change their roles at every moment, and are so lively that thereby, as by an internal motion, the entire business of life in the body seems to be promoted, as a cheerfulness of mind produced thereby proves, although nothing has been either won or learned. But since a game of chance is not a beautiful game, we will set it aside here. On the other hand, music and material for laughter are two kinds of play with aesthetic ideas, or also representations of the understanding, through which in the end nothing is thought, and which can gratify merely through their change and nonetheless in a lively manner; whereby they show quite clearly that the enlivening in both is merely bodily, although it is excited by ideas of the mind, and that the feeling of health, through a motion of the intestines corresponding to that play, constitutes the entire gratification of an animated company, which is praised as so refined and spiritual. Not the judging of harmony in tones or witty ideas, which with its beauty serves only as a necessary vehicle, but rather the promoted business of life in the body, the affect that moves the {{ 225 }} intestines and the diaphragm, in a word, the feeling of health (which otherwise cannot be felt without such an occasion), constitute the gratification that is found in being able to get at the body also through the soul and to use the latter as the physician of the former.

In music, this play goes from the sensation of the body to aesthetic ideas (of objects for affects), and then from these back again, but with redoubled force, to the body. In jest (which just as much as music deserves to be counted as an agreeable rather than a beautiful art), the play starts from thoughts which, taken as a whole, insofar as they want to express themselves sensibly, also occupy the body; and since the understanding in this presentation, in which it does not find what was expected, suddenly lets go, one feels the effect of this relaxation in the body through the vibration of the organs, which promotes the restoration of their equilibrium and has a beneficial influence on health.

In everything that is to excite a lively, convulsive laughter, there must be something absurd (in which, therefore, the understanding in itself can find no liking). ***Laughter is an affect arising from the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing.*** This very transformation, which is certainly not enjoyable for the understanding, nonetheless indirectly rejoices one very livelily for a moment. Thus the {{{ 333 }}} cause must consist in the influence of the representation on the body and its reciprocal effect on the mind; and indeed not insofar as the representation is objectively an object of gratification (for how can a deceived expectation gratify?), but solely through the fact that, as a {{ 226 }} mere play of representations, it produces an equilibrium of the vital forces in the body.

When someone relates that an Indian, seeing at the table of an Englishman in Surat a bottle of ale opened and all this beer, transformed into froth, rush out, indicated with many exclamations his great wonderment, and, to the Englishman's question, "What is there here to wonder at so much?", answered, "I am not wondering at its coming out, but rather how you managed to get it in," we laugh, and it gives us a hearty delight: not because we find ourselves perhaps cleverer than this ignorant man, or over anything else in this which the understanding would let us notice as pleasing, but rather because our expectation was strained and suddenly vanishes into nothing. Or if the heir of a wealthy relative wants to arrange a very solemn funeral service for him, but complains that he cannot quite succeed in this; for (he says), "The more money I give my mourners to look sad, the more cheerful they look," we laugh aloud, and the ground lies in the fact that an expectation is suddenly transformed into nothing. One must well note: that it must transform itself not into the positive opposite of an expected object — for that is always something and can often distress — but into nothing. For when someone excites great expectation in us by relating a story, and at the conclusion we immediately see its untruth, it causes us displeasure; such as, e.g., the story of people whose hair is supposed to have turned gray in a single night from great grief. On the other hand, if, in reply to such a tale, another rogue tells in great detail of the grief of a merchant who, returning from India to Europe with all his fortune {{ 227 }} in goods, was forced in a heavy storm to throw everything overboard, and grieved to such an extent that in that same night his ***wig*** turned gray: we laugh, and it gives us gratification, because we knock back and forth our own mistake concerning an object otherwise indifferent to us, or rather our pursued idea like a ball for some time longer, while we had merely intended to grasp and hold it fast. It is {{{ 334 }}} not here the dismissal of a liar or a fool that excites the gratification: for in itself, too, the latter story told with assumed seriousness would put a company into a bright laughter; and the former would usually also not be worth the attention.

It is remarkable: that in all such cases the joke must always contain something in itself which can deceive for a moment; hence when the appearance vanishes into nothing, the mind looks back again in order to try it once more with it, and is thus, by rapidly succeeding tension and relaxation, snapped back and forth and put into a state of oscillation: which, because the departure from what as it were pulled the string happened suddenly (not through a gradual letting go), must cause a mental movement and an internal bodily movement harmonizing with it, which continues involuntarily and produces fatigue, but at the same time also refreshment (the effects of a motion conducive to health).

For if one assumes that with all of our thoughts some movement in the organs of the body is at the same time harmoniously combined: then one will pretty well understand how to that sudden displacement of the mind, now into one, now into another standpoint to consider its object, {{ 228 }} a reciprocal tension and release of the elastic parts of our intestines, which communicates itself to the diaphragm, can correspond (like that which ticklish people feel): whereby the lungs expel the air with rapidly succeeding interruptions and thus produce a movement beneficial to health, which alone, and not what goes on in the mind, is the actual cause of gratification in a thought that at bottom represents nothing. — Voltaire said that heaven has given us two things as a counterweight to the many hardships of life: ***hope*** and ***sleep***. He could have added ***laughter*** to them; if only the means to excite it in rational people were as easily at hand, and the wit or the originality of humor required for it were not as rare as is frequent the talent to write poetry ***head-breakingly*** like mystical dreamers, ***neck-breakingly*** like geniuses, or ***heart-breakingly*** like sentimental novelists (and also, indeed, moralists of that kind).

One can thus, as it seems to me, well concede to Epicurus: that all gratification, even if it is occasioned by concepts which {{{ 335 }}} awaken aesthetic ideas, is ***animal***, i.e., bodily, sensation; without thereby in the least doing any detriment to the ***spiritual*** feeling of respect for moral ideas, which is no gratification, but rather a self-esteem (of humanity in us) that raises us above the need for gratification, or indeed even to the less noble feeling of ***taste***.

Something composed of both is found in ***naivete***, which is the outburst of the sincerity originally natural to humanity against the art of dissimulation that has become a second nature. One {{ 229 }} laughs at the simplicity that does not yet understand how to dissimulate; and yet also rejoices over the simplicity of nature, which here plays a trick on that art. One expected the everyday custom of artificial utterance cautiously calculated for beautiful appearance; and behold! it is uncorrupted, guiltless nature, which one was not at all expecting to meet and which the one who let it be seen also did not intend to expose. That the beautiful but false appearance, which usually means very much in our judgment, is here suddenly transformed into nothing, that as it were the rogue in ourselves is exposed, produces the movement of the mind in two opposite directions in succession, which at the same time healthfully shakes the body. But that something which is infinitely better than all assumed custom, the purity of the way of thinking (or at least the predisposition to it), is nonetheless not entirely extinguished in human nature, mixes seriousness and high esteem into this play of the power of judgment. Because it is only an appearance presenting itself for a short time, and the cover of the art of dissimulation is soon drawn over it again: a regret at the same time mixes with it, which is an emotion of tenderness that can very well combine as a play with such good-natured laughter and indeed usually does combine with it, and at the same time is accustomed to compensate the one who provides the material for it for the embarrassment over his not yet being wise in the way of the world. — An art of being ***naive*** is therefore a contradiction; but to present naivete in a fictitious person is indeed possible and is a beautiful, although also rare, art. With naivete, open-hearted simplicity, which nature does not make artificial only because it does not understand {{ 230 }} what the art of social intercourse is, must not be confused.

To that which is encouraging, closely related to the gratification of laughter, and belonging to the originality of the spirit but not to the talent of beautiful art, the ***humorous*** manner {{{ 336 }}} can also be added. ***Humor*** in the good sense namely means the talent of being able voluntarily to place oneself in a certain frame of mind in which all things are judged quite differently from usual (even conversely) and yet in accordance with certain principles of reason in such a state of mind. One who is involuntarily subject to such changes is ***moody***; but one who is able to assume them voluntarily and purposively (for the sake of a lively presentation by means of a laughter-exciting contrast), he and his presentation are called ***humorous***. This manner, however, belongs more to agreeable than to beautiful art, because the object of the latter must always exhibit some dignity in itself and thus requires a certain seriousness in presentation, just as taste does in judging.
