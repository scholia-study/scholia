## § 54. Remark

Between that ***which pleases merely in the judging of it*** and that which ***gratifies*** (pleases in sensation) there is, as we have often shown, an essential difference. The latter is something that one cannot demand of everyone in the same way as the former. Gratification (whatever its cause, even if it should lie in ideas) always seems to consist in {{{ 331 }}} the feeling that the human being's entire life is being advanced, and hence also his bodily well-being, i.e., health; {{ 223 }} so that ***Epicurus***, who took all gratification to be at bottom bodily sensation, may to that extent perhaps not have been wrong, and only misunderstood himself when he reckoned intellectual and even practical satisfaction among the gratifications. Once one keeps the latter distinction before one's eyes, one can explain how a gratification can itself displease the person who feels it (like the joy of a needy but right-minded person over the inheritance from his loving but stingy father), or how a deep pain can nonetheless please the person who suffers it (the sorrow of a widow over the death of her deserving husband), or how a gratification can please us over and above (like our gratification in the sciences we pursue), or a pain (e.g., hatred, envy, and vindictiveness) can moreover displease us. Here the satisfaction or dissatisfaction rests on reason and is one and the same as ***approval*** or ***disapproval***; but gratification and pain can rest only on feeling, or on the prospect of a possible ***well-being*** or ***ill-being*** (on whatever ground it may be).

Every changing free play of sensations (which have no aim as their basis) gratifies, because it furthers the feeling of health: whether or not, in the rational judging, we have a satisfaction in its object and even in this gratification; and this gratification can mount to the level of an affect, even though we take no interest in the object itself, at least none that would be proportioned to the degree of the latter. We can divide it into the ***game of chance***, the ***game of tone***, and the ***game of thought***. The ***first*** demands an ***interest***, whether of vanity or of self-seeking, which, however, is by far not as great as the interest in the manner in which we seek to procure it for ourselves; {{ 224 }} the ***second*** merely the alternation of the ***sensations***, each of which has its relation to affect, but without the degree of an affect, and sets aesthetic ideas astir; the ***third*** springs merely from the alternation of representations in the power of judgment, through which no thought carrying any interest with it is indeed produced, yet the mind is enlivened.

How gratifying the games must be, without any need to lay an interested aim as their basis, is shown by all our evening companies; for without a game hardly any of them can keep itself going. But the affects of hope, of fear, of joy, of anger, of scorn {{{ 332 }}} are in play there, in that they change their role every moment, and are so lively that thereby, as an inner motion, the whole business of life in the body seems to be furthered, as a cheerfulness of mind thereby produced proves, even though nothing has been either won or learned. But since the game of chance is not a beautiful game, we shall set it aside here. Music and material for laughter, by contrast, are two kinds of play with aesthetic ideas, or else with representations of the understanding, through which in the end nothing is thought, and which can gratify merely through their alternation and yet in a lively way; whereby they let it be recognized fairly clearly that the enlivening in both is merely bodily, even though it is aroused by ideas of the mind, and that the feeling of health, through a movement of the viscera corresponding to that play, constitutes the whole of the gratification, so prized as refined and full of spirit, of an animated company. It is not the judging of the harmony in tones or in sallies of wit, which serves with its beauty only as a necessary vehicle, but rather the furthered business of life in the body, the affect that stirs the {{ 225 }} viscera and the diaphragm, in a word the feeling of health (which otherwise does not let itself be felt without such an occasion), that make up the gratification one finds in being able to get at the body also by way of the soul and to employ the latter as physician of the former.

In music this play goes from the sensation of the body to aesthetic ideas (of objects for affects), and then back again from these, but with united force upon the body. In jesting (which, just as much as the former, deserves to be reckoned among the agreeable rather than the fine arts) the play sets out from thoughts that, taken all together, insofar as they seek to express themselves sensibly, also occupy the body; and since the understanding, in this presentation, in which it does not find what it expected, suddenly slackens, one feels the effect of this slackening in the body through the vibration of the organs, which furthers the restoration of their equilibrium and has a beneficial influence on health.

In everything that is to arouse a lively, convulsive laughter there must be something absurd (in which, accordingly, the understanding as such can find no satisfaction). ***Laughter is an affect that springs from the abrupt conversion of a tensed expectation into nothing.*** This very conversion, which is certainly not delightful for the understanding, does nonetheless indirectly delight very vividly for a moment. Hence the {{{ 333 }}} cause must lie in the influence of the representation on the body and its reciprocal action on the mind; and indeed not insofar as the representation is objectively an object of gratification (for how can a frustrated expectation gratify?), but solely through the fact that, as a {{ 226 }} mere play of representations, it brings about an equilibrium of the vital forces in the body.

If someone recounts that an Indian who, at the table of an Englishman in Surat, saw a bottle of ale being opened and all this beer, changed into foam, rushing out, expressed his great astonishment with many exclamations, and, upon the Englishman's question: what is there here to be so astonished at? answered: I am not astonished either that it comes out, but rather at how you managed to get it in, then we laugh, and it gives us a hearty delight: not because we perhaps find ourselves cleverer than this ignorant person, or on account of anything else pleasing that the understanding lets us notice in it; but rather our expectation was strained and suddenly vanishes into nothing. Or when the heir of a rich relation wishes to arrange for him a truly solemn funeral, but complains that he cannot quite bring it off; for (he says): the more money I give my mourners to look grieved, the merrier they look, then we laugh out loud, and the ground of it lies in this, that an expectation is suddenly converted into nothing. One must indeed observe: that it must convert itself not into the positive opposite of an expected object — for that is always something and can often grieve us, — but rather into nothing. For if someone stirs up great expectation in us with the telling of a tale, and at the close we at once see through its untruth, then it gives us displeasure; as, e.g., the tale of people who are supposed to have gotten gray hair in a single night from great grief. If, on the other hand, in reply to a tale of that sort another wag recounts very circumstantially the grief of a merchant who, returning from India to Europe with his whole fortune {{ 227 }} in wares, was compelled in a heavy storm to throw everything overboard and grieved so greatly over it that his ***wig*** turned gray that very night: then we laugh, and it gives us gratification, because for a while we keep batting our own blunder regarding an object otherwise indifferent to us — or rather the idea we are pursuing — back and forth like a ball, when we merely mean to grasp it and hold it fast. It is {{{ 334 }}} not here the dismissal of a liar or a blockhead that awakens the gratification: for even on its own the latter tale, told with assumed seriousness, would throw a company into bright laughter; and the former would ordinarily not even be worth the attention.

It is notable: that in all such cases the joke must always contain within it something that can deceive for a moment; hence when the semblance vanishes into nothing, the mind looks back once more in order to try it over again, and thus, through a rapidly successive tensing and relaxing, is jerked to and fro and set oscillating: which, because the springing-away from what as it were drew the string taut occurred suddenly (not through a gradual slackening), must produce a movement of the mind and, harmonizing with it, an inner bodily movement, which persists involuntarily and brings about fatigue, but along with it also a brightening (the effects of a motion conducive to health).

For if one assumes that some movement in the organs of the body is at the same time harmoniously bound up with all our thoughts: then one will grasp fairly well how, to that sudden displacement of the mind now into one and now into the other standpoint in order to consider its object {{ 228 }}, there can correspond a reciprocal tensing and releasing of the elastic parts of our viscera, which communicates itself to the diaphragm (like that which ticklish people feel): whereby the lungs expel the air in rapidly succeeding bursts and so bring about a movement salutary to health, a movement which by itself, and not what transpires in the mind, forms the true cause of the gratification we take in a thought that at bottom represents nothing at all. — Voltaire remarked that, as a counterweight against the many toils of life, heaven has bestowed on us two things: ***hope*** and ***sleep***. He could have added ***laughter*** to these; if only the means of arousing it in rational beings were as readily at hand, and the wit or the originality of temper required for it were not just as rare as the talent is frequent for composing in a ***head-breaking*** way like mystical brooders, in a ***neck-breaking*** way like geniuses, or in a ***heart-breaking*** way like sentimental novelists (and probably moralists of the same stripe).

Thus one can, it seems to me, quite well grant Epicurus the point: that every gratification, even where it is occasioned by concepts that {{{ 335 }}} awaken aesthetic ideas, is ***animal***, i.e., bodily, sensation; without thereby doing the slightest detriment to the ***spiritual*** feeling of respect for moral ideas, which is no gratification but a self-esteem (of the humanity in us) that raises us above the need for it, indeed without doing so even to the less noble feeling of ***taste***.

Something composed of both is found in ***naïveté***, which is the eruption of the sincerity originally natural to humanity against the art of dissimulation that has become a second nature. One {{ 229 }} laughs at the simplicity that does not yet understand how to dissemble; and yet one also delights in the simplicity of nature, which here draws a line through that art. One expected the everyday convention of the affected utterance carefully laid out for beautiful semblance; and behold! it is uncorrupted, guiltless nature that one was not at all prepared to meet with, and which the one who let it show through had no intention of laying bare. That the beautiful but false semblance, which ordinarily counts for a great deal in our judgment, is here suddenly converted into nothing, that the rogue in ourselves is, as it were, exposed, brings forth the movement of the mind in two opposed directions one after the other, which at the same time gives the body a wholesome shaking. But that something which is infinitely better than all assumed convention, the purity of the way of thinking (at least the predisposition to it), has after all not entirely died out in human nature, mingles seriousness and high esteem into this play of the power of judgment. But because it is only a phenomenon coming forth for a short time, and the cover of the art of dissimulation is soon drawn over again: at the same time a certain regret mingles into it, which is a stirring of tenderness that lets itself be very well combined, as play, with such good-hearted laughter, and indeed usually is combined with it, and at the same time tends to make up, to the one who furnishes the material for it, the embarrassment at his not yet having grown shrewd in the way of human beings. — An art of being ***naïve*** is therefore a contradiction; yet to represent naïveté in an invented character is quite possible, and is a beautiful, though also rare, art. Naïveté must not be confused with the open-hearted simplicity which does not artificialize nature only because it does not understand {{ 230 }} what the art of social intercourse is.

To that which is heartening, closely akin to the gratification from laughter, and belonging to the originality of spirit but precisely not to the talent of fine art, one may also count the ***humorous*** manner {{{ 336 }}}. ***Humor*** in the good sense, that is, means the talent of being able to put oneself voluntarily into a certain disposition of mind in which all things are judged quite otherwise than usual (indeed inverted) and yet in conformity with certain principles of reason within such a frame of mind. Whoever is involuntarily subject to such changes is ***moody***; but whoever is able to adopt them voluntarily and purposively (for the sake of a lively presentation by means of a laughter-provoking contrast), he and his delivery are called ***humorous***. This manner, however, belongs more to the agreeable than to the fine arts, because the object of the latter must always display some dignity in itself and therefore requires a certain seriousness in the presentation, just as taste requires it in the judging.
