## § 59. On beauty as a symbol of morality

To establish the reality of our concepts, intuitions are always required. Should the concepts be empirical, these intuitions go by the name of ***examples***. Should they instead be pure concepts of the understanding, the intuitions serving them are called ***schemata***. But if one demands that the objective reality of the concepts of reason—that is, of the ideas—be established, and indeed for the purpose of a theoretical cognition of them, then one is asking for something impossible, since no intuition can ever be given that would be adequate to them.

All ***hypotyposis*** (presentation, _subiectio sub adspectum_), as a rendering into the sensible, {{ 255 }} is of two kinds: it is either ***schematic***, when a concept that the understanding grasps has its matching intuition furnished _a priori_; or ***symbolic***, when beneath a concept—one that reason alone can think, and for which no sensible intuition could ever prove adequate—there is placed an intuition such that the procedure of the power of judgment is with respect to it merely analogous to what it does in schematizing, that is, agrees with it merely in the rule of that procedure and not in the intuition itself, hence merely as to the form of the reflection and not as to its content.

There is a use of the word ***symbolic***—one that recent logicians have indeed adopted, yet that distorts its sense and is incorrect—according to which it is opposed to the ***intuitive*** mode of representation; for the symbolic is only one species of the intuitive. The latter (the intuitive), namely, can be divided into the ***schematic*** and the ***symbolic*** mode of representation. {{{ 352 }}} Both are hypotyposes, that is, presentations (_exhibitiones_): not mere ***characterizations***, that is, designations of concepts by means of accompanying sensible signs that carry nothing pertaining to the object's own intuition but serve those concepts only, according to the law of the association of the imagination and hence with a subjective aim, as a means of reproduction; signs of this kind are either words or visible (algebraic, {{ 256 }} even mimetic) marks, as mere ***expressions*** for concepts[^*].

Accordingly, whatever intuitions we lay beneath concepts _a priori_ are therefore either ***schemata*** or ***symbols***: the first kind furnish direct presentations of the concept, the second kind indirect ones. A schema accomplishes this by demonstration, a symbol through an analogy (in the service of which empirical intuitions too are drawn upon), and in this analogy the power of judgment discharges a twofold task: first, to bring the concept to bear upon the object of some sensible intuition, and thereupon, secondly, to carry the mere rule of its reflection upon that intuition over to a wholly distinct object, of which the first serves only as the symbol. So it is that a monarchical state is represented through an ensouled body if it is governed according to internal laws of the people, but through a mere machine (such as a hand mill) if it is governed by a single absolute will—in either case, however, only ***symbolically***. For although no likeness whatever holds between a despotic state and a hand mill, one assuredly does hold between the rules by which we reflect on the two and on their causality. This matter has so far been little worked out, however much {{ 257 }} it merits a more searching inquiry; yet here is not the place to linger over it. Our language is full of such indirect presentations according to an analogy, whereby the expression harbors, in place of the concept's genuine schema, nothing more than a symbol for reflection. In this way the terms ***ground*** (a prop, a foundation), ***to depend*** (to be sustained from above), from which comes ***to flow*** (in place of to follow), ***substance*** (in Locke's phrasing: that which bears the accidents), and countless others count as symbolic rather than schematic hypotyposes, standing for concepts not through some direct intuition but solely by way of an analogy with one, that is, by the transfer of reflection upon {{{ 353 }}} an object of intuition to an entirely different concept, to which perhaps no intuition can ever directly correspond. If one may already call a mere mode of representation cognition (which is surely permissible when it serves as a principle not of the theoretical determination of the object, as to what it is in itself, but of the practical determination, as to what the idea of it is to become for us and for the purposive use of that idea): then all our cognition of God is merely symbolic; and whoever takes it to be schematic, together with such properties as understanding, will, and the like, which prove their objective reality only in beings of the world, lapses into anthropomorphism, just as, if he omits everything intuitive, he lapses into {{ 258 }} deism, whereby nothing at all is cognized, not even for practical purposes.

This, then, is my claim: the beautiful is the symbol of the morally good; and it is only in this respect (a relation that is natural to everyone, and that everyone also expects of others as a duty) that it pleases with a claim upon the assent of everyone else, while the mind is at the same time aware of a certain ennobling and of being lifted above the bare receptivity to a pleasure arising through sensory impressions, and esteems the worth of others too according to a similar maxim of their power of judgment. This is the ***intelligible*** toward which, as the previous section indicated, taste looks out—that with which our higher cognitive faculties themselves accord, and in whose absence sheer contradictions would spring up between their nature and the demands that taste raises. In this faculty the power of judgment does not see itself subjected, as it otherwise is in empirical judging, to a heteronomy of the laws of experience: with regard to the objects of such a pure satisfaction it gives the law to itself, just as reason does with regard to the faculty of desire; and, by reason both of this inner possibility within the subject and of the outer possibility of a nature agreeing with it, it sees itself referred to something in the subject itself and outside it, {{ 259 }} something that is neither nature nor freedom, yet is bound up with the ground of the latter, namely the supersensible, in which the theoretical faculty is joined into unity with the practical in a manner that is common to both and unknown to us. Let me single out a few points of this analogy, taking care meanwhile not to pass over the respects in which it differs.

1) The beautiful pleases ***immediately*** (but only in reflecting {{{ 354 }}} intuition, not, like morality, in the concept). 2) It pleases ***without any interest*** (the morally good, to be sure, is of necessity tied to an interest, though not to one that comes before the judgment upon the satisfaction, but rather to one that this judgment first calls forth). 3) The ***freedom*** of the imagination (and so of our faculty's sensible side) is represented, in the judging of the beautiful, as in agreement with the lawfulness of the understanding (in the moral judgment the will's freedom is conceived as the harmony of the will with itself under laws of reason holding universally). 4) The subjective principle for judging the beautiful is represented as ***universal***, that is, as valid for everyone, though not made recognizable by any universal concept whatever (morality's objective principle, for its part, is likewise declared universal, that is, valid for all subjects and at the same time for all actions of the same subject, and moreover as recognizable through a universal concept). Hence {{ 260 }} the moral judgment is not merely susceptible of determinate constitutive principles, but becomes possible ***only*** through founding the maxims upon these principles and upon their universality.

Attention to this analogy is common even to ordinary understanding; and we often designate beautiful objects of nature or of art with names that seem to take a moral judging for their basis. We speak of buildings or trees as majestic and stately, or of fields as smiling and cheerful; even colors are called innocent, modest, tender, because they arouse sensations that contain something analogous to the consciousness of a mental state produced by moral judgments. Taste makes possible, as it were, the transition from the charm of sense to a habitual moral interest without too violent a leap, in that it represents the imagination, even in its freedom, as determinable in a way purposive for the understanding, and teaches us to find a free satisfaction in objects of sense even without any charm of sense.

[^*]: The intuitive in cognition must be contrasted with the discursive (not with the symbolic). The former, now, is either ***schematic***, through ***demonstration***; or ***symbolic***, as a representation according to a mere ***analogy***.
