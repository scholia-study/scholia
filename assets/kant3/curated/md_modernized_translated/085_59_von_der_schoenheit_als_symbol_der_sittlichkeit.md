## § 59. On beauty as a symbol of morality

To establish the reality of our concepts, intuitions are always required. Should the concepts be empirical, these intuitions go by the name of ***examples***. Where the concepts in question are pure concepts of the understanding, the intuitions in turn are termed ***schemata***. Should someone even insist that the concepts of reason—the ideas, that is—have their objective reality set forth, and this for the sake of a theoretical cognition of them, then he demands an impossibility, since no intuition adequate to them can ever be furnished.

All ***hypotyposis*** (presentation, _subiectio sub adspectum_), as a rendering into the sensible, {{ 255 }} is of two kinds: it is either ***schematic***, when a concept that the understanding grasps has its matching intuition furnished _a priori_; or ***symbolic***, when beneath a concept—one that reason alone can think, and for which no sensible intuition could ever prove adequate—there is placed an intuition such that the procedure of the power of judgment is with respect to it merely analogous to what it does in schematizing, that is, agrees with it merely in the rule of that procedure and not in the intuition itself, hence merely as to the form of the reflection and not as to its content.

There is a use of the word ***symbolic***—one that recent logicians have indeed adopted, yet that distorts its sense and is incorrect—according to which it is opposed to the ***intuitive*** mode of representation; for the symbolic is only one species of the intuitive. The latter (the intuitive), namely, can be divided into the ***schematic*** and the ***symbolic*** mode of representation. {{{ 352 }}} Both are hypotyposes, that is, presentations (_exhibitiones_): not mere ***characterizations***, that is, designations of concepts by means of accompanying sensible signs that carry nothing pertaining to the object's own intuition but serve those concepts only, according to the law of the association of the imagination and hence with a subjective aim, as a means of reproduction; signs of this kind are either words or visible (algebraic, {{ 256 }} even mimetic) marks, as mere ***expressions*** for concepts[^*].

Accordingly, whatever intuitions we lay beneath concepts _a priori_ are therefore either ***schemata*** or ***symbols***: the first kind furnish direct presentations of the concept, the second kind indirect ones. A schema accomplishes this by demonstration, a symbol through an analogy (in the service of which empirical intuitions too are drawn upon), and in this analogy the power of judgment discharges a twofold task: first, to bring the concept to bear upon the object of some sensible intuition, and thereupon, secondly, to carry the mere rule of its reflection upon that intuition over to a wholly distinct object, of which the first serves only as the symbol. So it is that a monarchical state is represented through an ensouled body if it is governed according to internal laws of the people, but through a mere machine (such as a hand mill) if it is governed by a single absolute will—in either case, however, only ***symbolically***. For although no likeness whatever holds between a despotic state and a hand mill, one assuredly does hold between the rules by which we reflect on the two and on their causality. This matter has so far been little worked out, however much {{ 257 }} it merits a more searching inquiry; but this is not the occasion to dwell upon it. Such indirect presentations following an analogy abound in our language, and through them the expression harbors, in place of the concept's genuine schema, nothing more than a symbol for reflection. Thus the words ***ground*** (support, base), ***to depend*** (to be held from above), whence ***to flow*** (instead of to follow), ***substance*** (in Locke's expression: the bearer of the accidents), and countless further ones are hypotyposes and expressions for concepts that are symbolic rather than schematic, resting not on any direct intuition but solely on an analogy with one—that is, on carrying the reflection upon {{{ 353 }}} an object of intuition over to a wholly other concept, one with which perhaps no intuition can ever stand in direct correspondence. If a mere mode of representation may already be styled cognition (which is doubtless allowable when it constitutes a principle not for theoretically determining what the object is in itself, but for practically determining what the idea of it is to come to for us and for the purposive employment of that idea): then whatever cognition of God we possess is nothing more than symbolic; and whoever takes it for schematic—along with those properties, understanding, will, and so forth, that attest their objective reality in worldly beings alone—falls into anthropomorphism, whereas, should he drop everything intuitive, he sinks into {{ 258 }} deism, by which nothing whatever is cognized, not even for a practical aim.

Now I make this claim: the morally good has its symbol in the beautiful—and only under this aspect (a relation natural to everyone, and one that each person likewise demands of others as a duty) does it please while asserting a title to the assent of everyone else; and in this the mind is at once conscious of a certain ennoblement and of an elevation above the mere receptivity to a pleasure through impressions of the senses, and it values another's worth as well by a similar maxim of their power of judgment. This is the ***intelligible*** toward which, as the previous section indicated, taste looks out—that with which our higher cognitive faculties themselves accord, and in whose absence sheer contradictions would spring up between their nature and the demands that taste raises. It is in this faculty that the power of judgment, unlike in its ordinary empirical appraisals, does not find itself subject to a heteronomy of the laws of experience: as concerns the objects of so pure a satisfaction it prescribes the law to its own self, in the very way reason does as concerns the faculty of desire; and, in view alike of this interior possibility residing in the subject and of the exterior possibility of a nature that harmonizes with it, it sees itself brought into relation with something present in the subject itself as well as outside it, {{ 259 }} something that is neither nature nor freedom, yet stands linked to the ground of freedom—the supersensible, namely—wherein the theoretical faculty is bound together with the practical into unity in a manner shared by both and unknown to us. Let me single out a few points of this analogy, taking care meanwhile not to pass over the respects in which it differs.

1) The beautiful gives pleasure ***immediately*** (yet solely within reflecting {{{ 354 }}} intuition, and not, as morality does, within the concept). 2) It gives pleasure ***without any interest*** (the morally good is indeed bound of necessity to an interest, yet not to one that precedes the judgment upon the satisfaction, but to one that this judgment first brings about). 3) The ***freedom*** of the imagination (that is, the sensible side of our faculty) is, in judging the beautiful, presented as consonant with the understanding's lawfulness (the moral judgment, by contrast, thinks the will's freedom as that will's agreement with itself according to universal laws of reason). 4) The subjective principle for judging the beautiful is represented as ***universal***, that is, as valid for everyone, though not made recognizable by any universal concept whatever (morality's objective principle, for its part, is likewise declared universal, that is, valid for all subjects and at the same time for all actions of the same subject, and moreover as recognizable through a universal concept). Hence {{ 260 }} the moral judgment is not merely susceptible of determinate constitutive principles, but becomes possible ***only*** through founding the maxims upon these principles and upon their universality.

Regard for this analogy is likewise habitual to the common understanding; and beautiful objects of nature or of art we frequently label with names that appear to rest upon a moral appraisal. Buildings or trees we call majestic and magnificent, or meadows laughing and glad; colors themselves are termed innocent, modest, and tender, since they awaken feelings that hold within them something akin to the awareness of a state of mind brought about by moral judgings. Taste, as it were, renders the passage from sensory charm to a habitual moral interest possible without any too violent a leap, in that it exhibits the imagination, even in its freedom, as determinable so as to be purposive for the understanding, and it schools us to find a free satisfaction even in objects of sense that lack all sensory charm.

[^*]: The intuitive in cognition must be contrasted with the discursive (not with the symbolic). The former, now, is either ***schematic***, through ***demonstration***; or ***symbolic***, as a representation according to a mere ***analogy***.
