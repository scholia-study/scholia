## § 59. On Beauty as a Symbol of Morality

In order to demonstrate the reality of our concepts, intuitions are always required. If they are empirical concepts, the latter are called ***examples***. If those are pure concepts of the understanding, the latter are called ***schemata***. If one even demands that the objective reality of the concepts of reason, i.e., of ideas, and indeed for the sake of the theoretical cognition of them, be demonstrated, then one desires something impossible, because absolutely no intuition adequate to them can be given.

All ***hypotyposis*** (presentation, _subiectio sub adspectum_), as rendering sensible {{ 255 }}, is twofold: either ***schematic***, where to a concept which the understanding grasps, the corresponding intuition is given _a priori_; or ***symbolic***, where to a concept which only reason can think, and to which no sensible intuition can be adequate, an intuition is subordinated with which the procedure of the power of judgment is merely analogical to that which it observes in schematization, i.e., agrees with it merely in the rule of this procedure, not in the intuition itself, and consequently merely in the form of reflection, not in content.

It is a usage of the word ***symbolic*** that is indeed accepted by modern logicians, but is sense-distorting and incorrect, when one opposes it to the ***intuitive*** mode of representation; for the symbolic is only a species of the intuitive. The latter (the intuitive) can namely be divided into the ***schematic*** and the ***symbolic*** mode of representation. {{{ 352 }}} Both are hypotyposes, i.e., presentations (_exhibitiones_): not mere ***characterisms***, i.e., designations of concepts by accompanying sensible signs, which contain nothing at all belonging to the intuition of the object, but serve only, according to the law of association of the imagination, and consequently in a subjective respect, as a means of reproduction; of this kind are either words, or visible (algebraic, {{ 256 }} even mimetic) signs, as mere ***expressions*** for concepts[^*].

All intuitions that one subordinates to concepts _a priori_ are therefore either ***schemata*** or ***symbols***, of which the former contain direct, the latter indirect presentations of the concept. The former do this demonstratively, the latter by means of an analogy (for which one also avails oneself of empirical intuitions), in which the power of judgment performs a double business, applying first the concept to the object of a sensible intuition, and then, secondly, the mere rule of reflection on that intuition to an entirely different object, of which the former is only the symbol. Thus a monarchical state is represented by an animate body if it is ruled according to internal laws of the people, but by a mere machine (like a hand mill) if it is ruled by a single absolute will, but in both cases represented only ***symbolically***. For between a despotic state and a hand mill there is indeed no similarity, but there is certainly one between the rules for reflecting on both and their causality. This business has as yet been but little analyzed, much as it {{ 257 }} deserves a deeper investigation; however, this is not the place to dwell upon it. Our language is full of such indirect presentations according to an analogy, whereby the expression contains not the proper schema for the concept, but merely a symbol for reflection. Thus the words ***ground*** (support, basis), ***depending*** (being held from above), whence ***flowing*** (instead of following), ***substance*** (as Locke expresses it: the support of accidents), and countless others are not schematic but symbolic hypotyposes and expressions for concepts, not by means of a direct intuition, but only according to an analogy with it, i.e., the transfer of reflection on {{{ 353 }}} an object of intuition to an entirely different concept, to which perhaps an intuition can never directly correspond. If one may already call a mere mode of representation cognition (which is well permitted if it is a principle not of the theoretical determination of the object, as to what it is in itself, but of the practical, as to what the idea of it should become for us and for the purposive use of it): then all our cognition of God is merely symbolic; and he who takes it to be schematic, with the properties of understanding, will, etc., which demonstrate their objective reality only in world-beings, falls into anthropomorphism, just as he falls into {{ 258 }} deism if he leaves out everything intuitive, whereby absolutely nothing, not even in a practical respect, is cognized.

Now I say: the beautiful is the symbol of the morally good; and also only in this respect (a relation which is natural to everyone, and which everyone also expects of others as a duty) does it please with a claim to the assent of everyone else, whereby the mind is at the same time conscious of a certain refinement and elevation above the mere susceptibility to a pleasure through sensory impressions, and estimates others' worth according to a similar maxim of their power of judgment. This is the ***intelligible***, toward which, as the preceding paragraph indicated, taste looks, with which, namely, even our higher cognitive faculties harmonize, and without which nothing but contradictions would arise between their nature, compared with the claims that taste makes. In this faculty the power of judgment does not see itself subjected to a heteronomy of empirical laws, as is otherwise the case in empirical judging: in respect of the objects of so pure a liking, it gives the law to itself, just as reason does in respect of the faculty of desire; and sees itself referred, both on account of this internal possibility in the subject and on account of the external possibility of a nature agreeing with it, to something in the subject itself and outside of it, {{ 259 }} which is neither nature nor freedom, but which is nevertheless connected with the ground of the latter, namely the supersensible, in which the theoretical faculty is bound together with the practical in a common and unknown way into a unity. We will adduce some points of this analogy, while at the same time not leaving their difference unnoticed.

1) The beautiful pleases ***immediately*** (but only in the reflecting {{{ 354 }}} intuition, not, like morality, in the concept). 2) It pleases ***without any interest*** (the morally good is indeed necessarily connected with an interest, but not with such a one as precedes the judgment on the liking, but rather with one that is only thereby produced). 3) The ***freedom*** of the imagination (thus of the sensibility of our faculty) is represented in the judging of the beautiful as in agreement with the lawfulness of the understanding (in the moral judgment the freedom of the will is thought as the harmony of the latter with itself according to universal laws of reason). 4) The subjective principle of the judging of the beautiful is represented as ***universal***, i.e., valid for everyone, but not knowable through any universal concept (the objective principle of morality is also declared to be universal, i.e., for all subjects, and at the same time for all actions of the same subject, and thereby knowable through a universal concept). Hence {{ 260 }} the moral judgment is not only capable of determinate constitutive principles, but is possible ***only*** through the grounding of maxims on them and their universality.

The regard for this analogy is also common to ordinary understanding; and we often designate beautiful objects of nature or of art by names that seem to ground them on a moral judgment. We call buildings or trees majestic and magnificent, or fields laughing and gay; even colors are called innocent, modest, or tender, because they excite sensations that contain something analogical to the consciousness of a state of mind produced by moral judgments. Taste makes, as it were, the transition from sensible charm to habitual moral interest possible without too violent a leap, by representing the imagination even in its freedom as determinable purposively for the understanding, and by teaching us to find a free liking even in objects of sense without any sensible charm.

[^*]: The intuitive of cognition must be opposed to the discursive (not to the symbolic). The former is then either ***schematic***, by means of ***demonstration***; or ***symbolic***, as representation according to a mere ***analogy***.
