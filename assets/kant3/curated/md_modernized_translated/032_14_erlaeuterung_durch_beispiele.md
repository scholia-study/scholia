## {{ 39 }} § 14. Elucidation by examples

Aesthetic judgments can, just like theoretical (logical) ones, be divided into empirical and pure. The former are those that assert the agreeableness or disagreeableness, the latter those that assert the beauty of an object or of the way in which it is represented; the former are judgments of sense (material aesthetic judgments), while the latter (as formal ones) are alone properly judgments of taste.

{{{ 224 }}} A judgment of taste is therefore pure only insofar as no merely empirical satisfaction is mixed into the ground that determines it. This, however, always happens whenever charm or emotion has a share in the judgment through which something is to be declared beautiful.

Now once again various objections come forward that ultimately make it appear as though charm were not merely a necessary ingredient of beauty, but were even sufficient on its own for a thing to be called beautiful. A mere color, for example the green of a lawn, a mere tone (as distinct from sound and noise), such as that of a violin, is declared beautiful in itself by most people; even though both seem to have as their basis merely the matter of the representations, namely sensation alone, and for that reason would deserve to be called only agreeable {{ 40 }}. Yet at the same time one will notice that the sensations of color, as well as of tone, take themselves to be entitled to count as beautiful only insofar as both are ***pure***; which is a determination that already concerns the form, and is also the only thing about these representations that can be universally communicated with certainty: for the quality of the sensations themselves cannot be assumed to be in agreement across all subjects, and the agreeableness of one color over another, or of the tone of one musical instrument over that of another, can hardly be assumed to be judged in the same way by everyone.

If one assumes with ***Euler*** that the colors are equally rapid beats (_pulsus_) of the ether following one upon another, just as tones are of the air set trembling in sound, and, what is most important, that the mind perceives not merely through sense the effect of this upon the animation of the organ, but also through reflection perceives the regular play of the impressions (and hence the form in the connection of various representations) (of which I have no doubt whatever): then color and tone would not be mere sensations, but already a formal determination of the unity of a manifold of them, and could then also be counted in themselves as beauties.

But the purity of a simple kind of sensation means that its uniformity is not disturbed {{ 41 }} and interrupted by any foreign sensation, and it belongs merely to the form: because in this case one can abstract from the quality of that kind of sensation (whether and which color, or whether and which tone it presents). Hence all simple colors, insofar as they are pure, are held to be beautiful; the mixed ones do not have this {{{ 225 }}} advantage: precisely because, since they are not simple, one has no standard of judging whether they should be called pure or impure.

But as regards the beauty ascribed to an object on account of its form, insofar as, as people suppose, it can even be heightened by charm, this is a common error and one very detrimental to genuine, uncorrupted, well-grounded taste; although it is indeed true that charms may be added alongside the beauty, in order, through the representation of the object, to interest the mind beyond the dry satisfaction and thus to serve as a recommendation for taste and its cultivation, especially when it is still crude and unpracticed. But they really do damage the judgment of taste when they draw attention to themselves as grounds for judging the beauty. For it is so far from being the case that they contribute to it, that rather, as strangers, they must be admitted only with indulgence, and only insofar as they do not disturb that beautiful form, when taste is still weak and unpracticed.

In painting, sculpture, indeed in all the formative arts, in {{ 42 }} architecture and horticulture, insofar as they are fine arts, the ***drawing*** is what is essential, in which not what gratifies in sensation, but merely what pleases through its form, constitutes the basis of all disposition for taste. The colors that illuminate the outline belong to charm; they can indeed enliven the object itself for sensation, but cannot make it worthy of intuition and beautiful: rather, they are for the most part greatly restricted by what the beautiful form requires, and even where charm is admitted, they are ennobled by the former alone.

Every form of the objects of the senses (of the outer as well as, mediately, of the inner) is either ***shape*** or ***play***; in the latter case either a play of shapes (in space, mime and dance), or a mere play of sensations (in time). The ***charm*** of colors, or of the agreeable tones of an instrument, may be added, but the ***drawing*** in the former and the composition in the latter constitute the proper object of the pure judgment of taste; and that the purity of the colors as well as of the tones, or also their manifoldness and their contrast, seems to contribute to the beauty, is not to say that, because they are agreeable in themselves, they thereby furnish, as it were, a homogeneous addition to the satisfaction in the form, {{ 43 }} but rather that they only render this latter more precisely, more determinately, and more completely {{{ 226 }}} intuitable, and moreover enliven the representation through their charm by arousing and sustaining attention on the object itself.

Even what one calls ***decorations*** (Parerga), that is, whatever does not belong internally to the whole representation of the object as a constituent part, but only externally as an addition, and which enlarges the satisfaction of taste, still does this too only through its form: as with the frames of paintings, or the drapery on statues, or the colonnades around magnificent buildings. But if the decoration does not itself consist in the beautiful form, if, like a golden frame, it is applied merely in order to recommend the painting for approval through its charm: then it is called ***adornment*** and detracts from genuine beauty.

***Emotion***, a sensation in which agreeableness is produced only by means of a momentary inhibition followed by a stronger outpouring of the vital force, does not belong to beauty at all. Sublimity (with which the feeling of emotion is bound up), however, requires a standard of judging different from the one that taste takes as its basis; and thus a pure judgment of taste has, as its determining ground, neither charm nor emotion, in a word no sensation, as the matter of the aesthetic judgment.
