## {{ 39 }} § 14. Elucidation by examples

Aesthetic judgments can, just like theoretical (logical) ones, be divided into empirical and pure. The former are those that assert the agreeableness or disagreeableness, the latter those that assert the beauty of an object or of the way in which it is represented; the former are judgments of sense (material aesthetic judgments), while the latter (as formal ones) are alone properly judgments of taste.

{{{ 224 }}} A judgment of taste, accordingly, counts as pure only to the extent that its determining ground has no merely empirical satisfaction blended into it. This, however, always happens whenever charm or emotion has a share in the judgment through which something is to be declared beautiful.

Now once again various objections come forward that ultimately make it appear as though charm were not merely a necessary ingredient of beauty, but were even sufficient on its own for a thing to be called beautiful. Most people pronounce a mere color—say the green of a grassy plot—or a mere tone (to be distinguished from sound and noise), like that of a violin, to be beautiful in its own right; and yet both appear to rest merely upon the matter of the representations, that is, upon nothing but sensation, and would on that account merit being termed no more than agreeable {{ 40 }}. One will nevertheless observe at the same time that color-sensations no less than tone-sensations regard themselves as warranted in passing for beautiful only to the degree that each is ***pure***—a specification that bears already on the form, and moreover the sole feature of such representations that admits of certain and universal communication: for the qualitative character of the sensations cannot be presupposed as concordant in every subject, nor can it readily be granted that the agreeableness of one color above another, or of the tone of one musical instrument above that of a different one, is appraised alike by all.

Suppose, with ***Euler***, that the colors are simultaneous beats (_pulsus_) of the ether succeeding one another, as tones are of the air made to vibrate in sound, and—what matters most—that the mind apprehends not only, by way of sense, their effect upon the enlivening of the organ, but also, by way of reflection, the orderly play of the impressions (and thus the form in the combining of diverse representations) (something I do not in the least doubt): then color and tone would be, not bare sensations, but would already constitute a formal determination of a manifold's unity among them, and could accordingly be reckoned as beauties in their own right.

The purity of a simple mode of sensation, however, signifies that no alien sensation troubles {{ 41 }} or breaks in upon its uniformity, and such purity pertains to the form alone: for here one may leave out of account the quality of that mode of sensation (whether, and which, color it presents, or whether, and which, tone). For this reason every simple color, provided it is pure, passes for beautiful, whereas the mixed ones lack this {{{ 225 }}} distinction: exactly because, not being simple, they leave us without any measure for deciding whether to call them pure or impure.

As for the beauty attributed to an object because of its form, in respect of which people fancy that charm might actually enhance it, this is a widespread mistake, and one gravely damaging to taste that is genuine, incorruptible, and thorough; even though charms may, to be sure, be joined to the beauty besides, so as to interest the mind, through the object's representation, beyond the dry satisfaction, and thereby to commend taste together with its cultivation, above all while it is still raw and untrained. They do, however, genuinely detract from the judgment of taste once they attract notice to themselves as grounds for appraising the beauty. For it is so far from being the case that they contribute to it, that rather, as strangers, they must be admitted only with indulgence, and only insofar as they do not disturb that beautiful form, when taste is still weak and unpracticed.

In painting, in sculpture, indeed throughout all the plastic arts, in {{ 42 }} architecture and in gardening, so far as these count as fine arts, it is the ***drawing*** that is essential, wherein not that which delights in sensation, but merely that which is pleasing through its form, constitutes the ground of every arrangement directed at taste. The colors that light up the sketch fall under charm; while they can, for sensation, quicken the object in itself, they cannot render it beautiful and worthy of intuition: on the contrary, whatever the beautiful form demands mostly confines them very severely, and even in cases where charm is permitted, it is by that form alone that they are ennobled.

All form belonging to the objects of the senses (those of the outer, and, mediately, those of the inner as well) is either ***shape*** or ***play***; and in the latter case it is either a play of shapes (in space: mimicry and the dance) or a mere play of sensations (in time). The ***charm*** of the colors, or of the pleasant tones of the instrument, may indeed be superadded, yet it is the ***drawing*** in the first case and the composition in the second that make up the genuine object of a pure judgment of taste; and if the purity of the colors and of the tones alike, or even their variety and their contrast, seems to add to the beauty, this does not mean that, being agreeable on their own, they supply thereby a kind of like-natured supplement to the satisfaction in the form, {{ 43 }} but rather that they only make this latter more exactly, more definitely, and more fully {{{ 226 }}} intuitable, and besides enliven the representation by their charm, in that they awaken and hold attention upon the object itself.

Even what one calls ***decorations*** (Parerga), that is, whatever does not belong internally to the whole representation of the object as a constituent part, but only externally as an addition, and which enlarges the satisfaction of taste, still does this too only through its form: as with the frames of paintings, or the drapery on statues, or the colonnades around magnificent buildings. But should the decoration not consist in the beautiful form itself—should it, like a gilt frame, be attached merely so as to commend the painting to approval by means of its charm—then it goes by the name of ***adornment*** and does injury to genuine beauty.

***Emotion***—a sensation in which agreeableness comes about solely through a momentary checking, and the more forceful surge of the vital force that thereupon follows—has no place whatever in beauty. Sublimity, on the other hand—the feeling of emotion being bound up with it—calls for a criterion of appraisal other than the one taste lays down as its foundation; and so a pure judgment of taste possesses, for its determining ground, neither a charm nor an emotion—in short, no sensation of any kind—as the matter of the aesthetic judgment.
