## {{ 39 }} § 14. Elucidation by means of examples

Aesthetic judgments can, just like theoretical (logical) ones, be divided into empirical and pure. The former are those which assert agreeableness or disagreeableness, the latter those which assert beauty of an object or of its way of being represented; the former are judgments of sense (material aesthetic judgments), the latter (as formal) alone are proper judgments of taste.

{{{ 224 }}} A judgment of taste is thus pure only insofar as no merely empirical satisfaction is mixed in with its determining ground. But this always happens when charm or emotion has a share in the judgment through which something is to be declared beautiful.

Now once again several objections come forward, which in the end make it appear that charm is not merely a necessary ingredient of beauty, but is even sufficient by itself alone to be called beautiful. A mere color, e.g., the green of a lawn, a mere tone (as distinguished from sound and noise), like that of a violin, is declared by most to be beautiful in itself; although both seem to have merely the matter of representations, namely sensation alone, at their ground, and for that reason would deserve to be called only agreeable {{ 40 }}. Yet one will at the same time notice that the sensations of color as well as of tone hold themselves justified in counting as beautiful only insofar as both are ***pure***; which is a determination that already concerns the form, and is also the only thing about these representations that can with certainty be universally communicated: because the quality of the sensations themselves cannot be assumed to be unanimous in all subjects, and the agreeableness of one color in preference to another, or of the tone of one musical instrument in preference to that of another, can hardly be assumed to be judged in the same way by everyone.

If one assumes with ***Euler*** that colors are simultaneous, mutually succeeding beats (_pulsus_) of the ether, just as tones are beats of the air agitated in sound, and, what is most important, that the mind perceives the effect of this on the animation of the organ not merely through the sense but also through reflection perceives the regular play of the impressions (hence the form in the combination of various representations) (which I do not at all doubt): then color and tone would not be mere sensations but already a formal determination of the unity of a manifold of them, and then could also be counted as beauties in themselves.

But the purity of a simple kind of sensation means that the uniformity of it is disturbed and interrupted by no foreign sensation {{ 41 }}, and belongs merely to the form: because thereby one can abstract from the quality of that kind of sensation (whether and which color, or whether and which tone it represents). Hence all simple colors, insofar as they are pure, are held to be beautiful; the mixed ones do not have this {{{ 225 }}} advantage: precisely because, since they are not simple, one has no standard of judging whether one should call them pure or impure.

But as concerns the beauty attributed to an object on account of its form, insofar as it can, as one supposes, even be heightened through charm, this is a common error and very detrimental to genuine, uncorrupted, well-grounded taste; although, to be sure, charms can also be added to beauty in order to interest the mind through the representation of the object beyond the dry satisfaction and thus to serve to recommend taste and its cultivation, especially when it is still raw and unpracticed. But they really do detriment to the judgment of taste if they draw attention to themselves as grounds for judging beauty. For it is so far from being the case that they contribute to it that they must rather, as foreigners, be admitted only with indulgence, insofar as they do not disturb that beautiful form, when taste is still weak and unpracticed.

In painting, sculpture, indeed in all the pictorial arts, in {{ 42 }} architecture and horticulture, insofar as they are fine arts, the ***drawing*** is what is essential, in which it is not what gratifies in sensation but merely what pleases through its form that constitutes the ground of all aptitude for taste. The colors that illuminate the outline belong to charm; the object in itself they can indeed enliven for sensation, but cannot make it worthy of intuition and beautiful: rather, they are for the most part very much restricted by what the beautiful form requires, and even where charm is admitted, they are ennobled by the former alone.

All form of the objects of the senses (of the outer as well as, mediately, of the inner) is either ***shape*** or ***play***; in the latter case either play of shapes (in space, mimic art and dance) or mere play of sensations (in time). The ***charm*** of colors, or of agreeable tones of an instrument, can be added, but the ***drawing*** in the former and the composition in the latter constitute the proper object of the pure judgment of taste; and that the purity of the colors as well as of the tones, or also their manifoldness and their contrast, seems to contribute to beauty does not mean so much that they, because they are agreeable in themselves, supply as it were a homogeneous addition to the satisfaction in the form, {{ 43 }} but rather that they only make the latter more precise, more determinate, and more complete {{{ 226 }}} in intuition, and besides enliven the representation through their charm by arousing and maintaining the attention to the object itself.

Even what one calls ***ornaments*** (parerga), i.e., that which does not belong internally to the whole representation of the object as a constituent part, but only externally as an addition and enlarges the satisfaction of taste, does this too only through its form: like the frames of paintings, or the drapery on statues, or the colonnades around magnificent buildings. But if the ornament does not itself consist in the beautiful form, if it is merely attached, like the golden frame, to recommend the painting to approval through its charm: then it is called ***finery*** and does detriment to genuine beauty.

***Emotion***, a sensation in which agreeableness is effected only by means of a momentary inhibition followed by a stronger outpouring of the vital force, does not belong to beauty at all. Sublimity (with which the feeling of emotion is connected), however, requires a different standard of judging than that which taste lays at its ground; and so a pure judgment of taste has neither charm nor emotion, in a word no sensation, as the matter of the aesthetic judgment, for its determining ground.
