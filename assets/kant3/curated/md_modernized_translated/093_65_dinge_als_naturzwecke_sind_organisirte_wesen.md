## {{ 289 }} § 65. Things as Natural Ends Are Organized Beings

According to the character adduced in the preceding section, a thing which is to be cognized as a natural product and yet at the same time as possible only as a natural end must relate to itself reciprocally as cause and effect, which is a somewhat improper and indeterminate expression that requires derivation from a definite concept.

The causal connection, insofar as it is thought merely by the understanding, is a connection that constitutes a series (of causes and effects) which always goes downward; and the things themselves which, as effects, presuppose others as causes, cannot conversely be at the same time causes of the latter. This causal connection is called that of efficient causes (_nexus effectivus_). But against this, a causal connection can nonetheless also be thought in accordance with a concept of reason (of ends), which, if one regarded it as a series, would carry with it a dependency both downward and upward, in which the thing which is at one time designated as an effect nonetheless upwardly deserves the name of a cause of that very thing of which it is the effect. In the practical sphere (namely, of art) one easily finds such a connection, as, e.g., the house indeed is the cause of the money that is received for rent {{ 290 }}, but yet conversely the representation of this possible income was the cause of the building of the house. Such a causal connection is called that of final causes (_nexus finalis_). One could perhaps more suitably call the former the connection of {{{ 373 }}} real, the latter that of ideal causes, because by this designation it is at the same time understood that there can be no more than these two kinds of causality.

For a thing as a natural end there is now required, ***firstly***, that its parts (as regards their existence and their form) are possible only through their relation to the whole. For the thing itself is an end, consequently comprehended under a concept or an idea that must determine _a priori_ everything that is to be contained in it. But insofar as a thing is thought of as possible only in this way, it is merely a work of art, i.e., the product of a rational cause distinct from its matter (the parts), whose causality (in the procurement and connection of the parts) is determined by its idea of a whole possible thereby (consequently not by nature outside it).

But if a thing as a natural product is nonetheless to contain in itself and its inner possibility a relation to ends, i.e., to be possible only as a natural end and without the causality of concepts of rational beings outside it, then there is required, ***secondly***: that its parts {{ 291 }} combine into the unity of a whole by being reciprocally cause and effect of their form. For in such a way alone is it possible that, conversely (reciprocally), the idea of the whole should in turn determine the form and connection of all the parts: not as cause — for then it would be a product of art —, but as the ground of cognition of the systematic unity of the form and connection of all the manifold that is contained in the given matter, for the one who judges it.

For a body, therefore, which is to be judged in itself and in accordance with its inner possibility as a natural end, it is required that its parts reciprocally produce one another, as much in their form as in their connection, and thus produce a whole from their own causality, the concept of which in turn, conversely (in a being that possessed the causality according to concepts adequate to such a product), could be the cause of the product in accordance with a principle, so that the connection of ***efficient causes*** could at the same time be judged as an ***effect through final causes***.

In such a product of nature, each part is thought as existing not only ***through*** all the remaining parts, but also as existing ***for the sake of*** the others and of the whole, i.e., as an instrument (organ): which {{{ 374 }}} however is not enough (for it could also be an instrument of art and {{ 292 }} thus be represented as possible only as an end in general); but rather as an organ that ***produces*** the other parts (consequently each the other reciprocally), of which kind no instrument of art can be, but only of nature which supplies all material for instruments (even those of art): and only then and for this reason can such a product, as an _organized_ and ***self-organizing*** being, be called a ***natural end***.

In a watch one part is the instrument of the motion of the others, but one wheel is not the efficient cause of the production of another; one part is indeed there for the sake of the other, but not through it. Hence also the producing cause of the watch and of its form is contained not in nature (of this matter), but outside it in a being that can act according to ideas of a whole possible through its causality. Hence also one wheel in the watch does not produce another, still less does one watch produce other watches, in such a way that it used other matter for this (organized it); hence it also does not of itself replace its stolen parts, or compensate for their deficiency in the first formation by the accession of the remaining parts, or repair itself, as it were, when it has got out of order: all of which, on the contrary, we can expect from organized nature. — An organized being is therefore not merely a machine: for the latter has solely ***motive*** force; rather it possesses {{ 293 }} in itself ***formative*** force, and indeed such a force as it communicates to materials which do not have it (organizes them): thus a self-propagating formative force, which cannot be explained by the power of motion alone (mechanism).

One says far too little of nature and its faculty in organized products when one calls the latter an ***analogue*** of ***art***; for there one thinks of the artist (a rational being) as outside it. Rather, it organizes itself, and in every species of its organized products, indeed according to one and the same archetype on the whole, but yet also with suitable deviations which self-preservation requires according to circumstances. One comes closer perhaps to this inscrutable property if one calls it an ***analogue of life***: but then one must either endow matter as mere matter with a property (hylozoism) that conflicts with its essence; or associate with it an alien principle ***standing in community*** with it (a soul): {{{ 375 }}} for which, however, if such a product is to be a natural product, one either already presupposes organized matter as an instrument of that soul and thus does not make the latter in the least more comprehensible, or else must make the soul the artist of this structure and so withdraw the product from nature (bodily nature). Strictly speaking, the {{ 294 }} organization of nature has therefore nothing analogous to any causality that we know[^*]. Beauty of nature, because it is attributed to objects only in relation to reflection on the ***outer*** intuition of them, and consequently only on account of the form of the surface, can with right be called an analogue of art. But ***inner natural perfection***, such as is possessed by those things which are possible only as ***natural ends*** and are therefore called organized beings, is not, according to any analogy with any physical, i.e., natural power known to us, thinkable and explainable — indeed, since we ourselves belong to nature in the widest sense, not even through a precisely adequate analogy with human art.

The concept of a thing as in itself a natural end is therefore not a constitutive concept of the understanding or of reason, but can nonetheless be a regulative concept for the reflecting power of judgment, in order to guide research into objects of this kind, according to a {{ 295 }} remote analogy with our causality in accordance with ends in general, and to reflect on their supreme ground; the latter indeed not for the sake of cognition of nature or of its original ground, but rather of that very same practical faculty of reason in us with which we compared the cause of that purposiveness in analogy.

Organized beings are therefore the only ones in nature which, even if regarded in themselves and without a relation to other things, must nonetheless be thought of as possible only as its ends, and {{{ 376 }}} which thus first provide objective reality to the concept of an ***end*** that is not practical but is an end ***of nature***, and thereby provide for natural science the ground for a teleology, i.e., a mode of judging its objects in accordance with a special principle, to introduce which into it one would otherwise not be in the least justified (since one cannot in the least see _a priori_ the possibility of such a kind of causality).

[^*]: One can, conversely, throw light upon a certain connection, which however is also met with more in idea than in reality, through an analogy with the aforementioned immediate natural ends. Thus, in a recently undertaken complete transformation of a great people into a state, the word ***organization*** has frequently been very suitably employed for the establishment of magistracies, etc., and even of the entire body politic. For in such a whole, each member should indeed be not merely a means but at the same time also an end, and, while co-operating in the possibility of the whole, should in turn be determined as regards its place and function by the idea of the whole.
