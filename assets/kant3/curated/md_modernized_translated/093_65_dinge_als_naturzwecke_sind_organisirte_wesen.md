## {{ 289 }} § 65. Things as natural ends are organized beings

By the character adduced in the foregoing §, a thing meant to be cognized as a natural product while nonetheless being possible solely as a natural end must relate itself to itself reciprocally as cause and effect — an expression somewhat improper and indeterminate, one that calls for derivation from a determinate concept.

So far as it is conceived merely by means of the understanding, the causal connection is a linkage constituting a series (of causes and effects) that proceeds ever downward; and the very things that, being effects, presuppose others as cause cannot at the same time serve reciprocally as cause of those. This causal connection is called that of efficient causes (_nexus effectivus_). Yet on the other hand a causal connection may likewise be conceived according to a concept of reason (that of ends), a connection which, taken as a series, would bring with it dependency in both directions, downward and upward alike, such that the thing once labeled an effect still, in the upward direction, merits being called the cause of the very thing whose effect it is. Within the practical domain (that is, in art) such a linkage is readily met with — the house, for example, being indeed the cause of the monies received {{ 290 }} as rent, while conversely the representation of this prospective income was itself the cause of the house's construction. Such a causal linkage is called that of final causes (_nexus finalis_). One might perhaps more fittingly call the former the linkage of {{{ 373 }}} real causes, and the second that of ideal causes, since this naming at once makes it understood that causality admits of no more than these two kinds.

For a thing considered as a natural end it is now demanded, ***first of all***, that its parts—in respect of their existence as well as their form—be possible solely through the way they bear on the whole. For the thing is itself an end, and is therefore grasped under a concept or idea that has to determine _a priori_ all that is meant to be contained within it. But so far as a thing is held to be possible in this manner alone, it amounts to no more than a work of art, that is, the product of a rational cause set apart from that thing's matter (its parts), a cause whose causality (in gathering and joining the parts) is determined by the idea it possesses of a whole thereby rendered possible (and thus not by the nature outside it).

Should a thing, however, as a natural product, nonetheless harbor in itself and in its inner possibility a bearing upon ends—that is, to be possible solely as a natural end and apart from any causality of the concepts belonging to rational beings outside it—then what is demanded ***in the second place*** is this: that its parts {{ 291 }} bind themselves into the unity of a whole precisely by being, each toward the other, reciprocally cause and effect of their form. For only in this manner does it become possible that, conversely (reciprocally), the idea of the whole should in its turn determine how all the parts are formed and combined: not in the capacity of a cause—since then the thing would be a product of art—but rather, for whoever appraises it, as the ground of cognition for the systematic unity belonging to the form and joining of all the manifold that the given matter contains.

For a body, then, that is meant to be appraised in itself and by its inner possibility as a natural end, it is requisite that its parts should, all together and in respect of their form as much as their combination, bring one another forth reciprocally and so generate a whole from a causality of their own, the concept of which would in turn, conversely (within a being that possessed a causality according to concepts suited to such a product), be the cause of that same whole according to a principle, so that the connection of ***efficient causes*** might at once be appraised as an ***effect through final causes***.

Within such a product of nature every part, in the same way that it is there only ***through*** all the others, is likewise conceived as existing ***for the others'*** and for the whole's ***sake***, that is, as a tool (organ): which {{{ 374 }}} is nevertheless not sufficient (for it might equally be a tool of art and {{ 292 }} so be represented as possible merely as an end in general); rather, it must be conceived as an organ ***producing*** the remaining parts (and thus each one the others reciprocally), of a kind that can be no tool of art but belongs only to the nature that supplies all the material for tools (even those of art): and only then, and for that reason, may a product of this kind—an _organized_ being that ***organizes itself***—receive the name of a ***natural end***.

In a clock one part is the instrument for the movement of the others, but no one wheel is the efficient cause of the production of another; one part is indeed there for the sake of another, yet not through it. Hence the cause that produces the clock and its form is likewise not contained in nature (in this matter) but outside it, in a being that can act according to ideas of a whole made possible through its causality. For this reason, again, one wheel in the clock does not bring about another, and still less does a clock bring forth other clocks by putting other matter to that use (organizing it); nor, accordingly, does it of its own accord make up for the parts stripped away from it, or compensate for a shortcoming in its initial formation through the joining-in of the rest, or set itself to rights, as it were, once it has lapsed into disorder: all of which, on the contrary, we are entitled to look for from organized nature. — An organized being is therefore not merely a machine: for a machine has solely ***moving*** power; rather, it possesses {{ 293 }} within itself ***formative*** power, and indeed a power of such a kind that it imparts it to the materials that do not have it (organizing them): thus a self-propagating formative power that cannot be explained by the capacity for motion alone (mechanism).

Of nature and of its power in organized products one says all too little in terming this an ***analogue*** of ***art***; for in doing so one pictures the artist (a rational being) as lying beyond it. Rather, nature organizes itself, and in each species of its organized products, according to a single pattern on the whole, yet also with suitable deviations that self-preservation requires as circumstances demand. One draws nearer, perhaps, to this unfathomable property by naming it an ***analogue of life***: but here one must either bestow on matter, taken as mere matter, a property (hylozoism) that runs counter to its very essence; or link to it a principle of foreign kind ***standing in community*** with it (namely a soul): {{{ 375 }}} yet for this, should such a product be meant to be a product of nature, then one either takes organized matter for granted beforehand as the tool of that soul—so rendering that soul not a whit more intelligible—or else is obliged to turn the soul into the artisan of this edifice, thereby wresting the product away from nature (from the corporeal). Strictly speaking, then, the {{ 294 }} organization of nature has nothing analogous to any causality that we know[^*]. Nature's beauty, since it is attributed to objects only in relation to the reflection upon their ***outer*** intuition, and hence merely on account of the form of the surface, may with justice be termed an analogue of art. ***Inner natural perfection***, however, of the sort that belongs to those things which are possible solely as ***natural ends*** and are for that reason termed organized beings, can, on the analogy of no physical—that is, natural—capacity known to us, and indeed, seeing that we ourselves belong within nature taken in the widest sense, be rendered thinkable and explicable not even through a precisely fitting analogy with human art.

Accordingly, the concept of a thing that is in itself a natural end is no constitutive concept, whether of the understanding or of reason, and yet it may nonetheless function, for the reflecting power of judgment, as a regulative concept, serving—through a {{ 295 }} distant analogy to our own causality in accordance with ends generally—to guide the inquiry into objects of this sort and to prompt reflection upon their highest ground; this latter aim being, to be sure, directed not toward a knowledge of nature, nor toward that primal ground of it, but rather toward that very practical faculty of reason within us, by analogy with which we had been regarding the cause of that purposiveness.

Organized beings, then, are the sole ones in nature that—even taken by themselves and apart from any relation to other things—must all the same be conceived as possible only as ends of nature, and {{{ 376 }}} that are consequently the first to secure objective reality for the concept of an ***end*** that is not practical but rather an end ***of nature***, and in this way to furnish natural science with a foundation for a teleology, that is, for a manner of appraising its objects by a special principle—one that, apart from this, it would be flatly unwarranted to bring into it (since the possibility of such a species of causality can in no way be discerned _a priori_).

[^*]: Conversely, one can shed light on a certain combination — one that, however, is met with more in the idea than in reality — by an analogy with the immediate natural ends just mentioned. Thus, in the lately undertaken thoroughgoing transformation of a great people into a single state, the term ***organization*** has frequently been put to very apt use for the establishing of the magistracies, and so on, and even of the state's entire body. For within such a whole each member ought, to be sure, to be not a mere means but at once an end as well, and, by cooperating toward the possibility of the whole, to be in turn determined, as regards its place and function, through the idea of the whole.
