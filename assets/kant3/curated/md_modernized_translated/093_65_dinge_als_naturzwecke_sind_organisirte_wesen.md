## {{ 289 }} § 65. Things as natural ends are organized beings

According to the character set out in the preceding §, a thing that is to be recognized as a product of nature and yet at the same time as possible only as a natural end must stand toward itself reciprocally as both cause and effect, which is a somewhat improper and indeterminate expression that requires derivation from a definite concept.

The causal connection, insofar as it is thought merely through the understanding, is a linkage that constitutes a series (of causes and effects) which always proceeds downward; and the things themselves, which as effects presuppose others as their cause, cannot in turn be at the same time the cause of these. This causal connection is called that of efficient causes (_nexus effectivus_). By contrast, however, a causal connection can also be thought in accordance with a concept of reason (of ends), one that, if it were regarded as a series, would carry with it dependence both downward and upward, in which the thing that has once been designated an effect nonetheless, going upward, deserves the name of a cause of that thing of which it is the effect. In the practical sphere (namely in art) one readily finds such a linkage, as for instance the house is indeed the cause of the monies that are taken in {{ 290 }} as rent, yet conversely the representation of this possible income was likewise the cause of the building of the house. Such a causal linkage is called that of final causes (_nexus finalis_). One might perhaps more fittingly call the former the linkage of {{{ 373 }}} real causes, and the second that of ideal causes, since this naming at once makes it understood that causality admits of no more than these two kinds.

For a thing as a natural end there is now required, ***in the first place***, that its parts (as regards both their existence and their form) are possible only through their relation to the whole. For the thing itself is an end, and is consequently comprehended under a concept or an idea that must determine _a priori_ everything that is to be contained in it. Yet insofar as a thing is thought as possible only in this way, it is merely a work of art, i.e., the product of a rational cause distinct from the matter (the parts) of that thing, whose causality (in procuring and combining the parts) is determined by its idea of a whole thereby made possible (and hence not by the nature external to it).

But if a thing, as a product of nature, is nonetheless to contain in itself and in its inner possibility a relation to ends, i.e., to be possible only as a natural end and without the causality of the concepts of rational beings external to it, then there is required, ***in the second place***, that its parts {{ 291 }} combine into the unity of a whole in such a way that they are reciprocally cause and effect of one another's form. For only in this way is it possible that, conversely (reciprocally), the idea of the whole in turn determines the form and combination of all the parts: not as a cause — for then it would be a product of art — but as the ground of cognition, for the one who judges it, of the systematic unity of the form and combination of everything manifold that is contained in the given matter.

For a body, therefore, that is to be judged in itself and according to its inner possibility as a natural end, it is required that its parts, in respect of both their form and their combination, reciprocally produce one another all together and thus bring forth a whole out of their own causality, whose concept could in turn conversely (in a being possessing a causality according to concepts adequate to such a product) be the cause of that whole in accordance with a principle, so that the linkage of ***efficient causes*** could at the same time be judged as an ***effect through final causes***.

In such a product of nature each part is thought, just as it exists only ***through*** all the rest, so also as existing ***for the others'*** and the whole's ***sake***, i.e., as an instrument (organ): which {{{ 374 }}} is, however, not enough (for it could also be an instrument of art and {{ 292 }} thus be represented as possible merely as an end in general); rather, it must be thought as an organ ***producing*** the other parts (and hence each one reciprocally the others), such as can be no instrument of art but only of the nature that furnishes all the material for instruments (even those of art): and only then and for that reason will it be possible to call such a product, as an _organized_ and ***self-organizing*** being, a ***natural end***.

In a clock one part is the instrument for the movement of the others, but no one wheel is the efficient cause of the production of another; one part is indeed there for the sake of another, yet not through it. Hence the cause that produces the clock and its form is likewise not contained in nature (in this matter) but outside it, in a being that can act according to ideas of a whole made possible through its causality. Hence, too, no one wheel in the clock produces another, still less does one clock produce other clocks by using other matter for that purpose (organizing it); hence it also does not of itself replace parts that have been taken from it, or make good a deficiency in its original formation by the accession of the others, or repair itself, as it were, when it has fallen into disorder: all of which, by contrast, we can expect of organized nature. — An organized being is therefore not merely a machine: for a machine has solely ***moving*** power; rather, it possesses {{ 293 }} within itself ***formative*** power, and indeed a power of such a kind that it imparts it to the materials that do not have it (organizing them): thus a self-propagating formative power that cannot be explained by the capacity for motion alone (mechanism).

One says far too little of nature and its capacity in organized products when one calls this an ***analogue*** of ***art***; for in that case one thinks of the artist (a rational being) as external to it. Rather, nature organizes itself, and in each species of its organized products, according to a single pattern on the whole, yet also with suitable deviations that self-preservation requires as circumstances demand. One comes perhaps somewhat closer to this inscrutable property when one calls it an ***analogue of life***: but then one must either endow matter, as mere matter, with a property (hylozoism) that contradicts its essence; or associate with it an alien principle ***standing in community*** with it (a soul): {{{ 375 }}} for which, however, if such a product is to be a product of nature, one either already presupposes organized matter as the instrument of that soul, and so does not make the soul in the least more comprehensible, or else must make the soul the artificer of this structure and thereby withdraw the product from nature (from corporeal nature). Strictly speaking, then, the {{ 294 }} organization of nature has nothing analogous to any causality that we know[^*]. Beauty of nature, since it is ascribed to objects only in relation to reflection upon the ***outer*** intuition of them, and hence only on account of the form of the surface, can with justice be called an analogue of art. But ***inner natural perfection***, of the kind possessed by those things which are possible only as ***natural ends*** and for that reason are called organized beings, is, on the analogy of no physical, i.e., natural, capacity known to us, and indeed, since we ourselves belong to nature in the widest sense, not even by means of a precisely fitting analogy with human art, either thinkable or explicable.

The concept of a thing as in itself a natural end is therefore no constitutive concept of the understanding or of reason, yet it can still be a regulative concept for the reflecting power of judgment, serving, by a {{ 295 }} remote analogy with our own causality according to ends in general, to guide the investigation of objects of this kind and to reflect on their supreme ground; the latter, to be sure, not for the sake of knowledge of nature, or of that primordial ground of it, but rather for the sake of that very same practical faculty of reason in us by analogy with which we were considering the cause of that purposiveness.

Organized beings are therefore the only ones in nature that, even when considered on their own and without a relation to other things, must nonetheless be thought as possible only as ends of nature, and {{{ 376 }}} that are thus the first to confer objective reality on the concept of an ***end*** which is not a practical end but an end ***of nature***, and thereby to provide natural science with the ground for a teleology, i.e., for a way of judging its objects according to a special principle, such as one would otherwise be utterly unentitled to introduce into it (since one cannot at all have _a priori_ insight into the possibility of such a kind of causality).

[^*]: Conversely, one can shed light on a certain combination — one that, however, is met with more in the idea than in reality — by an analogy with the immediate natural ends just mentioned. Thus, in the recently undertaken complete reconstruction of a great people into a state, the word ***organization*** has often been very aptly employed for the ordering of the magistracies, and so forth, and even of the whole body of the state. For in such a whole each member should of course be not merely a means but at the same time also an end, and, in contributing to the possibility of the whole, should in turn be determined as to its place and function by the idea of the whole.
