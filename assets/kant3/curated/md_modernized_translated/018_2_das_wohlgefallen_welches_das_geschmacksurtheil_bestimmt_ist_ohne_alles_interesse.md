## § 2. The Satisfaction that Determines the Judgment of Taste is without any Interest

Interest is the name for the satisfaction that we combine with the representation of the existence of an object. Such a satisfaction therefore always has at the same time a relation to the faculty of desire, either as its determining ground or at least as necessarily connected with its determining ground. Now, however, when the question is whether something is beautiful, one does not want to know whether anything depends, or even could depend, for us or anyone else on the existence of the thing; but rather how we appraise it in mere contemplation (intuition or reflection). If someone asks me whether I find the palace that I see before me {{ 6 }} beautiful, I may well say: I do not love things of that kind, which are made merely for gaping at, or, like that Iroquois ***sachem***, that nothing in Paris pleased him better than the cook-shops; I may furthermore inveigh, in good ***Rousseauian*** fashion, against the vanity of the great who expend the sweat of the people on such dispensable things; I may {{{ 205 }}} finally quite easily convince myself that, if I found myself on an uninhabited island without any hope of ever coming again among human beings, and could conjure up such a magnificent edifice by my mere wish, I would not even give myself this trouble for it, if I already had a hut that was comfortable enough for me. One can grant me all this and approve of it; only that is not now what is at issue. One wants to know only whether the mere representation of the object in me is accompanied with satisfaction, however indifferent I may always be with regard to the existence of the object of this representation. One readily sees that it depends on what I make of this representation in myself, not on that in which I am dependent on the existence of the object, in order to say that it is ***beautiful*** and to prove that I have taste. Everyone must admit that the judgment about beauty in which the least interest is mingled is very partial and is not a pure judgment of taste. One must not be in the least biased in favor of the existence of the thing, but rather be entirely indifferent {{ 7 }} in this respect, in order to play the judge in matters of taste.

We cannot, however, better elucidate this proposition, which is of preeminent importance, than by contrasting the pure, disinterested[^*] satisfaction in the judgment of taste with that which is combined with interest: especially if we can at the same time be certain that there are no more kinds of interest than those that are just now to be named.

[^*]: A judgment about an object of satisfaction can be entirely ***disinterested*** yet still very ***interesting***, i.e., it is not grounded on any interest, but it produces an interest; of this sort are all pure moral judgments. But judgments of taste in themselves also ground no interest at all. Only in society does it become ***interesting*** to have taste, the ground of which will be indicated in what follows.
