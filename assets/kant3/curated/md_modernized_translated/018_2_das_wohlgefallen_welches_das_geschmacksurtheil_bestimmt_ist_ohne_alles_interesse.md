## § 2. The satisfaction which determines the judgment of taste is devoid of all interest

That satisfaction which we link to the representation of an object's existence is called interest. Such a satisfaction accordingly always relates as well to the faculty of desire, either as the ground determining it or at least as necessarily bound up with what determines it. Yet when the question arises whether something is beautiful, one does not wish to know whether the existence of the thing is of any concern, or could even be of concern, to us or to anyone at all; but rather how we judge it in mere contemplation (intuition or reflection). Should anyone ask me whether the palace standing before my {{ 6 }} eyes strikes me as beautiful, I might well answer that I care nothing for such structures, built solely to be stared at, or, in the manner of that Iroquois ***sachem***, that in Paris no place delighted him more than the eating-houses; beyond this I could, thoroughly in the ***Rousseauian*** vein, decry the vanity of the great, who lavish the people's sweat upon things so needless; and I might {{{ 205 }}} in the end persuade myself readily enough that, cast away on some uninhabited isle with no prospect of ever rejoining humankind, and able by a mere wish to summon such a splendid edifice into being, I would not stir myself even that much, provided I already possessed a hut affording me sufficient comfort. One may grant me all of this and give it approval; yet none of that is now the point under discussion. All one seeks to know is whether the mere representation of the object is accompanied within me by satisfaction, however indifferent I might remain regarding the existence of the object that this representation concerns. It is readily apparent that, when I declare it ***beautiful*** and demonstrate my possession of taste, everything turns on what I myself draw from this representation, rather than on that in which I remain dependent upon the object's existence. Everyone is bound to concede that any verdict upon beauty tainted by the slightest interest is thoroughly partial and no pure judgment of taste. To sit in judgment on questions of taste, one must harbor not the slightest partiality toward the thing's existence, but must remain wholly indifferent {{ 7 }} in this regard.

This proposition, however, being of outstanding importance, can be clarified in no better way than by setting the pure, disinterested[^*] satisfaction in the judgment of taste against that which is combined with interest: above all when we may at the same time be assured that there exist no further kinds of interest beyond those that are just now to be named.

[^*]: A judgment about an object of satisfaction can be entirely ***disinterested*** and yet very ***interesting***, i.e. it is grounded on no interest, but it brings forth an interest; of this kind are all pure moral judgments. But judgments of taste in themselves also give rise to no interest whatsoever. It is ***interesting*** to possess taste only within society, and the reason for this will be shown in what comes after.
