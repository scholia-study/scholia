## § 2. The satisfaction which determines the judgment of taste is devoid of all interest

That satisfaction which we link to the representation of an object's existence is called interest. Such a satisfaction accordingly always relates as well to the faculty of desire, either as the ground determining it or at least as necessarily bound up with what determines it. Yet when the question arises whether something is beautiful, one does not wish to know whether the existence of the thing is of any concern, or could even be of concern, to us or to anyone at all; but rather how we judge it in mere contemplation (intuition or reflection). If someone asks me whether I find the palace that I see before me {{ 6 }} beautiful, I may indeed reply that I have no love for such things, which are made merely to be gawked at, or, like that Iroquois ***sachem***, that nothing in Paris pleased him better than the cook-shops; I may moreover, in good ***Rousseauian*** fashion, rail against the vanity of the great, who squander the sweat of the people on such dispensable things; I may {{{ 205 }}} finally convince myself quite easily that, were I to find myself on an uninhabited island with no hope of ever returning to human society, and could conjure up such a magnificent building by my mere wish, I would not even take that trouble over it, so long as I already had a hut that was comfortable enough for me. All of this may be conceded to me and approved; but that is not what is now at issue. One wants only to know whether the object's mere representation is attended in me by satisfaction, no matter how indifferent I may be toward the existence of the object of this representation. It is easy to see that, in order to call it ***beautiful*** and to prove that I have taste, what matters is what I make of this representation within myself, and not that whereby I depend upon the object's existence. Everyone must admit that a judgment about beauty into which the least interest enters is highly partial and no pure judgment of taste. One must not be in the least biased in favor of the existence of the thing, but must in this respect be wholly indifferent {{ 7 }}, in order to play the judge in matters of taste.

This proposition, however, being of outstanding importance, can be clarified in no better way than by setting the pure, disinterested[^*] satisfaction in the judgment of taste against that which is combined with interest: above all when we may at the same time be assured that there exist no further kinds of interest beyond those that are just now to be named.

[^*]: A judgment about an object of satisfaction can be entirely ***disinterested*** and yet very ***interesting***, i.e. it is grounded on no interest, but it brings forth an interest; of this kind are all pure moral judgments. But judgments of taste in themselves also give rise to no interest whatsoever. Only in society does it become ***interesting*** to have taste, the ground of which will be indicated in what follows.
