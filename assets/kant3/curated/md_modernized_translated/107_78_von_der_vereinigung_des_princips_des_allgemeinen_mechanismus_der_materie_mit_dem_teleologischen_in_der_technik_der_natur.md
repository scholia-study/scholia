## § 78. On the Unification of the Principle of the Universal Mechanism of Matter with the Teleological in the Technique of Nature

It is of infinite importance to reason not to let go of the mechanism of nature in its generations and not to pass it by in explaining them: because without this, no insight into the nature of things can be attained. If it is granted to us even: that a supreme Architect immediately created the forms of nature, just as they have existed from of old, or predetermined those which continually form themselves in its course according to the very same pattern: yet our cognition of nature is not in the least promoted thereby: because we do not know at all that being's mode of action and its ideas, which are supposed to contain the principles of the possibility of natural beings, and cannot explain nature from it as from above down (_a priori_). But if we want, from the forms of the objects of experience, thus from below up (_a posteriori_), because we believe we find purposiveness in them, in order to explain them, to appeal to a cause acting according to purposes {{ 355 }}: then we would explain completely tautologically and deceive reason with words, without yet mentioning: that where we lose ourselves with this mode of explanation in the transcendent, whither our cognition of nature cannot follow, reason is enticed to rave poetically, to prevent which is precisely its preeminent vocation.

{{{ 411 }}} From the other side, it is an equally necessary maxim of reason not to pass by the principle of purposes in the products of nature: because, even if it does not make their mode of origination any more comprehensible to us, it is nevertheless a heuristic principle for investigating the particular laws of nature; even assuming that one did not want to make any use of it to explain nature itself according to it, in that, although they obviously display an intentional unity of purpose, one still only calls them natural purposes for so long, i.e., without seeking the ground of their possibility beyond nature. But because it must in the end come to the question regarding the latter: it is just as necessary for it to think a special kind of causality, which is not found in nature, as the mechanics of natural causes has its own, in that to the receptivity for more and other forms than those of which matter is capable according to the latter, there must also be added a spontaneity of a {{ 356 }} cause (which therefore cannot be matter), without which no ground can be given for those forms. To be sure, before taking this step, reason must proceed cautiously and not seek to explain teleologically every technique of nature, i.e., a productive capacity of it which in itself displays purposiveness of form for our mere apprehension (as with regular bodies), but must always regard it as merely mechanically possible for as long as possible; but to exclude the teleological principle entirely on that account, and—where the purposiveness for the rational investigation of the possibility of natural forms through their causes reveals itself quite undeniably as a relation to another kind of causality—always to want to follow the mere mechanism, must make reason just as fantastic and wandering about among chimeras of natural powers that cannot be thought at all, as a merely teleological mode of explanation that takes no account of the mechanism of nature would make it visionary.

On one and the very same thing of nature, both principles cannot be connected as principles of the explanation (deduction) of the one from the other, i.e., unified as dogmatic and constitutive principles of insight into nature for the determining power of judgment. If I assume, e.g., of a maggot, that it is to be regarded as a product of the mere mechanism of matter (of the new formation that it accomplishes for itself {{ 357 }} when its elements are set free through putrefaction), then I cannot now derive the very same product from the very same matter acting as a causality {{{ 412 }}} according to purposes. Conversely, if I assume the same product as a natural purpose, I cannot count on a mechanical mode of its generation and accept such as a constitutive principle for estimating it according to its possibility, and thus unify both principles. For one mode of explanation excludes the other; even assuming that objectively both grounds of the possibility of such a product rested on a single one, but we did not take this into consideration. The principle that is to make possible the compatibility of both in the estimation of nature according to them must be placed in that which lies outside of both (consequently also outside of the possible empirical representation of nature), but yet contains the ground of this, i.e., in the supersensible, and each of the two modes of explanation must be referred to it. Since we can now have of this nothing but the indeterminate concept of a ground that makes the estimation of nature according to empirical laws possible, but otherwise can determine it no more closely by any predicate, it follows that the unification of both principles cannot rest on a ground of the ***explanation*** (explication) of the possibility of a product according to given laws for the ***determining***, {{ 358 }} but only on a ground of the ***exposition*** (exposition) of the same for the reflecting power of judgment. — For to explain means to derive from a principle, which one must therefore be able to cognize and specify clearly. Now, the principle of the mechanism of nature and that of its causality according to purposes in one and the very same product of nature must indeed hang together in a single upper principle and flow from it in common, because otherwise they could not coexist in the contemplation of nature. But if this objectively common principle—which therefore also justifies the community of the maxims of natural research dependent on it—is of such a kind that it can indeed be indicated, but never determinately cognized and clearly specified for use in occurring cases: then no explanation, i.e., clear and determinate derivation, of the possibility of a natural product possible according to those two heterogeneous principles can be drawn from such a principle. Now, however, the common principle of the mechanical derivation on the one hand and the teleological derivation on the other is the ***supersensible***, which we must place at the basis of nature as phenomenon. Of this, however, in a theoretical respect, we cannot make for ourselves even the least affirmatively determinate concept. How, therefore, according to {{{ 413 }}} it, as a principle, nature (according to its particular laws) constitutes for us a system that can be recognized as possible both according to the principle of generation {{ 359 }} from physical causes and according to that of final causes, cannot be explained at all; but rather, if it happens that objects of nature occur which, according to the principle of mechanism (which always has a claim on a natural being), cannot be thought by us according to their possibility without basing ourselves on teleological principles, one can only presuppose that one may confidently investigate in accordance with laws of nature according to both (since the possibility of their product from one or the other principle is recognizable to our understanding), without stumbling at the apparent conflict that emerges between the principles of their estimation: because at least the possibility that both might also be objectively compatible in one principle (since they concern appearances that presuppose a supersensible ground) is assured.

Although, therefore, both the mechanism and the teleological (intentional) technicism of nature, with respect to the very same product and its possibility, may stand under a common upper principle of nature according to particular laws: we nevertheless—because this principle is ***transcendent***—cannot, according to the limitation of our understanding, unify both principles ***in the explanation*** of the very same generation of nature, if even the inner possibility of this product is ***comprehensible*** only through a causality according to purposes (such as organized {{ 360 }} matters are). Thus we must abide by the above principle of teleology: that according to the constitution of the human understanding, for the possibility of organic beings in nature, no other than an intentionally acting cause can be assumed, and the mere mechanism of nature cannot be sufficient at all for the explanation of these products of it; without, however, wishing thereby to decide by this principle with respect to the possibility of such things themselves.

For since this is only a maxim of the reflecting, not of the determining power of judgment, and therefore holds only subjectively for us, not objectively for the possibility of this kind of things themselves (where both modes of generation might well hang together in one and the same ground); since, furthermore, without any concept of a mechanism of nature to be encountered at the same time and added to the teleologically conceived mode of generation, such generation could not be estimated {{{ 414 }}} as a natural product at all: the above maxim therefore carries with it at the same time the necessity of a unification of both principles in the estimation of things as natural purposes, but not in order to put one entirely, or in certain parts, in the place of the other. For in the place of what is thought (at least by us) as possible only according to intention, no mechanism can be assumed; and in the place of what is recognized according to the latter as necessary, no contingency that would require a purpose as its determining ground {{ 361 }} can be assumed: but rather, one can only subordinate the one (mechanism) to the other (intentional technicism), which according to the transcendental principle of the purposiveness of nature may very well happen.

For where purposes are thought of as grounds of the possibility of certain things, there one must also assume means whose law of operation ***for itself*** requires nothing that presupposes a purpose, and is consequently mechanical and yet can be a subordinate cause of intentional effects. Hence, even in organic products of nature—and still more when, prompted by their infinite multitude, we assume the intentional in the connection of natural causes according to particular laws now also (at least by a permitted hypothesis) as the ***universal principle*** of the reflecting power of judgment for the whole of nature (the world)—a great and even universal connection of mechanical laws with teleological ones in the generations of nature can be thought of, without confusing the principles of their estimation and putting one in the place of the other: because in a teleological estimation, matter—even if the form it assumes is estimated as possible only according to intention—can nevertheless, according to its nature, in conformity with mechanical laws, also be subordinated as a means to that represented purpose; although, {{ 362 }} since the ground of this compatibility lies in that which is neither the one nor the other (neither mechanism nor connection of purposes), but is the supersensible substrate of nature of which we cognize nothing, for our (human) reason both modes of representation of the possibility of such objects are not to be fused together, but we can estimate them in no other way than as grounded on a supreme understanding according to the connection of final causes, whereby therefore nothing is taken away from the teleological mode of explanation.

But because it is completely undetermined, and for our reason also forever undeterminable, how much the mechanism of nature does as a means to {{{ 415 }}} every final intention in it; and because on account of the above-mentioned intelligible principle of the possibility of a nature in general, it can even be assumed that it is thoroughly possible according to both kinds of universally agreeing laws (the physical and those of final causes), although we cannot understand at all the way in which this occurs: we also do not know how far the mechanical mode of explanation possible for us may go, but only this much is certain: that, however far we may ever go in it, it must nevertheless always be insufficient for things that we once recognize as natural purposes, and we must therefore, according to the constitution of our understanding, subordinate those grounds altogether to a teleological {{ 363 }} principle.

On this is now founded the authorization, and on account of the importance that the study of nature according to the principle of mechanism has for our theoretical use of reason, also the vocation: to explain mechanically all products and events of nature, even the most purposive, as far as it lies in our power (the limits of which we cannot specify within this mode of investigation), but never to lose sight of the fact that those which we can even set up for investigation solely under the concept of a purpose of reason must, in accordance with the essential constitution of our reason, notwithstanding those mechanical causes, nevertheless ultimately be subordinated to the causality according to purposes.
