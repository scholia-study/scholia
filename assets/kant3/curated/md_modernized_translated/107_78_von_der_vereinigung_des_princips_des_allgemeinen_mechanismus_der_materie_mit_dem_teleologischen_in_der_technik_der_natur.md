## § 78. On combining the principle of matter's general mechanism with the teleological principle in nature's technique

It matters infinitely much to reason not to let go of the mechanism of nature in its productions and not to pass it by when explaining them: for apart from it there is no attaining any insight into the nature of things. Even if it be granted to us that a supreme architect directly created the forms of nature just as they have always existed, or predetermined those that in nature's course continuously form themselves after this very same pattern: our cognition of nature is thereby not advanced in the slightest: for we have no acquaintance whatever with that being's mode of action, nor with its ideas, which are supposed to contain the principles governing how natural beings are possible, and we are unable to explain nature by descending from it, as though from on high (_a priori_). If, however, in order to explain them, we appeal—setting out from the forms that the objects of experience present, and thus ascending from below (_a posteriori_), because we believe we encounter purposiveness in them—to a cause acting according to ends {{ 355 }}: then we would be explaining in a wholly tautological manner and deceiving reason with words, not to mention that, wherever this kind of explanation loses us in the extravagant, into which our cognition of nature cannot follow, reason is enticed into raving poetically, the prevention of which is precisely its most excellent vocation.

{{{ 411 }}} On the other side, reason has an equally necessary maxim: not to overlook the principle of ends in the products of nature: for even though this does not exactly render the manner of their origination more comprehensible to us, it nonetheless serves as a heuristic principle for tracking down nature's particular laws; granting, too, that one would not want to put it to any use in explaining nature itself accordingly, inasmuch as one still calls such products merely natural ends—even where they plainly display an intentional unity of ends—i.e., without searching beyond nature for the ground of their possibility. But because in the end the question regarding the latter must nevertheless come up: it is just as necessary for reason to conceive a special kind of causality, one not found in nature, as the mechanics of natural causes has its own, since to the receptivity for several and other forms than those matter is capable of by the latter, there must further supervene a spontaneity belonging to a {{ 356 }} cause (which accordingly cannot be matter), apart from which none of those forms could be given any ground. To be sure, before taking this step reason must proceed cautiously and not seek to declare every technique of nature—i.e., a productive faculty of nature that exhibits in itself a purposiveness of shape for our mere apprehension (as with regular bodies)—to be teleological, but must rather keep regarding it, for as long as it can, as merely mechanically possible; yet to want, on that account, to shut out the teleological principle entirely and, wherever purposiveness reveals itself quite undeniably to reason's inquiry into how natural forms are possible through their causes as a relation to another kind of causality, still to insist on following mere mechanism, would make reason just as fantastical and given to roaming amid figments of natural powers that cannot even be conceived, as a merely teleological manner of explanation, taking no account at all of nature's mechanism, would make it fanatically enthusiastic.

Within one and the selfsame natural thing, one cannot tie the two principles together as fundamental propositions by which the one is explained (deduced) from the other—in other words, they cannot be fused, for the determining power of judgment, into dogmatic and constitutive principles that afford insight into nature. Suppose, for instance, that I take a maggot to be something one must regard as produced by the sheer mechanism of matter (by the new formation it brings about of itself {{ 357 }} once putrefaction sets its elements free): in that case I cannot then trace the selfsame product back to this same matter regarded as a causality {{{ 412 }}} operating in accordance with ends. The reverse holds as well: should I regard that same product as a natural end, I am unable to rely on a mechanical way of generating it and to take up this mode as a constitutive principle for appraising how it is possible, thereby uniting the two principles. For the one manner of explanation rules out the other; even granting that both grounds of such a product's possibility objectively rested upon one and the same ground, to which, however, we paid no heed. The principle meant to make the two compatible in judging nature according to them must be located in what lies beyond both (and hence beyond any possible empirical representation of nature) yet contains the ground of that representation—that is, in the supersensible—while each of the two manners of explanation must be referred to it. Since, then, all we can possess of it is the indeterminate concept of a ground making possible the appraisal of nature by empirical laws—a concept we cannot, for the rest, pin down more closely through any predicate—it follows that what unites the two principles cannot be a ground of ***explanation*** (explication) of a product's possibility according to given laws, serving the ***determining*** power of judgment, {{ 358 }} but can only be a ground of ***discussion*** (exposition) of that possibility, serving the reflecting power of judgment. — For to explain is to derive from a principle that one must accordingly be able to cognize distinctly and to state. Now the principle of nature's mechanism and that of its causality according to ends must indeed, in one and the very same natural product, hang together within a single higher principle and issue jointly from it, since otherwise they could not coexist in our contemplation of nature. But if this objectively shared principle—which thereby also legitimates the shared maxims of natural research that depend on it—is of such a kind that it can indeed be pointed to, yet never determinately cognized and clearly stated for use in cases as they arise: then from such a principle no explanation, i.e., no clear and determinate derivation, can be drawn of how a natural product possible according to those two heterogeneous principles comes to be. But now the shared principle of the mechanical derivation on the one hand and the teleological one on the other is the ***supersensible***, which we must place beneath nature as appearance. Of this, however, we can form, for theoretical purposes, not the slightest affirmatively determinate concept. How, then, in accordance with {{{ 413 }}} this principle nature (according to its particular laws) is to make up for us a system cognizable as possible both by the principle of generating {{ 359 }} physical things and by that of final causes: this simply cannot be explained; rather, only if it turns out that objects of nature occur which, as to their possibility, we cannot think according to the principle of mechanism (which at all times has its claim upon a natural being) without leaning on teleological principles, does their occurrence presuppose that one may confidently investigate both in keeping with the laws of nature (once the possibility of their product from the one or the other principle is knowable to our understanding), without taking offense at the apparent conflict that arises between the principles for judging that product: because at least the possibility that the two might, even objectively, be reconcilable in one principle (given that they concern appearances presupposing a supersensible ground) stands secured.

Thus, even though both the mechanism and the teleological (intentional) technicism of nature may, in respect of the very same product and its possibility, fall under a common higher principle of nature according to particular laws: still, since this principle is ***transcendent***, we cannot, given how limited our understanding is, unite the two principles ***in explaining*** one and the same production of nature whenever the inner possibility of this product is ***intelligible*** solely through a causality according to ends (as organized {{ 360 }} kinds of matter indeed are). It thus remains with the above principle of teleology: that, given the constitution of the human understanding, for the possibility of organic beings in nature no cause can be assumed other than one acting intentionally, and that nature's mere mechanism can in no way suffice to explain these products of hers; yet without meaning thereby to decide anything, by way of this principle, about the possibility of such things themselves.

For this principle amounts to nothing more than a maxim belonging to the reflecting rather than the determining power of judgment, and it therefore holds good only subjectively, for us, and not objectively, for the very possibility of things of this kind (a case where both modes of generation could well be connected in one and the same ground); and since, moreover, in the absence of any concept—added to the teleologically conceived mode of generation—of a mechanism of nature to be encountered there alongside it, a generation of that sort could not be judged {{{ 414 }}} a natural product at all: the maxim just stated carries with it, at the same time, the necessity of uniting the two principles when things are judged as natural ends, yet not so as to put the one, wholly or in certain respects, in the place of the other. For no mechanism can take the place of what (by us, at any rate) is conceived as possible solely in accordance with an intention; and in place of what mechanism marks out as necessary one may assume no contingency standing in need of an end for its ground of determination {{ 361 }}: what is possible instead is merely to subordinate the one (mechanism) beneath the other (the intentional technicism), a step the transcendental principle of nature's purposiveness quite well allows.

For once ends are thought of as grounds on which certain things are possible, one has to posit means as well, means whose operative law ***of itself*** demands nothing that would presuppose an end, and which may thus be mechanical while still being a subordinate cause of intentional effects. For this reason, in organic products of nature—and all the more once, moved by their boundless number, we go on (at least as a permissible hypothesis) to take the intentional element in the connection of natural causes under particular laws as a ***universal principle*** of the reflecting power of judgment in regard to nature as a totality (the world)—it becomes possible to think a vast, indeed universal, joining of mechanical to teleological laws throughout what nature brings forth, and do so without mixing up the principles by which they are judged or putting the one where the other belongs: for in a teleological appraisal, matter, even when the form it assumes is judged to be possible only according to intention, can still, by its own nature and conformably to mechanical laws, be subordinated as a means to that represented end; though, {{ 362 }} inasmuch as the ground of this reconcilability lies in what is neither of the two (neither mechanism nor the linkage of ends) but is instead the supersensible substratum of nature, of which we cognize nothing, for our (the human) reason the two ways of representing the possibility of such objects cannot be melted into one, and we can appraise them in no other way than as grounded, in keeping with the connection of final causes, upon a supreme understanding, so that thereby nothing at all is taken from the teleological manner of explanation.

But since it is now entirely undetermined, and for our reason also undeterminable for all time, how much the mechanism of nature accomplishes as a means toward {{{ 415 }}} every final aim within it; and since, in view of the intelligible principle mentioned above concerning the possibility of a nature as such, it may indeed be assumed that nature is throughout possible according to both kinds of universally concordant laws (the physical laws and those of final causes), even though we can have no insight whatever into how this comes about: we likewise do not know how far the mechanical manner of explanation available to us reaches, but only this much is certain: that, however far we may ever advance in it, it will in every case fall short for things we have once recognized as natural ends, and that we must therefore, given the constitution of our understanding, subordinate all those grounds together to a teleological {{ 363 }} principle.

On this, then, rests the entitlement—and, because of the importance that studying nature by the principle of mechanism holds for our theoretical use of reason, the calling as well—to explain all products and events of nature, even the most purposive, mechanically as far as this ever lies within our power (whose bounds we cannot state within this manner of investigation), while yet never losing sight of the fact that those which we can so much as put forward for investigation solely under the concept of an end of reason must, as the essential constitution of our reason demands and notwithstanding those mechanical causes, ultimately be subordinated to the causality according to ends.
