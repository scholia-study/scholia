## § 78. On combining the principle of matter's general mechanism with the teleological principle in nature's technique

It matters infinitely much to reason not to let go of the mechanism of nature in its productions and not to pass it by when explaining them: for apart from it there is no attaining any insight into the nature of things. Even if it be granted to us that a supreme architect directly created the forms of nature just as they have always existed, or predetermined those that in nature's course continuously form themselves after this very same pattern: our cognition of nature is thereby not advanced in the slightest: for we have no acquaintance whatever with that being's mode of action, nor with its ideas, which are supposed to contain the principles governing how natural beings are possible, and we are unable to explain nature by descending from it, as though from on high (_a priori_). If, however, in order to explain them, we appeal—setting out from the forms that the objects of experience present, and thus ascending from below (_a posteriori_), because we believe we encounter purposiveness in them—to a cause acting according to ends {{ 355 }}: then we would be explaining in a wholly tautological manner and deceiving reason with words, not to mention that, wherever this kind of explanation loses us in the extravagant, into which our cognition of nature cannot follow, reason is enticed into raving poetically, the prevention of which is precisely its most excellent vocation.

{{{ 411 }}} On the other side, reason has an equally necessary maxim: not to overlook the principle of ends in the products of nature: for even though this does not exactly render the manner of their origination more comprehensible to us, it nonetheless serves as a heuristic principle for tracking down nature's particular laws; granting, too, that one would not want to put it to any use in explaining nature itself accordingly, inasmuch as one still calls such products merely natural ends—even where they plainly display an intentional unity of ends—i.e., without searching beyond nature for the ground of their possibility. But because in the end the question regarding the latter must nevertheless come up: it is just as necessary for reason to conceive a special kind of causality, one not found in nature, as the mechanics of natural causes has its own, since to the receptivity for several and other forms than those matter is capable of by the latter, there must further supervene a spontaneity belonging to a {{ 356 }} cause (which accordingly cannot be matter), apart from which none of those forms could be given any ground. To be sure, before taking this step reason must proceed cautiously and not seek to declare every technique of nature—i.e., a productive faculty of nature that exhibits in itself a purposiveness of shape for our mere apprehension (as with regular bodies)—to be teleological, but must rather keep regarding it, for as long as it can, as merely mechanically possible; yet to want, on that account, to shut out the teleological principle entirely and, wherever purposiveness reveals itself quite undeniably to reason's inquiry into how natural forms are possible through their causes as a relation to another kind of causality, still to insist on following mere mechanism, would make reason just as fantastical and given to roaming amid figments of natural powers that cannot even be conceived, as a merely teleological manner of explanation, taking no account at all of nature's mechanism, would make it fanatically enthusiastic.

In one and the very same thing of nature the two principles cannot be linked as principles for explaining (deducing) the one from the other, i.e., they cannot be united as dogmatic and constitutive principles of insight into nature for the determining power of judgment. If, for instance, I assume of a maggot that it is to be regarded as a product of the mere mechanism of matter (of the fresh formation it accomplishes on its own {{ 357 }} once its elements are set free through putrefaction): then I cannot go on to derive that very same product from that same matter taken as a causality {{{ 412 }}} acting according to ends. Conversely, if I take the same product to be a natural end, I cannot count on a mechanical mode of its generation and adopt this as a constitutive principle for judging its possibility, and so unite the two principles. For the one manner of explanation rules out the other; even granting that both grounds of such a product's possibility objectively rested upon one and the same ground, to which, however, we paid no heed. The principle meant to make the two compatible in judging nature according to them must be located in what lies beyond both (and hence beyond any possible empirical representation of nature) yet contains the ground of that representation—that is, in the supersensible—while each of the two manners of explanation must be referred to it. Now since of this we can have nothing but the indeterminate concept of a ground that renders the judging of nature according to empirical laws possible, while it otherwise admits of no closer determination by any predicate: it follows that the union of the two principles can rest not on a ground of ***explanation*** (explication) of how a product is possible according to given laws for the ***determining*** power of judgment, {{ 358 }} but only on a ground of ***discussion*** (exposition) of that same possibility for the reflecting power of judgment. — For to explain is to derive from a principle that one must accordingly be able to cognize distinctly and to state. Now the principle of nature's mechanism and that of its causality according to ends must indeed, in one and the very same natural product, hang together within a single higher principle and issue jointly from it, since otherwise they could not coexist in our contemplation of nature. But if this objectively shared principle—which thereby also legitimates the shared maxims of natural research that depend on it—is of such a kind that it can indeed be pointed to, yet never determinately cognized and clearly stated for use in cases as they arise: then from such a principle no explanation, i.e., no clear and determinate derivation, can be drawn of how a natural product possible according to those two heterogeneous principles comes to be. But now the shared principle of the mechanical derivation on the one hand and the teleological one on the other is the ***supersensible***, which we must place beneath nature as appearance. Of this, however, we can form, for theoretical purposes, not the slightest affirmatively determinate concept. How, then, in accordance with {{{ 413 }}} this principle nature (according to its particular laws) is to make up for us a system cognizable as possible both by the principle of generating {{ 359 }} physical things and by that of final causes: this simply cannot be explained; rather, only if it turns out that objects of nature occur which, as to their possibility, we cannot think according to the principle of mechanism (which at all times has its claim upon a natural being) without leaning on teleological principles, does their occurrence presuppose that one may confidently investigate both in keeping with the laws of nature (once the possibility of their product from the one or the other principle is knowable to our understanding), without taking offense at the apparent conflict that arises between the principles for judging that product: because at least the possibility that the two might, even objectively, be reconcilable in one principle (given that they concern appearances presupposing a supersensible ground) stands secured.

Thus, even though both the mechanism and the teleological (intentional) technicism of nature may, in respect of the very same product and its possibility, fall under a common higher principle of nature according to particular laws: still, since this principle is ***transcendent***, we cannot, given how limited our understanding is, unite the two principles ***in explaining*** one and the same production of nature whenever the inner possibility of this product is ***intelligible*** solely through a causality according to ends (as organized {{ 360 }} kinds of matter indeed are). It thus remains with the above principle of teleology: that, given the constitution of the human understanding, for the possibility of organic beings in nature no cause can be assumed other than one acting intentionally, and that nature's mere mechanism can in no way suffice to explain these products of hers; yet without meaning thereby to decide anything, by way of this principle, about the possibility of such things themselves.

For since this is merely a maxim of the reflecting, not of the determining power of judgment, and hence holds only subjectively for us and not objectively for the possibility of this sort of thing itself (where the two modes of generation might indeed cohere in one and the same ground); and since, moreover, without any concept—joined to the teleologically conceived mode of generation—of a mechanism of nature likewise to be found there, no such generation could be judged {{{ 414 }}} a natural product at all: the above maxim at the same time brings with it the necessity of uniting the two principles in judging things as natural ends, though not so as to set the one, wholly or in certain respects, in the place of the other. For in the place of what (by us at least) is thought as possible only according to intention, no mechanism can be set; and in the place of what is cognized as necessary according to mechanism, no contingency that would need an end as its determining ground {{ 361 }} can be assumed: rather, the one (mechanism) can only be subordinated to the other (the intentional technicism), which, on the transcendental principle of nature's purposiveness, is entirely permissible.

For where ends are conceived as grounds of the possibility of certain things, there one must also assume means whose law of operation ***of itself*** requires nothing that presupposes an end, and which can therefore be mechanical and yet a subordinate cause of intentional effects. Hence even in organic products of nature—and still more so if, prompted by their infinite multitude, we now also (at least by a permissible hypothesis) adopt the intentional element in the linkage of natural causes according to particular laws as a ***universal principle*** of the reflecting power of judgment for nature taken as a whole (the world)—we are able to conceive a great and even universal linkage of the mechanical laws with the teleological ones in the productions of nature, without confounding the principles for judging them or setting the one in the place of the other: because in a teleological judging, matter, even where the form it takes on is judged possible only according to intention, can nevertheless, by its own nature and in keeping with mechanical laws, be subordinated as a means to that represented end; although, {{ 362 }} since what grounds this compatibility resides in something that is neither the one nor the other (neither mechanism nor connection of ends), but rather the supersensible substratum of nature, of which we cognize nothing, for our (human) reason the two ways of representing how such objects are possible cannot be fused together, but rather we can judge them only as founded, after the manner in which final causes are linked, upon a supreme understanding, whereby nothing whatever is withdrawn from the teleological manner of explanation.

But since it is now entirely undetermined, and for our reason also undeterminable for all time, how much the mechanism of nature accomplishes as a means toward {{{ 415 }}} every final aim within it; and since, in view of the intelligible principle mentioned above concerning the possibility of a nature as such, it may indeed be assumed that nature is throughout possible according to both kinds of universally concordant laws (the physical laws and those of final causes), even though we can have no insight whatever into how this comes about: we likewise do not know how far the mechanical manner of explanation available to us reaches, but only this much is certain: that, however far we may ever advance in it, it will in every case fall short for things we have once recognized as natural ends, and that we must therefore, given the constitution of our understanding, subordinate all those grounds together to a teleological {{ 363 }} principle.

On this, then, rests the entitlement—and, because of the importance that studying nature by the principle of mechanism holds for our theoretical use of reason, the calling as well—to explain all products and events of nature, even the most purposive, mechanically as far as this ever lies within our power (whose bounds we cannot state within this manner of investigation), while yet never losing sight of the fact that those which we can so much as put forward for investigation solely under the concept of an end of reason must, as the essential constitution of our reason demands and notwithstanding those mechanical causes, ultimately be subordinated to the causality according to ends.
