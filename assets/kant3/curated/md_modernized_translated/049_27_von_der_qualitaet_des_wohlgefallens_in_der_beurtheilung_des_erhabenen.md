## § 27. On the quality of the satisfaction in judging the sublime

Respect is the feeling that our capacity is inadequate for attaining an idea ***that is a law for us***. The idea of combining any appearance whatever that might be given to us into the intuition of one whole is, however, an idea enjoined upon us by a law of reason, which acknowledges no determinate measure, valid for everyone and invariable, save the absolute whole. {{ 97 }} Yet our imagination, even when it strains to the utmost in carrying out the combining it is required to make — that of a given object into a whole of intuition, and thus toward the presentation of reason's idea — reveals its bounds and inadequacy, yet at the same time reveals its vocation to bring about accordance with that idea taken as a law. Hence, in nature, the feeling of the sublime amounts to respect for our own vocation, a respect we show toward an object of nature by a certain subreption (putting a respect for the object in the place of respect for the idea of humanity within our own self as subject), and this object makes intuitable to us, as it were, how the rational vocation of our cognitive powers stands above the greatest faculty of sensibility.

The feeling of the sublime is thus a feeling of displeasure that springs from the inadequacy of the imagination — in the aesthetic estimation of magnitude — to the estimation carried out by reason, and at the same time a pleasure aroused by the very agreement of this same judgment of the greatest sensible faculty's inadequacy with ideas of reason, insofar as striving toward those ideas is nevertheless a law for us. For it is a law for us (a law of reason), and part of our vocation, to reckon as small, in comparison with ideas of reason, everything that nature holds as an object of the senses that counts for us as great; and whatever stirs in us the feeling of this supersensible {{ 98 }} vocation is in harmony with that law. {{{ 258 }}} Now the imagination's greatest striving, in presenting the unity required for estimating magnitude, is a relation to something ***absolutely great***, and therefore also a relation to the law of reason that bids us adopt this alone as the highest measure of magnitudes. Thus the inward perception that every sensible yardstick falls short of reason's estimation of magnitude is an accordance with reason's own laws, and a displeasure that stirs in us the feeling of our supersensible vocation, in keeping with which it is purposive — and hence a pleasure — to regard every sensible yardstick as inadequate to the ideas of reason.

In representing the sublime in nature the mind feels itself ***moved***: whereas in the aesthetic judgment upon nature's beauty it rests in ***tranquil*** contemplation. This movement may (chiefly at its onset) be likened to a vibration, that is, to a swiftly alternating repelling and attracting produced by one and the same object. What is excessive for the imagination (the point to which it is driven in apprehending the intuition) is like an abyss where it dreads losing itself; and yet, for reason's idea of the supersensible, calling forth such a striving of the imagination is not excessive but lawful: {{ 99 }} so that it becomes attractive again in just the measure in which it had been repellent for mere sensibility. The judgment itself, however, stays throughout merely aesthetic, since, having no determinate concept of the object for its ground, it merely represents as harmonious — precisely through their contrast — the subjective play of the powers of the mind (imagination and reason). For just as, in judging the beautiful, imagination and ***understanding*** through their concord bring forth a subjective purposiveness of the mental powers, so here imagination and ***reason*** bring it forth through their discord: namely, a feeling that we possess pure, self-standing reason, that is, a faculty of estimating magnitude whose excellence can be made intuitable by nothing save the inadequacy of that very power which, in presenting magnitudes (of sensible objects), is itself without bounds.

To measure a space (as an act of apprehension) is at once to describe it, and hence to carry out an objective movement in the imagination together with a progression; whereas the combining of the multiplicity into a unity — a unity not of thought but of intuition, that is, of what has been apprehended in succession gathered into one instant — is a regression that undoes again the time-condition in the {{{ 259 }}} progression of the imagination and makes ***simultaneity*** intuitable. It is therefore (since temporal sequence is a condition both of inner sense and of an intuition) a subjective movement on the part of the {{ 100 }} imagination by which it does violence to inner sense — a violence that must be the more perceptible, the larger the quantum that the imagination draws together into a single intuition. The striving, then, to take up into one intuition a measure for magnitudes whose apprehension demands a perceptible span of time is a way of representing that, viewed subjectively, runs counter to purpose, yet objectively is requisite for estimating magnitude and so is purposive: whereby, all the same, this very violence inflicted on the subject by the imagination is deemed purposive ***for the entire vocation*** of the mind.

As for the ***quality*** of the feeling of the sublime, it consists in this: that it is a feeling of displeasure regarding the aesthetic faculty for judging an object, a displeasure that within that very judging is nonetheless represented as purposive; and this is possible because the subject's own incapacity uncovers the consciousness of an unlimited capacity belonging to that same subject, and the mind can pass aesthetic judgment on the latter only through the medium of the former.

Within the logical estimation of magnitude, the impossibility of ever attaining absolute totality by way of the progression of measuring the things of the sensible world in time and space was recognized as objective — that is, as an impossibility of ***thinking*** the infinite as something given, and not as merely {{ 101 }} subjective, that is, as an incapacity to ***grasp*** it: for here the degree of combination into one intuition, taken as a measure, is not attended to at all, but everything turns instead on a numerical concept. In an aesthetic estimation of magnitude, by contrast, the numerical concept must drop away or be transformed, and only the imagination's comprehension into the unity of the measure (thus with avoidance of the concepts belonging to a law for the successive production of concepts of magnitude) is purposive for it. — Now when a magnitude nearly attains the extreme limit of our capacity to combine it into a single intuition, and the imagination is nonetheless summoned by numerical magnitudes (with regard to which we are conscious that our capacity has no bounds) toward an aesthetic combining into a larger unity, then we feel ourselves, in mind, aesthetically shut in within limits; but the displeasure — in view of the necessary expansion of the imagination toward accordance with what has no bounds in our faculty of reason, namely the {{{ 260 }}} idea of the absolute whole, and hence in view of the unpurposiveness of the imagination's faculty — is all the same represented as purposive after all for ideas of reason and their awakening. Yet by this very means the aesthetic judgment itself becomes subjectively purposive for reason, as the wellspring of ideas — that is, for an intellectual combining in relation to which all aesthetic combining is small; and the object is received as sublime with a pleasure {{ 102 }} that becomes possible only through the mediation of a displeasure.
