## § 27. On the quality of the satisfaction in the judging of the sublime

The feeling of the inadequacy of our faculty for attaining an idea ***that is a law for us*** is respect. Now the idea of the comprehension of every appearance that may be given to us into the intuition of a whole is one that is imposed on us by a law of reason, which recognizes no other determinate measure, valid for everyone and unalterable, than the absolute whole. {{ 97 }} But our imagination, even in its greatest exertion with regard to the comprehension demanded of it of a given object into a whole of intuition (hence for the presentation of the idea of reason), demonstrates its limits and inadequacy, yet at the same time its vocation for effecting the adequacy with the same as a law. Thus the feeling of the sublime in nature is respect for our own vocation, which we demonstrate toward an object of nature through a certain subreption (the substitution of a respect for the object in place of one for the idea of humanity in our subject), which makes intuitable, as it were, the superiority of the rational vocation of our faculties of cognition over the greatest faculty of sensibility.

The feeling of the sublime is thus a feeling of displeasure arising from the inadequacy of the imagination in the aesthetic estimation of magnitude for the estimation through reason, and at the same time a pleasure thereby aroused, arising from the agreement of precisely this judgment of the inadequacy of the greatest sensible faculty with ideas of reason, insofar as the striving toward them is yet a law for us. For it is a law (of reason) for us, and belongs to our vocation, to estimate everything that nature, as an object of the senses, contains that is great for us as small in comparison with ideas of reason; and what arouses in us the feeling of this supersensible {{ 98 }} vocation agrees with that law. {{{ 258 }}} Now the greatest striving of the imagination in the presentation of the unit for the estimation of magnitude is a relation to something ***absolutely great***, consequently also a relation to the law of reason, to assume this alone as the supreme measure of magnitudes. Thus the inner perception of the inadequacy of every sensible standard for the estimation of magnitude by reason is an agreement with laws of reason, and a displeasure that arouses in us the feeling of our supersensible vocation, according to which it is purposive, hence a pleasure, to find every standard of sensibility inadequate to the ideas of reason.

In the representation of the sublime in nature the mind feels itself ***moved***: whereas in the aesthetic judgment about the beautiful in it it is in ***restful*** contemplation. This movement can (especially in its beginning) be compared with a vibration, i.e., with a rapidly alternating repulsion from and attraction to one and the same object. The excessive for the imagination (to which it is driven in the apprehension of the intuition) is as it were an abyss, in which it fears to lose itself; yet for the idea of reason concerning the supersensible it is not excessive, but lawful, to bring forth such a striving of the imagination: {{ 99 }} hence in the very same measure attractive again as it was repulsive for mere sensibility. But the judgment itself thereby always remains merely aesthetic, because, without having a determinate concept of the object at its ground, it represents merely the subjective play of the powers of mind (imagination and reason) as harmonious even through their contrast. For just as imagination and ***understanding*** produce subjective purposiveness of the powers of mind through their unanimity in the judging of the beautiful, so here imagination and ***reason*** produce it through their conflict: namely a feeling that we have pure, self-subsisting reason, or a faculty for the estimation of magnitude whose preeminence can be made intuitable through nothing except the insufficiency of that faculty which is itself unbounded in the presentation of magnitudes (of sensible objects).

The measurement of a space (as apprehension) is at the same time the description of it, hence an objective movement in the imagination and a progressus; the comprehension of multiplicity into the unity, not of thought but of intuition, hence of the successively apprehended into one instant, is by contrast a regressus, which cancels again the time-condition in the {{{ 259 }}} progressus of the imagination and makes ***simultaneity*** intuitable. It is thus (since temporal succession is a condition of inner sense and of an intuition) a subjective movement of the {{ 100 }} imagination, by which it does violence to inner sense, which must be all the more noticeable the greater the quantum is that the imagination comprehends into one intuition. The striving, therefore, to take up into a single intuition a measure for magnitudes which requires a noticeable time to apprehend is a kind of representation which, considered subjectively, is contrary to purpose, but objectively is requisite for the estimation of magnitude, hence purposive: yet in which the very same violence that is done to the subject through the imagination is judged as purposive ***for the whole vocation*** of the mind.

The ***quality*** of the feeling of the sublime is this: that it is a feeling of displeasure with respect to the aesthetic faculty for judging an object, which is yet at the same time represented therein as purposive; which is possible through this, that one's own incapacity discloses the consciousness of an unlimited capacity of the very same subject, and the mind can judge the latter aesthetically only through the former.

In the logical estimation of magnitude the impossibility of ever arriving at absolute totality through the progressus of the measurement of the things of the sensible world in time and space was recognized as objective, i.e., as an impossibility of ***thinking*** the infinite as given, and not as merely {{ 101 }} subjective, i.e., as an incapacity to ***grasp*** it: because there the degree of comprehension into one intuition as a measure is not regarded at all, but everything depends on a concept of number. But in an aesthetic estimation of magnitude the concept of number must fall away or be altered, and the comprehension of the imagination toward the unity of the measure (hence with the avoidance of the concepts of a law of the successive generation of the concepts of magnitude) is alone purposive for it. — If now a magnitude almost reaches the utmost of our faculty of comprehension into one intuition, and the imagination is yet invited by numerical magnitudes (of which we are conscious that our faculty is unbounded) to aesthetic comprehension into a greater unit, then we feel ourselves in our mind aesthetically enclosed within limits; but the displeasure is yet, with regard to the necessary enlargement of the imagination for adequacy with that which is unbounded in our faculty of reason, namely the {{{ 260 }}} idea of the absolute whole, hence the contrapurposiveness of the faculty of the imagination, represented as purposive for ideas of reason and their arousal. But precisely thereby the aesthetic judgment itself becomes subjectively purposive for reason, as the source of ideas, i.e., of such an intellectual comprehension for which all aesthetic comprehension is small; and the object is taken up as sublime with a pleasure {{ 102 }} that is possible only by means of a displeasure.
