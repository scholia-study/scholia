## § 27. On the quality of the satisfaction in judging the sublime

Respect is the feeling that our capacity is inadequate for attaining an idea ***that is a law for us***. The idea of combining any appearance whatever that might be given to us into the intuition of one whole is, however, an idea enjoined upon us by a law of reason, which acknowledges no determinate measure, valid for everyone and invariable, save the absolute whole. {{ 97 }} Yet our imagination, even when it strains to the utmost in carrying out the combining it is required to make — that of a given object into a whole of intuition, and thus toward the presentation of reason's idea — reveals its bounds and inadequacy, yet at the same time reveals its vocation to bring about accordance with that idea taken as a law. In nature, accordingly, the feeling of the sublime is respect for our own vocation, which we accord to an object of nature by way of a certain subreption (a confusing of things, whereby respect for the object stands in for the respect owed to the idea of humanity within us as subjects), the object making intuitable to us, as it were, the superiority that the rational vocation of our cognitive powers holds over the greatest capacity of sensibility.

So the feeling of the sublime is, on the one hand, a displeasure arising because the imagination, in its aesthetic estimation of magnitude, proves unequal to the estimation made through reason, and, on the other, a pleasure awakened alongside it out of the very concordance between this same judgment — the judgment of the greatest sensible power's inadequacy — and ideas of reason, insofar as the endeavor toward those ideas is, after all, a law for us. For it is a law for us (a law of reason), and part of our vocation, to reckon as small, in comparison with ideas of reason, everything that nature holds as an object of the senses that counts for us as great; and whatever stirs in us the feeling of this supersensible {{ 98 }} vocation is in harmony with that law. {{{ 258 }}} Now the imagination's greatest striving, in presenting the unity required for estimating magnitude, is a relation to something ***absolutely great***, and therefore also a relation to the law of reason that bids us adopt this alone as the highest measure of magnitudes. Thus the inward perception that every sensible yardstick falls short of reason's estimation of magnitude is an accordance with reason's own laws, and a displeasure that stirs in us the feeling of our supersensible vocation, in keeping with which it is purposive — and hence a pleasure — to regard every sensible yardstick as inadequate to the ideas of reason.

In representing the sublime in nature the mind feels itself ***moved***: whereas in the aesthetic judgment upon nature's beauty it rests in ***tranquil*** contemplation. Such movement, especially at its beginning, admits of comparison with a tremor — in other words, with a rapidly shifting repulsion and attraction exerted by the selfsame object. What exceeds the imagination's reach (the limit up to which it is driven in taking in the intuition) is, as it were, an abyss in which it is afraid of losing itself; and yet, for reason's idea of the supersensible, to bring forth such a striving of the imagination is not something excessive but rather something lawful: {{ 99 }} so that, by the very same measure in which it had repelled mere sensibility, it now draws one in once more. The judgment itself, however, remains here always merely aesthetic, because, without grounding itself on any determinate concept of the object, it represents the subjective interplay of the mental powers themselves (imagination and reason) as harmonious precisely through their contrast. For just as, in judging the beautiful, imagination and ***understanding*** through their concord bring forth a subjective purposiveness of the mental powers, so here imagination and ***reason*** bring it forth through their discord: namely, a feeling that we possess pure, self-standing reason, that is, a faculty of estimating magnitude whose excellence can be made intuitable by nothing save the inadequacy of that very power which, in presenting magnitudes (of sensible objects), is itself without bounds.

The measuring of a space (as apprehension) is at the same time a describing of it, and hence an objective motion within imagining, a progression; by contrast, the drawing-together of the multiplicity into unity — a unity not of thought but of intuition, namely of what has been successively apprehended, gathered into a single moment — is a regression, which annuls once again the temporal condition within the {{{ 259 }}} progression of the imagination and renders ***simultaneity*** intuitable. This regression, then (given that temporal succession is a condition of inner sense and of any intuition), amounts to a subjective motion of the {{ 100 }} imagination whereby the latter does violence to inner sense — violence bound to grow the more noticeable the greater the quantum that the imagination compresses into one intuition. The striving, then, to take up into one intuition a measure for magnitudes whose apprehension demands a perceptible span of time is a way of representing that, viewed subjectively, runs counter to purpose, yet objectively is requisite for estimating magnitude and so is purposive: whereby, all the same, this very violence inflicted on the subject by the imagination is deemed purposive ***for the entire vocation*** of the mind.

As for the ***quality*** of the feeling of the sublime, it consists in this: that it is a feeling of displeasure regarding the aesthetic faculty for judging an object, a displeasure that within that very judging is nonetheless represented as purposive; and this is possible because the subject's own incapacity uncovers the consciousness of an unlimited capacity belonging to that same subject, and the mind can pass aesthetic judgment on the latter only through the medium of the former.

In logical estimation of magnitude, it was recognized as objective that one can never, through the progression of measuring the things of the world of sense in time and space, arrive at absolute totality — objective, that is to say, an impossibility of ***thinking*** the infinite as given, rather than merely {{ 101 }} subjective, namely an inability to ***grasp*** it: for there no regard at all is paid to the degree of combining into an intuition as a measure, and everything hinges instead on a concept of number. In an aesthetic estimation of magnitude, by contrast, the numerical concept must drop away or be transformed, and only the imagination's comprehension into the unity of the measure (thus with avoidance of the concepts belonging to a law for the successive production of concepts of magnitude) is purposive for it. — Now when a magnitude comes close to the utmost of our power to combine it into one intuition, while the imagination is nonetheless called upon, through numerical magnitudes (in respect of which we know our power to be unbounded), toward an aesthetic combining into some larger unity, then we feel ourselves inwardly, as aesthetic beings, hemmed in by limits; and yet the displeasure — regarded in the light of the imagination's necessary expansion toward adequacy with that which, within our power of reason, knows no bounds, that is, with the {{{ 260 }}} idea of the absolute whole, and hence in the light of the unpurposiveness of the imagination's power — is all the same represented as purposive, after all, for ideas of reason and their arousal. Yet by this very means the aesthetic judgment itself becomes subjectively purposive for reason, as the wellspring of ideas — that is, for an intellectual combining in relation to which all aesthetic combining is small; and the object is received as sublime with a pleasure {{ 102 }} that becomes possible only through the mediation of a displeasure.
