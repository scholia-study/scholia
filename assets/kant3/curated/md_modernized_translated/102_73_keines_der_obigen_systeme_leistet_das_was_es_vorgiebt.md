## {{ 324 }} § 73. None of the above systems achieves what it purports to

What is it that all those systems want? Their intent is to account for our teleological judgments regarding nature, and in doing so they proceed such that one side disputes the truth of those judgments, and thus takes them for an idealism of nature (conceived as art), whereas the other side acknowledges them as true and undertakes to establish that a nature answering to the idea of final causes is possible.

1) On the one side, those systems that take up the cause of the idealism of final causes in nature do indeed grant, at their principle, a causality governed by laws of motion (whereby natural things come to exist in a purposive way); but what they refuse to it is ***intentionality***, that is, {{{ 393 }}} they refuse to grant that it is purposely directed toward bringing forth those purposive products of theirs, or, in other words, that an end serves as the cause. This is Epicurus's mode of explanation, according to which the distinction between a technique of nature and mere mechanism is entirely disavowed, and blind chance is taken as the ground of explanation not merely for the agreement of the products brought forth with our concepts of an {{ 325 }} end — hence for the technique — but even for the determination of the causes of this production according to laws of motion, hence for its mechanism; so that nothing at all, not even the semblance in our teleological judgment, is explained, and consequently the alleged idealism therein is by no means established.

***Spinoza***, on his side, would spare us every question as to the ground on which the ends of nature are possible, and would deprive this idea of all reality, in that he lets them pass not for products whatsoever but for accidents that inhere in an original being, and assigns to that being — as the substrate underlying those natural things — no causality but bare subsistence in regard to them, and (since this being, taken together with all natural things, which are accidents that inhere in it, carries unconditioned necessity) he does secure for the natural forms that unity of ground which all purposiveness demands, yet at the same moment tears away their contingency, apart from which no ***unity of ends*** is thinkable, and with it everything ***intentional***, just as he withdraws all understanding from the primordial ground of natural things.

But Spinozism does not achieve what it wants. It wants to specify a ground of explanation for the purposive connection (which it does not deny) among the things of nature, yet it merely cites the unity of the subject wherein they all inhere. Yet even if, for the beings of the world, {{ 326 }} one concedes to it this mode of existing as well, that ontological unity is still not thereby at once a ***unity of ends***, nor does it render such a unity in the least comprehensible. This last, namely, is an altogether peculiar kind of it, one which by no means arises from the linkage of things (beings of the world) within a single subject (the original being), but instead invariably brings with itself a bearing upon a ***cause*** endowed with understanding, and even were all these things gathered into one simple subject, it would nonetheless yield no purposive relation whatever: not unless one conceives them, in the first place, as inner ***effects*** of the substance regarded as a ***cause***, and, in the second, that same substance as a cause operating ***through its understanding***. Lacking such formal conditions, every unity amounts to nothing but a natural necessity; and where it is ascribed all the same to things that we {{{ 394 }}} represent as lying apart from one another, it becomes a blind necessity. But should one choose to give the name of purposiveness of nature to that which the School designates the transcendental perfection of things (with respect to their own essence) — the perfection on which all things possess within themselves whatever is demanded for their being just this thing and none other —: then what results is a childish trifling with words rather than concepts. For should all things have to be conceived as ends, so that to be a thing and to be an end come to the same, then fundamentally nothing remains that would merit being represented as an end in any special sense.

One readily gathers from this: that Spinoza, in leading our concepts {{ 327 }} of what is purposive in nature back to the awareness we have of ourselves in an all-comprising (though at once simple) being, and in looking for that form solely within the unity of this latter being, was bound to have as his aim the assertion, not of the realism, but only of the idealism of their purposiveness, though he could not actually bring even this about himself, since the mere representation of the substrate's unity cannot call forth so much as the idea of a purposiveness that is even merely unintentional.

2) Those who do not merely maintain the ***realism*** of natural ends but fancy that they can explain it as well suppose themselves able to grasp, at least so far as its possibility goes, a peculiar sort of causality — that, namely, of causes that act with intention; for otherwise they could not set about wishing to explain them. For even to be entitled to the boldest hypothesis, the ***possibility*** of what one assumes as a ground must at least be ***certain***, and one must be able to secure objective reality for the concept of it.

But the possibility of a living matter (whose concept contains a contradiction, since lifelessness, _inertia_, constitutes its essential character) cannot even be thought; that of a matter which is animated, and of nature in its entirety as an animal, admits of only a scanty {{ 328 }} use (on behalf of a hypothesis of purposiveness in nature at large), namely insofar as such organization on the small scale discloses itself to us in experience, whereas it can in no way be grasped _a priori_ with respect to its possibility. One is thus forced into a circle in the explaining, whenever one would trace the purposiveness of nature in organized beings back to the life of matter, and yet is acquainted with this life in no other way than within organized beings, so that, lacking {{{ 395 }}} experience of that kind, one can frame no concept of its possibility. Hylozoism, therefore, does not achieve what it promises.

***Theism***, in the end, can no better furnish a dogmatic grounding for the possibility of natural ends that would serve as a key to teleology; even though, among all the grounds of explanation for it, this one holds the advantage that, through an understanding it attributes to the original being, it most successfully wrests the purposiveness of nature away from idealism and brings in a deliberate causality for the production of that nature.

For here it would first of all have to be proved, sufficiently for the determining power of judgment, that the unity of ends in matter is impossible through the mere mechanism of matter, in order to be entitled to posit the ground of that unity, in a determinate manner, beyond nature. Yet we are able to elicit nothing beyond this: that, in keeping with the makeup and the bounds of our cognitive faculties (since we fail to discern the primary, inner ground of this very mechanism), we {{ 329 }} are in no manner obliged to look within matter for a principle of determinate purposive relations, and that no other manner of appraising the generation of its products as natural ends is left to us save that which proceeds through a supreme understanding as world-cause. But this furnishes a ground solely for the reflecting power of judgment, not for the determining one, and can under no circumstances warrant any objective assertion.
