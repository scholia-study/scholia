## {{ 324 }} § 73. None of the Above Systems Accomplishes What It Claims to Do

What do all those systems want? They want to explain our teleological judgments about nature, and they set to work in such a way that one part denies the truth of these judgments, and consequently explains them as an idealism of nature (represented as art); the other part recognizes them as true and promises to demonstrate the possibility of a nature in accordance with the idea of final causes.

1) The systems that contend for the idealism of final causes in nature, on the one hand, do indeed admit in its principle a causality according to laws of motion (through which natural things exist purposively); but they deny to it ***intentionality***, i.e., {{{ 393 }}} that it is intentionally determined to this, its purposive production, or, in other words, that a purpose is the cause. This is Epicurus's way of explaining, according to which the difference between a technique of nature and mere mechanics is entirely denied, and blind chance is assumed as the explanatory ground not only for the agreement of the generated products with our concepts of {{ 325 }} purpose, hence for the technique, but even for the determination of the causes of this generation according to laws of motion, hence their mechanics, so that nothing, not even the appearance in our teleological judgment, is explained, and consequently the alleged idealism in it is by no means demonstrated.

On the other hand, ***Spinoza*** wants to relieve us of all inquiry into the ground of the possibility of purposes in nature, and to strip this idea of all reality, by not regarding them as products at all, but rather as accidents inhering in an original being, and by attributing to this being, as the substratum of those natural things, not causality in respect of them, but mere subsistence, and (on account of the unconditional necessity of this being together with all natural things, as accidents inhering in it) indeed securing for natural forms the unity of the ground that is required for all purposiveness, but at the same time snatching away their contingency, without which no ***purposive unity*** can be thought, and with it everything ***intentional***, as well as depriving the original ground of natural things of all understanding.

Spinozism, however, does not accomplish what it intends. It wants to provide an explanatory ground for the purposive connection (which it does not deny) of the things of nature, and merely names the unity of the subject in which they all inhere. But even if one grants it this way of existing for world-beings, {{ 326 }} that ontological unity is nevertheless not yet immediately ***purposive unity***, and by no means makes the latter comprehensible. For the latter is a very special kind of unity, which does not follow at all from the connection of things (world-beings) in a subject (the original being), but rather carries with it throughout the relation to a ***cause*** that has understanding, and, even if one united all these things in a simple subject, never represents a purposive relation: unless one thinks of them first as inner ***effects*** of the substance as a ***cause***, and second of this very same substance as a cause ***through its understanding***. Without these formal conditions, all unity is mere natural necessity, and, if it is nevertheless attributed to things that we represent {{{ 394 }}} as external to one another, it is blind necessity. But if one wants to call that which the school calls the transcendental perfection of things (in relation to their own being)—according to which all things have within themselves everything that is required to be such a thing and no other—the purposiveness of nature: then that is a childish playing with words instead of concepts. For if all things must be thought as purposes, so that being a thing and being a purpose are one and the same, then there is basically nothing that would deserve to be represented especially as a purpose.

One sees well from this: that Spinoza, by tracing our concepts {{ 327 }} of the purposive in nature back to the consciousness of ourselves in an all-encompassing (yet at the same time simple) being, and seeking that form merely in the unity of the latter, must have had the intention to assert not the realism, but merely the idealism of its purposiveness, but yet could not achieve this himself, because the mere representation of the unity of the substratum cannot produce even the idea of a merely unintentional purposiveness.

2) Those who do not merely assert the ***realism*** of natural purposes, but also believe they explain it, think they can look into a special kind of causality, namely, of intentionally acting causes, at least as regards its possibility; otherwise they could not undertake to explain it. For to have the right to even the most daring hypothesis, at least the ***possibility*** of what one assumes as a ground must be ***certain***, and one must be able to assure its objective reality to its concept.

But the possibility of a living matter (the concept of which contains a contradiction, because lifelessness, _inertia_, constitutes its essential character) cannot even be thought; that of an animated matter and of the whole of nature, as an animal, can only be used in a scanty {{ 328 }} way (for the purpose of a hypothesis of purposiveness in nature in the large) in so far as it is revealed to us in experience in its organization in the small, but can by no means be understood _a priori_ as regards its possibility. A circle in explaining must therefore be committed if one wants to derive the purposiveness of nature in organized beings from the life of matter, and yet knows this life in turn only in organized beings, and thus without {{{ 395 }}} such experience can form no concept of its possibility. Hylozoism, therefore, does not perform what it promises.

Finally, ***theism*** can just as little dogmatically establish the possibility of natural purposes as a key to teleology; although it has the advantage over all other explanatory grounds in that, through an understanding that it attributes to the original being, it best snatches the purposiveness of nature away from idealism and introduces an intentional causality for its production.

For there, first of all, the impossibility of purposive unity in matter through its mere mechanism would have to be proven sufficiently for the determining power of judgment, in order to be entitled to place its ground beyond nature in a determinate way. We can, however, establish nothing further than that, according to the constitution and the limits of our cognitive faculties (since we do not look into the first, inner ground of this mechanism itself), we {{ 329 }} must in no way seek a principle of determinate relations of purpose in matter, but there remains for us no other way of judging the generation of its products as natural purposes than that through a supreme understanding as the cause of the world. But this is only a ground for the reflecting, not for the determining power of judgment, and can absolutely justify no objective assertion.
