## {{ 324 }} § 73. None of the above systems achieves what it purports to

What is it that all those systems want? They aim to explain our teleological judgments about nature, and they set about this in such a manner that one party denies the truth of these judgments — thereby declaring them to be an idealism of nature (represented as art) — while the other party grants that they are true and promises to demonstrate the possibility of a nature ordered according to the idea of final causes.

1) The systems that contend for the idealism of final causes in nature do, on the one hand, allow within their principle a causality operating according to laws of motion (through which natural things exist purposively); yet they deny it any ***intentionality***, that is, {{{ 393 }}} they deny that it is deliberately determined toward this purposive production of theirs, or, put differently, that an end is the cause. This is Epicurus's mode of explanation, according to which the distinction between a technique of nature and mere mechanism is entirely disavowed, and blind chance is taken as the ground of explanation not merely for the agreement of the products brought forth with our concepts of an {{ 325 }} end — hence for the technique — but even for the determination of the causes of this production according to laws of motion, hence for its mechanism; so that nothing at all, not even the semblance in our teleological judgment, is explained, and consequently the alleged idealism therein is by no means established.

On the other hand, ***Spinoza*** would relieve us of all inquiry into the ground of the possibility of the ends of nature, and strip this idea of all reality, by letting them count not as products at all but as accidents inhering in an original being, and by ascribing to this being — as the substrate of those natural things — not causality but merely subsistence with respect to them, and (owing to the unconditioned necessity of this being together with all natural things as accidents inhering in it) by securing for the natural forms the unity of ground that is required for all purposiveness, while at the same time wresting away their contingency, without which no ***unity of ends*** can be conceived, and along with it everything ***intentional***, just as it deprives the original ground of natural things of all understanding.

But Spinozism does not achieve what it wants. It wants to specify a ground of explanation for the purposive connection (which it does not deny) among the things of nature, yet it merely cites the unity of the subject wherein they all inhere. Yet even if, for the beings of the world, {{ 326 }} one concedes to it this mode of existing as well, that ontological unity is still not thereby at once a ***unity of ends***, nor does it render such a unity in the least comprehensible. For the latter is a quite special kind of unity, one that does not follow at all from the connection of things (beings of the world) in one subject (the original being), but rather carries with it throughout the relation to a ***cause*** that possesses understanding, and even if one were to unite all these things in a simple subject, it would still never constitute a purposive relation: unless one thinks of them, first, as inner ***effects*** of the substance as a ***cause***, and, second, of that very substance as a cause acting ***through its understanding***. Lacking such formal conditions, every unity amounts to nothing but a natural necessity; and where it is ascribed all the same to things that we {{{ 394 }}} represent as lying apart from one another, it becomes a blind necessity. But if one wishes to call what the School terms the transcendental perfection of things (in relation to their own essence) — according to which all things have in themselves everything required to be this thing and no other — the purposiveness of nature: then this amounts to a childish playing with mere words in place of concepts. For should all things have to be conceived as ends, so that to be a thing and to be an end come to the same, then fundamentally nothing remains that would merit being represented as an end in any special sense.

From this one clearly sees: that Spinoza, by tracing our concepts {{ 327 }} of the purposive in nature back to the consciousness of our own selves within an all-encompassing (yet at the same time simple) being, and by seeking that form merely in the unity of the latter, must have intended to assert not the realism but merely the idealism of the purposiveness of these things, yet could not even accomplish this himself, because the bare representation of the substrate's unity is incapable of producing even the idea of a purposiveness that is merely unintentional.

2) Those who not only assert the ***realism*** of natural ends but also presume to explain it believe that they can, at least as regards its possibility, gain insight into a special kind of causality, namely that of intentionally acting causes; otherwise they could not undertake to explain those ends. For even to be entitled to the boldest hypothesis, the ***possibility*** of what one assumes as a ground must at least be ***certain***, and one must be able to secure objective reality for the concept of it.

But the possibility of a living matter (whose concept contains a contradiction, since lifelessness, _inertia_, constitutes its essential character) cannot even be thought; the possibility of an animated matter, and of the whole of nature as an animal, can be employed in a meager {{ 328 }} fashion only insofar (for the purpose of a hypothesis of purposiveness in nature at large) as it is revealed to us in experience through the organization of such matter on the small scale, but can by no means be had insight into _a priori_ as regards its possibility. A circle must therefore be committed in the explanation, if one wishes to derive the purposiveness of nature in organized beings from the life of matter, while in turn knowing this life only in organized beings, and thus without {{{ 395 }}} experience of that sort can form no concept of its possibility. Hylozoism, therefore, does not achieve what it promises.

***Theism***, finally, is equally incapable of grounding dogmatically the possibility of natural ends as a key to teleology; although it does have, over all the other grounds of explanation for that possibility, the advantage that, by means of an understanding which it ascribes to the original being, it best rescues the purposiveness of nature from idealism and introduces an intentional causality for its production.

For here it would first of all have to be proved, sufficiently for the determining power of judgment, that the unity of ends in matter is impossible through the mere mechanism of matter, in order to be entitled to posit the ground of that unity, in a determinate manner, beyond nature. But we can bring out nothing further than that, given the constitution and the limits of our cognitive faculties (inasmuch as we do not have insight into the first, inner ground even of this mechanism), we {{ 329 }} must in no way seek in matter a principle of determinate purposive relations, but that there remains for us no other way of judging the generation of its products as natural ends than by means of a supreme understanding as the cause of the world. This, however, is a ground only for the reflecting, not for the determining power of judgment, and can by no means entitle us to any objective assertion.
