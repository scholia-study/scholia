## {{{ 405 }}} § 77. On the peculiarity of the human understanding through which the concept of a natural end becomes possible for us

In the remark we set out certain peculiarities of our faculty of cognition (even of its higher branch) which we are all too easily led to transfer onto the things themselves as objective predicates; these, however, bear upon ideas to which experience can supply no adequate object {{ 345 }}, and which could then do no more than serve as regulative principles as we pursue the latter. As regards the concept of a natural end, the situation is admittedly the same as far as concerns the cause of the possibility of such a predicate—a cause that can reside only in the idea; and yet the consequence answering to that idea (the product itself) is genuinely given within nature, so that the concept of a causality on the part of the latter—of a being acting according to ends—appears to elevate the idea of a natural end into a constitutive principle of it: and herein lies something that marks it off from every other idea.

Yet what marks it off consists in this: that the idea just mentioned is a principle of reason not for the understanding but for the power of judgment, and is thus nothing more than the application of an understanding as such to possible objects of experience; and this, moreover, in just those cases where the judgment can be not determining but only reflecting, so that the object, though given in experience, cannot in accordance with the idea even be ***determinately*** (much less fully adequately) ***judged,*** but can only be made the subject of reflection.

It concerns, then, a peculiarity of ***our*** (human) understanding in relation to the power of judgment as the latter reflects upon things of nature. But if that is so, then what must underlie the matter here is the idea of some other possible understanding distinct from the human one {{ 346 }} (just as, in the Critique of Pure Reason, we had to entertain in thought another possible intuition, supposing ours was to be held to be a special kind—namely the kind for which objects hold good only as appearances), so that one might say: given the special makeup of our understanding, certain products of nature ***must*** be ***by us***, as far as their possibility goes, ***regarded,*** as brought forth deliberately and as ends, though without thereby insisting that some special {{{ 406 }}} cause, one having the representation of an end for its determining ground, actually exists, and hence without disputing that an understanding other (and higher) than the human one might, even within nature's mechanism—that is, within a causal linkage for which no understanding is assumed as the sole cause—light upon that which grounds the possibility of such natural products.

What is at issue here, then, is how ***our*** understanding stands toward the power of judgment—namely, that in this relation we look for a certain contingency in the makeup of our own faculty, in order to mark out this peculiarity of our understanding as against other understandings that are possible.

This contingency shows itself quite naturally in the ***particular*** which the power of judgment is supposed to place beneath the ***universal*** furnished by the understanding's concepts; for the particular is left undetermined by the universal belonging to ***our*** (human) understanding; and it is a contingent matter in how many {{ 347 }} diverse ways things that nevertheless converge in a shared mark may present themselves to our perception. Our understanding is a power of concepts—that is, a discursive understanding—for which it is bound to be contingent of what kind, and how greatly varied, the particular may be that nature offers to it and that admits of being brought beneath its concepts. But since cognition calls for intuition as well, and since a power possessing a ***complete spontaneity of intuition*** would be a faculty of cognition set apart from sensibility and wholly independent of it—hence an understanding in the widest sense of the word—one may likewise conceive an ***intuitive*** understanding (negatively, that is, merely as one that is not discursive), which does not travel from the universal down to the particular and thereby to the individual (by way of concepts), and for which one does not meet with that contingency in the way nature, in its products, accords with the understanding under ***particular*** laws—the very contingency that makes it so difficult for ours to gather the manifold of those products into the unity of cognition; a labor that ours can carry through only by the agreement, itself highly contingent, of nature's marks with our power of concepts, whereas an intuiting understanding requires nothing of the sort.

Our understanding therefore has, with regard to the power of judgment, this feature of its own: that in {{ 348 }} the cognition it affords, the universal does not determine the particular, so that the latter cannot be derived from the former on its own; and yet this particular, amid the manifold of nature {{{ 407 }}}, is supposed to accord with the universal (by way of concepts and laws) so as to admit of subsumption beneath it—an accord which, in such circumstances, is bound to be highly contingent and, for the power of judgment, devoid of any determinate principle.

Now in order, even so, to be able at the very least to conceive the possibility of such an accord between the things of nature and the power of judgment (an accord we represent as contingent, and hence as possible only through an end aimed at it), we have at the same time to conceive another understanding, in reference to which—and indeed antecedently to any end attributed to it—we may represent as ***necessary*** that accord of nature's laws with our power of judgment which, for our own understanding, is thinkable only through ends as the connecting means.

Our understanding, namely, has the property that, in cognizing something—say, the cause of a product—it is obliged to move from the ***analytically universal*** (from concepts) toward the particular (the empirical intuition that is given); and here, with respect to the manifoldness of the latter, it settles nothing, but must instead expect that determination—which falls to the power of judgment {{ 349 }}—from the subsumption of the empirical intuition (where the object is a product of nature) beneath the concept. But we can equally conceive an understanding which, not being discursive like ours but intuitive, advances from the ***synthetically universal*** (the intuition of a whole taken as a whole) toward the particular—that is, from the whole down to its parts; an understanding, therefore, whose representation of the whole does not carry within itself the ***contingency*** with which the parts are combined, a contingency needed in order to render a determinate form of the whole possible, such as our understanding stands in need of, obliged as it is to proceed from the parts—taken as universally conceived grounds—toward the various possible forms, as consequences, that are to be subsumed beneath them. Given the makeup of our understanding, by contrast, a real whole of nature can only be viewed as the effect of the several moving forces of the parts acting in concert. Suppose, then, that we wish to represent the possibility of the whole not as resting on the parts, in the manner suited to our discursive understanding, but rather—following the pattern of the intuitive (archetypal) one—the possibility of the parts (in their makeup and combination) as resting on the whole: then, owing to this very same peculiarity of our understanding, it cannot come about that the whole should furnish the ground on which the linkage of the parts is possible (which in the discursive mode of cognition would be a contradiction), but only that the {{{ 408 }}} ***representation*** of a whole should contain the ground of the possibility both of its form {{ 350 }} and of the linkage of parts that belongs to it. But since the whole would in that case be an effect, a ***product***, whose ***representation*** is looked upon as the ***cause*** of its possibility—whereas one calls a product an end when the cause producing it has for its determining ground nothing but the representation of its own effect: from this it follows that it is solely a corollary of the special makeup of our understanding if we represent products of nature as possible under a kind of causality other than that of the natural laws of matter, namely under that of ends and final causes alone; and that this principle bears not on the possibility of such things themselves (even taken as phenomena) under this manner of production, but only on the judging of them that is open to our understanding. And here we grasp, at the same time, why in the science of nature we remain so far from content with an explanation of nature's products through a causality according to ends: it is because in such an explanation we are asking to judge natural production merely as suited to our capacity for judging it—that is, to the reflecting power of judgment—rather than as suited to the things themselves on behalf of the determining power of judgment. Nor is there any need, in all this, to establish that an _intellectus archetypus_ of that kind is really possible; it is enough that, in setting it over against our own discursive understanding, which has need of images (_intellectus ectypus_), and against the {{ 351 }} contingency of a makeup of that sort, we are led to the idea in question (that of an _intellectus archetypus_), and that this idea, for its part, harbors no contradiction.

If we now look upon a whole of matter, as regards its form, as a product of the parts together with their forces and their capacity to combine of their own accord (further kinds of matter, which the parts bring to one another, being supplied in thought): then what we picture to ourselves is a mechanical manner of generating it. But no concept of a whole as an end issues from such a route—a concept whose inner possibility depends through and through on the idea of a whole, an idea on which the very makeup and manner of operation of the parts depends, exactly as we are bound to picture an organized body. From this, however—as has this moment been shown—it does not follow that the mechanical generation of such a body is impossible; for to say that would be to say that it is impossible (that is, self-contradictory) ***for any understanding*** to picture such a unity in the linkage of the manifold unless the idea of that unity were at once the cause producing it, that is, unless there were deliberate production. And yet this would {{{ 409 }}} really follow, were we entitled to view material beings as things existing in themselves. For the unity making up the ground on which natural formations are possible would in that case amount to nothing more than the unity of space—which, however, is no real ground of the productions but {{ 352 }} merely their formal condition; though it does resemble the real ground we are after in this respect, that within it no part can be determined except in relation to the whole (whose representation, accordingly, lies at the basis of the parts' possibility). But since it is at any rate feasible to treat the material world as sheer appearance and to think of something as a thing in itself (which is not appearance) by way of a substratum, and to place beneath this substratum a corresponding intellectual intuition (even one that is not ours): then there would be present, for the nature to which we ourselves belong, a supersensible real ground—unknowable though it is for us—in which nature we should accordingly regard whatever within it is necessary as an object of the senses according to mechanical laws, but the accord and unity of the particular laws, and of the forms conforming to them—forms we are bound to judge contingent with respect to the former—we should regard, in that same nature as an object of reason (indeed the whole of nature as a system), according to teleological laws as well, judging it by two sorts of principle, without the mechanical mode of explanation being shut out by the teleological, as if the two were at odds with each other.

From this, too, one can discern what otherwise, though readily enough surmised, could hardly be maintained and proved with any certainty, {{ 353 }} namely that while the principle for deriving purposive natural products mechanically may indeed stand alongside the teleological one, it can in no way make the latter dispensable: that is to say, with a thing we are obliged to judge to be a natural end (an organized being) one may certainly try out every law of mechanical generation, whether already known or still to be discovered, and may even hope to make good headway with them, yet one can never be excused, for the possibility of such a product, from appealing to a ground of production wholly different from these—namely, causality through ends; and no human reason whatever (nor any finite reason resembling ours in quality, however far it might outstrip it in degree) can hope to make the generation of so much as a single blade of grass intelligible from purely mechanical causes. For if the teleological linkage of causes and {{{ 410 }}} effects is altogether indispensable to the power of judgment where the possibility of such an object is concerned—even merely in order to study that object along the guiding thread of experience; if, for outer objects taken as appearances, no sufficient ground bearing on ends is to be met with at all, but this ground, which likewise lies within nature, must after all be sought only in nature's supersensible substratum, from which every possible insight is nonetheless barred to us: then it is flatly impossible for us to draw, out of nature itself, grounds of explanation for {{ 354 }} connections directed to ends, and it becomes necessary, given the makeup of the human faculty of cognition, to seek the highest ground of this within an original understanding acting as the world-cause.
