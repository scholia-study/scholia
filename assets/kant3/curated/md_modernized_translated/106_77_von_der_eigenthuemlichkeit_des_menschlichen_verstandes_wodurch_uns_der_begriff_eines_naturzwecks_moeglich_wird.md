## {{{ 405 }}} § 77. On the peculiarity of the human understanding through which the concept of a natural end becomes possible for us

In the remark we set out certain peculiarities of our faculty of cognition (even of its higher branch) which we are all too easily led to transfer onto the things themselves as objective predicates; these, however, bear upon ideas to which experience can supply no adequate object {{ 345 }}, and which could then do no more than serve as regulative principles as we pursue the latter. With the concept of a natural end matters stand just the same, as far as concerns the cause of the possibility of such a predicate, a cause that can lie nowhere but in the idea; yet the effect that corresponds to this idea (the product itself) is after all given in nature, and the concept of a causality belonging to nature—of a being acting in accordance with ends—seems to make of the idea of a natural end a constitutive principle for it: and therein it holds something that distinguishes it from every other idea.

But this distinguishing feature lies in the following: that the idea in question is a principle of reason belonging not to the understanding but rather to the power of judgment, and hence amounts merely to the application of an understanding in general to possible objects of experience; and this specifically where a judgment can be not determining but only reflecting, so that although the object is indeed given in experience, in accordance with the idea it can never even be ***determinately*** (let alone with full adequacy) ***judged,*** but can only be reflected upon.

It concerns, then, a peculiarity of ***our*** (human) understanding in relation to the power of judgment as the latter reflects upon things of nature. But if that is the case, then the idea of another possible understanding, one distinct from the human, must lie at the basis here {{ 346 }} (just as the Critique of Pure Reason obliged us to hold in mind another possible intuition, on the supposition that ours was to count as a particular kind—namely the kind for which objects are valid merely as appearances), so that one may say: in keeping with the special constitution of our understanding, certain natural products ***must*** be ***by us***, in respect of their possibility, ***regarded,*** as produced intentionally and as ends, yet without on that account demanding that there really exist some particular {{{ 406 }}} cause having the representation of an end for its determining ground, and hence without denying that an understanding other (and higher) than the human might, even within nature's mechanism—that is, within a causal connection for which an understanding is not exclusively assumed as its cause—come upon the ground of the possibility of such products of nature.

What is at issue here, then, is how ***our*** understanding stands toward the power of judgment—namely, that in this relation we look for a certain contingency in the makeup of our own faculty, in order to mark out this peculiarity of our understanding as against other understandings that are possible.

This contingency shows itself quite naturally in the ***particular*** which the power of judgment is supposed to place beneath the ***universal*** furnished by the understanding's concepts; for the particular is left undetermined by the universal belonging to ***our*** (human) understanding; and it is a contingent matter in how many {{ 347 }} diverse ways things that nevertheless converge in a shared mark may present themselves to our perception. Our understanding is a power of concepts—that is, a discursive understanding—for which it is bound to be contingent of what kind, and how greatly varied, the particular may be that nature offers to it and that admits of being brought beneath its concepts. But since cognition calls for intuition as well, and since a power possessing a ***complete spontaneity of intuition*** would be a faculty of cognition set apart from sensibility and wholly independent of it—hence an understanding in the widest sense of the word—one may likewise conceive an ***intuitive*** understanding (negatively, that is, merely as one that is not discursive), which does not travel from the universal down to the particular and thereby to the individual (by way of concepts), and for which one does not meet with that contingency in the way nature, in its products, accords with the understanding under ***particular*** laws—the very contingency that makes it so difficult for ours to gather the manifold of those products into the unity of cognition; a labor that ours can carry through only by the agreement, itself highly contingent, of nature's marks with our power of concepts, whereas an intuiting understanding requires nothing of the sort.

Our understanding therefore has, with regard to the power of judgment, this feature of its own: that in {{ 348 }} the cognition it affords, the universal does not determine the particular, so that the latter cannot be derived from the former on its own; and yet this particular, amid the manifold of nature {{{ 407 }}}, is supposed to accord with the universal (by way of concepts and laws) so as to admit of subsumption beneath it—an accord which, in such circumstances, is bound to be highly contingent and, for the power of judgment, devoid of any determinate principle.

Now in order, nonetheless, at least to be able to think it possible that the things of nature should agree in this manner with the power of judgment (an agreement we represent as contingent, and hence as possible only through an end directed toward it), we must at the same time think another understanding, in relation to which—and indeed prior to any end ascribed to it—we are able to represent as ***necessary*** that agreement of the natural laws with our power of judgment which for our understanding is thinkable only through ends as the means of connection.

Our understanding, for its part, has this property: that in its cognition—of the cause of a product, for instance—it must proceed from the ***analytically universal*** (from concepts) to the particular (the given empirical intuition); whereby, as regards the manifoldness of the latter, it determines nothing, but must instead await that determination, a task belonging to the power of judgment {{ 349 }}, expecting it to arise once the empirical intuition (should the object be a natural product) has been brought under the concept. But we can equally conceive an understanding which, not being discursive like ours but intuitive, advances from the ***synthetically universal*** (the intuition of a whole taken as a whole) toward the particular—that is, from the whole down to its parts; an understanding, therefore, whose representation of the whole does not carry within itself the ***contingency*** with which the parts are combined, a contingency needed in order to render a determinate form of the whole possible, such as our understanding stands in need of, obliged as it is to proceed from the parts—taken as universally conceived grounds—toward the various possible forms, as consequences, that are to be subsumed beneath them. In keeping with how our understanding is constituted, on the other hand, a real whole of nature is to be regarded solely as the effect of the concurring moving forces of its parts. Suppose, then, that we wish to represent the possibility of the whole not as resting on the parts, in the manner suited to our discursive understanding, but rather—following the pattern of the intuitive (archetypal) one—the possibility of the parts (in their makeup and combination) as resting on the whole: then, owing to this very same peculiarity of our understanding, it cannot come about that the whole should furnish the ground on which the linkage of the parts is possible (which in the discursive mode of cognition would be a contradiction), but only that the {{{ 408 }}} ***representation*** of a whole should contain the ground of the possibility both of its form {{ 350 }} and of the linkage of parts that belongs to it. But since the whole, on this account, would then be an effect, a ***product***, whose ***representation*** is regarded as the ***cause*** of its possibility—while a product is termed an end when the cause of it has for its determining ground nothing beyond the representation of its effect: it follows from this that it is merely a consequence of the peculiar constitution of our understanding when we picture products of nature as possible through a species of causality distinct from that of the natural laws of matter, namely through that of ends and final causes alone; and that this principle concerns not the possibility of such things in themselves (even considered as phenomena) under this mode of generation, but only the appraisal of them that is possible for our understanding. And here we grasp, at the same time, why in the science of nature we remain so far from content with an explanation of nature's products through a causality according to ends: it is because in such an explanation we are asking to judge natural production merely as suited to our capacity for judging it—that is, to the reflecting power of judgment—rather than as suited to the things themselves on behalf of the determining power of judgment. Nor is there any need, in all this, to establish that an _intellectus archetypus_ of that kind is really possible; it is enough that, in setting it over against our own discursive understanding, which has need of images (_intellectus ectypus_), and against the {{ 351 }} contingency of a makeup of that sort, we are led to the idea in question (that of an _intellectus archetypus_), and that this idea, for its part, harbors no contradiction.

If we now look upon a whole of matter, as regards its form, as a product of the parts together with their forces and their capacity to combine of their own accord (further kinds of matter, which the parts bring to one another, being supplied in thought): then what we picture to ourselves is a mechanical manner of generating it. But no concept of a whole as an end issues from such a route—a concept whose inner possibility depends through and through on the idea of a whole, an idea on which the very makeup and manner of operation of the parts depends, exactly as we are bound to picture an organized body. From this, though—as was just demonstrated—it in no way follows that a mechanical generation of such a body would be impossible; for that would be tantamount to asserting that such a unity in the linkage of the manifold is impossible (that is, contradictory) ***for any understanding*** to represent to itself unless the idea of that unity were at once its generating cause, in other words unless production were intentional. And yet this would {{{ 409 }}} really follow, were we entitled to view material beings as things existing in themselves. For in that case the unity constituting the ground of the possibility of nature's formations would be nothing but the unity of space, which, however, is no real ground of what is generated but {{ 352 }} merely the formal condition thereof; though it bears some resemblance to the real ground we seek, in that within it not a single part admits of determination save through its relation to the whole (whose representation therefore underlies the possibility of the parts). Yet since it is at all events possible to view the material world as mere appearance and to conceive something as a thing in itself (one that is not appearance) serving as a substratum, and to lay beneath this substratum a corresponding intellectual intuition (granted that it is not our own): there would then obtain, for the nature to which we ourselves belong, a supersensible real ground—albeit one unknowable to us—within which nature we would accordingly consider whatever is necessary in it as an object of the senses under mechanical laws, but the harmony and unity of the particular laws, together with the forms answering to them—forms that, with respect to those laws, we must judge to be contingent—we would consider, in that same nature taken as an object of reason (indeed, nature as a whole as a system), under teleological laws besides, appraising it by two kinds of principle, without the mechanical manner of explanation being excluded by the teleological, as though the two contradicted one another.

From this, too, one can discern what otherwise, though readily enough surmised, could hardly be maintained and proved with any certainty, {{ 353 }} namely that while the principle for deriving purposive natural products mechanically may indeed stand alongside the teleological one, it can in no way make the latter dispensable: that is to say, with a thing we are obliged to judge to be a natural end (an organized being) one may certainly try out every law of mechanical generation, whether already known or still to be discovered, and may even hope to make good headway with them, yet one can never be excused, for the possibility of such a product, from appealing to a ground of production wholly different from these—namely, causality through ends; and no human reason whatever (nor any finite reason resembling ours in quality, however far it might outstrip it in degree) can hope to make the generation of so much as a single blade of grass intelligible from purely mechanical causes. For if the teleological linkage of causes and {{{ 410 }}} effects is altogether indispensable to the power of judgment where the possibility of such an object is concerned—even merely in order to study that object along the guiding thread of experience; if, for outer objects taken as appearances, no sufficient ground bearing on ends is to be met with at all, but this ground, which likewise lies within nature, must after all be sought only in nature's supersensible substratum, from which every possible insight is nonetheless barred to us: then it is flatly impossible for us to draw, out of nature itself, grounds of explanation for {{ 354 }} connections directed to ends, and it becomes necessary, given the makeup of the human faculty of cognition, to seek the highest ground of this within an original understanding acting as the world-cause.
