## {{{ 405 }}} § 77. On the Peculiarity of the Human Understanding, by Means of Which the Concept of a Natural End Becomes Possible for Us

We have adduced in the remark peculiarities of our (even the higher) cognitive faculty, which we are easily tempted to transfer to the things themselves as objective predicates; but they concern ideas to which no corresponding object can be given in experience {{ 345 }}, and which could then serve only as regulative principles in the pursuit of the latter. To be sure, it is the same with the concept of a natural end as regards the cause of the possibility of such a predicate, which can lie only in the idea; but the consequence corresponding to it (the product itself) is nevertheless given in nature, and the concept of a causality of the latter, as of a being acting according to ends, seems to make the idea of a natural end into a constitutive principle of the same: and in this it has something that distinguishes it from all other ideas.

But this distinguishing feature consists in this: that the thought idea is not a principle of reason for the understanding, but for the power of judgment, and thus merely the application of an understanding in general to possible objects of experience; and indeed where the judgment cannot be determining, but merely reflecting, and thus the object is indeed given in experience, but concerning it, in accordance with the idea, judgment can by no means be ***determinately*** made (let alone fully adequately), but rather it can only be reflected upon.

It therefore concerns a peculiarity of ***our*** (human) understanding in respect of the power of judgment in its reflection on things of nature. But if that is so, then there must lie at the basis here the idea of an understanding other than the human one {{ 346 }} (just as in the Critique of Pure Reason we had to have in thought another possible intuition, if ours was to be regarded as a special kind, namely that for which objects hold only as appearances), so that one could say: certain natural products ***must***, according to the special constitution of our understanding, ***be considered by us*** as regards their possibility as generated intentionally and as ends, without yet thereby demanding that there really is a special {{{ 406 }}} cause which has the representation of an end as its determining ground, hence without denying that another (higher) understanding than the human one might find the ground of the possibility of such products of nature even in the mechanism of nature, i.e., in a causal connection for which an understanding is not exclusively assumed as cause.

It therefore depends here on the relation of ***our*** understanding to the power of judgment, namely, that we seek therein a certain contingency of the constitution of our own, in order to mark this peculiarity of our understanding in distinction from other possible ones.

This contingency is found quite naturally in the ***particular***, which the power of judgment is to bring under the ***universal*** of the concepts of understanding; for by the universal of ***our*** (human) understanding the particular is not determined; and it is contingent in how many different {{ 347 }} ways distinct things, which nevertheless agree in a common characteristic, can come before our perception. Our understanding is a faculty of concepts, i.e., a discursive understanding, for which it must indeed be contingent what kind and how very different the particular may be that can be given to it in nature and that can be brought under its concepts. But because intuition also belongs to cognition, and a faculty of a ***complete spontaneity of intuition*** would be a cognitive faculty distinct from sensibility and completely independent of it, and thus understanding in the most general signification: one can therefore also think an ***intuitive*** understanding (negatively, namely merely as non-discursive), which does not go from the universal to the particular and thus to the individual (through concepts), and for which that contingency of the agreement of nature in its products according to ***particular*** laws with the understanding is not encountered, which makes it so difficult for ours to bring the manifold thereof to the unity of cognition; a business that ours can bring about only through the agreement of natural characteristics with our faculty of concepts, which is very contingent, but of which an intuiting understanding has no need.

Our understanding therefore has this peculiarity for the power of judgment, that in {{ 348 }} cognition by means of it the particular is not determined by the universal, and this therefore cannot be derived from the latter alone; but nevertheless this particular in the manifold of nature {{{ 407 }}} is to agree with the universal (through concepts and laws) in order to be able to be subsumed under it, which agreement under such circumstances must be very contingent and without a determinate principle for the power of judgment.

In order, nevertheless, to be able at least to think the possibility of such an agreement of the things of nature with the power of judgment (which we represent as contingent, hence as possible only through an end directed thereto), we must at the same time think another understanding, in relation to which—and indeed prior to any end attributed to it—we can represent as ***necessary*** that agreement of natural laws with our power of judgment, which for our understanding is thinkable only through the connecting medium of ends.

For our understanding has the property that in its cognition, e.g., of the cause of a product, it must go from the ***analytical-universal*** (from concepts) to the particular (the given empirical intuition); whereby it therefore determines nothing in respect of the manifold of the latter, but must expect this determination for the power of judgment {{ 349 }} from the subsumption of the empirical intuition (if the object is a product of nature) under the concept. Now, however, we can also think an understanding which, because it is not discursive like ours but intuitive, goes from the ***synthetic-universal*** (the intuition of a whole as such) to the particular, i.e., from the whole to the parts; which therefore, and whose representation of the whole, does not contain within itself the ***contingency*** of the connection of the parts in order to make possible a determinate form of the whole, which our understanding requires, which must proceed from the parts as universally thought grounds to different possible forms, as consequences, to be subsumed under them. According to the constitution of our understanding, on the contrary, a real whole of nature is to be regarded only as the effect of the concurrent moving forces of the parts. If we therefore wish to represent to ourselves, not the possibility of the whole as depending on the parts, as is in accordance with our discursive understanding, but rather, in accordance with the intuitive (archetypal) understanding, the possibility of the parts (as regards their constitution and connection) as depending on the whole: then, according to that very same peculiarity of our understanding, this cannot happen in such a way that the whole contains the ground of the possibility of the connection of the parts (which in the discursive mode of cognition would be a contradiction), but only that the {{{ 408 }}} ***representation*** of a whole contains the ground of the possibility of the form of the same {{ 350 }} and of the connection of the parts belonging thereto. But since the whole would then be an effect, a ***product***, the ***representation*** of which is regarded as the ***cause*** of its possibility, and the product of a cause whose determining ground is merely the representation of its effect is called an end: it follows therefrom that it is merely a consequence of the special constitution of our understanding if we represent products of nature as possible for us according to another kind of causality than that of the natural laws of matter, namely only according to that of ends and final causes, and that this principle does not concern the possibility of such things themselves (even regarded as phenomena) according to this mode of generation, but only the judgment of them that is possible for our understanding. Whereby we at the same time see why we are far from satisfied in natural science with an explanation of the products of nature through causality according to ends, because, namely, we demand in it to judge natural generation merely in a manner adequate to our faculty for judging it, i.e., to the reflecting power of judgment, and not to the things themselves for the benefit of the determining power of judgment. In this connection, it is also not at all necessary to prove that such an _intellectus archetypus_ is possible, but only that we are led to that idea (of an _intellectus archetypus_) by contrasting our discursive understanding, which stands in need of images (_intellectus ectypus_), and the {{ 351 }} contingency of such a constitution, and that this idea contains no contradiction.

If we now consider a whole of matter as regards its form as a product of the parts and of their forces and faculties of combining by themselves (adding other matters that they supply to one another): we then represent to ourselves a mechanical mode of its generation. But in such a way there emerges no concept of a whole as an end, the internal possibility of which absolutely presupposes the idea of a whole on which the very constitution and mode of action of the parts depend, as we must nevertheless represent to ourselves an organized body. But from this it does not follow, as has just been shown, that the mechanical generation of such a body is impossible; for that would be as much as to say that it is impossible (i.e., contradictory) ***for any understanding*** to represent to itself such a unity in the connection of the manifold without the idea of it being at the same time its generating cause, i.e., without intentional production. Nevertheless, this would {{{ 409 }}} indeed follow if we were entitled to regard material beings as things in themselves. For then the unity which constitutes the ground of the possibility of natural formations would be merely the unity of space, which, however, is not a real ground of generations but {{ 352 }} only the formal condition thereof; although it has some similarity with the real ground we are seeking in that in it no part can be determined except in relation to the whole (the representation of which therefore lies at the basis of the possibility of the parts). But since it is nevertheless at least possible to regard the material world as mere appearance, and to think something as a thing in itself (which is not appearance) as substrate, and to underlay this with a corresponding intellectual intuition (even if it is not ours): a supersensible real ground, although unknowable for us, would then obtain for the nature to which we ourselves belong, in which we would therefore consider what in it is necessary as an object of the senses according to mechanical laws, but at the same time consider the agreement and unity of the particular laws and of the forms according to them—which we must judge as contingent in respect of the former—in it as an object of reason (indeed the whole of nature as a system) according to teleological laws, and would judge it according to two kinds of principles, without the mechanical mode of explanation being excluded by the teleological one, as if they contradicted each other.

From this we can also see that which one could otherwise indeed easily suspect, but could hardly assert and prove with certainty, {{ 353 }} namely, that although the principle of a mechanical derivation of purposive natural products can consist alongside the teleological one, it could by no means make the latter superfluous: i.e., in a thing which we must judge as a natural end (an organized being), we can indeed try all known and still to be discovered laws of mechanical generation and may even hope to make good progress therewith, but we can never be excused from appealing to a ground of generation entirely distinct from them, namely causality through ends, for the possibility of such a product; and absolutely no human reason (nor any finite reason which in quality were similar to ours, however much it might exceed it in degree) can hope to understand the generation of even a single blade of grass from merely mechanical causes. For if the teleological connection of causes and {{{ 410 }}} effects is absolutely indispensable for the power of judgment for the possibility of such an object, even in order merely to study it on the guide-line of experience; if for external objects as appearances a sufficient ground relating to ends cannot be encountered at all, but this ground, which also lies in nature, must nevertheless be sought only in the supersensible substrate of the latter, from which, however, all possible insight is cut off from us: then it is absolutely impossible for us to draw explanatory grounds for {{ 354 }} connections of ends from nature itself, and it is necessary, according to the constitution of the human cognitive faculty, to seek the supreme ground for this in an original understanding as the cause of the world.
