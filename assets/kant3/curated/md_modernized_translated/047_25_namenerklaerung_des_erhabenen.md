## § 25. Nominal definition of the sublime

That which is ***absolutely great*** is what we call ***sublime***. Yet being great and being a magnitude are entirely distinct concepts (_magnitudo_ and _quantitas_). Likewise, to ***simply*** (_simpliciter_) ***say*** that something is great is quite another matter than saying that it is ***absolutely great*** {{ 81 }} (_absolute, non comparative magnum_). This last is what ***is great beyond all comparison***. — But what is the expression really getting at when it says that a thing is great, or small, or middling? What gets designated by it is no pure concept of the understanding; even less an intuition of the senses; and equally little a concept of reason, for it brings along with it no principle of cognition at all. It has to be, then, a concept proper to the power of judgment, or one derived from such a concept, and must set beneath it, as its ground, a subjective purposiveness belonging to the representation with respect to the power of judgment. That a thing amounts to a magnitude (_quantum_) can be discerned from the object on its own, apart from any comparison with others: namely, whenever a plurality of what is homogeneous together makes up a single unit. ***How great*** it is, however, always requires something else, which is likewise a magnitude, to serve as its measure. Since, however, in appraising magnitude what matters is not only the plurality (number) but likewise the greatness of the unit (the measure), while the greatness of that unit once more calls for some further thing to serve as a measure by which it might be compared: we come to see that every determination of magnitude in appearances can yield no absolute concept of a magnitude at all, but on each occasion only a concept of comparison.

Now if I say simply that something is great, it appears that I have no comparison in mind at all, at least none with any {{ 82 }} objective measure, since by this it is in no way settled how large the object may be. Yet even though the scale of comparison is only subjective, the judgment none the less lays claim to universal agreement; the judgments "the man is beautiful" and "he is tall" do not confine themselves merely to the subject who judges, but, like theoretical judgments, require the agreement of everyone.

{{{ 249 }}} But because a judgment through which a thing is designated outright as great does not merely intend to say that the object has a magnitude, but ascribes this magnitude to it at the same time by preference over many others of like kind — without, though, specifying that preference in any determinate way — there is indeed laid at its foundation a standard that one assumes may be accepted as one and the same for every person, a standard useful for no logical (mathematically determined) estimation of magnitude, but serviceable solely for an aesthetic one, being a measure that rests as its ground merely subjectively upon the judgment reflecting about magnitude. For the rest it may be empirical — such as the middling size of the human beings known to us, of animals of a certain kind, of trees, houses, mountains, and the like; or else a measure given _a priori_, one that, on account of the shortcomings of the subject doing the judging, is held to subjective conditions for its exhibition in concreto: thus, in practical affairs, the greatness of some particular virtue, or of the public freedom and justice within a country; {{ 83 }} or, in theoretical affairs, how great the correctness or incorrectness of an observation or measurement that has been made may be, and the like.

What is noteworthy here is this: that although we take no interest at all in the object — that is, its existence leaves us indifferent — the sheer magnitude of it, even where it is looked upon as formless, is still able to bring with it a satisfaction communicable to all, and thereby holds within it the consciousness of a subjective purposiveness in how we put our cognitive faculties to use; yet this is not a satisfaction taken in the object, as happens with the beautiful (given that the thing may lack form), a case in which the reflecting power of judgment discovers itself purposively disposed toward cognition as such, but rather a satisfaction taken in the imagination's own enlargement of itself.

Whenever we (under the qualification mentioned above) declare of an object outright that it is great, what we pass is no mathematically determining judgment, but a mere judgment of reflection upon the representation of that object, a representation subjectively purposive for a certain use of our powers of cognition in the appraisal of magnitude; and we thereupon always join to the representation a kind of respect, just as, with what we plainly name small, a kind of contempt. For the rest, the judging of things as great or small applies to everything whatsoever, even to all the qualities they possess; which is why we speak even of beauty as great or small: the reason for this {{{ 250 }}} being that whatever we may, following the prescription of the power of judgment, {{ 84 }} present in intuition (and thus represent aesthetically) is, without exception, appearance, and consequently a quantum as well.

But when we name a thing not merely great, but great in an unqualified, absolute way, in every regard (past all comparison) — that is, sublime — one quickly grasps this: that for it we allow no fitting standard to be looked for anywhere outside the thing, but only inside it. It is a magnitude equal to nothing beyond its own self. It follows from this, then, that the sublime is to be looked for not among the things of nature but only within our ideas; yet in which of these it may lie must be reserved for the deduction.

The explanation given above may also be put thus: ***Sublime is that in comparison with which everything else is small.*** Here it is readily seen: that nothing can be given in nature, however great we may judge it to be, which, viewed in a different relation, could not be diminished to the point of the infinitely small; and, conversely, nothing so small that, set beside yet smaller standards, it could not, for our imagination, be magnified up to the size of a world. Telescopes have placed in our hands abundant matter for making the former remark, microscopes for the latter. Nothing, then, capable of being an object of the senses is, regarded on this {{ 85 }} footing, to be called sublime. But for just this reason — that our imagination harbors an endeavor to press onward into the infinite, whereas our reason puts forward a demand for absolute totality as for a genuinely real idea — that very unfitness of our capacity for appraising the magnitude of the things of the world of sense becomes, with regard to this idea, the awakening of the feeling of a supersensible capacity within us; and it is the use which the power of judgment naturally makes of certain objects on behalf of the latter (feeling), and not the object of sense, that is absolutely great, while against it every other use is small. Hence it is the mental attunement produced by a certain representation occupying the reflecting power of judgment, and not the object, that deserves to be called sublime.

We may accordingly append to the foregoing formulas for the explanation of the sublime this further one: ***Sublime is that of which the very ability to think it proves a power of the mind exceeding every measure*** ***of sense.***
