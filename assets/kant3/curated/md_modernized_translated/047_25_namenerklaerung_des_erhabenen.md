## § 25. Definition of the name of the sublime

We call ***sublime*** that which is ***absolutely great***. But to be great and to be a magnitude are entirely different concepts (_magnitudo_ and _quantitas_). Likewise, to ***say simply*** (_simpliciter_) that something is great is also something entirely other than to say that it is ***absolutely great*** {{ 81 }} (_absolute, non comparative magnum_). The latter is that ***which is great beyond all comparison***. — But what now does the expression that something is great, or small, or middling mean to say? It is no pure concept of the understanding that is thereby designated; still less an intuition of sense; and just as little a concept of reason, because it carries with it no principle of cognition at all. It must therefore be a concept of the power of judgment, or stem from such a one, and lay at the ground a subjective purposiveness of the representation in relation to the power of judgment. That something is a magnitude (_quantum_) can be cognized from the thing itself without any comparison with others: namely if a multiplicity of the homogeneous together constitutes a unit. But ***how great*** it is always requires something else, which is also a magnitude, for its measure. But because in the judging of magnitude it depends not merely on the multiplicity (number) but also on the magnitude of the unit (the measure), and the magnitude of this latter again always requires something else as a measure with which it can be compared: we see that all determination of the magnitude of appearances can absolutely yield no absolute concept of a magnitude, but always only a comparative concept.

If now I simply say that something is great, then it seems that I have no comparison in mind at all, at least with no {{ 82 }} objective measure, because thereby it is not at all determined how great the object is. But although the standard of comparison is merely subjective, the judgment nonetheless lays claim to universal assent; the judgments "the man is beautiful" and "he is great" do not restrict themselves merely to the judging subject, but, like theoretical judgments, demand the assent of everyone.

{{{ 249 }}} But because in a judgment by which something is simply designated as great it is meant to be said not merely that the object has a magnitude, but this is at the same time ascribed to it preferentially before many others of the same kind, without however determinately specifying this preference: there is indeed laid at its ground a standard that one presupposes one can assume to be the very same for everyone, but which is usable for no logical (mathematically determinate) but only an aesthetic judging of magnitude, because it is a standard lying merely subjectively at the ground of the judgment reflecting about magnitude. It may, moreover, be empirical, like, for instance, the mean magnitude of the human beings known to us, of animals of a certain kind, of trees, houses, mountains, and the like; or a standard given _a priori_, which through the deficiencies of the judging subject is restricted to subjective conditions of the presentation in concreto: as, in the practical, the magnitude of a certain virtue, or of public freedom and justice in a country; {{ 83 }} or, in the theoretical, the magnitude of the correctness or incorrectness of an observation or measurement that has been made, and the like.

Here it is now remarkable: that, even though we have no interest at all in the object, i.e., its existence is indifferent to us, yet the mere magnitude of it, even when it is considered as formless, can carry with it a satisfaction that is universally communicable, hence contains a consciousness of a subjective purposiveness in the use of our faculties of cognition; but not, say, a satisfaction in the object, as in the case of the beautiful (because it can be formless), where the reflecting power of judgment finds itself purposively attuned in relation to cognition in general, but rather in the enlargement of the imagination in itself.

If we (under the above-named restriction) say of an object simply that it is great: then this is no mathematically determining but a mere judgment of reflection about the representation of it, which is subjectively purposive for a certain use of our powers of cognition in the estimation of magnitude; and we then always connect with the representation a kind of respect, just as with that which we simply call small, a contempt. Moreover, the judging of things as great or small extends to everything, even to all the qualities of them; hence we call even beauty great or small: the ground of which {{{ 250 }}} is to be sought in this, that whatever we may present in intuition according to the prescription of the power of judgment {{ 84 }} (hence represent aesthetically) is one and all appearance, hence also a quantum.

But if we call something not merely great, but absolutely, in every respect (beyond all comparison) great, i.e., sublime, then one soon sees: that we permit no standard adequate to it to be sought outside it, but merely in it. It is a magnitude that is equal only to itself. That the sublime is therefore to be sought not in the things of nature but solely in our ideas follows from this; but in which of these it lies must be reserved for the deduction.

The above explanation can also be expressed thus: ***Sublime is that in comparison with which everything else is small.*** Here one sees easily: that nothing can be given in nature, however great it may also be judged by us, that could not, considered in another relation, be degraded down to the infinitely small; and conversely nothing so small that it could not, in comparison with still smaller standards, be enlarged for our imagination up to a world-magnitude. Telescopes have provided us with abundant material for making the former, microscopes the latter remark. Nothing, therefore, that can be an object of the senses is, considered on this {{ 85 }} footing, to be called sublime. But precisely because there lies in our imagination a striving toward progress into the infinite, but in our reason a claim to absolute totality as to a real idea: even that inadequacy of our faculty for the estimation of the magnitude of the things of the sensible world for this idea is the arousal of the feeling of a supersensible faculty in us; and it is the use that the power of judgment naturally makes of certain objects for the sake of the latter (feeling), but not the object of the senses, that is absolutely great, while against it every other use is small. Hence it is the disposition of the spirit, through a certain representation occupying the reflecting power of judgment, but not the object, that is to be called sublime.

We can thus add to the previous formulas for the explanation of the sublime still this one: ***Sublime is that of which the mere capacity even to think it proves a faculty of the mind that surpasses every standard*** ***of the senses.***
