## § 25. Nominal definition of the sublime

That which is ***absolutely great*** is what we call ***sublime***. Yet being great and being a magnitude are entirely distinct concepts (_magnitudo_ and _quantitas_). Likewise, to ***simply*** (_simpliciter_) ***say*** that something is great is quite another matter than saying that it is ***absolutely great*** {{ 81 }} (_absolute, non comparative magnum_). This last is what ***is great beyond all comparison***. — But what, then, does the expression that something is great, or small, or middling actually mean? It is not a pure concept of the understanding that is thereby designated; still less an intuition of the senses; and just as little a concept of reason, since it carries with it no principle of cognition whatever. It must therefore be a concept belonging to the power of judgment, or else derive from such a one, and must lay at its basis a subjective purposiveness of the representation in relation to the power of judgment. That something is a magnitude (_quantum_) can be recognized from the thing itself, without any comparison with others: namely, when a multiplicity of the homogeneous together constitutes a unity. ***How great*** it is, however, always requires something else, which is likewise a magnitude, to serve as its measure. But since, in judging magnitude, it depends not merely on the multiplicity (number) but also on how large the unit (the measure) is, and the size of this latter in turn always needs something else as a measure against which it may be compared: we see that all determination of the magnitude of appearances can furnish no absolute concept of a magnitude whatever, but always merely a comparative one.

Now if I say simply that something is great, it appears that I have no comparison in mind at all, at least none with any {{ 82 }} objective measure, since by this it is in no way settled how large the object may be. Yet even though the scale of comparison is only subjective, the judgment none the less lays claim to universal agreement; the judgments "the man is beautiful" and "he is tall" do not confine themselves merely to the subject who judges, but, like theoretical judgments, require the agreement of everyone.

{{{ 249 }}} But since, in a judgment whereby something is designated simply as great, one does not merely mean to say that the object possesses a magnitude, but this magnitude is at the same time attributed to it in preference to many others of the same kind, without, however, stating this preference definitely: a scale is indeed made its basis which one presupposes may be taken as the very same for everyone, though it is serviceable for no logical (mathematically determinate) judging of magnitude, but only for an aesthetic one, since it is a scale grounded merely subjectively in the judgment that reflects upon magnitude. It may, for the rest, be empirical — for instance the average size of those human beings familiar to us, of animals of a given species, of trees, houses, mountains, and so forth; or else a standard furnished _a priori_, one that, owing to the limitations of the judging subject, is confined to subjective conditions of its presentation in concreto: as, in practical matters, the magnitude of a certain virtue, or of civic freedom and justice within a nation; {{ 83 }} or, in theoretical matters, the magnitude of the accuracy or inaccuracy of an observation or measurement carried out, and so forth.

Now here it is remarkable: that even though we take no interest whatever in the object — that is, are indifferent to its existence — its mere magnitude, even when it is regarded as formless, can nonetheless carry with it a satisfaction that is universally communicable, and hence involves an awareness of a subjective purposiveness in the employment of our cognitive faculties; not, however, a satisfaction in the object, as in the case of the beautiful (since it may be formless), where the reflecting power of judgment finds itself purposively attuned in relation to cognition in general, but rather a satisfaction in the expansion of the imagination in itself.

When we (subject to the restriction named above) say of an object simply that it is great: this is not a mathematically determining but a mere reflective judgment upon our representation of it — a representation subjectively purposive for a certain employment of our cognitive powers in appraising magnitude; and we then invariably link to that representation a kind of respect, just as, to what we simply call small, a kind of contempt. For the rest, the judging of things as great or small applies to everything whatsoever, even to all the qualities they possess; which is why we speak even of beauty as great or small: the reason for this {{{ 250 }}} being that whatever we may, following the prescription of the power of judgment, {{ 84 }} present in intuition (and thus represent aesthetically) is, without exception, appearance, and consequently a quantum as well.

But when we call something not merely great, but absolutely, wholly, in every respect (beyond all comparison) great, that is, sublime, one soon sees: that we permit no standard appropriate to it to be sought outside it, but only within it. It is a magnitude that is equal only to itself. That the sublime, accordingly, is to be sought not in the things of nature but solely in our ideas follows from this; but wherein among these it resides must be held over for the deduction.

The explanation given above may also be put thus: ***Sublime is that in comparison with which everything else is small.*** Here it is readily seen: that nothing can be given in nature, however great we may judge it to be, which, viewed in a different relation, could not be diminished to the point of the infinitely small; and, conversely, nothing so small that, set beside yet smaller standards, it could not, for our imagination, be magnified up to the size of a world. Telescopes have placed in our hands abundant matter for making the former remark, microscopes for the latter. Nothing, then, capable of being an object of the senses is, regarded on this {{ 85 }} footing, to be called sublime. But precisely because there lies in our imagination a striving toward advance into the infinite, while in our reason there lies a claim to absolute totality as to a real idea: this very inadequacy of our faculty for appraising the magnitude of the things of the sensible world is, in respect of that idea, the arousal of the feeling of a supersensible faculty within us; and it is the use that the power of judgment, by its nature, makes of certain objects for the sake of the latter (feeling), and not the object of sense, that is absolutely great, whereas against it every other use is small. Hence it is the mental attunement produced by a certain representation occupying the reflecting power of judgment, and not the object, that deserves to be called sublime.

We may accordingly append to the foregoing formulas for the explanation of the sublime this further one: ***Sublime is that of which the very ability to think it proves a power of the mind exceeding every measure*** ***of sense.***
