## § 40. On taste as a kind of _sensus communis_

The power of judgment is often given the name of a sense — when it is not so much its reflection as merely the result of that reflection that draws notice — and one speaks of a sense for truth, of a sense for decency, for justice, and so forth; even though one knows, or should in fairness know, that no sense is what could house these concepts, and still less that a sense might possess the least capacity to pronounce universal rules: rather, that no representation of this kind concerning truth, propriety, beauty, or justice could ever enter our thoughts, were we not able to raise ourselves above the senses to higher faculties of cognition. ***Common human understanding***, which, as merely sound (as yet uncultivated) understanding, is regarded as the least that can ever be expected of anyone who lays claim to the name of a human being, therefore also bears the wounding honor of being labeled with the appellation of common sense (_sensus communis_); and this in such a way that by the word ***common*** (not merely in our own language, which in this respect really does contain an ambiguity {{ 157 }}, but in several others as well) one understands the same as the _vulgare_, what one meets with everywhere, the possession of which is by no means any merit or distinction.

By _sensus communis_, however, one must understand the idea of a ***shared*** sense, i.e., a faculty of judging that in its reflection takes account, in thought (_a priori_), of everyone else's way of representing, so as ***as it were*** to hold its judgment up against collective human reason and thereby escape the illusion that, arising from subjective private conditions which could readily be taken for objective, would exert a detrimental influence on the judgment. {{{ 294 }}} This now comes about by one's holding one's judgment up not so much against the actual as rather against the merely possible judgments of others, and putting oneself in the place of everyone else, simply by abstracting from the limitations that contingently attach to our own act of judging: which in turn is brought about by leaving out, as far as possible, whatever is matter, i.e., sensation, in the state of representation, and attending solely to the formal peculiarities of one's representation or state of representation. Now this operation of reflection may perhaps seem far too artificial to be ascribed to the faculty we call ***common*** sense; but it only looks {{ 158 }} that way when one expresses it in abstract formulas; in itself nothing is more natural than to abstract from charm and emotion when one is seeking a judgment intended to hold good as a universal rule.

The maxims of common human understanding set out below do not, admittedly, belong here as portions of the critique of taste, and yet they may help to illuminate its principles. They are these: 1. To think for oneself; 2. To think in the position of everyone else; 3. To always think in agreement with oneself. The first is the maxim of the ***unprejudiced***, the second of the ***enlarged***, the third of the ***consistent*** way of thinking. The first is the maxim of a reason that is never ***passive***. The inclination toward the latter, and thus toward a heteronomy of reason, goes by the name of ***prejudice***; and the weightiest prejudice of them all consists in picturing nature as exempt from those rules which the understanding, through its own essential law, sets at its base: namely, ***superstition***. Liberation from superstition is called ***enlightenment***[^*]: for although this designation applies to liberation from prejudices in general as well, superstition still, in a preeminent sense (_in sensu {{ 159 }} eminenti_), merits the label of prejudice, since the blindness into which superstition casts one, indeed which it even demands as an obligation, {{{ 295 }}} renders especially recognizable the need to be guided by others, and hence the condition of a passive reason. As for the second maxim of the way of thinking, we are otherwise quite accustomed to calling someone narrow (***limited***, the opposite of ***enlarged***) whose talents do not suffice for any great use (especially the intensive kind). But here it is not a question of the faculty of cognition, but of the ***way of thinking*** required to make a purposive use of it: which, however small the scope and degree to which a person's natural endowment reaches, nonetheless marks out a man of ***enlarged way of thinking*** when he sets himself above the subjective private conditions of the judgment, between which so many others are as though hemmed in, and reflects on his own judgment from a ***universal standpoint*** (which he is able to fix only by transposing himself into the vantage point of others). The third maxim, namely that of the ***consistent*** {{ 160 }} way of thinking, is the hardest to attain and can be reached only through the combination of the first two and after a repeated observance of them that has become a proficiency. One might put it thus: the first of these maxims belongs to the understanding, the second to the power of judgment, the third to reason. —

I take up again the thread dropped through this episode and say: that taste can with more right be called _sensus communis_ than can sound understanding; and that the aesthetic power of judgment, sooner than the intellectual one, can bear the name of a shared sense[^**], provided one really does wish to apply the term sense to an effect that mere reflection produces upon the mind: for in that case sense signifies the feeling of pleasure. One could even define taste as the faculty for judging that which makes our feeling in a given representation ***universally communicable*** without the mediation of a concept.

The skill of human beings in communicating their thoughts likewise requires a relation between imagination and understanding, in order to pair intuitions with concepts and these in turn with concepts, {{ 161 }} which flow together into a cognition; but then the accord of the two powers of the mind is ***lawful***, under the constraint of determinate {{{ 296 }}} concepts. Only where imagination in its freedom awakens the understanding, and the latter, without concepts, sets the imagination into a regular play: there the representation is communicated, not as a thought, but as an inner feeling of a mind in a purposive condition.

Taste, accordingly, is the faculty for judging _a priori_ the communicability of the feelings that attach to a given representation (in the absence of any mediating concept).

If one were permitted to assume that the mere universal communicability of one's feeling must in itself already carry with it an interest for us (which, however, one is not entitled to infer from the nature of a merely reflecting power of judgment): then one would be able to explain how it comes about that the feeling in the judgment of taste is required of everyone as though it were a duty.

[^*]: One readily sees that enlightenment, though easy in thesi, is in hypothesi a difficult matter and slow to carry out: for to be not passive with one's reason, but at all times self-legislating, is indeed something quite easy for the human being who wishes only to conform to his essential end and does not demand to know what lies beyond his understanding; but since the striving toward the latter is scarcely to be prevented, and since there will never be any lack of others who promise, with great confidence, to be able to satisfy this desire for knowledge: it must be very difficult to preserve or to establish the merely negative element (which constitutes enlightenment proper) in the way of thinking (especially the public one).
[^**]: One could designate taste as _sensus communis aestheticus_, and common human understanding as _sensus communis logicus_.
