## § 40. Of Taste as a Kind of _sensus communis_

One often gives to the power of judgment, when not so much its reflection as rather merely the result of the latter is noticeable, the name of a sense, and speaks of a sense of truth, of a sense for decency, justice, etc.; although one indeed knows, or at least in fairness ought to know, that it is not a sense in which these concepts can have their seat, still less that the latter has the least capacity for pronouncing universal rules: but rather that no representation of this kind of truth, propriety, beauty, or justice could ever enter our thoughts if we could not raise ourselves above the senses to higher cognitive faculties. ***Common human understanding***, which, as merely sound (not yet cultivated) understanding, one regards as the least that one can ever expect from anyone who claims the name of a human being, has therefore also the offensive honor of being designated with the name of common sense (_sensus communis_); and indeed in such a way that by the word ***common*** (not merely in our language, which here indeed {{ 157 }} contains an ambiguity, but also in several others) one understands as much as the _vulgare_, which is met with everywhere, to possess which is absolutely no merit or privilege.

But under the _sensus communis_ one must understand the idea of a ***communal*** sense, i.e., of a faculty of judgment which in its reflection takes account in thought (_a priori_) of the mode of representation of every other, in order ***as it were*** to compare its judgment with the entire human reason, and thereby to escape the illusion which, arising from subjective private conditions that could easily be taken for objective, would have a detrimental influence on the judgment. {{{ 294 }}} Now this is done by comparing one's judgment with the not so much actual as rather merely possible judgments of others, and by putting oneself in the position of everyone else, merely by abstracting from the limitations which contingently attach to our own judging: which in turn is brought about by leaving out as far as possible what is matter, i.e., sensation, in the state of representation, and paying attention solely to the formal peculiarities of one's representation or state of representation. Now this operation of reflection seems perhaps too artificial to be attributed to the faculty we call the ***common*** sense; yet it only {{ 158 }} looks so when expressed in abstract formulas; in itself, nothing is more natural than to abstract from charm and emotion when seeking a judgment that is to serve as a universal rule.

The following maxims of common human understanding do not indeed belong here as parts of the critique of taste, but they can nonetheless serve to elucidate its principles. They are the following: 1. To think for oneself; 2. To think in the position of everyone else; 3. Always to think in agreement with oneself. The first is the maxim of the ***unprejudiced***, the second of the ***broadened***, the third of the ***consistent*** way of thinking. The first is the maxim of a never ***passive*** reason. The propensity to the latter, and consequently to the heteronomy of reason, is called ***prejudice***; and the greatest of all is to represent nature as not subject to the rules which the understanding lays at its basis by its own essential law: i.e., ***superstition***. Deliverance from superstition is called ***enlightenment***[^*]: because, although this designation belongs also to deliverance from prejudices in general, the former nonetheless preeminently (_in sensu {{ 159 }} eminenti_) deserves to be called a prejudice, since the blindness into which superstition puts a person, and indeed even demands of them as an obligation, {{{ 295 }}} makes preeminently recognizable the need to be guided by others, and hence the state of a passive reason. As regards the second maxim of the way of thinking, we are otherwise quite accustomed to call limited (***narrow-minded***, the opposite of ***broadened***) someone whose talents do not suffice for any great use (primarily intensive). But here we are not talking about the faculty of cognition, but about the ***way of thinking*** of making a purposive use of it: which, however small the scope and degree to which a person's natural endowment reaches, nonetheless indicates a person of a ***broadened way of thinking*** if he sets himself above the subjective private conditions of judgment, within which so many others are as if bracketed, and reflects on his own judgment from a ***universal standpoint*** (which he can determine only by placing himself in the standpoint of others). The third maxim, namely that of the ***consistent*** {{ 160 }} way of thinking, is the most difficult to attain, and can be attained only through the combination of the first two and after repeated adherence to them has become a skill. One can say: the first of these maxims is the maxim of the understanding, the second of the power of judgment, the third of reason. —

I resume the thread left behind due to this episode and say: that taste can with more right be called _sensus communis_ than can sound understanding; and that the aesthetic power of judgment can rather than the intellectual carry the name of a communal sense[^**], if one indeed wishes to use the word sense for an effect of mere reflection on the mind: for there one understands by sense the feeling of pleasure. One could even define taste as the faculty for judging that which makes our feeling in a given representation ***universally communicable*** without the mediation of a concept.

The skill of human beings in communicating their thoughts to one another also requires a relation of the imagination and the understanding, in order to associate intuitions with concepts and these in turn with concepts, {{ 161 }} which flow together into a cognition; but then the agreement of both mental powers is ***lawful*** under the constraint of definite {{{ 296 }}} concepts. Only where the imagination in its freedom awakens the understanding, and the latter, without concepts, puts the imagination into a regular play: there does the representation communicate itself, not as a thought, but as the inner feeling of a purposive state of mind.

Taste is therefore the faculty for judging _a priori_ the communicability of the feelings that are combined with a given representation (without the mediation of a concept).

If one could assume that the mere universal communicability of one's feeling must in itself already carry an interest for us (which, however, we are not justified in concluding from the nature of a merely reflecting power of judgment): then one would be able to explain how it is that the feeling in a judgment of taste is as it were required of everyone as a duty.

[^*]: One soon sees that enlightenment is indeed easy in thesi, but in hypothesi a difficult and slow thing to carry out: because to be not passive with one's reason, but rather always self-legislating, is indeed something quite easy for a human being who wants only to be adequate to his essential end and does not demand to know what is beyond his understanding; but since the striving for the latter is hardly to be prevented, and there will never be a lack of others who promise with great confidence to be able to satisfy this curiosity: it must be very difficult to preserve or establish in the way of thinking (especially the public one) what is merely negative (which constitutes actual enlightenment).
[^**]: One could designate taste as _sensus communis aestheticus_, common human understanding as _sensus communis logicus_.
