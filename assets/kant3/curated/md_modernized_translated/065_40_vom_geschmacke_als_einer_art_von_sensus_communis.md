## § 40. On taste as a kind of _sensus communis_

We frequently confer on the power of judgment the title of a sense — doing so whenever what strikes us is not so much its reflection as simply the outcome to which that reflection leads — and we go on to talk of a sense for truth, a sense for decency, for justice, and the like; and this although we know, or at any rate ought reasonably to know, that no sense could serve as the seat of such concepts, and far less could possess even the slightest capacity for issuing universal rules: on the contrary, that a representation of this sort — of truth, fittingness, beauty, or justice — could never so much as occur to us in thought, unless we were capable of lifting ourselves beyond the senses toward higher faculties of cognition. ***Common human understanding***, viewed as merely sound (not yet cultivated) understanding and taken to be the very least one may ever expect of whoever would claim for himself the title of a human being, thus incurs as well the mortifying honor of having the name of common sense (_sensus communis_) fastened upon it; and this in such fashion that under the word ***common*** (not only in our own tongue, which genuinely harbors an ambiguity here {{ 157 }}, but likewise in many another) one takes it to mean roughly the _vulgare_, that which is encountered on every side, and to possess which is quite simply neither a merit nor a distinction.

By _sensus communis_, however, one must understand the idea of a ***shared*** sense, i.e., a faculty of judging that in its reflection takes account, in thought (_a priori_), of everyone else's way of representing, so as ***as it were*** to hold its judgment up against collective human reason and thereby escape the illusion that, arising from subjective private conditions which could readily be taken for objective, would exert a detrimental influence on the judgment. {{{ 294 }}} This is accomplished when one measures one's judgment less against the judgments others actually make than against those they might merely make, and transposes oneself into each other person's position, doing so precisely by discounting the restrictions that happen, by chance, to cling to one's own judging: and this, in turn, is achieved by setting aside, so far as one can, whatever within the state of representation counts as matter, that is, as sensation, and by heeding solely the formal features of one's representation or of one's state of representation. This procedure of reflection may, to be sure, appear far too contrived for us to attribute it to the faculty we designate ***common*** sense; yet it presents {{ 158 }} such an appearance only once it is cast in abstract formulas, whereas nothing could be more natural in itself than to leave charm and emotion out of account whenever one is in search of a judgment meant to serve as a universal rule.

The maxims of common human understanding set out below do not, admittedly, belong here as portions of the critique of taste, and yet they may help to illuminate its principles. These are they: 1. To do one's own thinking; 2. To think from the standpoint of every other person; 3. To think, at all times, in accord with oneself. The first is the maxim of the ***unprejudiced***, the second of the ***enlarged***, the third of the ***consistent*** way of thinking. This first one is the maxim belonging to a reason that never turns ***passive***. The inclination toward the latter, and thus toward a heteronomy of reason, goes by the name of ***prejudice***; and the weightiest prejudice of them all consists in picturing nature as exempt from those rules which the understanding, through its own essential law, sets at its base: namely, ***superstition***. Liberation from superstition is called ***enlightenment***[^*]: for although this designation applies to liberation from prejudices in general as well, superstition still, in a preeminent sense (_in sensu {{ 159 }} eminenti_), merits the label of prejudice, since the blindness into which superstition casts one, indeed which it even demands as an obligation, {{{ 295 }}} renders especially recognizable the need to be guided by others, and hence the condition of a passive reason. As for the second maxim of the way of thinking, we are otherwise quite accustomed to calling someone narrow (***limited***, the opposite of ***enlarged***) whose talents do not suffice for any great use (especially the intensive kind). What is at issue here, however, is not the faculty of cognition, but rather the ***way of thinking*** that puts such a faculty to a purposive use: a way of thinking that, however modest the range and extent reached by a person's natural gift, nonetheless betokens a man of ***enlarged way of thinking*** once he lifts himself above those subjective private conditions of the judgment, amid which so many other people remain as though boxed in, and, from a ***universal standpoint*** (which he can fix only by transposing himself into another's position), reflects upon his own judgment. The third maxim in turn, the one bearing on a ***consistent*** {{ 160 }} way of thinking, proves the hardest of all to reach, and it can be won only by joining the two earlier maxims together and by following them so habitually that such compliance has ripened into a settled skill. One might put it thus: the first of these maxims belongs to the understanding, the second to the power of judgment, the third to reason. —

Resuming now the thread that this digression led me to drop, I would say the following: that taste, with greater justification than sound understanding, may be termed _sensus communis_; and that the aesthetic power of judgment, rather than the intellectual, is entitled to carry the name of a shared sense[^**], if indeed one insists on using the word sense for an effect wrought upon the mind by mere reflection: for in such a case what one means by sense is the feeling of pleasure. Indeed, one might define taste as that faculty of appraising which renders our feeling, given some particular representation, ***universally communicable*** apart from any mediation by a concept.

Human beings' aptitude for imparting their thoughts to one another calls, too, for a certain relation between imagination and understanding, so as to join intuitions to concepts and, conversely, concepts to these, {{ 161 }} such that they coalesce into a cognition; in that case, however, the concord of the mind's two powers is ***lawful***, subject to the compulsion of determinate {{{ 296 }}} concepts. It is solely where the imagination, in its freedom, rouses the understanding, and the latter, dispensing with concepts, casts the imagination into an orderly play, that the representation communicates itself — not as a thought, but as an inner feeling of the mind in a purposive state.

Taste, accordingly, is the faculty for judging _a priori_ the communicability of the feelings that attach to a given representation (in the absence of any mediating concept).

Were we entitled to suppose that the sheer universal communicability of one's feeling must, of itself, already bring an interest along with it for us (something one is nevertheless not warranted in concluding from the character of a merely reflecting power of judgment), we should then be in a position to explain why the feeling in a judgment of taste is demanded of everyone, as it were, in the manner of a duty.

[^*]: It is soon apparent that enlightenment, however easy in thesi, is in hypothesi an arduous matter, and one slow in the doing: for although being self-legislating with one's reason at every moment, rather than passive, is a thoroughly light matter for the person who seeks only what befits his essential purpose and has no wish to know whatever surpasses his understanding, still — since the drive toward that latter thing can hardly be forestalled, and since others who pledge with great assurance that they can appease this thirst for knowledge will never be wanting — keeping alive or bringing into being the merely negative element (in which enlightenment properly consists) within the way of thinking (above all the public way) is bound to be very hard indeed.
[^**]: One could designate taste as _sensus communis aestheticus_, and common human understanding as _sensus communis logicus_.
