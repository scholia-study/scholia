## § 70. Representation of this Antinomy

Insofar as reason has to do with nature as the sum total of objects of outer senses, it can base itself on laws which the understanding partly itself prescribes _a priori_ to nature, partly can extend without limit through the empirical determinations occurring in experience. For the application of the first kind of laws, namely the ***universal*** ones of material nature in general, the power of judgment needs no special principle of reflection; for there it is determining, because an objective principle is given to it through the understanding. But as regards the particular laws which can become known to us only through experience, there can be among them so great a manifoldness and heterogeneity that the power of judgment must serve as its own principle, even merely to search for and track down a law in the appearances of nature, since it needs such a law as a guide if it is to have any hope at all of a coherent cognition of experience according to a thoroughgoing lawfulness of nature, and the unity of the same according to empirical laws. With this contingent unity of particular laws it can now happen: that {{ 314 }} the power of judgment in its reflection starts from two maxims, one of which is suggested to it _a priori_ by the mere understanding, while the other is occasioned by particular experiences which bring reason into play, in order to institute the judging of corporeal nature and its laws according to a special principle. It then happens that these two kinds of maxims seem unable to coexist easily {{{ 387 }}} alongside one another, and consequently a dialectic arises which misleads the power of judgment in the principle of its reflection.

***The first maxim*** of the same is the ***thesis***: All generation of material things and their forms must be judged as possible according to merely mechanical laws.

***The second maxim*** is the ***antithesis***: Some products of material nature cannot be judged as possible according to merely mechanical laws (their judging requires an entirely different law of causality, namely that of final causes).

If we were now to transform these regulative principles for investigation into constitutive ones of the possibility of the objects themselves, they would read as follows:

***Thesis***: All generation of material things is possible according to merely mechanical laws.

***Antithesis***: Some generation of the same is not possible according to merely mechanical {{ 315 }} laws.

In this latter quality, as objective principles for the determining power of judgment, they would contradict one another, and consequently one of the two propositions would be necessarily false; but that would then indeed be an antinomy, though not of the power of judgment, but rather a conflict in the legislation of reason. Reason, however, can prove neither the one nor the other of these principles: because we can have no determining principle _a priori_ concerning the possibility of things according to merely empirical laws of nature.

On the other hand, as regards the first-stated maxim of a reflecting power of judgment, it in fact contains no contradiction at all. For if I say: I must ***judge*** all events in material nature, and consequently also all forms as products of the same as to their possibility, according to merely mechanical laws, I do not thereby say: they ***are possible under these alone*** (to the exclusion of every other kind of causality); rather, this is only intended to indicate: I ***ought*** at all times to ***reflect*** upon them ***according to the principle*** of the mere mechanism of nature, and consequently to investigate this as far as I am able, because without placing it at the basis of investigation, there can be no proper cognition of nature at all. This does not, on the occasion of appropriate prompting, prevent the second maxim, namely in the case of some natural forms (and, {{ 316 }} on their occasion, even of nature as a whole), from searching for and reflecting upon a principle {{{ 388 }}} that is entirely different from explanation according to the mechanism of nature, namely the principle of final causes. For reflection according to the first maxim is not thereby abolished, but rather we are commanded to pursue it as far as we can; nor is it thereby said that those forms would not be possible according to the mechanism of nature. It is only asserted that ***human reason***, in following the former maxim and in this manner, will never be able to discover the least ground of what constitutes the specific character of a natural end, though it can indeed find other cognitions of natural laws; whereby it is left undecided whether, in the unknown inner ground of nature itself, the physical-mechanical and the purposive connection in the same things might not hang together in a single principle: only that our reason is not capable of uniting them in such a principle, and the power of judgment, hence as ***reflecting*** (from a subjective ground), and not as determining (in accordance with an objective principle of the possibility of things in themselves), is compelled to think for certain forms in nature a principle other than that of the mechanism of nature as the ground of their possibility.
