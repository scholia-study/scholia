## § 70. Representation of this antinomy

Insofar as reason has to do with nature as the sum total of the objects of the outer senses, it can ground itself on laws that the understanding partly prescribes to nature _a priori_ of itself, and partly is able to extend without limit through the empirical determinations occurring in experience. For the application of the first kind of laws, namely the ***universal*** ones of material nature as such, the power of judgment needs no special principle of reflection; for there it is determining, since an objective principle is given to it through the understanding. But as concerns the particular laws, which can be made known to us only through experience, there can be among them so great a manifoldness and heterogeneity that the power of judgment must serve as a principle to itself merely in order to search out and track down a law even in the appearances of nature, since it requires such a law as a guiding thread if it is so much as to hope for a coherent cognition from experience in accordance with a thoroughgoing lawfulness of nature, the unity of that nature under empirical laws. Given this contingent unity of the particular laws, it can now come about: that {{ 314 }} in its reflection the power of judgment sets out from two maxims, one of which the mere understanding hands to it _a priori_; while the other is prompted by particular experiences that bring reason into play, so as to conduct the judging of corporeal nature and its laws according to a special principle. It then happens that these two kinds of maxims give the appearance of not being well able {{{ 387 }}} to subsist side by side, so that a dialectic emerges which leads the power of judgment astray in the principle of its reflection.

***The first maxim*** of it is the ***thesis***: All generation of material things and their forms must be judged to be possible in accordance with purely mechanical laws.

***The second maxim*** is the ***antithesis***: Some products of material nature cannot be judged possible under mechanical laws taken alone (their judging demands an altogether different law of causality, namely that of final causes).

Were one now to convert these regulative principles for inquiry into constitutive principles of the possibility of the objects themselves, they would read as follows:

***Thesis***: All generation of material things is possible in accordance with purely mechanical laws.

***Antithesis***: Some generation of them is not possible under mechanical {{ 315 }} laws alone.

In this latter quality, as objective principles for the determining power of judgment, they would contradict one another, so that one of the two propositions would necessarily be false; but this would then be an antinomy, though not one of the power of judgment, but rather a discord within the legislation of reason. Reason, however, can demonstrate neither of these two principles: for we can possess no _a priori_ determining principle for the possibility of things according to merely empirical laws of nature.

As for the maxim of a reflecting power of judgment set out first, by contrast, it in fact contains no contradiction whatsoever. For when I say: I must ***judge*** all occurrences in material nature, and hence all forms too, as products of it with respect to their possibility, according to purely mechanical laws, I am not thereby saying: they ***are, on that basis alone*** (to the exclusion of every other kind of causality), ***possible***; rather it is only meant to indicate this: that I ***ought*** always, ***in accordance with the principle*** of nature's mere mechanism, to ***reflect*** upon them, and thus to pursue this mechanism as far as I can, because, without laying it at the basis of inquiry, there can be no proper cognition of nature whatever. This, now, does not prevent the second maxim, on occasional prompting — namely in the case of certain natural forms (and {{ 316 }} on their prompting even of the whole of nature) — from {{{ 388 }}} tracking, according to a principle, and reflecting upon them by a principle wholly distinct from explanation by nature's mechanism, namely the principle of final causes. For the reflection according to the first maxim is not thereby annulled; on the contrary, it is enjoined that one pursue it as far as one can; nor is it thereby said that those forms would be impossible under the mechanism of nature. The claim is merely that ***human reason***, in adhering to this maxim and proceeding in this way, will never discover the least ground for what makes up the specific character of a natural end, though it will indeed discover other cognitions of natural laws; whereby it remains left open whether, in the inner ground of nature itself — which is unknown to us — the physical-mechanical connection and the connection of ends may not, in the very same things, cohere within a single principle: only that our reason is incapable of uniting them within such a principle, and the power of judgment is therefore compelled, as ***reflecting*** (on a subjective ground), and not as determining power of judgment (conforming to an objective principle of the possibility of things in themselves), to think, for certain forms in nature, a principle other than that of natural mechanism as the ground of their possibility.
