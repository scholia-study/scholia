## {{ 192 }} § 49. On the mental powers that make up genius

Of certain products, from which one expects that they should present themselves, at least in part, as fine art, one says: they are without ***spirit***; even though, as far as taste is concerned, one finds nothing in them to censure. A poem can be quite pretty and elegant, yet it is without spirit. A story is precise and orderly, yet without spirit. A solemn address is thorough and at the same time refined, yet without spirit. Many a conversation is not without entertainment, yet still without spirit; even of a woman one may say: she is pretty, talkative, and well-mannered, yet without spirit. What, then, is it that one understands here by spirit?

***Spirit***, taken in its aesthetic sense, denotes the enlivening principle within the mind. But the means by which this principle animates the soul—the material that it puts to this use—is what sets the mind's powers purposively astir, that is, into a play of the sort that keeps itself going and of itself reinforces the powers directed to it.

Now I contend that this principle is nothing other than the faculty {{{ 314 }}} for presenting ***aesthetic ideas***; and by an aesthetic idea I mean a representation of the imagination that occasions much thought without any determinate thought, {{ 193 }} that is, ***concept***, being capable of being adequate to it, so that no language can ever wholly attain it and make it intelligible. — One sees readily enough that it is the counterpart (pendant) to a ***rational idea***, which is, the other way about, a concept for which no ***intuition*** (imaginative representation) could ever prove adequate.

For the imagination, taken as a productive cognitive faculty, is exceedingly potent in fashioning, so to speak, a second nature out of the stuff supplied to it by the actual one. We divert ourselves with it wherever experience strikes us as too commonplace; and we recast that experience too—still, to be sure, on analogical laws, yet also on principles seated higher up, in reason (principles no less natural to us than those whereby the understanding grasps empirical nature)—and in doing so we are sensible of our liberty from that law of association which clings to the empirical use of the said faculty, a law under which nature does indeed lend us material, though this material can be worked up by us into something wholly other, namely into what outstrips nature.

Representations of the imagination of this sort one is entitled to call ***ideas***: in part because they do at least strive after something that lies beyond the frontier of experience, thus attempting to approach a presentation of the concepts of reason (the intellectual ideas), which {{ 194 }} lends them the semblance of an objective reality; and, on the other side—indeed principally—because, as inner intuitions, they can have no concept fully answering to them. The poet makes bold to give sensible embodiment to rational ideas of invisible beings—the kingdom of the blessed, the kingdom of the damned, eternity, the creation, and suchlike; or else to bring within the reach of sense, past the barriers of experience, things that do have their instances in experience, such as death, envy, and every vice, and likewise love, renown, and suchlike, by means of an imagination that vies with reason's anticipatory play in reaching toward a maximum, doing so in a completeness for which nature affords no instance; and it is, properly speaking, the art of poetry wherein the faculty of aesthetic ideas can manifest itself in its full extent. This faculty, however, considered by itself alone, is strictly no more than a talent (belonging to the imagination).

When, now, a representation of the imagination {{{ 315 }}} is subsumed under a concept—one belonging to the concept's presentation, yet by itself provoking so much thought as could never be gathered into a determinate concept, and thereby enlarging the concept itself aesthetically without limit—then the imagination proves creative here and sets the faculty of intellectual ideas (reason) into activity, prompting it, on the occasion of a representation, to think more (something that does belong to the object's concept) than can be grasped and rendered {{ 195 }} distinct within it.

Forms which do not of themselves make up the presentation of a given concept, but which—merely as accessory representations of the imagination—give expression to the consequences tied to it and to its affinity with others, one terms ***attributes*** (aesthetic ones) of an object whose concept, being a rational idea, admits of no adequate presentation. Thus Jupiter's eagle, the lightning in its talons, serves as an attribute of the mighty sovereign of the heavens, and the peacock as an attribute of the splendid queen of heaven. They set before us not (as the ***logical attributes*** do) what our concepts of the sublimity and majesty of creation contain, but something else, which prompts the imagination to range over a host of kindred representations that awaken more thought than can be captured in any word-determined concept; and thereby they furnish an ***aesthetic idea***, which serves that rational idea in lieu of a logical presentation, though properly in order to quicken the mind, insofar as it discloses to it the vista of a boundless field of kindred representations. But fine art accomplishes this not in painting or sculpture alone (where the term "attributes" is commonly employed); poetry and eloquence, too, draw the spirit that animates their works entirely from the aesthetic attributes of their objects, which run parallel to the logical ones and lend the imagination {{ 196 }} an impetus to think, in connection with them—though in an undeveloped fashion—more than admits of being gathered into a concept, and therefore into a determinate verbal expression. — To keep things brief, I must confine myself to just a few instances.

When the great king gives expression to himself in one of his poems thus: »Let us take our leave of life without murmuring and with nothing to regret, leaving the world behind us even then still heaped with our benefactions. Even so the sun, having run its daily course, still sheds a gentle light across the heavens; and the final rays, {{{ 316 }}} which it dispatches into the air, are its parting sighs for the welfare of the world«: thus does he quicken his rational idea of a cosmopolitan cast of mind, at the very close of life, by an attribute which the imagination (recollecting all the pleasant things of a lovely summer's day now spent, such as a cloudless evening summons up in our minds) links to that representation, and which rouses a multitude of sensations and accessory representations to which no words answer. Conversely, an intellectual concept can, the other way round, serve as attribute to a representation of the senses, and so animate the latter through the idea of the supersensible—yet only insofar as the aesthetic element subjectively bound up with the consciousness of that representation is drawn upon for the purpose. Thus, {{ 197 }} to take an example, a certain poet, describing a fair morning, writes: »The sun poured forth, as peace pours forth from virtue.« The consciousness of virtue—even where one only imaginatively puts oneself in the position of a person of virtue—diffuses through the mind a wealth of sublime and tranquillizing feelings, together with a limitless prospect of a glad futurity such as no expression suited to a determinate concept can wholly capture.[^*]

In a word, the aesthetic idea is a representation of the imagination adjoined to a given concept and combined with so rich a manifold of partial representations, in its free deployment, that no expression marking out a determinate concept can be discovered for it—an idea that accordingly lets one think, over and above a concept, a great deal that cannot be named, whose feeling animates the cognitive faculties and weds spirit to language, which is otherwise mere letter.

The mental powers, then, whose combination (in a certain proportion) {{ 198 }} makes up ***genius***, are the imagination and the understanding. Only—since in employing the imagination for cognition the imagination stands under the coercion of the understanding and under the restriction of matching the latter's concept, whereas for aesthetic purposes {{{ 317 }}} the imagination is free to supply, over and above that agreement with the concept, unsought yet copious undeveloped material that the understanding disregarded in framing its concept, material which the understanding then turns to account not so much objectively, for cognition, as subjectively, for quickening the cognitive powers, and hence indirectly for cognitions after all—genius consists, strictly, in that fortunate relation, teachable by no science and learnable by no diligence, of lighting upon ideas for a given concept and, on the other hand, of striking the ***expression*** for them whereby the subjective disposition of mind so produced, as the accompaniment of a concept, may be imparted to others. It is this latter talent, strictly, that goes by the name of spirit; for to render the unnameable in the state of mind attending a given representation, and to make it universally communicable—whether the expression reside in words, in painting, or in sculpture—demands a faculty for seizing the fleeting play of the imagination and fusing it into a concept (one that is, just on that account, original, and {{ 199 }} that at the same time discloses a fresh rule derivable from no antecedent principles or examples), a concept that lets itself be communicated without any coercion of rules.

When, in the light of these analyses, we cast a backward glance at the explanation given above of what we term ***genius***, the following emerges: ***first***, that genius is a gift for art and not for science, where distinctly cognized rules have to precede and govern the procedure; ***second***, that, being an artistic gift, it presupposes a determinate concept of the product as an end—hence understanding—but likewise a representation (indeterminate though it be) of the material, that is, of the intuition serving to present this concept, and thus a certain relation of imagination to understanding; that, ***third***, it displays itself not so much in carrying out the intended end by presenting a determinate ***concept*** as rather in the delivery, or the expression, of ***aesthetic ideas*** that supply abundant material for that intention, and hence exhibits the imagination, in its emancipation from every rule-guided direction, as nonetheless purposive for presenting the given concept; and that, ***fourth*** and lastly, the unsought, undesigned subjective purposiveness {{{ 318 }}} in the free concord of the imagination with the lawful character {{ 200 }} of the understanding presupposes a proportion and disposition of these powers such as no observance of rules—be it of science or of mechanical imitation—could ever effect, and such as the subject's own nature alone can bring forth.

According to these presuppositions, genius is the exemplary originality of a subject's natural endowment in the ***free*** employment of its cognitive faculties. So considered, the product of a genius (with respect to what in it falls to genius rather than to learning that might be acquired or to schooling) is a model—not for imitation (for then the very thing in it that is genius, and that makes the work's spirit, would be forfeited), but for emulation by another genius, in whom it rouses the sense of his own originality to bring into art a freedom from rule-constraint of such a kind that art thereby gains for itself a fresh rule, and the talent shows itself thereby to be exemplary. But since genius is a favorite of nature, such as can be looked upon only as a rare phenomenon, its example gives rise, for other fine minds, to a school, that is, methodical instruction by rules, so far as these admit of being extracted from such products of spirit and their distinctive character; and for these persons fine art is to that degree an imitation, one for which nature, by way of a genius, furnished the rule.

But this imitation turns into ***aping*** when the pupil ***copies*** everything {{ 201 }} down to the very flaws that the genius was obliged to permit merely because they could not readily be removed without enfeebling the idea. This audacity is a merit in the genius alone; and a certain ***boldness*** of expression, and in general many a departure from the common rule, well befits it, yet is in no way worthy of imitation, but remains always, in itself, a defect that one is bound to try to remove—one for which, nonetheless, the genius is as it were licensed, since the inimitable quality of its flight of spirit would suffer under anxious circumspection. ***Mannering*** is a second sort of aping—that of mere ***peculiarity*** (originality) as such, undertaken so as to get as far as one possibly can from imitators, yet without possessing the talent to be, therewith, ***exemplary*** as well. — There are, admittedly, two sorts (_modus_) of arranging one's thoughts in delivery, the one termed ***manner*** (_modus aestheticus_), the other ***method*** (_modus logicus_), and they differ in this: {{{ 319 }}} that the former has no measuring-rod but the ***feeling*** of unity in the presentation, whereas the latter follows determinate ***principles*** in the matter; and so for fine art only the former holds good. But a product of art earns the name ***mannered*** only when the exposition of its idea in it is ***contrived*** for the sake of oddity, rather than made appropriate {{ 202 }} to the idea. The showy (the precious), the tortuous and the affected, resorted to merely in order to set oneself apart from the commonplace (but without spirit), resemble the demeanor of a man of whom it is said that he listens to himself talk, or who stands and moves as though he were on a stage to be gaped at—conduct that unfailingly gives away a bungler.

[^*]: Perhaps nothing more sublime has ever been uttered, nor a thought more sublimely voiced, than in that inscription upon the temple of ***Isis*** (Mother ***Nature***): »I am everything that is, that has been, and that shall be, and no mortal has ever lifted my veil.« ***Segner*** turned this idea to account in an ingenious vignette prefixed to his treatise on natural science, so as to imbue beforehand the pupil whom he stood ready to lead into this temple with that hallowed awe which is to tune the mind toward solemn attentiveness.
