## {{ 192 }} § 49. On the faculties of the mind that constitute genius

One says of certain products, of which one expects that they should show themselves, at least in part, as beautiful art: they are without ***spirit***; even though one finds nothing to censure in them as far as taste is concerned. A poem can be quite neat and elegant, but it is without spirit. A history is precise and orderly, but without spirit. A solemn speech is thorough and at the same time graceful, but without spirit. Many a conversation is not without entertainment, but yet without spirit; even of a woman one may well say: she is pretty, talkative, and well-mannered, but without spirit. What then is it that one understands here by spirit?

***Spirit***, in the aesthetic meaning, signifies the animating principle in the mind. That, however, by which this principle animates the soul, the material that it employs for this, is what sets the powers of the mind purposively into motion, i.e., into such a play as sustains itself and even strengthens the powers for it.

Now I maintain that this principle is nothing other than the faculty {{{ 314 }}} for the presentation of ***aesthetic ideas***; by an aesthetic idea, however, I understand that representation of the imagination which occasions much thinking, without it being possible for any determinate thought, {{ 193 }} i.e., ***concept***, to be adequate to it, which consequently no language fully attains and can make comprehensible. — One readily sees that it is the counterpart (pendant) of a ***rational idea***, which is, conversely, a concept to which no ***intuition*** (representation of the imagination) can be adequate.

The imagination (as a productive faculty of cognition) is, namely, very powerful in creating, as it were, another nature out of the material that the actual one gives it. We entertain ourselves with it where experience seems too commonplace to us; we also transform the latter: still always in accordance with analogical laws, but yet also in accordance with principles that lie higher up in reason (and which are just as natural to us as those by which the understanding apprehends empirical nature); in which we feel our freedom from the law of association (which attaches to the empirical use of that faculty), in accordance with which material is indeed lent to us by nature, but this can be worked up by us into something quite different, namely into that which surpasses nature.

One can call such representations of the imagination ***ideas***: on the one hand because they at least strive toward something lying beyond the bounds of experience and thus seek to come close to a presentation of the concepts of reason (the intellectual ideas), which gives {{ 194 }} them the appearance of an objective reality; on the other hand, and indeed principally, because as inner intuitions no concept can be fully adequate to them. The poet ventures to make sensible rational ideas of invisible beings, the realm of the blessed, the realm of hell, eternity, creation, and the like; or also that which does indeed find examples in experience, e.g., death, envy, and all vices, likewise love, fame, and the like, beyond the limits of experience, by means of an imagination that emulates the precedent of reason in attaining a maximum, sensibly in a completeness for which no example is found in nature; and it is properly poetry in which the faculty of aesthetic ideas can show itself in its full measure. This faculty, however, considered for itself alone, is properly only a talent (of the imagination).

If now a representation of the imagination is laid {{{ 315 }}} beneath a concept that belongs to its presentation, but which for itself alone occasions so much thinking as can never be grasped together in a determinate concept, hence aesthetically enlarges the concept itself in an unbounded way: then the imagination is herein creative and sets the faculty of intellectual ideas (reason) into motion, namely to think more on the occasion of a representation (which does indeed belong to the concept of the object) than can be apprehended and made distinct {{ 195 }} in it.

One calls those forms which do not constitute the presentation of a given concept itself, but only, as supplementary representations of the imagination, express the consequences connected therewith and the kinship of it with others, ***attributes*** (aesthetic) of an object whose concept, as a rational idea, cannot be adequately presented. Thus the eagle of Jupiter, with the lightning in its claws, is an attribute of the mighty king of heaven, and the peacock of the splendid queen of heaven. They do not, like the ***logical attributes***, represent what lies in our concepts of the sublimity and majesty of creation, but rather something else, which gives the imagination occasion to spread itself over a multitude of kindred representations that let one think more than can be expressed in a concept determined by words; and they yield an ***aesthetic idea*** that serves that rational idea in place of logical presentation, properly, however, to animate the mind, in that it opens up for it the prospect into an immeasurable field of kindred representations. Beautiful art, however, does this not only in painting or sculpture (where the name of attributes is customarily used); rather, poetry and rhetoric also take the spirit that animates their works solely from the aesthetic attributes of the objects, which go alongside the logical ones and give the imagination {{ 196 }} an impetus to think more thereby, although in an undeveloped way, than can be grasped together in a concept, hence in a determinate linguistic expression. — For the sake of brevity I must restrict myself only to a few examples.

When the great king expresses himself in one of his poems thus: "Let us depart from life without grumbling and without regretting anything, in that we then still leave the world behind heaped with benefactions. Thus the sun, after it has completed its daily course, still spreads a mild light across the sky; and the last rays {{{ 316 }}} that it sends into the air are its last sighs for the welfare of the world": then he animates his rational idea of a cosmopolitan disposition even at the end of life by means of an attribute that the imagination (in the recollection of all the agreeable things of a beautiful summer's day brought to completion, which a serene evening calls into our mind) associates with that representation, and which sets in motion a multitude of sensations and supplementary representations for which no expression is found. On the other hand, even an intellectual concept can conversely serve as an attribute of a representation of the senses and thus animate this latter by means of the idea of the supersensible; but only insofar as the aesthetic that subjectively attaches to the consciousness of the latter is used for this. Thus {{ 197 }} a certain poet, for example, says in the description of a beautiful morning: "The sun streamed forth, as repose streams from virtue." The consciousness of virtue, even if one only sets oneself in thought in the place of a virtuous person, spreads in the mind a multitude of sublime and calming feelings and a boundless prospect into a joyful future, which no expression that is commensurate with a determinate concept fully attains.[^*]

In a word, the aesthetic idea is a representation of the imagination associated with a given concept, which is bound up with such a manifoldness of partial representations in the free use of it that for it no expression designating a determinate concept can be found, which therefore lets one think much that is unnameable in addition to a concept, the feeling of which animates the faculties of cognition and connects spirit with language, as mere letter.

The powers of the mind, then, whose union (in a certain proportion) {{ 198 }} constitutes ***genius***, are imagination and understanding. Only, since in the use of the imagination for cognition the imagination is subjected to the constraint of the understanding and to the limitation of being commensurate with the concept of the latter; whereas in an aesthetic respect {{{ 317 }}} the imagination is free, in order to provide, beyond that agreement with the concept, yet unsought, rich, undeveloped material for the understanding, to which the latter took no regard in its concept, but which it applies not so much objectively for cognition as subjectively for the animation of the powers of cognition, hence indirectly nonetheless also for cognitions: so genius properly consists in the happy relation, which no science can teach and no diligence can learn, of finding ideas for a given concept and, on the other hand, of hitting upon the ***expression*** for these, through which the subjective disposition of mind thereby produced, as the accompaniment of a concept, can be communicated to others. The latter talent is properly that which one calls spirit; for to express the unnameable in the state of mind attendant on a certain representation and to make it universally communicable, whether the expression now consist in language, or painting, or sculpture: this requires a faculty for apprehending the rapidly passing play of the imagination and uniting it into a concept (which for that very reason is original and {{ 199 }} at the same time opens up a new rule that could not have been inferred from any preceding principles or examples) that can be communicated without the constraint of rules.

If, after these analyses, we look back at the explanation given above of that which one calls ***genius***, we find: ***first***, that it is a talent for art, not for science, in which distinctly known rules must precede and determine the procedure within it; ***second***, that, as a talent for art, it presupposes a determinate concept of the product as an end, hence understanding, but also a (though indeterminate) representation of the material, i.e., of the intuition, for the presentation of this concept, hence a relation of the imagination to the understanding; that ***third*** it shows itself not so much in the execution of the proposed end in the presentation of a determinate ***concept***, but rather in the delivery, or the expression, of ***aesthetic ideas***, which contain rich material for that aim, hence makes the imagination, in its freedom from all guidance by rules, nevertheless represent itself as purposive for the presentation of the given concept; that finally ***fourth*** the unsought, unintentional subjective purposiveness {{{ 318 }}} in the free correspondence of the imagination to the lawfulness {{ 200 }} of the understanding presupposes such a proportion and disposition of these faculties as no observance of rules, whether of science or of mechanical imitation, can effect, but which only the nature of the subject can bring forth.

According to these presuppositions, genius is: the exemplary originality of the natural endowment of a subject in the ***free*** use of his faculties of cognition. In such a way the product of a genius (with respect to that in it which is to be ascribed to genius, not to possible learning or to the school) is an example not for imitation (for then that which is genius in it and constitutes the spirit of the work would be lost), but for emulation by another genius, who is thereby awakened to the feeling of his own originality, to exercise freedom from the constraint of rules in art in such a way that art thereby itself acquires a new rule, by which the talent shows itself as exemplary. But because genius is a favorite of nature, the like of which one has to regard only as a rare phenomenon: so its example brings forth for other good minds a school, i.e., a methodical instruction in accordance with rules, insofar as one has been able to draw them from those products of the spirit and their peculiarity; and for these beautiful art is to that extent imitation, for which nature, through a genius, gave the rule.

But this imitation becomes ***aping*** if the pupil imitates everything {{ 201 }} ***exactly***, down to that which the genius has had to allow only as a deformity, because it could not well be removed without weakening the idea. This courage is a merit in a genius alone; and a certain ***boldness*** in the expression, and in general many a deviation from the common rule, well suits him, but is in no way worthy of imitation, but always remains in itself a fault that one must seek to remove, but for which the genius is, as it were, privileged, since the inimitable quality of his flight of spirit would suffer through anxious caution. ***Mannerism*** is another kind of aping, namely of mere ***peculiarity*** (originality) in general, in order to remove oneself as far as possible from imitators, without, however, possessing the talent for being at the same time ***exemplary***. — There are, to be sure, two kinds (_modus_) in general of the arrangement of one's thoughts in delivery, of which the one is called ***manner*** (_modus aestheticus_), the other ***method*** (_modus logicus_), which are distinguished from one another in this: {{{ 319 }}} that the former has no other standard than the ***feeling*** of unity in the presentation, but the other follows determinate ***principles*** herein; for beautiful art, therefore, only the former holds. But a product of art is called ***mannered*** only when the delivery of its idea in it is ***aimed*** at singularity and not made commensurate {{ 202 }} with the idea. The ostentatious (precious), the strained, and the affected, in order merely to distinguish oneself from the common (but without spirit), are similar to the behavior of one of whom one says that he hears himself speak, or who stands and walks as if he were on a stage in order to be gaped at, which always betrays a bungler.

[^*]: Perhaps nothing more sublime has ever been said, or a thought ever expressed more sublimely, than in that inscription over the temple of ***Isis*** (mother ***Nature***): "I am all that is, that was, and that will be, and my veil has no mortal uncovered." ***Segner*** made use of this idea through an ingenious vignette prefixed to his natural philosophy, in order to fill his pupil, whom he was ready to lead into this temple, beforehand with the holy awe that is meant to attune the mind to solemn attentiveness.
