## § 75. The concept of nature's objective purposiveness is a critical principle of reason for the reflecting power of judgment

It makes a considerable difference, after all, whether I say: certain things of nature, or indeed nature in its entirety, can be brought forth only by a cause that resolves upon action in accordance with intentions; or whether I hold that, ***given the peculiar constitution of my cognitive faculties***, I am unable to judge the possibility of those things and their {{{ 398 }}} production otherwise than by supposing for it a cause that operates according to intentions, and thus a being whose productivity is modeled on the causality proper to an understanding. In the former case I aim to decide something regarding the object and am obliged to exhibit the objective reality of a concept I have adopted; in the latter, reason does no more than regulate the employment of my cognitive faculties in keeping with their peculiar character and with the essential conditions governing both their extent and their bounds. Thus the first principle is an ***objective*** principle for the determining power of judgment, the second a subjective principle merely for the reflecting power of judgment, and hence a maxim {{ 334 }} of the latter that reason imposes upon it.

For we find it indispensable to place the concept of an intention beneath nature if we wish to investigate it, even only in its organized products, through sustained observation; and so this concept is already, for the empirical employment of our reason, an utterly necessary maxim. It is evident that, once such a guiding thread for studying nature has been adopted and found to hold good, we must at least try the aforesaid maxim of the power of judgment on nature as a whole too, since by following it a number of further laws of nature might well be brought to light that, given the narrowness of our insight into the inner workings of nature's mechanism, would otherwise stay hidden from us. As regards this second use, however, that maxim of the power of judgment, though useful, is not something we cannot do without, since nature taken as a whole is never presented to us as organized (in the narrowest meaning of the term noted earlier). When it comes to nature's products, by contrast — which have to be assessed as deliberately formed in this way and no other if one is to gain even an experiential cognition of their inner makeup — that maxim of the reflecting power of judgment is indispensable in essence: for it is not so much as possible to conceive of them as organized things unless one binds to that conception the idea of a production carried out with {{ 335 }} intention.

Now the concept of a thing the existence or form of which we picture to ourselves as possible under the condition of an end is inseparably connected with the concept of that thing's contingency (according to natural laws). Hence the natural things that strike us as possible solely as ends furnish the chief evidence that the world as a whole is contingent, and they alone supply the ground of proof — one that holds for common understanding no less {{{ 399 }}} than for the philosopher — for that whole's dependence upon, and derivation from, a being that exists beyond the world and (on account of that purposive form) is intelligent: with the result that teleology can bring its inquiries to no complete resolution except within a theology.

But what, in the end, does even the most complete teleology prove? Does it perhaps prove that such an intelligent being exists? No; nothing more than that, given the constitution of our cognitive faculties — that is, in linking experience to the supreme principles of reason — we can frame no concept whatsoever of how such a world is possible, save by conceiving for it an ***intentionally acting*** supreme cause. Objectively, therefore, we are unable to establish the proposition that an intelligent original being exists; we can do so only subjectively, on behalf of how our power of judgment is employed as it reflects on those ends {{ 336 }} within nature — ends that admit of being thought under no principle other than one of intentional causality exercised by a supreme cause.

Should we attempt to prove the supreme proposition dogmatically, drawing on teleological grounds, we would find ourselves caught up in perplexities out of which there would be no disentangling ourselves. For in that event such conclusions would need to be founded on the following claim: organized beings in the world can be possible in no other manner than through an intentionally acting cause. But that, because we can pursue these things in their causal connection only under the idea of ends and can cognize that connection according to its conformity to law, we would thereby also be entitled to presuppose the very same thing for every thinking and cognizing being as a necessary condition — one attaching, therefore, to the object and not merely to our subject: this is what we would here inevitably have to want to assert. But with an assertion of this sort we make no headway. For since we do not really ***observe*** the ends in nature as intentional, but only, in reflecting upon its products, ***think*** this concept into them as a guiding thread for the power of judgment, they are not given to us through the object. _A priori_ it is even impossible for us to justify such a concept, as regards its objective reality, as admissible. It therefore remains, absolutely, a proposition resting solely on subjective conditions — {{ 337 }} namely, on the power of judgment reflecting in a manner suited to our cognitive faculties — a proposition that, were one to express it as holding objectively and dogmatically, would run: There is a God; whereas now, for {{{ 400 }}} us human beings, only the restricted formula is permitted: We cannot in any way think and render intelligible to ourselves the purposiveness that must be laid at the ground even of our cognition of the inner possibility of many things of nature, except by representing them, and the world in general, to ourselves as a product of an intelligent cause (of a God).

Now if this proposition, resting as it does on an unavoidably necessary maxim of our power of judgment, fully satisfies every speculative as well as practical use of our reason in any ***human*** aim whatever: then I would gladly know what it is we forfeit in not being able to demonstrate it as holding for higher beings too — namely, from purely objective grounds (which, unfortunately, surpass our capacity). For it is quite certain that, on merely mechanical principles of nature, we can neither adequately acquaint ourselves with organized beings and their inner possibility nor, far less, account for them to ourselves; so certain, indeed, that one may say without hesitation: it is preposterous for human beings even to conceive such an undertaking, or to {{ 338 }} entertain the hope that one day some Newton might arise capable of rendering intelligible the generation of so much as a blade of grass by natural laws set in order by no intention at all; on the contrary, one must simply refuse human beings any such insight. But that in nature, too, supposing we could penetrate to its principle in the specification of its general laws known to us, a sufficient ground for the possibility of organized beings — without placing an intention beneath their production (that is, within nature's bare mechanism) — ***could*** not possibly lie hidden: this again would be far too presumptuous a judgment on our part; for how are we to know that? Probabilities fall away entirely here, where what is at stake are judgments of pure reason. — Thus, concerning the proposition whether a being acting according to intentions lies, as world-cause (and hence as author), at the ground of what we rightly call natural ends, we can pass no objective judgment at all, neither affirmative nor negative; only this much is certain, that if we are nonetheless to judge at least by what our own nature permits us to grasp (by the conditions and limits of our reason), we can place absolutely nothing other than an intelligent being at the ground of the possibility of those natural ends: which alone accords with the maxim of our reflecting power of judgment, and hence with a ground that is subjective, {{ 339 }} {{{ 401 }}} yet inseparably attaching to the human species.
