## § 75. The Concept of an Objective Purposiveness of Nature Is a Critical Principle of Reason for the Reflecting Power of Judgment

It is, after all, something quite different whether I say: the production of certain things of nature, or even of nature as a whole, is possible only through a cause that determines itself to action in accordance with intentions; or: I cannot, ***according to the peculiar constitution of my cognitive faculties***, judge about the possibility of those things and their {{{ 398 }}} production otherwise than by thinking of a cause acting in accordance with intentions as their basis, and hence thinking of a being that is productive in accordance with the analogy with the causality of an understanding. In the former case I want to establish something about the object and am bound to demonstrate the objective reality of an assumed concept; in the second, reason determines only the use of my cognitive faculties in a manner adequate to their peculiarity and to the essential conditions both of their scope and of their limits. Thus the first principle is an ***objective*** principle for the determining power of judgment, the second a subjective principle merely for the reflecting power of judgment, and hence a maxim {{ 334 }} of the latter which reason imposes upon it.

For we have an indispensable need to ascribe to nature the concept of an intention if we want to research it even merely in its organized products through continued observation; and this concept is therefore already an absolutely necessary maxim for the empirical use of our reason. It is obvious: that since once such a guideline for studying nature has been adopted and found to be approved, we must at least also try this thought-of maxim of the power of judgment on nature as a whole, because in accordance with it many more of its laws might yet be found, which would otherwise remain hidden from us given the limitation of our insights into the interior of its mechanism. But in respect of this latter use, that maxim of the power of judgment is indeed useful, but not indispensable, because nature as a whole is not given to us as organized (in the above-cited narrowest meaning of the word). On the other hand, in respect of its products, which must be judged only as intentionally formed so and not otherwise in order even merely to obtain an empirical cognition of their inner constitution, that maxim of the reflecting power of judgment is essentially necessary: because even the thought of them as organized things is impossible without combining with it the thought of a production with {{ 335 }} intention.

Now the concept of a thing whose existence or form we represent to ourselves as possible under the condition of an end is inseparably combined with the concept of its contingency (in accordance with laws of nature). Hence also natural things, which we find possible only as ends, constitute the most prominent proof for the contingency of the world-whole, and are the sole ground of proof, valid for common understanding just {{{ 399 }}} as much as for the philosopher, of its dependence and its origin upon a being existing outside the world and indeed (for the sake of that purposive form) an intelligent being: so that teleology therefore finds no completion of the disclosure for its investigations except in a theology.

But what, in the end, does even the most complete teleology prove? Does it prove, perhaps, that such an intelligent being exists? No; nothing further than that we, in accordance with the constitution of our cognitive faculties, and thus in combining experience with the supreme principles of reason, can form absolutely no concept of the possibility of such a world other than by thinking of an ***intentionally-acting*** supreme cause of it. Objectively, therefore, we cannot demonstrate the proposition: there is an intelligent original being; but only subjectively for the use of our power of judgment in its reflection on the ends {{ 336 }} in nature, which can be thought under no other principle than that of an intentional causality of a supreme cause.

If we wanted to demonstrate the supreme proposition dogmatically, from teleological grounds: then we would be beset by difficulties from which we could not extricate ourselves. For then these inferences would have to be based on the proposition: organized beings in the world are possible only through an intentionally-acting cause. But that, because we can pursue these things only under the idea of ends in their causal connection and recognize them in accordance with their lawfulness, we should also be justified in presupposing this very same thing as a necessary condition for every thinking and cognizing being, and hence as attaching to the object and not merely to our subject: this we would, in this case, inevitably have to want to assert. But we cannot get by with such an assertion. For since we do not actually ***observe*** the ends in nature as intentional, but only in reflection on its products ***think*** this concept additionally as a guideline for the power of judgment: they are therefore not given to us through the object. _A priori_ it is even impossible for us to justify such a concept as acceptable in accordance with its objective reality. There remains therefore absolutely only a proposition resting on subjective conditions, {{ 337 }} namely on the power of judgment reflecting adequately to our cognitive faculties, which, if one expressed it as objectively-dogmatically valid, would say: There is a God; but for {{{ 400 }}} us humans only the limited formula is permitted: We cannot think and make comprehensible to ourselves the purposiveness, which must be laid at the basis even of our cognition of the inner possibility of many natural things, otherwise than by representing it, and indeed the world in general, as a product of an intelligent cause (a God).

If, then, this proposition, founded on an unavoidably necessary maxim of our power of judgment, is perfectly sufficient for all use, speculative as well as practical, of our reason in every ***human*** intention: then I would like to know what we should lack in its not being possible for us to prove it as valid also for higher beings, namely from pure objective grounds (which unfortunately exceed our capacity). For it is quite certain that we cannot even sufficiently learn to know, much less explain, organized beings and their inner possibility in accordance with merely mechanical principles of nature; and indeed so certain that one can boldly say: it is absurd for human beings even to conceive of such a design, or to {{ 338 }} hope that some day a Newton might arise who could make comprehensible even the production of a blade of grass according to laws of nature which no intention has ordered; rather, one must absolutely deny this insight to human beings. But that in nature, on the other hand, if we could penetrate to its principle in the specification of its universal laws known to us, a sufficient ground of the possibility of organized beings without attributing an intention to their production (hence in their mere mechanism) could not lie hidden at all: that would again be judged too presumptuously on our part; for how do we know that? Probabilities fall away completely here, where it is a question of judgments of pure reason. — Thus we cannot judge objectively at all, either affirmatively or negatively, on the proposition: whether a being acting in accordance with intentions lies as a world-cause (hence as author) at the basis of what we with right call natural ends; only this much is certain, that if we are to judge at least in accordance with what is permitted us to see through our own nature (according to the conditions and limits of our reason), we can absolutely lay nothing other than an intelligent being at the basis of the possibility of those natural ends: which alone is in accordance with the maxim of our reflecting power of judgment, and consequently with a subjective ground {{ 339 }} {{{ 401 }}} that nonetheless unremittingly attaches to the human race.
