## § 75. The concept of nature's objective purposiveness is a critical principle of reason for the reflecting power of judgment

It is, after all, one thing entirely to claim: the production of certain things of nature, or even of the whole of nature, is possible only through a cause that determines itself to act according to intentions; and quite another to say that I can, ***in keeping with the peculiar constitution of my cognitive faculties***, judge the possibility of those things and their {{{ 398 }}} production in no other way than by conceiving, as their ground, a cause acting according to intentions, and hence a being that is productive by analogy with the causality of an understanding. In the first case I mean to settle something about the object and am bound to establish the objective reality of a concept I have assumed; in the second, reason merely fixes the use of my cognitive faculties in a manner suited to their peculiarity and to the essential conditions of both their scope and their limits. Thus the first principle is an ***objective*** principle for the determining power of judgment, the second a subjective principle merely for the reflecting power of judgment, and hence a maxim {{ 334 }} of the latter that reason imposes upon it.

For we find it indispensable to place the concept of an intention beneath nature if we wish to investigate it, even only in its organized products, through sustained observation; and so this concept is already, for the empirical employment of our reason, an utterly necessary maxim. It is evident that, once such a guiding thread for studying nature has been adopted and found to hold good, we must at least try the aforesaid maxim of the power of judgment on nature as a whole too, since by following it a number of further laws of nature might well be brought to light that, given the narrowness of our insight into the inner workings of nature's mechanism, would otherwise stay hidden from us. But with respect to the latter use that maxim of the power of judgment is indeed useful, yet not indispensable, because nature as a whole is not given to us as organized (in the strictest sense of the word cited above). With respect to its products, on the other hand — which must be judged as intentionally shaped just so and not otherwise if we are to obtain so much as an empirical cognition of their inner constitution — that maxim of the reflecting power of judgment is essentially necessary: for even the thought of them as organized things is impossible without joining to it the thought of a production with {{ 335 }} intention.

Now the concept of a thing the existence or form of which we picture to ourselves as possible under the condition of an end is inseparably connected with the concept of that thing's contingency (according to natural laws). For this reason the things of nature that we find possible only as ends constitute the foremost proof of the contingency of the world-whole and are the sole ground of proof — valid for the common understanding just as {{{ 399 }}} much as for the philosopher — of that whole's dependence upon, and origin from, a being existing outside the world and (for the sake of that purposive form) an intelligent one: so that teleology finds no completion of the disclosure it seeks for its inquiries except in a theology.

But what, in the end, does even the most complete teleology prove? Does it perhaps prove that such an intelligent being exists? No; nothing more than that, given the constitution of our cognitive faculties — that is, in linking experience to the supreme principles of reason — we can frame no concept whatsoever of how such a world is possible, save by conceiving for it an ***intentionally acting*** supreme cause. Objectively, then, we cannot make out the proposition: there exists an intelligent primordial being; but only subjectively, for the employment of our power of judgment in its reflection upon the ends {{ 336 }} in nature, which cannot be thought according to any principle other than that of an intentional causality of a highest cause.

Were we to try to establish the supreme proposition dogmatically, on teleological grounds, we would become entangled in difficulties from which we could not extricate ourselves. For then these inferences would have to rest on the proposition: the organized beings in the world are possible in no way other than through an intentionally acting cause. But that, because we can pursue these things in their causal connection only under the idea of ends and can cognize that connection according to its conformity to law, we would thereby also be entitled to presuppose the very same thing for every thinking and cognizing being as a necessary condition — one attaching, therefore, to the object and not merely to our subject: this is what we would here inevitably have to want to assert. But with an assertion of this sort we make no headway. For since we do not really ***observe*** the ends in nature as intentional, but only, in reflecting upon its products, ***think*** this concept into them as a guiding thread for the power of judgment, they are not given to us through the object. _A priori_ it is even impossible for us to justify such a concept, as regards its objective reality, as admissible. It therefore remains, absolutely, a proposition resting solely on subjective conditions — {{ 337 }} namely, on the power of judgment reflecting in a manner suited to our cognitive faculties — a proposition that, were one to express it as holding objectively and dogmatically, would run: There is a God; whereas now, for {{{ 400 }}} us human beings, only the restricted formula is permitted: We cannot in any way think and render intelligible to ourselves the purposiveness that must be laid at the ground even of our cognition of the inner possibility of many things of nature, except by representing them, and the world in general, to ourselves as a product of an intelligent cause (of a God).

Now if this proposition, grounded on an inescapably necessary maxim of our power of judgment, is perfectly satisfactory for every use of our reason, both speculative and practical, in any ***human*** aim: then I should very much like to know what we thereby lose by being unable to prove it valid for higher beings as well — that is, on purely objective grounds (which, sadly, exceed our capacity). For it is entirely certain that we cannot come to know organized beings and their inner possibility even adequately, still less explain them to ourselves, on merely mechanical principles of nature; and indeed so certain that one may boldly say: it is absurd for human beings so much as to form such a design, or to {{ 338 }} hope that some day a Newton might yet arise who would make comprehensible even the production of a single blade of grass according to natural laws that no intention has ordered; rather, we must flatly deny this insight to human beings. But that in nature, too, supposing we could penetrate to its principle in the specification of its general laws known to us, a sufficient ground for the possibility of organized beings — without placing an intention beneath their production (that is, within nature's bare mechanism) — ***could*** not possibly lie hidden: this again would be far too presumptuous a judgment on our part; for how are we to know that? Probabilities fall away entirely here, where what is at stake are judgments of pure reason. — Thus, concerning the proposition whether a being acting according to intentions lies, as world-cause (and hence as author), at the ground of what we rightly call natural ends, we can pass no objective judgment at all, neither affirmative nor negative; only this much is certain, that if we are nonetheless to judge at least by what our own nature permits us to grasp (by the conditions and limits of our reason), we can place absolutely nothing other than an intelligent being at the ground of the possibility of those natural ends: which alone accords with the maxim of our reflecting power of judgment, and hence with a ground that is subjective, {{ 339 }} {{{ 401 }}} yet inseparably attaching to the human species.
