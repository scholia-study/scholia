## {{{ 251 }}} § 26. On the estimation of the magnitude of natural things required for the idea of the sublime

Estimating magnitude through numerical concepts (or, in algebra, through their signs) proceeds mathematically, whereas estimating it in bare intuition — by what the eye can gauge — proceeds aesthetically. Now it is true that we arrive at determinate concepts of {{ 86 }} ***how large*** a thing is only by means of numbers (or, failing that, by approximations formed from numerical series that run on to infinity), and the unit of these is the measure; to that degree, every logical estimating of magnitude is mathematical. Yet since we are obliged all the same to take the magnitude of the measure as already known, then, were this too to be estimated only through numbers — whose unit would in turn have to be some further measure, and hence estimated mathematically — we should never reach a first, foundational measure, and therefore never gain any determinate concept of a given magnitude. So the magnitude of the foundational measure has to be estimated in one way only: by grasping it directly in an intuition and putting it to use, via the imagination, for presenting numerical concepts — which is to say that in the last analysis every appraisal of the magnitude of natural objects is aesthetic (determined, that is, subjectively and not objectively).

Now while the mathematical estimating of magnitude admits of no greatest (numbers being potent without limit), the aesthetic estimating of magnitude does admit of a greatest; and my claim about it is this: that once it is judged as an absolute measure, than which the subject doing the judging can hold none greater to be subjectively possible, it brings the idea of the sublime along with it and awakens that stirring of feeling which no mathematical appraisal of magnitudes by numbers is able to produce (save so far as {{ 87 }} that aesthetic foundational measure is kept alive and active in the imagination throughout): for the mathematical kind exhibits only relative magnitude, arrived at by comparison with others of like sort, whereas the aesthetic kind exhibits magnitude outright, as far as the mind is able to take it up in a single intuition.

Taking a quantum up into the imagination in intuition, so that it may serve as a measure or as a unit for estimating magnitude by numbers, calls for two operations of this power: ***apprehension*** (_apprehensio_) and ***comprehension*** (_comprehensio aesthetica_). Apprehension poses no problem, since it can run on to {{{ 252 }}} infinity; comprehension, however, grows harder the further apprehension pushes ahead, and quickly attains its ceiling — the aesthetically largest foundational measure for estimating magnitude. For once apprehension has gone far enough that the earliest-apprehended part-representations of sense-intuition are already beginning to die away in the imagination as it moves on to apprehend still more, then it is losing on the one side just what it gathers on the other, and comprehension meets with a greatest past which it cannot advance.

This lets us account for something ***Savary*** notes in his reports from Egypt: that to feel the full stirring {{ 88 }} that the pyramids' magnitude produces, one must neither draw very near to them nor stand too far off. For in the latter case the apprehended parts (the stones stacked one on another) are pictured only dimly, and their representation works no effect upon the subject's aesthetic judgment. In the former case, though, the eye requires a stretch of time to carry the apprehension through from the base up to the summit; and here the earlier parts keep partly dying away before the imagination has taken in the later ones, so that comprehension never comes out complete. — The very same account can equally serve to explain the consternation, or sort of embarrassment, that (so the story goes) takes hold of a visitor stepping for the first time into the church of Saint Peter in Rome. For at work here is a feeling that one's imagination falls short of the idea of a whole when it comes to presenting it — a point at which the imagination hits its ceiling and, straining to push past it, collapses back into itself, yet is thereby set into a moving kind of satisfaction.

I shall for now bring forward nothing about why this satisfaction comes about — a satisfaction linked to a representation from which one would look for it least of all, namely one that makes us aware of the unsuitability, and thus the subjective unpurposiveness, of the representation for the power of judgment as it estimates magnitude; I note only that, if the aesthetic judgment is to be ***pure*** (***with no teleological*** one, as a judgment of reason, {{ 89 }} ***intermixed***) and is to furnish an example fully fitted to a critique of the ***aesthetic*** power of judgment, then one has to point to the sublime not in works of art (say, buildings, columns, and the like), where a human end fixes the form as well as the size, nor in things of nature ***whose very concept carries a determinate {{{ 253 }}} end within it*** (say, animals whose natural function is known), but in nature untamed (and even there only so far as it holds in itself no charm, and no stirring born of real danger), purely so far as it comprises magnitude. For in a representation of this kind nature holds nothing that could count as monstrous (nor as splendid or as ghastly); the magnitude taken up may have swelled as far as you please, provided only that the imagination can still gather it into a whole. ***Monstrous*** is what we call an object when by its sheer magnitude it undoes the very end that makes up its concept. ***Colossal***, by contrast, is the name for the bare presentation of a concept that is well-nigh too vast for any presentation at all (verging on the relatively monstrous): for the aim of presenting a concept is here made harder by the fact that the object's intuition all but exceeds our power of apprehension. — A pure judgment upon the sublime, however, must take no end of the object whatever as its determining ground, if it is meant to be aesthetic {{ 90 }} and unmixed with any judgment of understanding or of reason.

Since anything meant to please the merely reflecting power of judgment apart from interest must carry within its representation a subjective purposiveness — one universally valid as such — and yet here, unlike with the beautiful, the judging rests on no purposiveness of the object's ***form***, the question presents itself: just what is this subjective purposiveness? and how does it come to be laid down as a norm, such that in the bare estimating of magnitude — indeed the sort pushed clear to the point where our power of imagination proves unequal to presenting the concept of a magnitude — it can supply a ground for universally valid satisfaction?

In the assembling that the representation of magnitude demands, the imagination presses on to infinity all on its own, with nothing to check it; but the understanding steers it by way of numerical concepts, for which the imagination has to hand over the schema: and while this procedure, insofar as it belongs to the logical estimating of magnitude, does carry something objectively purposive under the concept of an end (as any measuring does), it carries nothing that would be purposive and pleasing for the aesthetic power of judgment. Nor is there anything in this deliberate purposiveness that would {{ 91 }} {{{ 254 }}} compel one to drive the magnitude of the measure — and with it the ***comprehension*** of a manifold into one intuition — up to the very limit of the imagination's power and as far as its presentations can ever stretch. For in the understanding's estimating of magnitudes (arithmetic) one comes just as far whether the comprehension of units is carried to the number 10 (in the decadic scheme) or only to 4 (in the tetradic); the further production of magnitude, whether in assembling or, where the quantum is given in intuition, in apprehending, gets done merely progressively (not comprehensively) on some adopted principle of progression. In this mathematical estimating of magnitude the understanding is just as well served and satisfied whether the imagination selects for its unit a magnitude graspable at a glance — say a foot or a rod — or selects a German mile, or even an earth's diameter, of which apprehension is indeed feasible but not comprehension into a single intuition of the imagination (not by the _comprehensio aesthetica_, though quite well by the _comprehensio logica_ into a numerical concept). In either case the logical estimating of magnitude runs on to infinity unimpeded.

But now the mind gives ear within itself to reason's voice, which for every given magnitude — even those that, though never able to be apprehended in full, are still (in sensory representation) {{ 92 }} judged as given in full — insists on totality, hence calls for comprehension into ***one*** intuition and for the ***presentation*** of all the members of a numerical series that grows step by step, and which exempts not even the infinite (space and time already elapsed) from this demand, making it unavoidable instead to think that infinite (in the judgment of ordinary reason) as ***wholly*** (in respect of its totality) ***given***.

The infinite, though, is large in an unqualified way (not just by comparison). Set beside it, everything else (of that same class of magnitudes) turns out small. But the chief point — that we are able to think it even so much as ***a whole*** — bespeaks a power of the mind that outreaches every scale the senses provide. For that would demand a comprehension yielding a scale, as unit, that stood to the infinite in some determinate ratio statable in numbers: and that is impossible. Yet ***to be able so much as to think*** the given infinite, and to do so free of contradiction, demands within the human mind a power supersensible in its own right. For only by way of this power, and {{{ 255 }}} of its idea of a noumenon — a noumenon that grants no intuition of itself and yet is set down as the substrate beneath our intuition of the world as sheer appearance — does the infinite of the sensible world come, in the pure intellectual estimating of magnitude, to be drawn ***wholly*** ***beneath*** a single concept, {{ 93 }} although it can never be thought out fully in the mathematical kind ***by numerical concepts***. Indeed, the sheer capacity to conceive the infinite of supersensible intuition as given (within its intelligible substrate) outreaches every scale that sensibility affords and is, past all comparison, greater even than the capacity for mathematical estimating; not, admittedly, in a theoretical regard on behalf of our cognitive power, but still as an expansion of the mind, which feels itself equal to overstepping the bounds of sensibility in another regard — the practical one.

Nature, then, is sublime in those appearances of hers whose intuition brings with it the idea of her infinity. And this last can come about in no other way than through the failure of even our imagination's utmost effort at estimating an object's magnitude to prove adequate. Yet for mathematical estimating of magnitude the imagination is a match for any object, able to furnish an adequate measure for it, since the understanding's numerical concepts can, by progression, render any measure adequate to any given magnitude. It must be, then, the ***aesthetic*** estimating of magnitude in which one feels the effort at comprehension outrun the imagination's capacity to gather the progressive apprehension into one whole of intuition, and in which one at the same time perceives the inadequacy of this {{ 94 }} capacity — boundless as it is in its advance — for laying hold of a foundational measure fit, with the least outlay of understanding, for estimating magnitude, and for putting it to that use. Now nature's own unchanging foundational measure is her absolute whole, which for her as appearance amounts to infinity gathered together. Since this foundational measure is, however, a concept in conflict with itself — for an absolute totality of a progression that has no end is impossible — whatever magnitude in a natural object exhausts, to no purpose, the imagination's entire power of comprehension must lead the concept of nature onward to a supersensible substrate (which grounds both nature and, along with it, our own capacity to think), a substrate whose greatness outstrips every measure the senses supply, and which for that reason lets one judge as ***sublime*** not {{{ 256 }}} the object so much as the mind's own disposition while appraising it.

And so, just as the aesthetic power of judgment, in appraising the beautiful, refers the imagination in its free play to the ***understanding***, so as to accord with the latter's ***concepts*** as such (leaving them undetermined): so, in appraising a thing as sublime, it refers that same faculty to ***reason***, so as to accord subjectively with reason's ***ideas*** (no matter which), that is, to call up a cast of mind that answers to, and sits well with, the one that {{ 95 }} the sway of determinate ideas (practical ones) over feeling would induce.

One gathers from this as well that true sublimity must be looked for solely within the mind of the person judging, rather than in the natural object whose appraisal occasions this attunement of his. For who would ever call sublime the misshapen mountain masses heaped in wild disorder upon one another, together with their ice-pyramids, or the gloomy, storming sea, and so forth? But the mind feels itself uplifted in its own appraising when, letting itself go in contemplation of such things — heedless of their form — to the imagination and to a reason that, though bound up with it quite without any determinate end, merely enlarges it, it nonetheless finds the imagination's entire might unequal to that reason's ideas.

Instances of the mathematically sublime in nature, met with in bare intuition, are supplied by every case where what is set before the imagination is not so much a bigger numerical concept as a large unit to serve as measure (so as to shorten the numerical series). A tree we gauge by a man's height will at any rate afford a scale for a mountain; and should that mountain run to about a mile in height, it can serve as the unit for the number expressing the earth's diameter, so as to make the latter intuitable — the earth's diameter for the planetary system familiar to us, {{ 96 }} that system for the system of the Milky Way; and the boundless throng of such Milky-Way systems, going by the name of nebulae, which presumably make up yet another such system among themselves, leaves us no bound to look for here. Now, in appraising aesthetically a whole so immense, the sublime resides not so much in the numerical magnitude as in this: that with each advance we arrive at units greater still; and to this the systematic partitioning of the world's structure {{{ 257 }}} lends its aid, presenting to us everything great in nature as once more small, and, more properly, presenting our imagination in the whole of its boundlessness — and nature together with it — as vanishing away before the ideas of reason, whenever it is called upon to supply a presentation adequate to them.
