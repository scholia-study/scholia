## {{{ 251 }}} § 26. On the estimation of the magnitude of the things of nature that is requisite for the idea of the sublime

The estimation of magnitude through concepts of number (or their signs in algebra) is mathematical, but that in mere intuition (by the measure of the eye) is aesthetic. Now we can indeed obtain determinate concepts of {{ 86 }} ***how great*** something is only through numbers (or, at any rate, approximations through number series progressing into the infinite), whose unit is the measure; and to this extent all logical estimation of magnitude is mathematical. But since the magnitude of the measure must yet be assumed as known, then, if this were now in turn to be estimated only through numbers, whose unit would have to be another measure, hence mathematically, we could never have a first or basic measure, hence also no determinate concept of a given magnitude. Thus the estimation of the magnitude of the basic measure must consist merely in this, that one can immediately grasp it in an intuition and use it through the imagination for the presentation of the concepts of number: i.e., all estimation of the magnitude of the objects of nature is ultimately aesthetic (i.e., subjectively and not objectively determined).

Now for the mathematical estimation of magnitude there is indeed no greatest (for the power of numbers goes into the infinite); but for the aesthetic estimation of magnitude there certainly is a greatest; and of this I say: that, when it is judged as an absolute measure beyond which no greater is subjectively (for the judging subject) possible, it carries with it the idea of the sublime and produces that emotion which no mathematical estimation of magnitudes through numbers can effect (except insofar as {{ 87 }} that aesthetic basic measure is thereby kept alive in the imagination): because the latter always presents only the relative magnitude through comparison with others of the same kind, but the former presents the magnitude absolutely, so far as the mind can grasp it in one intuition.

To take up a quantum intuitively into the imagination, in order to be able to use it as a measure or as a unit for the estimation of magnitude through numbers, there belong two actions of this faculty: ***apprehension*** (_apprehensio_) and ***comprehension*** (_comprehensio aesthetica_). With apprehension there is no difficulty: for with it the imagination can go into the {{{ 252 }}} infinite; but comprehension becomes ever more difficult the farther apprehension advances, and soon reaches its maximum, namely the aesthetically greatest basic measure for the estimation of magnitude. For when apprehension has gone so far that the partial representations of the intuition of sense that were first apprehended already begin to be extinguished in the imagination, while it advances to the apprehension of more: then it loses on the one side just as much as it gains on the other, and in comprehension there is a greatest beyond which it cannot get.

From this can be explained what ***Savary*** remarks in his reports on Egypt: that one must not come very near the pyramids, just as little as be too far away from them, in order to get the full emotion {{ 88 }} from their magnitude. For if the latter, then the parts that are apprehended (the stones of them, one upon another) are represented only obscurely, and their representation has no effect on the aesthetic judgment of the subject. But if the former, then the eye requires some time to complete the apprehension from the base up to the apex; but in this the first ones are always in part extinguished before the imagination has taken up the last ones, and the comprehension is never complete. — The very same thing can also suffice to explain the bewilderment, or kind of embarrassment, that, as is reported, comes over the spectator on first entering St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. For here there is a feeling of the inadequacy of his imagination for the idea of a whole in order to present it, in which the imagination reaches its maximum and, in the striving to extend it, sinks back into itself, but is thereby transported into a moving satisfaction.

I will for now adduce nothing yet of the ground of this satisfaction, which is connected with a representation from which one should least expect it, namely one that lets us notice the inadequacy, hence also the subjective contrapurposiveness, of the representation for the power of judgment in the estimation of magnitude; but only remark that, if the aesthetic judgment is to be ***pure*** (***mixed with no teleological*** one as a judgment of reason {{ 89 }}) and an example perfectly suited to the critique of the ***aesthetic*** power of judgment is to be given thereby, one must exhibit the sublime not in products of art (e.g., buildings, columns, etc.), where a human end determines the form as well as the magnitude, nor in things of nature ***whose concept already carries with it a determinate end {{{ 253 }}}*** (e.g., animals of a known natural determination), but in raw nature (and even in this only insofar as it carries with it for itself no charm, or emotion from actual danger), merely insofar as it contains magnitude. For in this kind of representation nature contains nothing that would be monstrous (nor anything magnificent or horrible); the magnitude that is apprehended may have grown as far as one likes, provided only that it can be comprehended into a whole through the imagination. An object is ***monstrous*** if by its magnitude it nullifies the end that constitutes its concept. But ***colossal*** is the name given to the mere presentation of a concept that is almost too great for all presentation (borders on the relatively monstrous): because the end of the presentation of a concept is made difficult by the fact that the intuition of the object is almost too great for our faculty of apprehension. — But a pure judgment about the sublime must have no end of the object as its determining ground if it is to be aesthetic {{ 90 }} and not mixed with any judgment of understanding or of reason.

Because everything that is to please the merely reflecting power of judgment without interest must carry with it in its representation a subjective and, as such, universally valid purposiveness, yet here no purposiveness of the ***form*** of the object (as in the case of the beautiful) lies at the ground of the judging, the question arises: what is this subjective purposiveness? and through what is it prescribed as a norm, so as to provide a ground for universally valid satisfaction in the mere estimation of magnitude, and indeed of that which has been driven even to the inadequacy of our faculty of imagination in the presentation of the concept of a magnitude?

The imagination advances of itself, without anything hindering it, into the infinite in the composition that is requisite for the representation of magnitude; but the understanding guides it through concepts of number, for which the former must supply the schema: and in this procedure, as belonging to the logical estimation of magnitude, there is indeed something objectively purposive according to the concept of an end (such as every measurement is), but nothing purposive and pleasing for the aesthetic power of judgment. There is also in this intentional purposiveness nothing that would {{ 91 }} {{{ 254 }}} constrain one to drive the magnitude of the measure, hence the ***comprehension*** of the many into one intuition, up to the limit of the faculty of imagination and as far as this may ever reach in presentations. For in the understanding's estimation of magnitudes (in arithmetic) one comes just as far whether one drives the comprehension of units up to the number 10 (in the decadic system), or only up to 4 (in the tetractic system); the further generation of magnitude in composition, or, if the quantum is given in intuition, in apprehension is performed merely progressively (not comprehensively) according to an assumed principle of progression. The understanding is just as well served and satisfied in this mathematical estimation of magnitude whether the imagination chooses for the unit a magnitude that one can grasp in a single glance, e.g., a foot or a rod, or whether it chooses a German mile, or even an earth-diameter, the apprehension of which is indeed possible, but not the comprehension of it into one intuition of the imagination (not through _comprehensio aesthetica_, although quite well through _comprehensio logica_ into a concept of number). In both cases the logical estimation of magnitude goes unhindered into the infinite.

But now the mind hears in itself the voice of reason, which demands totality for all given magnitudes, even for those which can indeed never be wholly apprehended, although they are nonetheless judged (in the sensible representation) {{ 92 }} as wholly given, hence demands comprehension into ***one*** intuition and ***presentation*** for all those members of a progressively increasing number series, and does not exempt even the infinite (space and elapsed time) from this demand, but rather makes it unavoidable to think of it (in the judgment of common reason) as ***given wholly*** (according to its totality).

But the infinite is absolutely (not merely comparatively) great. Compared with this, everything else (of the same kind of magnitudes) is small. But, what is most important, even to be able to think it as ***a whole*** indicates a faculty of the mind that surpasses every standard of the senses. For this there would be required a comprehension that would yield a measure as a unit which had a determinate relation, expressible in numbers, to the infinite: which is impossible. But to be able ***even to think*** the given infinite nonetheless without contradiction, there is required in the human mind a faculty that is itself supersensible. For only through this and {{{ 255 }}} its idea of a noumenon, which itself permits no intuition but is yet laid as the substratum at the ground of the intuition of the world, as mere appearance, is the infinite of the sensible world wholly comprehended ***under*** a concept in the pure intellectual estimation of magnitude, {{ 93 }} although in the mathematical estimation ***through concepts of number*** it can never be wholly thought. Even a faculty of being able to think the infinite of supersensible intuition as given (in its intelligible substratum) surpasses every standard of sensibility and is great beyond all comparison even with the faculty of mathematical estimation; not indeed in theoretical respect for the sake of the faculty of cognition, but yet as an enlargement of the mind, which feels itself capable of overstepping the limits of sensibility in another (the practical) respect.

Nature is thus sublime in those of its appearances the intuition of which carries with it the idea of its infinity. Now this latter can come about in no other way than through the inadequacy of even the greatest striving of our imagination in the estimation of the magnitude of an object. Now for the mathematical estimation of magnitude the imagination is equal to every object, in order to give an adequate measure for it, because the concepts of number of the understanding can, through progression, make every measure adequate to any given magnitude. Thus it must be the ***aesthetic*** estimation of magnitude in which the striving toward comprehension, which exceeds the faculty of the imagination, to grasp the progressive apprehension into a whole of intuition, is felt, and at the same time the inadequacy of this faculty, {{ 94 }} unbounded in its progression, is perceived, to grasp a basic measure suitable for the estimation of magnitude with the least expenditure of the understanding and to use it for the estimation of magnitude. Now the proper unalterable basic measure of nature is the absolute whole of it, which in it, as appearance, is comprehended infinity. But since this basic measure is a self-contradictory concept (on account of the impossibility of the absolute totality of a progressus without end): the magnitude of a natural object on which the imagination fruitlessly expends its whole faculty of comprehension must lead the concept of nature to a supersensible substratum (which lies at the ground of it and at the same time of our faculty for thinking), which is great beyond every standard of the senses and hence lets us judge as ***sublime*** not so much {{{ 256 }}} the object as rather the disposition of mind in the estimation of it.

Thus, just as the aesthetic power of judgment, in judging the beautiful, relates the imagination in its free play to the ***understanding***, in order to agree with its ***concepts*** in general (without determination of them): so in judging a thing as sublime it relates the same faculty to ***reason***, in order to agree subjectively with its ***ideas*** (indeterminate which ones), i.e., to bring forth a disposition of mind that is conformable to, and compatible with, that which the influence of determinate ideas (practical ones) upon feeling would produce {{ 95 }}.

One sees from this also that the true sublimity must be sought only in the mind of the one judging, not in the object of nature the judging of which occasions this disposition of it. Who indeed would want to call sublime shapeless masses of mountains, towering up over one another in wild disorder, with their pyramids of ice, or the dark, raging sea, etc.? But the mind feels itself elevated in its own judging when, in contemplating them without regard to their form, abandoning itself to the imagination and to a reason connected with it though entirely without a determinate end, merely enlarging the former, it nevertheless finds the entire might of the imagination inadequate to its ideas.

Examples of the mathematically sublime in nature in mere intuition are provided for us by all those cases where there is given to us, not so much a greater concept of number, as rather a great unit as a measure (for the shortening of the number series) for the imagination. A tree, which we estimate by the height of a man, gives at any rate a standard for a mountain; and if this were, say, a mile high, it can serve as the unit for the number that expresses the earth's diameter, in order to make the latter intuitable, the earth's diameter for the planetary system known to us, {{ 96 }} this for that of the Milky Way; and the immeasurable multitude of such Milky Way systems under the name of nebulae, which presumably in turn constitute among themselves a like system, lets us expect no limits here. Now the sublime in the aesthetic judging of so immeasurable a whole lies not so much in the magnitude of the number as in this, that in our progress we always arrive at ever greater units; to which the systematic division of the structure of the world {{{ 257 }}} contributes, which represents to us everything great in nature as in turn small, but properly represents our imagination in its entire boundlessness, and with it nature, as vanishing in comparison with the ideas of reason, if it is to provide a presentation adequate to them.
