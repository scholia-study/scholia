## § 3. The Satisfaction in the Agreeable is Combined with Interest

***Agreeable is that which pleases the senses in sensation.*** Here the occasion at once presents itself to censure a quite common confusion of the twofold meaning that the word sensation can have, and to draw attention to it. All satisfaction (so one says or thinks) is itself sensation (of a pleasure). Consequently {{{ 206 }}} everything that pleases is, precisely in this, that it pleases, agreeable (and, according to the {{ 8 }} different degrees or also relations to other agreeable sensations, ***charming, lovely, delightful, gladdening***, etc.). But if that is granted, then impressions of the senses, which determine inclination, or principles of reason, which determine the will, or mere reflected forms of intuition, which determine the power of judgment, are, as far as concerns the effect on the feeling of pleasure, entirely one and the same. For this would be the agreeableness in the sensation of one's state, and since after all in the end all the cultivation of our faculties must issue in the practical and be united therein as in their goal, one could expect of them no other estimation of things and their worth than that which consists in the gratification they promise. In the end it does not matter at all in what way they arrive at this; and since the choice of means alone can here make a difference, human beings might well accuse one another of foolishness and lack of understanding, but never of baseness and malice: for they all, each according to his own way of seeing things, run after one goal, which for everyone is gratification.

If a determination of the feeling of pleasure or displeasure is called sensation, this expression means something entirely different from what it means when I call the representation of a thing (through the senses, as a receptivity belonging to the faculty of cognition) sensation. For in the latter {{ 9 }} case the representation is related to the object, but in the former solely to the subject, and serves for no cognition at all, not even for that through which the subject ***cognizes*** itself.

In the above explanation, however, we understand by the word sensation an objective representation of the senses; and in order not to be always in danger of being misinterpreted, we will call that which must always remain merely subjective and can absolutely not constitute a representation of an object by the otherwise customary name of feeling. The green color of the meadows belongs to ***objective*** sensation, as the perception of an object of sense; but its agreeableness belongs to ***subjective*** sensation, through which no object is represented: i.e., to feeling, through which the object is considered as an object of satisfaction (which is not a cognition of it).

That now my judgment about an object, through which I declare it to be {{{ 207 }}} agreeable, expresses an interest in it, is already clear from the fact that through sensation it arouses a desire for objects of that kind, hence the satisfaction presupposes not the mere judgment about it, but the relation of its existence to my state, insofar as it is affected by such an object. Hence one says of the agreeable not merely: it ***pleases***, but: it ***gratifies***. It is not {{ 10 }} a mere approval that I devote to it, but rather inclination is thereby produced; and to that which is agreeable in the liveliest way there belongs so little any judgment about the constitution of the object that those who are always intent only on enjoyment (for that is the word with which one designates the inwardness of gratification) gladly dispense with all judging.
