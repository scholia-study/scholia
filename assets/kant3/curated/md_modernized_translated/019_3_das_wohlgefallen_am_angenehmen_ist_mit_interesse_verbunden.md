## § 3. The satisfaction in the agreeable is combined with interest

***That is agreeable which pleases the senses in sensation.*** Here at once an occasion presents itself to censure a very common confusion of the twofold meaning that the word sensation can carry, and to draw attention to it. All satisfaction (so one says or thinks) is itself sensation (of a pleasure). Consequently {{{ 206 }}} everything that pleases is, precisely in this, that it pleases, agreeable (and, according to the {{ 8 }} various degrees, or also relations to other agreeable sensations, ***graceful, lovely, delightful, gladdening*** and so forth). Once that is granted, however, then—as regards the effect on the feeling of pleasure—it is wholly indifferent whether inclination is determined by impressions of the senses, the will by principles of reason, or the power of judgment by mere reflected forms of intuition. For this effect would be the agreeableness in the sensation of one's own state, and since, after all, all working of our faculties must ultimately issue in the practical and converge there as in its goal, one could ask of them no other appraisal of things and of their worth than the one that consists in the gratification they promise. The manner in which they arrive at it is in the end of no consequence at all; and since here the choice of means alone can make any difference, human beings might indeed charge one another with folly and want of understanding, but never with baseness and malice: for after all they are, each according to his own way of viewing things, running toward one goal, which for everyone is gratification.

The word sensation, when applied to a determination of the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, means something wholly other than it does when I apply it to the representation of a thing (given through the senses, as a receptivity pertaining to the faculty of cognition). For in the latter {{ 9 }} instance the representation is related to the object, whereas in the former it is connected merely with the subject and contributes to no cognition at all, not even to that whereby the subject ***cognizes*** itself.

In the above explanation, though, the word sensation stands for us as an objective representation of the senses; and, in order not to risk perpetual misinterpretation, we will designate whatever must forever remain purely subjective, and can by no means amount to a representation of an object, with the name otherwise customary for it, namely feeling. As the perception of an object of sense, the meadows' green color pertains to ***objective*** sensation; its agreeableness, however, pertains to ***subjective*** sensation, whereby no object at all is represented—that is, to feeling, whereby the object comes to be regarded as an object of satisfaction (which involves no cognition of it).

That my judgment concerning an object—by which I pronounce it {{{ 207 }}} agreeable—gives expression to an interest in it is already evident from this: that by means of sensation it arouses a craving for objects of that sort, so that the satisfaction presupposes, not the bare judging of the object, but rather the relation of its existence to my own state insofar as that state is affected by such an object. Hence of the agreeable one does not merely say: it ***pleases***, but rather: it ***gratifies***. It is not {{ 10 }} a mere assent that I bestow on it; rather, inclination is thereby produced; and to that which is agreeable in the liveliest way there belongs so utterly no judgment about the constitution of the object that those who are ever bent solely on enjoyment (for that is the word by which one denotes the inwardness of gratification) gladly excuse themselves from all judging.
