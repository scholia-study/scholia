## § 50. On the combination of taste with genius in products of fine art

When the question is which matters more in things of fine art, whether that genius shows itself in them or that taste does, this amounts to just the same as if one were to ask whether in this more turns on imagination than on the power of judgment. Now since, with respect to the former, an art rather deserves to be called ***spirited***, but with respect to the latter alone a ***beautiful*** art: the latter is at least, as the indispensable condition (_conditio sine qua non_), the foremost thing to which one must attend in judging an art as fine art. To be rich and original in ideas is not so necessarily required for the sake of beauty, but rather the fittingness of that imagination in its freedom to the lawfulness of the understanding. For all the wealth of the former, in its lawless freedom, {{ 203 }} brings forth nothing but nonsense; the power of judgment, by contrast, is the faculty for adapting it to the understanding.

Taste is, ***just*** as the power of judgment in general is, the discipline (or training) of genius; it severely clips its wings and makes it well-mannered or polished; but at the same time it gives it guidance as to what it should range over and how far, so as to remain purposive; and by bringing clarity and order into the fullness of thoughts, it makes the ideas durable, capable of a lasting and at the same time universal approval, of being followed by others, and of an ever-advancing culture. If, then, in a conflict of the two properties in one product {{{ 320 }}} something is to be sacrificed, it would rather have to happen on the side of genius: and the power of judgment, which in things of fine art delivers its verdict from principles of its own, will sooner permit an infringement upon the freedom and wealth of the imagination than upon the understanding.

For fine art, then, ***imagination***, ***understanding***, ***spirit***, and ***taste*** would be required[^*].

[^*]: The three first-mentioned faculties receive their ***unification*** only through the fourth. ***Hume***, in his History, gives the English to understand that, although in their works they yield to no people in the world with respect to the proofs of the three first properties considered ***separately***, they must nonetheless fall short of their neighbors, the French, in that which unites them.
