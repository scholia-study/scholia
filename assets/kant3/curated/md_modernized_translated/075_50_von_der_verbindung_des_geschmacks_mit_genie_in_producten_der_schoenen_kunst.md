## § 50. On the combination of taste with genius in products of beautiful art

If the question is what matters more in matters of beautiful art, whether it is that genius show itself in them, or whether it is that taste show itself, then that is just as much as if it were asked whether in it more depends on imagination than on the power of judgment. Since now an art deserves, in regard to the former, rather to be called a ***rich in spirit*** art, but in regard to the second alone to be called a ***beautiful*** art: so the latter is at least, as the indispensable condition (_conditio sine qua non_), the foremost thing that one has to look to in the judging of art as beautiful art. To be rich and original in ideas is not so necessarily required for the sake of beauty, but rather the commensurateness of that imagination in its freedom with the lawfulness of the understanding. For all the richness of the former produces in its lawless freedom {{ 203 }} nothing but nonsense; the power of judgment, by contrast, is the faculty for adapting it to the understanding.

Taste is, ***just*** like the power of judgment in general, the discipline (or training) of genius, clips its wings very much and makes it well-mannered or polished; but at the same time it gives it a guidance as to where and how far it should spread itself in order to remain purposive; and in that it introduces clarity and order into the abundance of thoughts, it makes the ideas durable, capable of a lasting and at the same time also universal approval, of being followed by others, and of an ever-advancing culture. If, then, in the conflict of the two sorts of properties in a product {{{ 320 }}} something must be sacrificed, it would have to happen rather on the side of genius: and the power of judgment, which in matters of beautiful art pronounces its verdict from its own principles, will sooner permit damage to be done to the freedom and the richness of the imagination than to the understanding.

For beautiful art, then, ***imagination***, ***understanding***, ***spirit***, and ***taste*** would be requisite[^*].

[^*]: The three former faculties first acquire their ***unification*** through the fourth. ***Hume***, in his history, gives the English to understand that, although in their works they would yield nothing to any people in the world in regard to the evidences of the three former properties, considered ***separately***, they would nevertheless have to stand behind their neighbors, the French, in that one which unites them.
