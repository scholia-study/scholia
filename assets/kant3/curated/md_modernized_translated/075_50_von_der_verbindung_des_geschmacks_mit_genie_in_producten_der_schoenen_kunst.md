## § 50. On the combination of taste with genius in products of fine art

When the question is which matters more in things of fine art, whether that genius shows itself in them or that taste does, this amounts to just the same as if one were to ask whether in this more turns on imagination than on the power of judgment. Now since, with respect to the former, an art rather deserves to be called ***spirited***, but with respect to the latter alone a ***beautiful*** art: the latter is at least, as the indispensable condition (_conditio sine qua non_), the foremost thing to which one must attend in judging an art as fine art. For the sake of beauty it is not so indispensably necessary to be rich and original in ideas; what is required, rather, is that this imagination, in its freedom, should accord with the lawfulness of the understanding. For, in its lawless freedom, all the abundance of the former produces {{ 203 }} nothing but nonsense; by contrast, the power of judgment is the capacity for suiting it to the understanding.

Taste, ***just*** like the power of judgment as such, is the discipline (or chastening) of genius; it trims its wings severely and renders it civil or refined; yet it likewise supplies it with guidance about what, and how far, it may spread itself so as to stay purposive; and, by introducing clarity and order into the abundance of thought, it renders the ideas enduring, fit for a lasting and withal universal acclaim, for emulation by others, and for a culture that ever presses forward. If, therefore, in the clash of these two properties within a single product {{{ 320 }}} something has to be surrendered, it ought sooner to occur on genius's side: for it is the power of judgment—delivering its pronouncement in matters of fine art from principles all its own—that will more readily countenance a curtailment of the imagination's freedom and wealth than of the understanding.

For fine art, then, ***imagination***, ***understanding***, ***spirit***, and ***taste*** would be required[^*].

[^*]: Only by means of the fourth do the three faculties first named attain their ***unification***. ***Hume***, in his History, lets the English know that, though in their productions they concede nothing to any nation on earth as regards the evidences of the three earlier qualities taken ***separately***, they must yet rank below their neighbors, the French, in that quality which brings these together.
