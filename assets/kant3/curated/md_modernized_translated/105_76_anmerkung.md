## § 76. Remark

This consideration, which very much deserves to be carried out at length within transcendental philosophy, may here be introduced only in passing, by way of clarification (not as a proof of what has been set forth here).

As a faculty of principles, reason presses in its furthest demand toward the unconditioned, while the understanding, by contrast, is only ever of service to it under some given condition that has to be supplied. Without concepts of the understanding, however, to which objective reality must be given, reason cannot judge objectively (synthetically) at all, and as theoretical reason it contains, taken by itself, absolutely no constitutive but merely regulative principles. One soon becomes aware: that, where the understanding cannot follow, reason becomes extravagant and comes forward with ideas that are indeed well-founded (as regulative principles) but not with objectively valid concepts; the understanding, however, which cannot keep step with it, yet which would be needed for validity with respect to objects, restricts the validity of those ideas of reason to the subject alone, though universally for all beings of this kind, i.e., to the condition that, in accordance with the nature of our (human) faculty of cognition, or indeed generally in accordance with the concept ***that we*** can ***form for ourselves*** of the faculty of a finite rational being as such, it can and must be thought in no way other than this: yet without thereby maintaining that the ground of such a judgment {{ 340 }} lies in the object. We shall adduce examples that admittedly carry too much importance and also too much difficulty to be forced upon the reader here straightaway as proven propositions, but which can give him material for reflection and can serve to clarify what is here our peculiar business.

For the human understanding it is inescapably necessary to draw a distinction between what is possible and what is actual in things. The ground for this lies in the subject and in the nature of its faculties of cognition. For were their exercise not to call for two wholly disparate elements—understanding to supply concepts and sensible intuition to supply the objects answering to them—no such distinction {{{ 402 }}} (of the possible from the actual) would arise at all. Were our understanding an intuitive one, it would possess no objects save the actual. Concepts (bearing merely on an object's possibility) and sensible intuitions (which afford us something without on that account letting it be cognized as an object) would both drop away. Now, however, our entire distinction of the merely possible from the actual rests on this: that the former designates only the positing of a thing's representation relative to our concept, and to the capacity for thinking in general, whereas the latter designates the thing's being posited in itself (outside this concept). The distinction of possible things from actual ones is therefore one that holds merely subjectively for the human understanding, since we can, namely, always still have something in thought even though it is not, or represent something to ourselves as given even though we as yet have no concept of it. The propositions, then—that things may be possible without being actual, and hence that mere possibility licenses no inference whatever to actuality—hold quite correctly for human {{ 341 }} reason, without thereby proving that this difference lies in the things themselves. For that this admits of no such inference, so that those propositions, while they certainly do apply to objects as well—insofar as our faculty of cognition, being sensibly conditioned, concerns itself likewise with objects of the senses—yet do not apply to things as such, becomes clear from reason's ceaseless requirement that some entity or other (the primordial ground) be assumed as existing with unconditioned necessity, an entity in which possibility and actuality are no longer meant to be told apart, and for whose idea the understanding has strictly no concept, i.e., can hit upon no way of picturing to itself such a thing along with its manner of existing. For if it ***thinks*** it (however it may choose to think it), then it is represented merely as possible. Should it be conscious of the thing as given in intuition, it is then actual, without thinking anything of possibility in the process. Hence the concept of an absolutely necessary being is indeed an indispensable idea of reason, but a problematic concept unattainable for the human understanding. But it does hold for the use of our faculties of cognition according to their peculiar constitution, and hence not of the object, and thereby not for every cognizing being: because thinking and intuition, as two different conditions for exercising its faculties of cognition, and hence for the possibility and actuality of things, {{{ 403 }}} I cannot presuppose in every such being. For an understanding in which this distinction did not occur, it would mean: all objects that I cognize ***are*** (exist); and the possibility of some that nevertheless did not exist, i.e., their contingency if they do exist, and hence also the necessity to be distinguished from it, could not at all {{ 342 }} come into the representation of such a being. But what our understanding here finds so burdensome—matching reason with its concepts—is merely this: that for it, as human understanding, that is extravagant (i.e., impossible under the subjective conditions of its cognition) which reason nevertheless erects into a principle as pertaining to the object. — The maxim that governs throughout is this: that wherever the cognition of objects outstrips the capacity of the understanding, we think all of them in keeping with those subjective conditions for exercising our faculties which cling necessarily to our nature (i.e., the human one); and should the judgments delivered in this manner (as, with the extravagant concepts, could not be otherwise) prove unable to be constitutive principles that fix the object as it stands, they will yet abide as regulative ones—immanent and dependable in their exercise, and matched to the human purpose.

Just as reason, in the theoretical consideration of nature, finds itself compelled to posit the idea of an unconditioned necessity belonging to its primordial ground: so too, in the practical sphere, it presupposes its own unconditioned causality (with respect to nature)—that is, freedom—inasmuch as it is conscious of its moral command. But since here the objective necessity of the action, as a duty, is set in opposition to that which it would have as an occurrence if its ground were located in nature rather than in freedom (that is, in reason's own causality), and the morally absolutely necessary action is regarded physically as entirely contingent (i.e., that what necessarily ***ought*** to happen nevertheless often does not happen): it is clear that it derives solely from the subjective character of our practical faculty that the moral laws have to be represented as commands (and the actions that accord with them {{ 343 }} as duties), and that reason gives voice to this necessity not by means of a ***being*** (an occurring) but by means of an ought-to-be: which would not take place if reason were considered, as regards its causality, without sensibility (as the subjective condition under which it is applied to objects of nature), and hence as a cause in an intelligible world thoroughly agreeing with the moral law, {{{ 404 }}} where between ought and doing, between a practical law of what is possible through us and a theoretical law of what is actual through us, there would be no distinction. Now even granting that an intelligible world—one where each thing would be actual for the sole reason that it (as something good) is possible, freedom itself included as that world's formal condition—remains for us an extravagant concept, one incapable of furnishing a constitutive principle by which to determine an object together with its objective reality: this freedom does nevertheless, given the makeup of our (partly sensible) nature and faculty, serve us and every rational being bound up with the world of sense—so far as we can represent such beings to ourselves under the makeup of our reason—as a universal ***regulative*** principle, which, rather than determining objectively the constitution of freedom as a form of causality, instead, with validity no less than if it did so determine it, turns the rule of actions conforming to that idea into commands binding on everyone.

In the same way, with regard to the case we have in hand, this too may be conceded: no distinction between the mechanism of nature and its technic—that is, the linking of ends within it—would present itself to us if our understanding were not so constituted that it has to move from the general to the particular, whereby the power of judgment, faced with the particular, can discern no {{ 344 }} purposiveness in it and can therefore reach no determining judgments unless it is furnished with a general law beneath which that particular may be subsumed. But since the particular, taken as such, harbors something contingent in relation to the universal, while reason all the same requires unity, and thus lawfulness, in how nature's particular laws hang together (a lawfulness of the contingent that goes by the name of purposiveness), and since deriving those particular laws from the universal ones—as regards the contingency they carry within them—is _a priori_ impossible by way of determining the concept of the object: the concept of nature's purposiveness in its products will accordingly be one that is necessary for the human power of judgment in relation to nature, yet does not touch the determination of the objects themselves—in short, a principle of reason that is subjective and meant for the power of judgment, a principle which, being regulative and not constitutive, holds for our ***human power of judgment*** with the very same necessity as though it were an objective principle.
