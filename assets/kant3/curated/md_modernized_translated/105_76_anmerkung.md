## § 76. Remark

This consideration, which very much deserves to be carried out at length within transcendental philosophy, may here be introduced only in passing, by way of clarification (not as a proof of what has been set forth here).

Reason is a faculty of principles and, in its most extreme demand, aims at the unconditioned; whereas the understanding always stands at its service only under a certain condition that must be given. Without concepts of the understanding, however, to which objective reality must be given, reason cannot judge objectively (synthetically) at all, and as theoretical reason it contains, taken by itself, absolutely no constitutive but merely regulative principles. One soon becomes aware: that, where the understanding cannot follow, reason becomes extravagant and comes forward with ideas that are indeed well-founded (as regulative principles) but not with objectively valid concepts; the understanding, however, which cannot keep step with it, yet which would be needed for validity with respect to objects, restricts the validity of those ideas of reason to the subject alone, though universally for all beings of this kind, i.e., to the condition that, in accordance with the nature of our (human) faculty of cognition, or indeed generally in accordance with the concept ***that we*** can ***form for ourselves*** of the faculty of a finite rational being as such, it can and must be thought in no way other than this: yet without thereby maintaining that the ground of such a judgment {{ 340 }} lies in the object. We shall adduce examples that admittedly carry too much importance and also too much difficulty to be forced upon the reader here straightaway as proven propositions, but which can give him material for reflection and can serve to clarify what is here our peculiar business.

For the human understanding it is inescapably necessary to draw a distinction between what is possible and what is actual in things. The ground for this lies in the subject and in the nature of its faculties of cognition. For if two entirely heterogeneous components were not required for the exercise of these faculties—understanding for concepts and sensible intuition for the objects corresponding to them—then there would be no such distinction {{{ 402 }}} (between the possible and the actual). Were our understanding an intuitive one, it would possess no objects save the actual. Concepts (bearing merely on an object's possibility) and sensible intuitions (which afford us something without on that account letting it be cognized as an object) would both drop away. Now, however, our entire distinction of the merely possible from the actual rests on this: that the former designates only the positing of a thing's representation relative to our concept, and to the capacity for thinking in general, whereas the latter designates the thing's being posited in itself (outside this concept). The distinction of possible things from actual ones is therefore one that holds merely subjectively for the human understanding, since we can, namely, always still have something in thought even though it is not, or represent something to ourselves as given even though we as yet have no concept of it. The propositions, then—that things may be possible without being actual, and hence that mere possibility licenses no inference whatever to actuality—hold quite correctly for human {{ 341 }} reason, without thereby proving that this difference lies in the things themselves. For that this cannot be inferred from them, and hence that those propositions do indeed also hold of objects insofar as our faculty of cognition, as sensibly conditioned, likewise occupies itself with objects of the senses, but not of things in general, is evident from the unremitting demand of reason to assume some something or other (the primordial ground) as existing unconditionally necessarily, in which possibility and actuality are no longer to be distinguished at all, and for which idea our understanding possesses no concept whatsoever, that is, can discover no manner in which it is to represent to itself such a thing and its mode of existing. For if it ***thinks*** it (however it may choose to think it), then it is represented merely as possible. Should it be conscious of the thing as given in intuition, it is then actual, without thinking anything of possibility in the process. Hence the concept of an absolutely necessary being is indeed an indispensable idea of reason, but a problematic concept unattainable for the human understanding. But it does hold for the use of our faculties of cognition according to their peculiar constitution, and hence not of the object, and thereby not for every cognizing being: because thinking and intuition, as two different conditions for exercising its faculties of cognition, and hence for the possibility and actuality of things, {{{ 403 }}} I cannot presuppose in every such being. For an understanding in which this distinction did not occur, it would mean: all objects that I cognize ***are*** (exist); and the possibility of some that nevertheless did not exist, i.e., their contingency if they do exist, and hence also the necessity to be distinguished from it, could not at all {{ 342 }} come into the representation of such a being. But what our understanding here finds so burdensome—matching reason with its concepts—is merely this: that for it, as human understanding, that is extravagant (i.e., impossible under the subjective conditions of its cognition) which reason nevertheless erects into a principle as pertaining to the object. — Here, then, the maxim always holds that we think all objects, where their cognition exceeds the capacity of the understanding, according to the subjective conditions of the exercise of our faculties that necessarily attach to our (i.e., human) nature; and if the judgments passed in this way (as also cannot be otherwise with respect to the extravagant concepts) cannot be constitutive principles that determine the object as it is constituted, they will nonetheless remain regulative principles, immanent and secure in application, suited to the human aim.

Just as reason, in the theoretical consideration of nature, finds itself compelled to posit the idea of an unconditioned necessity belonging to its primordial ground: so too, in the practical sphere, it presupposes its own unconditioned causality (with respect to nature)—that is, freedom—inasmuch as it is conscious of its moral command. But since here the objective necessity of the action, as a duty, is set in opposition to that which it would have as an occurrence if its ground were located in nature rather than in freedom (that is, in reason's own causality), and the morally absolutely necessary action is regarded physically as entirely contingent (i.e., that what necessarily ***ought*** to happen nevertheless often does not happen): it is clear that it derives solely from the subjective character of our practical faculty that the moral laws have to be represented as commands (and the actions that accord with them {{ 343 }} as duties), and that reason gives voice to this necessity not by means of a ***being*** (an occurring) but by means of an ought-to-be: which would not take place if reason were considered, as regards its causality, without sensibility (as the subjective condition under which it is applied to objects of nature), and hence as a cause in an intelligible world thoroughly agreeing with the moral law, {{{ 404 }}} where between ought and doing, between a practical law of what is possible through us and a theoretical law of what is actual through us, there would be no distinction. But now although an intelligible world, in which everything would be actual simply because it (as something good) is possible, and even freedom as its formal condition, is for us an extravagant concept, unfit to serve as any constitutive principle for determining an object and its objective reality: the latter nevertheless serves, according to the constitution of our (in part sensible) nature and faculty, for us and for all rational beings standing in connection with the world of sense, so far as we can represent them to ourselves according to the constitution of our reason, as a universal ***regulative*** principle, which does not determine objectively the character of freedom qua form of causality, but rather—and indeed with no less validity than if this did occur—makes the rule of actions according to that idea into commands for everyone.

Likewise, as regards the case before us, one may also grant: we would find no distinction between the mechanism of nature and the technic of nature, i.e., its connection in terms of ends, were our understanding not of the kind that it must proceed from the universal to the particular, so that the power of judgment, with respect to the particular, can cognize no {{ 344 }} purposiveness, and hence can pass no determining judgments, without having a universal law under which it could subsume the particular. But since now the particular, as such, contains, with respect to the universal, something contingent, and yet reason nonetheless also demands unity, and hence lawfulness, in the combination of particular laws of nature (which lawfulness of the contingent is called purposiveness), and since the derivation of the particular laws from the universal ones, with respect to what the former contain in themselves as contingent, is impossible _a priori_ through determination of the concept of the object: the concept of nature's purposiveness in the products it yields will be a concept necessary for the human power of judgment with respect to nature, but not one bearing on the determination of the objects themselves—hence a subjective principle of reason for the power of judgment, which, as regulative (not constitutive), holds just as necessarily for our ***human power of judgment*** as if it were an objective principle.
