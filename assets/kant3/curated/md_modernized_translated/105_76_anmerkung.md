## § 76. Remark

This consideration, which well deserves to be elaborately developed in transcendental philosophy, may enter here only episodically for illustration (not for proof of what is here put forward).

Reason is a faculty of principles and in its extreme demand goes to the unconditioned; whereas the understanding is always at its service only under a certain condition that must be given. Without concepts of the understanding, however, to which objective reality must be given, reason cannot judge objectively (synthetically) at all, and as theoretical reason contains in itself absolutely no constitutive, but merely regulative principles. One soon becomes aware: that, where the understanding cannot follow, reason becomes transcendent and manifests itself in indeed well-founded ideas (as regulative principles), but not in objectively valid concepts; while the understanding, which cannot keep pace with it, yet would still be necessary for validity in regard to objects, restricts the validity of those ideas of reason merely to the subject, yet generally for all of this species, i.e., to the condition that according to the nature of our (human) cognitive faculty, or even in general according to the concept ***which we can make for ourselves*** of the faculty of a finite rational being in general, it cannot and must not be thought otherwise than thus: without, however, asserting that the ground of such a judgment {{ 340 }} lies in the object. We wish to adduce examples which indeed possess too much importance and also difficulty to be forced upon the reader here immediately as proved propositions, but which may give him material for reflection and serve to illustrate what is here our peculiar business.

It is indispensably necessary for the human understanding to distinguish between the possibility and the actuality of things. The ground of this lies in the subject and in the nature of its cognitive faculties. For if two entirely heterogeneous parts were not required for this exercise of theirs, namely understanding for concepts and sensible intuition for objects corresponding to them, then there would be no such distinction {{{ 402 }}} (between the possible and the actual). Indeed, if our understanding were intuitive, it would have no objects but the actual. Concepts (which concern merely the possibility of an object) and sensible intuitions (which give us something without thereby allowing us to cognize it as an object) would both fall away. But now all our distinction of the merely possible from the actual rests on this: that the former signifies only the position of the representation of a thing with respect to our concept and, in general, the faculty of thinking, while the latter signifies the positing of the thing in itself (apart from this concept). Thus the distinction between possible and actual things is one that holds merely subjectively for the human understanding, since we can indeed still have something in thoughts although it is not actual, or represent something to ourselves as given although we do not yet have a concept of it. The propositions, therefore: that things can be possible without being actual, that consequently from mere possibility absolutely no conclusion can be drawn as to actuality, hold quite correctly for human {{ 341 }} reason, without thereby proving that this difference lies in the things themselves. For that this cannot be inferred from it, and consequently that those propositions indeed hold of objects insofar as our cognitive faculty as sensibly conditioned also occupies itself with objects of the senses, but not of things in general, is evident from the incessant demand of reason to assume some something (the original ground) as existing with unconditional necessity, in which possibility and actuality are no longer to be distinguished at all, and for which idea our understanding has absolutely no concept, i.e., can find no way to represent such a thing and its mode of existence to itself. For if it ***thinks*** it (let it think it as it will), it is represented merely as possible. If it is conscious of it as given in intuition, then it is actual, without thinking anything at all of possibility in this connection. Therefore, the concept of an absolutely necessary being is indeed an indispensable idea of reason, but a problematic concept unattainable for the human understanding. It holds, however, for the use of our cognitive faculties according to their peculiar constitution, consequently not of the object, and therewith for every cognizing being: since I cannot in the case of every being {{{ 403 }}} presuppose thinking and intuition as two different conditions of the exercise of its cognitive faculties, and consequently of the possibility and actuality of things. For an understanding in which this distinction did not occur, it would be said: all objects that I cognize ***are*** (exist); and the possibility of some that did not exist, i.e., their contingency if they do exist, and hence also the necessity to be distinguished from it, would not enter {{ 342 }} at all into the representation of such a being. But what makes it so difficult for our understanding to equal reason here with its concepts is merely this: that for it as human understanding, that is transcendent (i.e., impossible under the subjective conditions of its cognition) which reason nevertheless makes into a principle as belonging to the object. — In this connection, the maxim always holds that, where their cognition exceeds the faculty of the understanding, we think all objects according to the subjective conditions of the exercise of its faculties that are necessarily attached to our (i.e., human) nature; and if the judgments made in this way (as cannot be otherwise in regard to transcendent concepts) cannot be constitutive principles that determine the object as it is constituted, they will nevertheless remain regulative principles, immanent and secure in their exercise, and appropriate to the human purpose.

Just as reason, in the theoretical consideration of nature, must assume the idea of an unconditional necessity of its original ground, so in the practical consideration it presupposes its own unconditional causality (with regard to nature), i.e., freedom, in being conscious of its moral command. But because here the objective necessity of the action as duty is opposed to that which it would have as an occurrence if its ground lay in nature and not in freedom (i.e., in the causality of reason), and the morally absolutely necessary action is regarded physically as entirely contingent (i.e., that what ***ought*** necessarily to happen nevertheless often does not happen), so it is clear that it arises only from the subjective constitution of our practical faculty that the moral laws must be represented as commands (and the actions {{ 343 }} in conformity with them as duties), and that reason expresses this necessity not through a ***being*** (happening), but through an ought-to-be: which would not take place if reason were considered without sensibility (as the subjective condition of its application to objects of nature) according to its causality, and thus as a cause in an intelligible world thoroughly {{{ 404 }}} in agreement with the moral law, where no difference would exist between ought and deed, between a practical law of what is possible through us and the theoretical law of what is actual through us. Yet although an intelligible world, in which everything would be actual merely because it is possible (as something good), and even freedom itself as its formal condition, is for us a transcendent concept that is not fit for any constitutive principle to determine an object and its objective reality, yet the latter nevertheless serves, according to the constitution of our (in part sensible) nature and faculty, for us and all rational beings connected with the world of sense, so far as we can represent them to ourselves according to the constitution of our reason, as a universal ***regulative*** principle, which does not objectively determine the constitution of freedom as the form of causality, but rather — and indeed with no less validity than if this were done — makes the rule of actions according to that idea into commands for everyone.

In the same way, as concerns our present case, one can also admit: we would find no distinction between the mechanism of nature and the technique of nature, i.e., the connection of ends in it, if our understanding were not of the kind that must go from the universal to the particular, so that the power of judgment can recognize no purposiveness {{ 344 }} with regard to the particular, and consequently can make no determining judgments, without having a universal law under which it can subsume it. But since the particular as such contains something contingent with respect to the universal, while reason nevertheless also requires unity, and thus lawfulness, in the combination of particular laws of nature (which lawfulness of the contingent is called purposiveness), and since the derivation of particular laws from the universal ones is, with respect to what they contain of the contingent, impossible _a priori_ through determination of the concept of the object: so the concept of the purposiveness of nature in its products will be a concept necessary for the human power of judgment with regard to nature, but not one that concerns the determination of the objects themselves, hence a subjective principle of reason for the power of judgment, which as regulative (not constitutive) holds just as necessarily for our ***human power of judgment*** as if it were an objective principle.
