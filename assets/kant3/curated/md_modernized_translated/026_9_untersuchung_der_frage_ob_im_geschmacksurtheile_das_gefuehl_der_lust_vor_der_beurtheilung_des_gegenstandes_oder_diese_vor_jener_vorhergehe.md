## {{ 27 }} § 9. Inquiry into the question: whether in the judgment of taste the feeling of pleasure comes before the judging of the object, or the judging comes before the pleasure

Resolving this task furnishes the key to the critique of taste, and it therefore warrants our fullest attention.

Were the pleasure in the given object to precede everything else, so that in the judgment of taste nothing but its {{{ 217 }}} universal communicability came to be ascribed to the representation of the object, then a procedure of this kind would stand in conflict with itself. For a pleasure of that sort could be nothing but the mere agreeableness in sensory sensation, and hence by its very nature could possess only private validity, since it would depend without mediation upon that representation whereby the object ***is given***.

Accordingly, what must underlie the judgment of taste, as its subjective condition, and must give rise to the pleasure in the object, is the universal communicability of the mental state present in the given representation. But nothing admits of universal communication except cognition, and representation so far as it pertains to cognition. For it is only in that respect that the latter is purely objective, and it is thereby alone that it gains a universal point of reference, one to which the representational power of all is compelled {{ 28 }} to conform. Now, should the determining ground of the judgment regarding this universal communicability of the representation have to be conceived as merely subjective—that is, without any concept of the object—then it can be none other than the state of mind encountered in the mutual relation of the representational powers to one another, insofar as these set a given representation in relation to ***cognition in general***.

The cognitive powers, set into play by this representation, are thereby caught up in a free play, for no determinate concept restricts them to any particular rule of cognition. The mental state in this representation must accordingly be that of a feeling of the free play of the representational powers, engaged with a given representation, toward a cognition in general. Now, for a representation through which an object is given to yield cognition at all, there are needed ***imagination*** to combine the manifold of the intuition, and ***understanding*** to supply the unity of the concept uniting these representations. This condition of a ***free play*** of the cognitive faculties, present where a representation gives us an object, must be capable of universal communication—since cognition, taken as the determining of the object to which given representations (in whatever subject they may be) are supposed to conform {{ 29 }}, is the one mode of representing that holds for everyone.

Since it is supposed to occur without a determinate concept being presupposed, the subjective universal communicability of the manner of representing in a judgment of taste can consist in nothing except that mental state which belongs to the {{{ 218 }}} free play of imagination and understanding (so far as these two harmonize with each other, as is needed for a ***cognition in general***), our awareness being that this subjective relation, so fitted for cognition in general, must hold good for everyone, and hence be universally communicable, no less than every determinate cognition is—cognition which itself invariably rests, as upon its subjective condition, on that very relation.

This merely subjective (aesthetic) judging of the object—or of the representation whereby it is given—comes before any pleasure taken in it and is the ground of that pleasure in the harmony of the cognitive faculties; and it is solely on that generality of the subjective conditions of the judging of the objects that the universal subjective validity of the satisfaction rests—the satisfaction we associate with the representation of the object we pronounce beautiful.

That being able to communicate one's mental state, even if only with respect to the cognitive faculties, carries a pleasure with it could easily be shown from the natural human propensity to sociability (empirically {{ 30 }} and psychologically). But that is not enough for our purpose. The pleasure we feel we demand of every other person, in a judgment of taste, as something necessary, as though, whenever we pronounce a thing beautiful, this pleasure were to count as a quality belonging to the object and determined in it by concepts; yet beauty, taken apart from any bearing on the subject's feeling, is, of itself, nothing at all. The discussion of this question, however, we must reserve until the answering of that one: whether and how aesthetic judgments are possible _a priori_.

For the present we turn to the slighter question of how, within the judgment of taste, we come to be conscious of a mutual subjective concord of the cognitive powers among themselves—whether by aesthetic means, through inner sense and sensation alone, or intellectually, through the awareness of the deliberate activity by which we bring those powers into play.

Suppose the given representation that prompts the judgment of taste were a concept—one that, in the judging of the object, brought understanding and imagination together so as to cognize the object; then our awareness of this relation would be an intellectual one (just as it is in that objective schematism of the power of judgment which the Critique treats). But then the judgment would also not have been made in relation to pleasure and displeasure, {{{ 219 }}} and hence would be no judgment of taste. Now, however, the judgment of taste {{ 31 }} determines the object, independently of concepts, with respect to satisfaction and the predicate of beauty. That subjective unity of the relation, therefore, can become recognizable only by way of sensation. The animation of the two faculties (imagination and understanding) toward an activity indeterminate and yet, by occasion of the given representation, unanimous—namely the activity that belongs to a cognition in general—is that sensation of which the judgment of taste postulates the universal communicability. An objective relation, to be sure, admits only of being thought; yet, so far as it is subjective in respect of its conditions, it can all the same be felt in the effect it has upon the mind; and where a relation places no concept at its foundation (as with that of the representational powers to a cognitive faculty in general), there is likewise no consciousness of it available except by the sensing of the effect—the effect residing in the eased play of the two powers of the mind (imagination and understanding), quickened by their mutual attunement. A representation that, though singular and without comparison to others, nevertheless has an accord with the conditions of universality that make up the general business of the understanding, puts the cognitive faculties into the proportioned attunement that we demand for all cognition and therefore hold to be valid for everyone who is determined to judge through understanding and {{ 32 }} senses in combination (for every human being).

Explanation of the beautiful inferred from the second moment.

***Beautiful*** is what, without a concept, pleases universally.
