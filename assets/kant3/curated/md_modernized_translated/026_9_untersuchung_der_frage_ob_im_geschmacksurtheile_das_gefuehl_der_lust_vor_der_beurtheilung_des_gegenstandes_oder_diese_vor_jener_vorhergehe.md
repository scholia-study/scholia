## {{ 27 }} § 9. Examination of the question: whether in the judgment of taste the feeling of pleasure precedes the judging of the object, or the latter precedes the former

The solution to this problem is the key to the critique of taste and therefore deserves every attention.

If the pleasure in the given object came first, and only the {{{ 217 }}} universal communicability of it were to be attributed, in the judgment of taste, to the representation of the object, then such a procedure would be self-contradictory. For a pleasure of that sort could be nothing but the mere agreeableness in sensory sensation, and hence by its very nature could possess only private validity, since it would depend immediately on the representation through which the object ***is given***.

It is therefore the universally communicable quality of the mental state within the given representation which, serving as the subjective condition of the judgment of taste, must lie at its basis and must issue in the pleasure taken in the object. But nothing can be communicated universally except cognition and representation, insofar as it belongs to cognition. For only to that extent is the latter solely objective, and only thereby does it have a universal point of reference with which the representational power of everyone is brought into agreement {{ 28 }} by necessity. Now if the determining ground of the judgment concerning this universal communicability of the representation is to be conceived as merely subjective, namely without a concept of the object, then it can be nothing other than the mental state that is met with in the relation of the representational powers to one another, so far as they relate a given representation to ***cognition in general***.

The powers of cognition that this representation sets into play are here engaged in a free play, since no determinate concept confines them to any particular rule of cognition. The mental state accompanying this representation must accordingly be the feeling of a free play of the representational powers upon a given representation for a cognition in general. Now, for a representation through which an object is given to yield cognition at all, there are needed ***imagination*** to combine the manifold of the intuition, and ***understanding*** to supply the unity of the concept uniting these representations. This state of a ***free play*** of the cognitive faculties in the case of a representation through which an object is given must admit of being communicated universally: because cognition—understood as that determination of the object with which given representations (in whatever subject they occur) are to agree {{ 29 }}—is the sole manner of representing that is valid for everyone.

The subjective universal communicability of this kind of representation in a judgment of taste—since it is meant to obtain without presupposing any determinate concept—can amount to nothing but the mental state present in the {{{ 218 }}} free play of imagination and understanding (so far as these mutually agree, as a ***cognition in general*** requires), for we are conscious that this subjective relation, apt for cognition in general, must be valid for everyone alike, and hence be universally communicable, exactly as any determinate cognition is, which itself always rests on that relation as its subjective condition.

This merely subjective (aesthetic) judging of the object—or of the representation whereby it is given—comes before any pleasure taken in it and is the ground of that pleasure in the harmony of the cognitive faculties; and it is solely on that generality of the subjective conditions of the judging of the objects that the universal subjective validity of the satisfaction rests—the satisfaction we associate with the representation of the object we pronounce beautiful.

That being able to communicate one's mental state, even if only with respect to the cognitive faculties, carries a pleasure with it could easily be shown from the natural human propensity to sociability (empirically {{ 30 }} and psychologically). But that is not enough for our purpose. The pleasure that we feel, we require of everyone else in the judgment of taste as necessary, just as if, when we call something beautiful, it were to be regarded as a property of the object determined in it according to concepts; although beauty, apart from any relation to the feeling of the subject, is in itself nothing. The discussion of this question, however, we must reserve until the answering of that one: whether and how aesthetic judgments are possible _a priori_.

For now we still occupy ourselves with the lesser question: in what manner we become conscious, in the judgment of taste, of a reciprocal subjective agreement of the cognitive powers with one another, whether aesthetically through mere inner sense and sensation, or intellectually through the consciousness of our intentional activity by which we set them into play.

Suppose the given representation that prompts the judgment of taste were a concept—one that, in the judging of the object, brought understanding and imagination together so as to cognize the object; then our awareness of this relation would be an intellectual one (just as it is in that objective schematism of the power of judgment which the Critique treats). But then the judgment would also not have been made in relation to pleasure and displeasure, {{{ 219 }}} and hence would be no judgment of taste. Now, however, the judgment of taste {{ 31 }} determines the object, independently of concepts, with respect to satisfaction and the predicate of beauty. Hence that subjective unity of the relation can make itself known only through sensation. The enlivening of both faculties (the imagination and the understanding) to an activity that is indeterminate but yet, by means of the prompting of the given representation, unanimous—namely the activity that belongs to a cognition in general—is the sensation whose universal communicability the judgment of taste postulates. An objective relation can, to be sure, only be thought, but, insofar as it is subjective as regards its conditions, it can nonetheless be felt in its effect upon the mind; and in the case of a relation that lays no concept at its basis (such as that of the representational powers to a faculty of cognition in general), no other consciousness of it is possible than through sensation of the effect that consists in the facilitated play of the two powers of the mind (the imagination and the understanding) enlivened by their reciprocal accord. A representation that, though singular and without comparison to others, nevertheless has an accord with the conditions of universality which constitute the business of the understanding in general, brings the cognitive faculties into the proportioned attunement that we demand for all cognition and therefore hold to be valid for everyone who is determined to judge through understanding and {{ 32 }} senses in combination (for every human being).

Explanation of the beautiful inferred from the second moment.

***Beautiful*** is that which pleases universally without a concept.
