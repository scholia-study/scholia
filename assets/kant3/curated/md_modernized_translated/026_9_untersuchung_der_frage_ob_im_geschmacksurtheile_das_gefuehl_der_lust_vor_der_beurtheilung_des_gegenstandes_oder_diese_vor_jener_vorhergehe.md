## {{ 27 }} § 9. Investigation of the question: whether in the judgment of taste the feeling of pleasure precedes the judging of the object or the latter precedes the former

The solution of this problem is the key to the critique of taste and hence worthy of all attention.

If the pleasure in the given object went first, and only the {{{ 217 }}} universal communicability of it were to be ascribed to the representation of the object in the judgment of taste, then such a procedure would stand in contradiction with itself. For such a pleasure would be no other than the mere agreeableness in sensation and therefore by its nature could have only private validity, because it would depend immediately on the representation through which the object is ***given***.

Thus it is the universal capacity for communication of the state of mind in the given representation which, as the subjective condition of the judgment of taste, must lie at its ground and must have the pleasure in the object as its consequence. But nothing can be universally communicated except cognition and representation, insofar as it belongs to cognition. For only insofar is the latter objective and only thereby has a universal point of reference with which the power of representation of everyone is constrained to agree {{ 28 }}. If now the determining ground of the judgment concerning this universal communicability of the representation is to be thought of as merely subjective, namely without a concept of the object, then it can be no other than the state of mind that is encountered in the relation of the powers of representation to one another, insofar as they relate a given representation to ***cognition in general***.

The powers of cognition that are set into play by this representation are hereby in a free play, because no determinate concept restricts them to a particular rule of cognition. Thus the state of mind in this representation must be that of a feeling of the free play of the powers of representation upon a given representation for a cognition in general. Now for a representation through which an object is given, in order that cognition in general arise from it at all, there belong ***imagination*** for the composition of the manifold of intuition and ***understanding*** for the unity of the concept that unites the representations. This state of a ***free play*** of the powers of cognition upon a representation through which an object is given must be capable of being universally communicated: because cognition, as a determination of the object with which given representations (in whatever subject it may be) are to agree {{ 29 }}, is the only kind of representation that is valid for everyone.

The subjective universal communicability of the kind of representation in a judgment of taste, since it is to take place without presupposing a determinate concept, can be nothing other than the state of mind in the {{{ 218 }}} free play of the imagination and the understanding (insofar as they agree with one another, as is requisite for a ***cognition in general***), in that we are conscious that this subjective relation suited to cognition in general must hold for everyone just as well, and consequently must be universally communicable, as is any determinate cognition, which after all always rests on that relation as its subjective condition.

This merely subjective (aesthetic) judging of the object, or of the representation through which it is given, now precedes the pleasure in it and is the ground of this pleasure in the harmony of the faculties of cognition; but on that universality of the subjective conditions of the judging of objects alone is grounded this universal subjective validity of the satisfaction that we connect with the representation of the object that we call beautiful.

That being able to communicate one's state of mind, even if only with respect to the faculties of cognition, carries a pleasure with it, this one could easily demonstrate from the natural propensity of the human being to sociability (empirically {{ 30 }} and psychologically). But that is not enough for our aim. The pleasure that we feel we expect of everyone else in the judgment of taste as necessary, just as if it were to be regarded as a property of the object that is determined in it according to concepts when we call something beautiful; although beauty, without relation to the feeling of the subject, is nothing in itself. The discussion of this question, however, we must reserve until the answering of the following one: whether and how aesthetic judgments are possible _a priori_.

For now we still occupy ourselves with the lesser question: in what manner we become conscious of a reciprocal subjective agreement of the powers of cognition with one another in the judgment of taste, whether aesthetically through mere inner sense and sensation, or intellectually through the consciousness of our intentional activity by which we set them into play.

If the given representation that occasions the judgment of taste were a concept that united understanding and imagination in the judging of the object into a cognition of the object, then the consciousness of this relation would be intellectual (as in the objective schematism of the power of judgment, which the Critique treats). But the judgment would then also not be made in relation to pleasure and displeasure, {{{ 219 }}} hence it would not be a judgment of taste. Now, however, the judgment of taste determines {{ 31 }} the object independently of concepts with regard to satisfaction and to the predicate of beauty. Thus that subjective unity of the relation can make itself known only through sensation. The animation of both faculties (of the imagination and the understanding) to an indeterminate but yet, by means of the occasion of the given representation, unanimous activity, namely that which belongs to a cognition in general, is the sensation whose universal communicability the judgment of taste postulates. An objective relation can indeed only be thought, but, insofar as it is subjective as to its conditions, can yet be felt in its effect on the mind; and in the case of a relation that lays no concept at its ground (such as that of the powers of representation to a faculty of cognition in general), there is also no other consciousness of it possible than through the sensation of the effect that consists in the facilitated play of both powers of mind (the imagination and the understanding) animated through reciprocal accordance. A representation that, as singular and without comparison with others, nevertheless has an accordance with the conditions of universality that constitutes the business of the understanding in general, brings the faculties of cognition into the proportioned disposition that we demand for all cognition and therefore also hold to be valid for everyone (for every human being) who is determined to judge through the combination of understanding and {{ 32 }} sense.

Explanation of the beautiful inferred from the second moment.

***Beautiful*** is that which pleases universally without a concept.
