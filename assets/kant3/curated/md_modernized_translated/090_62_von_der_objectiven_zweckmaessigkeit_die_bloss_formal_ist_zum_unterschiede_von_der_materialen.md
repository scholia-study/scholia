## § 62. On objective purposiveness that is merely formal, as distinguished from the material kind

Every geometrical figure drawn according to a principle exhibits in itself a manifold and often admired objective purposiveness, namely a fitness for the solution of many problems by a single principle, and indeed of each individual problem in infinitely varied ways. The purposiveness here is manifestly objective and intellectual, not merely subjective and aesthetic. For it expresses the adequacy of the figure for producing many intended shapes, and is cognized by reason. Yet this purposiveness does not make possible the concept of the object itself, that is, the object is not regarded as possible merely with a view to this use.

In a figure as simple as the circle there lies the ground for {{ 272 }} the solution of a host of problems, each of which taken by itself would demand various apparatus, and which follows as if of its own accord as one among the infinitely many excellent properties of this figure. If the task is, for example, to construct a triangle from a given base line and the angle opposite to it, the problem is indeterminate, that is, it can be solved in infinitely manifold ways. Yet the circle comprises them all together, as the geometrical locus of all triangles that conform to this condition. Or let two lines cross one another so that the rectangle contained by the two segments of the first equals that contained by the two segments of the second: to all appearances, resolving such a task carries much difficulty. But all lines that intersect within the circle whose {{{ 363 }}} circumference bounds each of them divide themselves of their own accord in this proportion. The other curved lines in turn furnish still further purposive solutions that were not at all thought of in the rule constituting their construction. Every conic section, whether considered on its own or set beside the others, teems with principles by which a multitude of possible problems may be resolved, no matter how plain the definition that fixes its concept. — It is a genuine joy to watch the ardor with which the ancient geometers pursued these properties belonging to lines of this {{ 273 }} kind, refusing to be unsettled by the question, raised by narrow intellects, of what good such knowledge could possibly do; the parabola, for instance, they studied without knowing the law of gravity here on earth — the law that would have furnished them the means of applying that curve to the flight path of projected heavy bodies (whose gravitational direction throughout their motion may be taken as parallel); or the ellipse, never guessing that gravity resides in the celestial bodies as well, nor acquainted with its law at the various distances from the center of attraction, the very thing that makes those bodies trace this line in unconstrained motion. Even as they toiled here for later generations without themselves being aware of it, they took pleasure in a purposiveness residing within the essence of things, one they were nevertheless able to exhibit wholly _a priori_ in its necessity. Plato, himself a master of this science, fell — on account of such an original constitution of things, whose discovery lets us do without all experience, and on account of the mind's power to draw the harmony of beings out of their supersensible principle (to which one may add those numerical properties that the mind toys with in music) — into an enthusiasm that lifted him past the concepts of experience up to ideas which, to his mind, seemed explicable only through an intellectual fellowship with the origin of all beings. It is no wonder that he turned away from his school anyone unversed in geometry, in that {{ 274 }} he supposed himself able to derive, from the pure intuition dwelling inwardly in the human spirit, what Anaxagoras had inferred from objects of experience and their purposive connection. For the very ground of that great admiration of nature — a ground residing not so much beyond us as in our own reason — is to be found in the necessity attaching to whatever is purposive and framed as though it had been set up on purpose for our use, and yet appears to pertain from the outset to the essence of things, taking no account whatever of that use; and here it is quite {{{ 364 }}} pardonable that such admiration, by way of misunderstanding, should little by little have mounted to the pitch of fanaticism.

This intellectual purposiveness, however, although it is objective (and not subjective like the aesthetic), can nonetheless, with respect to its possibility, be quite well comprehended, though only in general, as merely formal (not real), that is, as a purposiveness without there being any need to lay an end at its foundation, and hence without any need of teleology for it. The circle's figure is an intuition that the understanding has settled upon in conformity with a principle: this principle, whose unity I posit arbitrarily and lay down as a concept, once brought to bear on a form of intuition — namely space — which likewise is encountered in me solely as a representation, and that _a priori_, renders intelligible the unity of the many rules that flow from constructing the said concept, rules purposive in a variety of possible {{ 275 }} aims, and it does so without allowing us to place beneath this purposiveness any ***end*** or any other ground for it. Matters stand otherwise here than they would if, within some collection of ***things*** outside me shut in by certain limits — a garden, say — I were to encounter order and regularity among the trees, flower beds, walkways, and the like, which I could not hope to derive _a priori_ from my bounding of a space carried out by some arbitrary rule of my choosing: for these are things that exist, things that have to be given empirically if they are to be cognized, rather than a bare representation within me fixed _a priori_ in accordance with a principle. The latter (empirical) purposiveness therefore depends, as something ***real***, upon the concept of an end.

But the ground of the admiration of a purposiveness, even though one perceived in the essence of things (insofar as their concepts can be constructed), can also be very well grasped, and grasped indeed as legitimate. These diverse rules, whose unity (issuing from a single principle) is what stirs up such admiration, are without exception synthetic, and rather than following from any ***concept*** of the object — of the circle, say — they demand that this object be presented in intuition. Through this, however, this unity takes on the appearance of having empirically an external ground of the rules distinct from our power of representation, and {{ 276 }} thus that the agreement of the object with the need for rules, a need proper to the understanding, is contingent in itself, and hence possible only through an end expressly directed to that agreement. Now, to be sure, {{{ 365 }}} this very harmony, because, notwithstanding all this purposiveness, it is nonetheless cognized not empirically but _a priori_, ought of itself to bring us to acknowledge that space—by whose determination (through the imagination conformably to a concept) the object was alone possible—is no property of the things outside me but only a mode of representing in me, and thus that, into the figure I trace ***suited to a concept***, that is, into my own way of representing whatever is externally given to me, be it in itself what it may, it is I who ***introduce the purposiveness***, and that I receive no empirical instruction concerning it from this object, and consequently that I require for that purposiveness no particular end in the object outside me. Yet since such a consideration already calls for a critical employment of reason, and therefore cannot straightaway be included in the appraisal of the object according to its properties: what the latter appraisal hands me directly is nothing but a uniting of heterogeneous rules (even as regards whatever is unlike in them) under one principle — a principle that, without demanding for itself any special ground lying beyond my concept, and beyond my representation _a priori_ in general, {{ 277 }} is all the same cognized by me _a priori_ as genuinely valid. Now ***astonishment*** is a shock the mind receives at the incompatibility of a representation, and of the rule given through it, with the principles already lying at its foundation, a shock that therefore produces a doubt as to whether one has really seen or judged correctly; ***admiration***, however, is an ever-recurring astonishment despite the vanishing of this doubt. The latter, accordingly, is an entirely natural upshot of that observed purposiveness within the essence of things (taken as appearances) — an upshot that cannot on this account be faulted, given that the agreement between that form of sensible intuition (the one we name space) and the faculty of concepts (namely the understanding) is not merely inexplicable to us because it happens to be just this form and no other, but is over and above that enlarging for the mind, inviting it as it were to sense something reaching yet further beyond those sensible representations, wherein — unknown to us though it be — the final ground of that concord might be met with. Knowing this ground is admittedly not something we require so long as nothing more than the formal purposiveness of our representations _a priori_ is at issue; yet the mere obligation to gaze out that far already inspires, at once, admiration for the object that drives us to do so.

It is customary to call the properties just noted — those of geometrical {{{ 366 }}} shapes, and indeed those of numbers as well — ***beauty***, owing to a certain purposiveness they possess for all manner of _a priori_ cognitive use, a purposiveness one would not have looked for from the {{ 278 }} simplicity of their construction; and one will speak, for instance, of this or that ***beautiful*** property of the circle, uncovered in one way or another. It is, however, by no aesthetic judging that we find them purposive; not a conceptless judging, one that would bring to notice a merely ***subjective*** purposiveness within the unconstrained interplay of our cognitive powers: but an intellectual judging that proceeds by concepts, one giving us to cognize distinctly an objective purposiveness, i.e., a fitness for ends of every kind (manifold to infinity). It would be more fitting to name it a ***relative perfection*** rather than a beauty belonging to the mathematical figure. Nor, indeed, can the term ***intellectual beauty*** be suitably allowed at all: since otherwise the word beauty would forfeit every determinate meaning, or else the intellectual satisfaction would forfeit any precedence it holds over the sensible. It would sooner be a ***demonstration*** of such properties that one could pronounce beautiful, since through it the understanding, as the power of concepts, together with the imagination, as the power of presenting them _a priori_, feel themselves fortified (which, taken with the precision that reason imports, goes jointly under the name of the elegance of such a demonstration): for here at least the satisfaction, even though its ground resides in concepts, is subjective, whereas perfection brings an objective {{ 279 }} satisfaction along with it.
