## § 62. On objective purposiveness that is merely formal, as distinguished from the material kind

Every geometrical figure drawn according to a principle exhibits in itself a manifold and often admired objective purposiveness, namely a fitness for the solution of many problems by a single principle, and indeed of each individual problem in infinitely varied ways. The purposiveness here is manifestly objective and intellectual, not merely subjective and aesthetic. For it expresses the adequacy of the figure for producing many intended shapes, and is cognized by reason. Yet this purposiveness does not make possible the concept of the object itself, that is, the object is not regarded as possible merely with a view to this use.

In a figure as simple as the circle there lies the ground for {{ 272 }} the solution of a host of problems, each of which taken by itself would demand various apparatus, and which follows as if of its own accord as one among the infinitely many excellent properties of this figure. If the task is, for example, to construct a triangle from a given base line and the angle opposite to it, the problem is indeterminate, that is, it can be solved in infinitely manifold ways. Yet the circle comprises them all together, as the geometrical locus of all triangles that conform to this condition. Or suppose two lines are to intersect one another such that the rectangle formed from the two parts of the one equals the rectangle formed from the two parts of the other: the solution of the problem seems, to all appearance, to present much difficulty. But all lines that intersect within the circle whose {{{ 363 }}} circumference bounds each of them divide themselves of their own accord in this proportion. The other curved lines in turn furnish still further purposive solutions that were not at all thought of in the rule constituting their construction. All the conic sections, taken by themselves and in comparison with one another, are fertile in principles for the solution of a host of possible problems, however simple the definition may be that determines their concept. — It is a true delight to behold the zeal of the ancient geometers as they inquired into these properties of lines of this {{ 273 }} kind, without letting themselves be led astray by the question of narrow minds as to what use such knowledge might serve; for example the parabola's properties, without any acquaintance with the terrestrial law of gravity, which would have supplied them the application of that curve to the trajectory of heavy bodies (whose direction of gravity in their motion may be regarded as parallel); or those of the ellipse, without suspecting that a gravity is also to be found in the heavenly bodies, and without knowing its law at different distances from the point of attraction, which brings it about that they describe this line in free motion. All the while that, unbeknownst to themselves, they were laboring for posterity in this, they delighted in a purposiveness in the essence of things that they could nonetheless present entirely _a priori_ in its necessity. Plato, himself a master in this science, was carried, by such an original constitution of things, which we can dispense with all experience in order to discover, and by the mind's capacity to be able to draw the harmony of beings from their supersensible principle (to which are further added the properties of numbers, with which the mind plays in music), into that enthusiasm which raised him above the concepts of experience to ideas that appeared explicable to him solely through an intellectual community with the source of all beings. It is no wonder that he turned away from his school anyone unversed in geometry, in that {{ 274 }} he supposed himself able to derive, from the pure intuition dwelling inwardly in the human spirit, what Anaxagoras had inferred from objects of experience and their purposive connection. For it is precisely in the necessity of that which is purposive and so constituted as if it had been deliberately arranged for our use, yet which seems to belong originally to the essence of things without any regard to our use, that the ground lies for the great admiration of nature, not so much outside us as within our own reason; and here it is surely {{{ 364 }}} pardonable that this admiration should, through misunderstanding, gradually have risen to the point of fanaticism.

This intellectual purposiveness, however, although it is objective (and not subjective like the aesthetic), can nonetheless, with respect to its possibility, be quite well comprehended, though only in general, as merely formal (not real), that is, as a purposiveness without there being any need to lay an end at its foundation, and hence without any need of teleology for it. The figure of the circle is an intuition that has been determined by the understanding in accordance with a principle: the unity of this principle, which I adopt at will and lay at the basis as a concept, and which, once applied to a form of intuition (namely space) that is likewise met with in me merely as a representation and indeed _a priori_, makes comprehensible the unity of the many rules issuing from the construction of that concept, rules that are purposive in various possible {{ 275 }} respects, without our being permitted to attribute to this purposiveness an ***end*** or any other ground of it. The case here is not the same as when, in some aggregate of ***things*** outside me enclosed within certain boundaries, for example a garden, I come upon order and regularity of the trees, flower beds, paths, and so forth, which I cannot hope to infer _a priori_ from my delimitation of a space made according to some arbitrary rule: because these are existing things that must be given empirically in order to be able to be cognized, and not a mere representation in me determined _a priori_ according to a principle. The latter (empirical) purposiveness therefore depends, as something ***real***, upon the concept of an end.

But the ground of the admiration of a purposiveness, even though one perceived in the essence of things (insofar as their concepts can be constructed), can also be very well grasped, and grasped indeed as legitimate. The manifold rules whose unity (springing from one principle) arouses this admiration are one and all synthetic and do not flow from a ***concept*** of the object, for example of the circle, but require that this object be given in intuition. Through this, however, this unity takes on the appearance of having empirically an external ground of the rules distinct from our power of representation, and {{ 276 }} thus that the agreement of the object with the need for rules, a need proper to the understanding, is contingent in itself, and hence possible only through an end expressly directed to that agreement. Now, to be sure, {{{ 365 }}} this very harmony, because, notwithstanding all this purposiveness, it is nonetheless cognized not empirically but _a priori_, ought of itself to bring us to acknowledge that space—by whose determination (through the imagination conformably to a concept) the object was alone possible—is no property of the things outside me but only a mode of representing in me, and thus that, into the figure I trace ***suited to a concept***, that is, into my own way of representing whatever is externally given to me, be it in itself what it may, it is I who ***introduce the purposiveness***, and that I receive no empirical instruction concerning it from this object, and consequently that I require for that purposiveness no particular end in the object outside me. But because this reflection already requires a critical use of reason, and hence cannot at once already be contained in judging the object by its properties: the latter directly furnishes me nothing more than a bringing-together of heterogeneous rules (even with respect to what is dissimilar in them) within one principle, which, without calling for any particular ground of it that would lie outside my concept and, more generally, outside my representation _a priori_, {{ 277 }} is nonetheless cognized by me _a priori_ as truly holding. Now ***astonishment*** is a shock the mind receives at the incompatibility of a representation, and of the rule given through it, with the principles already lying at its foundation, a shock that therefore produces a doubt as to whether one has really seen or judged correctly; ***admiration***, however, is an ever-recurring astonishment despite the vanishing of this doubt. Consequently the latter is a wholly natural effect of that observed purposiveness in the essence of things (as appearances), an effect that also cannot be censured in this respect, since the accord of that form of sensible intuition (which is called space) with the faculty of concepts (the understanding) is not only inexplicable to us for the very reason that it is precisely this one and no other, but is moreover expansive for the mind, prompting it as it were to intimate something further lying beyond those sensible representations, wherein, though unknown to us, the ultimate ground of that accord may be found. To be sure, we have no need to know this ground when it is merely a matter of the formal purposiveness of our representations _a priori_; but even merely having to look out that far instills, at the same time, admiration for the object that compels us to it.

One is accustomed to call the properties just mentioned, both of geometrical {{{ 366 }}} figures and also of numbers, ***beauty***, on account of a certain purposiveness of theirs, not expected from the {{ 278 }} simplicity of their construction, for all sorts of _a priori_ cognitive use; and one speaks, for example, of some ***beautiful*** property or other of the circle, discovered in this or that manner. Yet it is no aesthetic judging through which we find them purposive; no judging devoid of a concept, such as would make noticeable a merely ***subjective*** purposiveness in the free play of our powers of cognition: but rather an intellectual judging by concepts, one that gives us distinctly to cognize an objective purposiveness, that is, a fitness for all sorts of (infinitely manifold) ends. One would sooner have to call it a ***relative perfection*** than a beauty of the mathematical figure. The designation of an ***intellectual beauty*** cannot in general be properly permitted either: for otherwise the word beauty would have to lose all determinate meaning, or the intellectual satisfaction would have to lose all precedence over the sensible. It would sooner be a ***demonstration*** of such properties that one could pronounce beautiful, since through it the understanding, as the power of concepts, together with the imagination, as the power of presenting them _a priori_, feel themselves fortified (which, taken with the precision that reason imports, goes jointly under the name of the elegance of such a demonstration): for here at least the satisfaction, even though its ground resides in concepts, is subjective, whereas perfection brings an objective {{ 279 }} satisfaction along with it.
