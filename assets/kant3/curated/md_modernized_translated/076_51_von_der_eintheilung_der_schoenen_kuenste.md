## {{ 204 }} § 51. On the division of the beautiful arts

One can in general call beauty (whether it be natural beauty or the beauty of art) the ***expression*** of aesthetic ideas: only that in beautiful art this idea must be occasioned by a concept of the object, whereas in beautiful nature the mere reflection upon a given intuition, without a concept of what the object is supposed to be, is sufficient for awakening and communicating the idea of which that object is regarded as the ***expression***.

If, then, we wish to divide the beautiful arts, we can, at least by way of experiment, choose no more convenient principle for this than the analogy of art with the kind of expression of which human beings make use in speaking in order to communicate themselves to one another as completely as possible, i.e., not merely in accordance with their concepts but also their sensations[^*]. — This consists in the ***word***, the ***gesture***, and the ***tone*** (articulation, gesticulation, and modulation). Only the combination of these {{ 205 }} three kinds of expression constitutes the complete communication of the speaker. For thought, intuition, and sensation are thereby conveyed to the other simultaneously and united.

There are, then, only three kinds of beautiful arts: the ***arts of speech***, the {{{ 321 }}} ***visual arts***, and the art ***of the play of sensations*** (as outer impressions of the senses). One could also arrange this division dichotomously, so that beautiful art would be divided into that of the expression of thoughts, or of intuitions, and these in turn merely in accordance with their form, or their matter (the sensation). But it would then look too abstract and not so commensurate with common concepts.

1) The arts of speech are ***rhetoric*** and ***poetry***. ***Rhetoric*** is the art of conducting a business of the understanding as a free play of the imagination; ***poetry***, of carrying out a free play of the imagination as a business of the understanding.

The ***orator***, then, announces a business and carries it out as if it were merely a ***play*** with ideas in order to entertain the listeners. The ***poet*** announces merely an entertaining ***play*** with ideas, and yet as much comes out of it for the understanding as if he had had only the intention of conducting its business. The combination and harmony of the two {{ 206 }} faculties of cognition, sensibility and understanding, which cannot, to be sure, do without one another, but which nevertheless also do not allow themselves to be readily united without constraint and mutual injury, must seem to be unintentional and to come together of itself; otherwise it is not ***beautiful*** art. Hence everything labored and painstaking must be avoided in it; for beautiful art must be free art in a double sense: both that it not be a labor that is a remunerative occupation, the magnitude of which can be judged, exacted, or paid for in accordance with a determinate standard; and also that the mind feel itself occupied, but at the same time, without looking out toward another end, feel itself (independently of remuneration) satisfied and aroused.

The orator, then, gives indeed something that he does not promise, namely an entertaining play of the imagination; but he also falls short in something that he does promise, and which is, after all, his announced business, namely to occupy the understanding purposively. The poet, by contrast, promises little and announces a mere play with ideas, but accomplishes something that is worthy of a business, namely to provide nourishment for the understanding in play and to give life to its concepts through the imagination: hence the former at bottom less, the latter more, than he promises.

2) The visual arts, or those of the expression of ideas in {{ 207 }} ***sensible intuition*** (not through representations of the mere imagination, {{{ 322 }}} which are aroused through words), are either those of ***sensible truth*** or of ***sensible illusion***. The first is called ***plastic art***, the second ***painting***. Both make shapes in space into the expression for ideas: the former makes shapes cognizable for two senses, sight and feeling (although for the latter not with regard to beauty), the latter only for the first. The aesthetic idea (archetype, original image) lies at the basis of both in the imagination: but the shape that constitutes the expression of it (ectype, copy) is given either in its bodily extension (as the object itself exists) or in accordance with the way in which this paints itself in the eye (in accordance with its appearance on a surface); or else, which is also the former, either the relation to an actual end, or only the semblance of it, is made the condition of the reflection.

To ***plastic art***, as the first kind of beautiful visual arts, belong ***sculpture*** and ***architecture***. The ***first*** is that which presents concepts of things bodily, as they ***could exist in nature*** (though, as beautiful art, with regard to aesthetic purposiveness); the ***second*** is the art of presenting concepts of things that are ***possible only through art***, and whose form has for its determining ground not nature but {{ 208 }} an arbitrary end, for this aim, but yet at the same time also aesthetically purposively. In the latter a certain ***use*** of the artificial object is the main thing, as the condition to which the aesthetic ideas are restricted. In the former the mere ***expression*** of aesthetic ideas is the chief aim. Thus statues of human beings, gods, animals, and the like are of the first kind; but temples, or magnificent buildings for the purpose of public assemblies, or also dwellings, triumphal arches, columns, cenotaphs, and the like, erected in honored memory, belong to architecture. Indeed, all household furnishings (the work of the joiner and the like things for use) can be counted in it: because the commensurateness of the product to a certain use constitutes the essential of a ***building***; whereas a mere ***sculptural work***, which is made solely for viewing and is meant to please for itself, is, as bodily presentation, mere imitation of nature, though with regard to aesthetic ideas: in which, then, the ***sensible truth*** may not go so far that it cease to appear as art and a product of choice.

***The art of painting***, as the second kind of visual arts, which presents {{{ 323 }}} ***sensible illusion*** artfully combined with ideas, I would divide into that of the beautiful ***depiction*** of ***nature*** and that of the beautiful ***arrangement*** of its ***products***. The first would be ***painting {{ 209 }} proper***, the second ***the art of pleasure gardens***. For the first gives only the illusion of bodily extension; the second indeed gives this in truth, but only the illusion of utility and use for other ends than merely for the play of the imagination in the contemplation of its forms[^**]. The latter is nothing other than the adornment of the ground with the same manifoldness (grasses, flowers, shrubs, and trees, even waters, hills, and valleys) with which nature presents it to view, only differently and arranged in a manner commensurate with certain ideas. The beautiful arrangement of bodily things, however, is {{ 210 }} also given only for the eye, like painting; the sense of feeling can provide no intuitive representation of such a form. To painting in the broad sense I would further count the decoration of rooms by means of tapestries, ornamental displays, and all beautiful furnishings that serve merely for ***viewing***; likewise the art of dressing with taste (rings, snuffboxes, etc.). For a flower bed of all sorts of flowers, a room with all sorts of ornaments (even including the ladies' finery) constitute at a magnificent festival a kind of picture, which, just like those so called properly (which do not, say, have the intention of ***teaching*** history or natural knowledge), is there merely for viewing, in order to entertain the imagination in free play with ideas and to occupy the aesthetic power of judgment without a determinate end. The workmanship in all this adornment may always be very different mechanically {{{ 324 }}} and require quite different artists; the judgment of taste concerning what is beautiful in this art is nevertheless determined in one and the same way insofar: namely to judge only the forms (without regard to an end) as they offer themselves to the eye, singly or in their composition, in accordance with the effect they have on the imagination. — But how the visual art can be counted (by analogy) under gesture in a language is justified by this, {{ 211 }} that the spirit of the artist gives through these shapes a bodily expression of what and how he has thought, and makes the thing itself speak, as it were, mimically: a very common play of our fantasy, which attributes to lifeless things, in accordance with their form, a spirit that speaks out of them.

3) The art of the beautiful play of sensations (which are generated from without and which nevertheless must allow themselves to be universally communicated) can concern nothing other than the proportion of the various degrees of the disposition (tension) of the sense to which the sensation belongs, i.e., its tone; and in this broad meaning of the word it can be divided into the artificial play of the sensations of hearing and of those of sight, hence into ***music*** and ***the art of color***. — It is remarkable: that these two senses, besides the receptivity for impressions, as much of it as is requisite for obtaining, by means of their concepts, from outer objects, are still capable of a particular sensation connected therewith, of which one cannot quite make out whether it has the sense or the reflection for its basis; and that this affectibility can nevertheless sometimes be lacking, although the sense, as far as its use for the cognition of objects is concerned, is otherwise not at all deficient, but rather even excellently fine {{ 212 }}. That is to say, one cannot say with certainty: whether a color or a tone (sound) is merely an agreeable sensation, or in itself already a beautiful play of sensations, and as such carries with it a satisfaction in the form in the aesthetic judging. If one considers the rapidity of the vibrations of light, or, in the second kind, of the air, which probably far surpasses all our capacity for judging immediately in perception the proportion of the temporal division through them: then one should believe that only the ***effect*** of these tremblings on the elastic parts of our body is sensed, but that the ***temporal division*** through them is not noticed and {{{ 325 }}} drawn into judging, hence that with colors and tones only agreeableness, not beauty of their composition, is combined. But if, by contrast, one considers, ***first***, the mathematical, which can be said about the proportion of these vibrations in music and their judging, and judges the contrast of colors, as is fair, by analogy with the latter; if one takes into account, ***second***, the, though rare, examples of human beings who, with the best sight in the world, have not been able to distinguish colors and, with the sharpest hearing, not tones, likewise, for those who can do this, the perception of an altered quality (not merely of the degree of sensation) in the various intensities on the scale of colors or of tones, likewise that the number of these {{ 213 }} is determined for ***comprehensible*** differences: then one might find oneself constrained to regard the sensations of both not as mere impressions of sense, but as the effect of a judging of form in the play of many sensations. The difference that the one or the other opinion makes in the judging of the ground of music would, however, only change the definition to the effect that one would declare it either, as we have done, the ***beautiful*** play of sensations (through hearing), or of ***agreeable*** sensations. Only according to the first kind of explanation will music be represented wholly as a ***beautiful***, but according to the second as an ***agreeable*** art (at least in part).

[^*]: The reader will not judge this sketch of a possible division of the beautiful arts as an intended theory. It is only one of the various attempts that one can and should still make.
[^**]: That the art of pleasure gardens can be regarded as a kind of art of painting, although it presents its forms bodily, seems strange; but since it really takes its forms from nature (the trees, shrubs, grasses, and flowers from forest and field, at least originally) and to that extent is not art, say, like plastic art, and also has, as the condition of its arrangement, no concept of the object and its end (as, say, architecture does), but merely the free play of the imagination in contemplation: so it agrees to that extent with merely aesthetic painting, which has no determinate theme (entertainingly combining air, land, and water through light and shadow). — In general the reader will judge this only as an attempt at the combination of the beautiful arts under one principle, which this time is meant to be that of the expression of aesthetic ideas (by analogy with a language), and will not regard it as a derivation of them held to be decided.
