## {{ 204 }} § 51. On the division of the fine arts

Beauty in general — be it the beauty of nature or of art — may be called the ***expression*** of aesthetic ideas: save that in fine art this idea has to be occasioned by a concept of the object, whereas in beautiful nature mere reflection on some given intuition, apart from any concept of what the thing is meant to be, is enough to awaken and communicate the idea for which that object counts as the ***expression***.

So, if we mean to divide the fine arts, we can find — at least for a trial — no principle more convenient than the analogy between art and that manner of expression which human beings use when they speak, so as to communicate to one another, as fully as can be done, not their concepts alone but their sensations too[^*]. — Such expression lies in the ***word***, the ***gesture***, and the ***tone*** (that is, articulation, gesticulation, and modulation). Only the joining of these {{ 205 }} three modes of expression yields the speaker's complete communication. For thereby thought, intuition, and sensation are carried over to the other person at one and the same time and in union.

Accordingly there exist just three sorts of fine art: the ***verbal***, the {{{ 321 }}} ***formative***, and that ***of the play of sensations*** (taken as outer impressions of sense). One might also set up this division dichotomously, so that fine art would be split into the art expressing thoughts and the art expressing intuitions, and this last again according merely to its form or else its material (sensation). But it would then turn out too abstract and less answerable to ordinary concepts.

1) The verbal arts comprise ***rhetoric*** and ***poetry***. ***Rhetoric*** is the skill of pursuing a task of the understanding as though it were a free play of imagination; ***poetry***, the skill of executing a free play of imagination as though it were a task of the understanding.

The ***orator***, then, gives notice of a task and executes it as though it were nothing but a ***play*** with ideas, meant to divert his listeners. The ***poet*** gives notice of nothing but an entertaining ***play*** with ideas, and yet the yield for the understanding turns out as great as if his aim had merely been to carry on its work. The union and concord of the two {{ 206 }} cognitive faculties — sensibility and understanding — which, though they cannot dispense with one another, still cannot readily be brought together except under compulsion and reciprocal damage, must seem to come about unintentionally and to fall into place of its own accord; otherwise it is not ***fine*** art. For this reason all that is contrived and painstaking must be kept out of it; for fine art has to be free art in a twofold sense: first, that it not be a wage-earning labor whose extent could be assessed, exacted, or remunerated by some fixed measure; and second, that the mind, while engaged, should nonetheless — with no eye to any further end — feel itself (regardless of pay) satisfied and stirred.

The orator, then, does supply something he never pledged, namely a diverting play of imagination; yet he also curtails what he did pledge, and what is after all his declared task, namely to keep the understanding purposively engaged. The poet, by contrast, pledges little and gives notice of a bare play with ideas, yet delivers something worthy of a task, namely to feed the understanding as if in sport and, through imagination, to breathe life into its concepts: so that the one, at bottom, gives less, and the other more, than is promised.

2) The formative arts — those that express ideas in {{ 207 }} ***sensory intuition*** (not by way of representations of mere imagination, {{{ 322 }}} which words call forth) — fall either under ***sensory truth*** or under ***sensory semblance***. The first is termed ***plastic art***, the second ***painting***. Both turn shapes in space into the expression of ideas: the former makes shapes recognizable to two senses, sight and touch (though to touch not on the score of beauty), the latter to sight alone. The aesthetic idea (the archetype, the original image) underlies both in imagination; but the shape that gives it expression (the ectype, the after-image) is furnished either through its corporeal extension (the way the object exists in itself) or through the manner in which it depicts itself to the eye (its appearance across a surface); or — this coming to the same as the former — either the reference to an actual end, or merely the semblance of one, is made the condition for reflection.

Under ***plastic art***, the first species of the fine formative arts, fall ***sculpture*** and ***architecture***. The ***first*** is the one that renders in bodily form concepts of things ***as they might exist in nature*** (yet, being fine art, with an eye to aesthetic purposiveness); the ***second*** is the art whose task is to render — for this aim, though at once in an aesthetically purposive manner — concepts of things possible ***only through art***, things whose form takes as its determining ground, not nature, but {{ 208 }} some arbitrary end. With the latter a certain ***use*** of the artificial object is the chief matter, to which, as a condition, the aesthetic ideas stand confined. With the former the bare ***expression*** of aesthetic ideas is the chief aim. Thus statues of men, gods, animals, and suchlike belong to the first species; whereas temples, or splendid edifices meant for public gatherings, and likewise dwellings, triumphal arches, columns, cenotaphs, and suchlike raised for commemorative honor, pertain to architecture. Indeed every article of household furnishing (the cabinetmaker's work and suchlike things for use) may be reckoned here: because the product's fitness for a certain use makes up the essence of a ***work of building***; whereas a bare ***sculptural work***, fashioned solely to be looked at and meant to please in its own right, is, considered as a corporeal presentation, nothing but an imitation of nature, though still with an eye to aesthetic ideas: in which the ***sensory truth*** must not be pressed so far that the thing ceases to look like art and a product of choice.

***Painting***, the second species of formative art, which sets forth {{{ 323 }}} ***sensory semblance*** artificially bound up with ideas, I would separate into the beautiful ***depiction*** of ***nature*** and the beautiful ***assembling*** of her ***products***. The former would be ***proper {{ 209 }} painting***, the latter ***landscape gardening***. For the first gives merely the semblance of corporeal extension; the second gives that in truth, but only the semblance of employment and use toward ends other than the bare play of fancy in the beholding of its forms[^**]. This latter amounts to nothing more than decking the ground with that same variety (grasses, flowers, shrubs, and trees, and even waters, hills, and dales) with which nature sets it out for intuition, only put together in another way and to suit certain ideas. Yet the beautiful assembling of corporeal things, too, is offered {{ 210 }} to the eye alone, just as painting is; the sense of touch can supply no intuitive representation of a form of that kind. Under painting in the wide sense I would further reckon the adorning of rooms with tapestries, ornamental fixtures, and every kind of beautiful furnishing that serves only for ***viewing***; likewise the art of dressing with taste (rings, snuffboxes, and the rest). For a bed of every sort of flower, a room with every sort of trinket (even the ladies' finery included) make up, at some grand celebration, a kind of picture which, like the pictures properly so named (those not perhaps aiming to ***teach*** history or natural science), is there only to be gazed at, so as to keep the imagination busy in its free play among ideas and, with no fixed end, to engage the aesthetic power of judgment. However much the workmanship in all this ornamentation may differ mechanically {{{ 324 }}} and call for wholly different artificers, the judgment of taste is, as to what is beautiful in this art, still settled in one and the same way: namely, to appraise the forms alone (with no regard for any end), in the way they offer themselves to the eye, whether singly or in combination, by the effect they work upon the imagination. — That formative art may, on the analogy, be reckoned as gesture within a language finds its warrant {{ 211 }} in this: through such shapes the artist's spirit lends a corporeal expression to what he has thought and how, and gets the thing itself, as it were, to speak in mime — a most everyday play of our fancy, which reads into lifeless things, in keeping with their form, a spirit uttering itself out of them.

3) The art of the beautiful play of the sensations (sensations produced from without, and which must all the same admit of universal communication) can bear on nothing but the ratio among the several degrees of attunement (tension) belonging to the sense in which the sensation resides, that is, on its tone; and in this wide acceptation of the term it may be split into the artificial play of the sensations of hearing and of those of sight, hence into ***music*** on the one hand and the ***art of color*** on the other. — It is a striking thing that these two senses, over and above the receptivity for impressions needed to derive something from outer objects by means of their concepts, are further capable of a special sensation coupled with that receptivity, one of which it cannot rightly be settled whether it rests on sense or on reflection; and that this affectibility can nonetheless at times be wanting, even though in other respects the sense, so far as its employment for cognizing objects goes, is not in the least defective, but on the contrary quite exceptionally refined {{ 212 }}. That is to say, one cannot declare with certainty whether a color or a tone (sound) is a merely agreeable sensation, or is already of itself a beautiful play of sensations that, as such, brings with it in aesthetic judging a satisfaction in the form. If one reflects on how fast the tremors of light — or, in the second case, of the air — occur, a rapidity that in all likelihood far outstrips our whole capacity to appraise, directly in perception, the ratio of the temporal division they involve, one would suppose that only the ***effect*** of these oscillations upon the elastic portions of our body is felt, while the ***temporal division*** they produce goes unremarked and {{{ 325 }}} undrawn into judging, so that with colors and tones only agreeableness, and not beauty of their composition, is conjoined. If one weighs, on the other side, ***first***, the mathematics that may be stated concerning the ratio of these vibrations in music and their appraisal, and appraises the contrast of colors, as is only fair, on the analogy with music; if one calls to mind, ***secondly***, the admittedly rare cases of persons who, though blessed with the keenest eyes in the world, could not tell colors apart, and with the sharpest ears could not tell tones apart, and likewise, for those capable of it, the noticing of an altered quality (and not just of the sensation's intensity) across the different tensions on the color-scale or tone-scale, and likewise that the number of these {{ 213 }} is fixed for ***comprehensible*** distinctions: then one might well feel driven to view the sensations of both, not as a bare sense-impression, but as the effect of an appraisal of form within the play of many sensations. The difference made by adopting the one view or the other in judging the ground of music would, however, alter only the definition to this extent, that one would declare it either, as we have done, the ***beautiful*** play of sensations (through hearing) or the play of ***agreeable*** sensations. On the first mode of explanation alone is music represented wholly as ***beautiful***, whereas on the second it comes out as ***agreeable*** art (at least in part).

[^*]: The reader is not to take this outline of a possible way of dividing the fine arts for a theory meant in earnest. It is merely one among the several attempts that may and ought still to be made.
[^**]: It may look odd that landscape gardening should be counted a species of painting, given that it sets out its forms corporeally; but since it in fact draws those forms from nature itself (its trees, bushes, grasses, and blossoms taken from wood and field, at least to begin with), and to that extent is not art in the manner of plastic art, and moreover makes no concept of the object and its end (as architecture does) the condition of how it is put together, resting instead on the free play of imagination in the beholding: to just that extent it agrees with painting of the purely aesthetic sort, which fixes on no determinate theme (composing air, land, and water divertingly by means of light and shade). — On the whole the reader should treat this as no more than an essay at gathering the fine arts under a single principle — on this occasion, the expression of aesthetic ideas (after the analogy of a language) — and not as a derivation of them put forward as settled.
