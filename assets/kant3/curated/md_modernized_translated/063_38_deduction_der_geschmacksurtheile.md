## § 38. Deduction of Judgments of Taste

If it is granted that in a pure judgment of taste the satisfaction in the object is combined with the mere judging of its form: then it is nothing other than the subjective purposiveness {{{ 290 }}} of the form for the power of judgment, which we feel combined in the mind with the representation of the object. Since the power of judgment, in regard to the formal rules of judging, without any matter (either sense sensation or concept), can be directed only to the subjective {{ 151 }} conditions of the use of the power of judgment in general (which is restricted neither to a particular kind of sense nor to a particular concept of the understanding); consequently to that subjective element which one can presuppose in all human beings (as required for possible cognition in general): the agreement of a representation with these conditions of the power of judgment must be able to be assumed _a priori_ as valid for everyone. That is, the pleasure or subjective purposiveness of the representation for the relation of the cognitive faculties in the judging of a sensible object in general can be rightly demanded of everyone[^*].

Remark. {{ 152 }}

This deduction is so easy because it does not need to justify any objective reality of a concept; for beauty is not a concept of the object, and the judgment of taste is not a judgment of cognition. It asserts only: that we are justified in presupposing in every human being generally the same subjective conditions of the power of judgment that we find in ourselves; and furthermore, that we have correctly subsumed the given object under these conditions. Now, although this latter has unavoidable difficulties not attaching to the logical power of judgment (since in the latter one subsumes under concepts, but in the aesthetic power of judgment under a {{{ 291 }}} merely sensible relation of the imagination and the understanding harmonizing mutually with one another in the represented form of the object, where the subsumption can easily deceive): yet this does not in any way diminish the legitimacy of the claim of the power of judgment to count on universal assent, which claim comes down only to judging the correctness of the principle from subjective grounds as valid for everyone. For as regards the difficulty and the doubt concerning the correctness of the subsumption under that principle, they make the legitimacy of the claim to this validity of an aesthetic judgment in general, and consequently the principle itself, just as little doubtful as the likewise (though not as often and easily) faulty subsumption of the logical power of judgment under its principle can make the latter, which is objective, doubtful. But if the question were to be: How is it possible to assume nature _a priori_ as an aggregate of objects of taste? this problem would have relation to teleology, because it would have to be regarded as a purpose of nature that {{ 153 }} was essential to its concept to set up forms purposive for our power of judgment. But the correctness of this assumption is still very much to be doubted, whereas the reality of natural beauties lies open to experience.

[^*]: In order to be justified in claiming universal assent to a judgment of the aesthetic power of judgment that is based merely on subjective grounds, it is enough that one admit: 1) In all human beings, the subjective conditions of this faculty, as regards the relation of the cognitive powers set into activity therein to a cognition in general, are identical; which must be true, because otherwise human beings could not communicate their representations and even cognition itself to one another. 2) The judgment has taken regard merely of this relation (consequently the ***formal condition*** of the power of judgment) and is pure, i.e., mixed neither with concepts of the object nor with sensations as determining grounds. If there has been an error in regard to this latter, that concerns only the incorrect application to a particular case of the authority that a law gives us, whereby the authority in general is not abolished.
