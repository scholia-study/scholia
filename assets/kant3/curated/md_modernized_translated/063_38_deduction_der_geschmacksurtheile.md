## § 38. Deduction of judgments of taste

If we concede that, in a pure judgment of taste, the satisfaction taken in the object is combined with the mere judging of its form: then this is nothing other than the subjective purposiveness {{{ 290 }}} of that form for the power of judgment, which we feel to be combined, in the mind, with the representation of the object. Now since the power of judgment, in respect of the formal rules of judging, and devoid of all matter (be it sensation of sense or concept), can address itself solely to the subjective {{ 151 }} conditions of the use of the power of judgment in general (a use restricted neither to a particular mode of sense nor to any particular concept of the understanding); since it thus addresses that subjective factor presupposable in every human being (as required for cognition to be possible at all): the fact that a representation accords with these conditions of the power of judgment must be capable of being assumed _a priori_ as holding for everyone. That is to say, in the judging of a sensible object as such, the pleasure — or the representation's subjective purposiveness with respect to the relation of the cognitive faculties — may rightfully be expected of everyone[^*].

Remark. {{ 152 }}

This deduction is so easy precisely because it need not vindicate any objective reality of a concept; for beauty is no concept pertaining to the object, and a judgment of taste is not a judgment of cognition. It asserts only: that we are entitled to presuppose universally in every human being the same subjective conditions of the power of judgment that we find within ourselves; and, beyond that, only that we have correctly subsumed the given object under these conditions. Now although this latter point carries unavoidable difficulties, which do not attach to the logical power of judgment (because in the latter one subsumes under concepts, whereas in the aesthetic one subsumes under a {{{ 291 }}} merely felt relation of the imagination and the understanding as they reciprocally accord with one another in the represented form of the object, where the subsumption may easily go astray): still nothing is thereby detracted from the legitimacy of the power of judgment's claim to count on universal assent, a claim that comes down only to judging the correctness of the principle as valid for everyone on subjective grounds. For as concerns the difficulty and the doubt about whether the subsumption under that principle is correct, this renders the legitimacy of the claim to such validity of an aesthetic judgment in general — and hence the principle itself — just as little doubtful as the equally (though not so frequent or easy) faulty subsumption of the logical power of judgment under its principle can render doubtful that latter principle, which is objective. But suppose the question were: How can nature be assumed _a priori_ as an aggregate of objects of taste? then this task would stand in relation to teleology, since it would have to be regarded as an end of nature, one {{ 153 }} essentially attached to its concept, to establish forms purposive for our power of judgment. But the correctness of this assumption remains very much open to doubt, whereas the actuality of natural beauties lies plainly before experience.

[^*]: In order to have the right to lay claim to universal assent for a judgment of the aesthetic power of judgment which rests on merely subjective grounds, it suffices to grant the following: 1) that in all human beings the subjective conditions of this faculty are identical, as regards the relation, to cognition in general, of the cognitive powers thereby set into play; which has to be true, for otherwise human beings would be unable to communicate their representations, or even cognition itself. 2) that the judgment has attended merely to this relation (and hence to the ***formal condition*** of the power of judgment) and is pure, that is, mingled neither with concepts of the object nor with sensations as determining grounds. Even where an error has been committed in respect of this latter point, it concerns only the mistaken application, to a particular case, of the authorization that a law confers upon us, whereby the authorization as such is in no way abolished.
