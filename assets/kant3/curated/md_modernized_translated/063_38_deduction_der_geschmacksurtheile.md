## § 38. Deduction of judgments of taste

Granting that, within a pure judgment of taste, the satisfaction in the object goes hand in hand with the mere judging of its form: then it is simply that form's subjective purposiveness {{{ 290 }}} for the power of judgment, which we sense in the mind as united with the representation of the object. Now the power of judgment, where the formal rules of judging are at issue and all matter is set aside (whether sensation of sense or concept), can be directed only toward those subjective {{ 151 }} conditions under which the power of judgment is exercised at all (conditions confined neither to some particular manner of sensing nor to any particular concept of the understanding), and hence toward that subjective element which one may presuppose in every human being (as requisite for any possible cognition whatever); and so it must be possible to assume _a priori_, as valid for everyone, that a representation agrees with these conditions of the power of judgment. That is to say, in the judging of any sensible object whatever, the pleasure — in other words, the representation's subjective purposiveness for the relation among the cognitive faculties — is something one may justly ask of every person[^*].

Remark. {{ 152 }}

The reason this deduction proves so effortless is that it has no need to justify any objective reality belonging to a concept; for beauty is not a concept of the object, and a judgment of taste is no cognitive judgment. Its whole claim is this: that we are entitled to take the very subjective conditions of the power of judgment which we discover in ourselves and to presuppose them, quite generally, in every human being; and, beyond this, only that we have rightly, under these conditions, subsumed the given object. Now even though this last point brings with it inescapable difficulties that never burden the logical power of judgment — for there one subsumes things under concepts, while in the aesthetic case one subsumes them under a {{{ 291 }}} merely sensible relation between imagination and understanding, which mutually attune themselves to one another as the object's presented form is beheld, so that the subsumption can readily deceive — none of this, all the same, strips away anything from the rightful claim of the power of judgment to reckon on universal assent, a claim amounting to no more than this: that the principle's correctness is, on subjective grounds, to be judged valid for everyone. For where the difficulty and the doubt bear upon whether the subsumption under that principle has been carried out correctly, this leaves the legitimacy of the claim to such validity in an aesthetic judgment at large — and thus the principle itself — every bit as little in doubt as a mistaken subsumption on the part of the logical power of judgment beneath its own principle (a lapse just as possible there, though neither so frequent nor so easy) could cast doubt upon that objective principle behind it. Were the question instead to be: How is it possible to assume _a priori_ that nature forms a totality of objects of taste? then such a task bears upon teleology, for the setting-up of forms purposive for our power of judgment would have to count as a purpose of nature, one {{ 153 }} bound up essentially with the concept of nature itself. But the correctness of this assumption remains very much open to doubt, whereas the actuality of natural beauties lies plainly before experience.

[^*]: To be entitled to demand universal assent for a verdict of the aesthetic power of judgment that rests on nothing but subjective grounds, it is enough that one concede the following: 1) that, across every human being, the subjective conditions of this faculty are one and the same, so far as concerns how the cognitive powers there set into activity stand toward cognition in general; and this must hold, since otherwise people could not communicate their representations to one another, nor even cognition itself. 2) that the judgment has regarded only this relation (and so the ***formal condition*** of the power of judgment), while being pure as well — that is, commingled neither with any concepts of the object nor with sensations acting as determining grounds. Even where an error has been committed in respect of this latter point, it concerns only the mistaken application, to a particular case, of the authorization that a law confers upon us, whereby the authorization as such is in no way abolished.
