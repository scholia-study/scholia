## {{ 400 }} § 85. On physicotheology

Physicotheology is reason's attempt to infer, from the ***ends*** of nature (which can only be cognized empirically), the supreme cause of nature and its properties. A moral theology (ethicotheology) would be the attempt to infer, from the moral end of rational beings in nature (which can be cognized _a priori_), that cause and its properties.

The former naturally comes before the latter. For if we mean to infer ***teleologically*** from the things in the world to a cause of the world, then ends of nature must first be given, for which {{{ 437 }}} we then have to seek a final end, and for this in turn the principle of the causality of that supreme cause.

In accordance with the teleological principle, many investigations of nature can and must be carried out without our having any occasion to ask after the ground of the possibility of acting purposively, which we encounter in various of the products {{ 401 }} of nature. But if we should now wish to possess a concept of this as well, then for that purpose we command no insight reaching any further than simply the maxim of the reflecting power of judgment: namely, that were even a solitary organic product of nature to be presented to us, the character of our faculty of cognition would let us conceive, as its ground, none other than a cause belonging to nature itself (be it to the whole of nature or only to this fragment of it) that holds within it, through understanding, the causality for producing such a thing; a principle for judging that, while advancing us not one step in accounting for natural things and how they arise, does nevertheless throw open to us, past the bounds of nature, a certain vista, whereby the concept of an original being—elsewhere so infertile—might perhaps be given a closer determination.

Now I say: physicotheology, however far it may be pushed, can nevertheless disclose to us nothing of a ***final end*** of creation; for it does not even reach as far as the question about it. It can, to be sure, justify the concept of an intelligent cause of the world as a concept that, subjectively, is alone suited to the constitution of our cognitive faculty regarding the possibility of the things that we can render intelligible to ourselves in terms of ends, but it can determine this concept no further, in either a theoretical or a practical respect; and its attempt fails to attain its {{ 402 }} aim of grounding a theology, but instead remains always only a physical teleology: because in it the relation to ends is always regarded, and must be regarded, only as conditioned within nature; and consequently it cannot even so much as raise the question of the end for which nature itself exists (the ground of which must be sought outside nature), though it is on the determinate idea of this end that the determinate concept of that supreme intelligent cause of the world, and hence the possibility of a theology, depends.

For what purpose the things in the world serve one another; for what purpose the manifold within a single thing is good for that thing itself; how one even comes to have ground for supposing that nothing whatever in the world exists to no purpose, but rather that everything, ***in nature***, is good for something or other, granted the condition that certain things (as ends) ought to exist—so that for the power of judgment our reason accordingly has {{{ 438 }}} within its capacity no principle for the possibility of the object of its inescapable teleological judging save this one, that it place the mechanism of nature under the architectonic of an understanding-endowed author of the world: all of this the teleological survey of the world performs most gloriously and to our extreme admiration. But because the data, and hence the principles, for ***determining*** that concept of an intelligent cause of the world (as the supreme artist) are merely empirical, they license no inference to any properties beyond those that experience discloses to us in its effects, which, seeing that it can never take in the whole of nature as a system, {{ 403 }} is bound often to run up against grounds of proof that (by all appearances) are at odds both with that concept and with one another, yet can never—even were we equal to surveying the entire system empirically, so far as bare nature is concerned—lift us above nature to the end of nature's own existence and thereby to the determinate concept of that higher intelligence.

If one makes small the task whose solution is the business of a physicotheology, its solution appears easy. For if one squanders the concept of a ***divinity*** on every intelligent being we might conceive, of which there may be one or several, possessing many and very great, yet precisely not all, of the properties that are required for founding a nature in agreement with the greatest possible end; or if one thinks it nothing to make good, in a theory, the deficiency of what the grounds of proof achieve by means of arbitrary additions, and, wherever one has ground merely to assume ***much*** perfection (and what is much for us?), holds oneself entitled to presuppose ***all possible*** perfection: then physical teleology makes weighty claims to the glory of grounding a theology. But when it is demanded that one indicate what, then, drives us and moreover entitles us to make those additions, we shall seek in vain, among the principles of the theoretical use of reason—which absolutely demands that in explaining an {{ 404 }} object of experience one ascribe to it no more properties than empirical data for its possibility are to be found—for any ground for our justification. On closer examination we would see that in truth an idea of a highest being, resting on a quite different use of reason (the practical), lies at the ground in us _a priori_, an idea that drives us to make good the deficient representation of a physical teleology of the original ground of ends in nature up to the {{{ 439 }}} concept of a divinity; and we would not falsely imagine that we had brought about this idea—and with it a theology—through the theoretical use of reason applied to physical knowledge of the world, still less that we had proved its reality.

One cannot count it so severely to the ancients' blame that they thought of their gods as very manifoldly different, partly in respect of their powers and partly in respect of their intentions and dispositions of will, yet all of them—their supreme head not excepted—as still restricted in a human manner. For when they considered the arrangement and course of things in nature, they did indeed find ground enough to assume something more than the mechanical as their cause, and to suspect behind the machinery of this world the intentions of certain higher causes, which they could not think of otherwise than as superhuman. But because they {{ 405 }} encountered good and evil, the purposive and the counterpurposive, so greatly mixed in it, at least for our insight, and could not permit themselves nevertheless to assume secretly underlying wise and beneficent ends, of which they yet saw no proof, for the sake of the arbitrary idea of a supremely perfect author: their judgment about the supreme cause of the world could hardly have turned out otherwise, so far, namely, as they proceeded quite consistently according to maxims of the merely theoretical use of reason. Others, who wanted to be physicists and theologians at one and the same time, supposed they could satisfy reason by seeing to the absolute unity that reason requires in the principle of natural things—doing so by way of the idea of a being in which, as the one and only substance, all those things would figure as merely inhering determinations: a substance that, while not the cause of the world through any understanding, would still be that subject in which all the understanding possessed by the beings of the world is to be encountered; hence a being that, while bringing nothing forth according to ends, would still be one in which all things, owing to the oneness of the subject whose mere determinations they are, had of necessity to stand in a purposive relation to one another, even in the absence of any end or intention. In this way they brought in the idealism of final causes: for they converted the unity—so difficult to elicit—of a plurality of purposively linked substances from a causal dependence ***on one*** thing into the inherence ***in one*** {{ 406 }} thing; and this system, viewed subsequently from the side of the inhering beings of the world as ***pantheism***, and from the side of the solely subsisting subject qua original being (at a later date) as ***Spinozism***, did not so much settle the {{{ 440 }}} question about the first ground of nature's purposiveness as instead pronounce it void, since that latter concept, stripped of every bit of its reality, was reduced to a mere misreading of a general ontological concept of a thing as such.

On merely theoretical principles for the use of reason (the sole foundation on which physicotheology rests), one can accordingly never elicit a concept of a divinity that would suffice for our teleological judging of nature. For we either pronounce the whole of teleology a mere illusion on the part of the power of judgment as it judges the causal connection of things, and flee to the single principle of a bare mechanism of nature, which—by reason of the unity of the substance whose determinations, in their manifoldness, are all that nature is said to be—merely gives us the appearance of harboring a general relation to ends; or else, choosing to hold fast to the principle of realism regarding this special sort of causality rather than to the aforementioned idealism of final causes, we may set beneath natural ends either a multiplicity of intelligent original beings or but a single such being: yet once, for grounding the concept of it, we have nothing at our disposal beyond principles of experience taken from the actual linkage of ends {{ 407 }} in the world, then on the one side we can devise no remedy against the discord that nature, as regards the unity of ends, exhibits across many examples, while on the other we can never derive from all this a concept of one single intelligent cause—arrived at as we do arrive at it, warranted by mere experience alone—determinate enough to serve any theology whatsoever, in whichever manner, be it theoretical or practical, that theology might be put to use.

Physical teleology does indeed drive us to seek a theology, but it can produce none, however far we may track nature through experience and come to the aid, by ideas of reason (which for physical problems must be theoretical), of the connection of ends discovered in it. What use is it, one will rightly complain, that we lay a great understanding, one immeasurable for us, at the basis of all these arrangements and let it order this world according to its intentions? when nature tells us nothing of the final intention, nor ever can, without which we can yet make for ourselves no common point of reference for all these natural ends, no sufficient teleological principle, whether for cognizing the ends collectively in one system, or for making for ourselves, of the supreme understanding as the cause of such a nature, a {{{ 441 }}} concept that could serve our power of judgment, as it reflects teleologically upon that nature, {{ 408 }} as a measure. I would in that case indeed have an ***understanding of art*** for scattered ends, but no ***wisdom*** for a final end, which yet must properly contain the determining ground of the former. Yet in default of a final end—one that pure reason alone can supply _a priori_ (since every end in the world is empirically conditioned and can hold nothing beyond what is good for this or that purpose as a contingent aim, never what is good without qualification), and one that alone could instruct me which properties, which degree, and which proportion I must ascribe in thought to the supreme cause of nature so as to judge it as a teleological system: with what warrant, and how, am I here to enlarge as I please my very confined concept of that primordial understanding (a concept I can rest only on my meager acquaintance with the world), stretching it—out of the power of this original being to make its ideas actual, out of its will to do so, and so forth—clear up to the idea of an all-wise, infinite being? Were this to be undertaken theoretically, it would presuppose omniscience in my own self, so that I might see into the ends of nature in their whole interconnection and, over and above that, be able to conceive every other possible plan against which the present one would have, on good grounds, to be judged the best. For short of such a completed cognition of the effect, I am unable to arrive at any determinate concept of the supreme cause—a concept discoverable solely in that of an intelligence unlimited in every {{ 409 }} regard, which is to say the concept of a divinity—and so to erect a groundwork for theology.

We may therefore, however far physical teleology be extended, safely state, in keeping with the principle adduced above: that the makeup and governing principles of our cognitive power leave us able to conceive nature, in those purposive arrangements of it that have come to our knowledge, in no manner other than as the work of an understanding to which nature stands subordinated. But whether that understanding, in producing the whole of nature, may besides have entertained some final intention (one that would then not reside within the nature of the sensible world): this is something the theoretical study of nature can never lay open to us; rather, for all our knowledge of nature it stays unsettled whether that supreme cause is the original ground of nature everywhere in view of a final end, or is so instead through an understanding that the sheer necessity of its own nature determines toward the bringing-forth of certain forms {{{ 442 }}} (on the analogy of what, in the case of animals, we term the artistic instinct)—and this without any need, on that score, to credit it even with wisdom, much less with a highest wisdom bound up with all the further properties needed for the perfection of its product.

Physicotheology is thus a misunderstood physical teleology, {{ 410 }} usable only as a preparation (propaedeutic) for theology, and adequate to that aim only through the accession of a further principle on which it can support itself, but not in itself, as its name would claim.
