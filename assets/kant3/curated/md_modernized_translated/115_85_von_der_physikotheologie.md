## {{ 400 }} § 85. On physico-theology

Physico-theology is the attempt of reason to infer from the ***ends*** of nature (which can only be cognized empirically) to the supreme cause of nature and its properties. A moral theology (ethico-theology) would be the attempt to infer from the moral end of rational beings in nature (which can be cognized _a priori_) to that cause and its properties.

The former naturally precedes the second. For if we want to infer ***teleologically*** from the things in the world to a world-cause: then ends of nature must first be given, for which {{{ 437 }}} we have afterward to seek a final end, and for this then the principle of the causality of this supreme cause.

According to the teleological principle many investigations of nature can and must take place, without one's having cause to inquire after the ground of the possibility of acting purposively which we encounter in various of the products {{ 401 }} of nature. But if one now wants to have a concept of this too, then we have for it absolutely no further insight than merely the maxim of the reflecting power of judgment: namely, that, even if only a single organic product of nature were given to us, we, according to the constitution of our cognitive faculty, can think no other ground for it than that of a cause of nature itself (whether of the whole of nature or even only of this piece of it) that contains the causality for it through understanding; a principle of judging by which we are indeed brought no further in the explanation of the natural things and their origin, but which nevertheless opens up to us some prospect beyond nature, in order perhaps to be able to determine more closely the otherwise so fruitless concept of an original being.

Now I say: physico-theology, however far it may also be driven, can yet disclose to us nothing of a ***final end*** of creation; for it does not even reach so far as the question after it. It can therefore indeed justify the concept of an intelligent world-cause as a concept that is subjectively, for the constitution of our cognitive faculty alone, suited to the possibility of the things that we can make comprehensible to ourselves according to ends, but it cannot determine this concept further either in a theoretical or in a practical respect; and its attempt does not attain its {{ 402 }} aim of grounding a theology, but rather remains always only a physical teleology: because the relation of ends in it is and must always be considered only as conditioned within nature; hence it cannot even so much as bring into question the end for which nature itself exists (the ground of which must be sought outside nature), on whose determinate idea, however, the determinate concept of that higher intelligent world-cause, hence the possibility of a theology, depends.

For what the things in the world are useful to one another; for what the manifold in a thing is good for this thing itself; how one even has ground to assume that nothing in the world is in vain, but rather everything is good for something ***in nature***, under the condition that certain things (as ends) should exist, whereby therefore our reason has, for the power of judgment, no other principle within its capacity for the possibility of the object of its unavoidable teleological judging {{{ 438 }}} than that of subordinating the mechanism of nature to the architectonic of an intelligent author of the world: all this the teleological consideration of the world accomplishes very splendidly and to the utmost admiration. But because the data, hence the principles, for ***determining*** that concept of an intelligent world-cause (as the highest artist) are merely empirical: they permit no inference to any further properties than those that experience reveals to us in the effects of it, which, since it can never grasp the whole of nature as a system, {{ 403 }} must often run into grounds of proof that (in appearance) contradict that concept and one another, but can never, even if we were capable of surveying empirically the whole system too, insofar as it concerns mere nature, raise us above nature to the end of its existence itself, and thereby to the determinate concept of that higher intelligence.

If one makes small the task with whose resolution a physico-theology is concerned, then its resolution seems easy. For if one squanders the concept of a ***deity*** on every intelligent being thought by us, of which there may be one or several, which has many and very great, but precisely not all the properties that are required for the grounding of a nature in agreement with the greatest possible end in general; or if one holds it to be nothing to supplement, in a theory, the lack of what the grounds of proof accomplish by arbitrary additions, and, where one has ground only to assume ***much*** perfection (and what is much for us?), holds oneself there entitled to presuppose ***all possible*** perfection: then physical teleology makes important claims to the fame of grounding a theology. But if it is demanded to indicate what then drives us, and moreover entitles us, to make those supplements: then we shall seek in vain, in the principles of the theoretical use of reason, which thoroughly demands that for the explanation of an {{ 404 }} object of experience no more properties be ascribed to it than empirical data for their possibility are to be encountered, a ground for our justification. On closer examination we would see that really an idea of a highest being, which rests on an entirely different use of reason (the practical), lies at the basis _a priori_ in us, which drives us to supplement the defective representation of a physical teleology of the original ground of the ends in nature up to the {{{ 439 }}} concept of a deity; and we would not falsely imagine that we had brought about this idea, and with it a theology, through the theoretical use of reason on the physical acquaintance with the world, still less that we had proved its reality.

One cannot reckon it so highly to the blame of the ancients if they thought their gods as very manifoldly different, partly according to their faculty, partly according to their intentions and volitions, but all of them, not excepting even their chief, as still always limited in a human manner. For when they considered the arrangement and course of things in nature, they indeed found ground enough to assume something more than the mechanical as the cause of it and to suspect, behind the machinery of this world, intentions of certain higher causes which they could think only as superhuman. But because they {{ 405 }} encountered the good and the evil, the purposive and the contrapurposive in it, at least for our insight, very mixed, and could not allow themselves to assume, in secret, wise and beneficent ends lying nonetheless at the basis, of which they yet saw no proof, on behalf of the arbitrary idea of a most perfect author: their judgment about the supreme world-cause could hardly turn out otherwise, insofar namely as they proceeded quite consistently according to maxims of the merely theoretical use of reason. Others, who wanted to be theologians at the same time as physicists, thought to find satisfaction for reason in this, that they provided for the absolute unity of the principle of natural things, which reason demands, by means of the idea of a being in which, as sole substance, those things would be only determinations inhering throughout: which substance would indeed not be the cause of the world through understanding, but in which yet, as in a subject, all the understanding of the world-beings would be encountered; consequently a being which would indeed bring forth nothing according to ends, but in which yet all things, on account of the unity of the subject of which they are merely determinations, even without end and intention, necessarily had to relate purposively to one another. Thus they introduced the idealism of final causes: in that they transformed the so difficult-to-extract unity of a multitude of purposively connected substances, instead of the causal dependence ***on one***, into that of inherence ***in one*** {{ 406 }} substance; which system, considered subsequently from the side of the inhering world-beings, as ***pantheism***, and from the side of the alone-subsisting subject as the original being (later on), as ***Spinozism***, did not so much resolve the {{{ 440 }}} question of the first ground of the purposiveness of nature, as rather declare it null and void, in that the latter concept, robbed of all its reality, was made into the mere misinterpretation of a universal ontological concept of a thing in general.

According to merely theoretical principles of the use of reason (on which physico-theology alone grounds itself), the concept of a deity that would be sufficient for our teleological judging of nature can therefore never be brought forth. For we either declare all teleology to be a mere deception of the power of judgment in the judging of the causal connection of things and flee to the sole principle of a mere mechanism of nature, which, on account of the unity of the substance of which nature is nothing but the manifold of its determinations, merely seems to us to contain a universal relation to ends; or, if we want, instead of this idealism of final causes, to remain attached to the principle of the realism of this special kind of causality, then we may lay many intelligent original beings, or only a single one, at the basis of the natural ends: as soon as we have at hand, for the grounding of the concept of it, nothing but principles of experience taken from the actual connection of ends {{ 407 }} in the world, we can on the one hand find no remedy against the discordance which nature, with regard to the unity of ends, sets up in many examples, and on the other hand we can never draw from it, for any theology usable in any way (theoretically or practically), the concept of a single intelligent cause determinately enough, just as we bring it forth, entitled by mere experience.

Physical teleology does indeed drive us to seek a theology, but it can bring forth none, however far we may also trace nature through experience and may come to the aid of the connection of ends discovered in it through ideas of reason (which, for physical problems, must be theoretical). What does it help, one will rightly complain, that we lay a great, a for us immeasurable understanding at the basis of all these arrangements and let it order this world according to intentions? if nature tells us nothing, nor can ever tell us anything, of the final aim, without which we can yet make for ourselves no common point of reference of all these natural ends, no sufficient teleological principle, partly to cognize the ends together in a system, partly to make for ourselves of the supreme understanding, as cause of such a nature, a {{{ 441 }}} concept that could serve as a standard for our power of judgment reflecting teleologically about it. {{ 408 }} I would then indeed have an ***artistic understanding*** for scattered ends; but no ***wisdom*** for a final end, which yet really must contain the determining ground of the former. But in the lack of a final end, which only pure reason can provide _a priori_ (because all ends in the world are empirically conditioned and can contain nothing but what is good for this or for that as a contingent intention, not what is absolutely good), and which alone would teach me: what properties, what degree, and what relation of the supreme cause of nature I have to think for myself in order to judge it as a teleological system; how and with what right may I there expand at will my very limited concept of that original understanding, which I can ground on my slight acquaintance with the world, of the power of this original being to bring its ideas to actuality, of its will to do so, etc., and supplement it up to the idea of an all-wise infinite being? This would, if it were to happen theoretically, presuppose omniscience in myself, in order to see into the ends of nature in their whole connection and, on top of that, to be able to think all other possible plans, in comparison with which the present one would have to be judged with ground as the best. For without this completed cognition of the effect I can infer no determinate concept of the supreme cause, which can be encountered only in that of an intelligence infinite in every {{ 409 }} respect, i.e., the concept of a deity, and bring about a foundation for theology.

We can therefore, with all possible expansion of physical teleology, well say, in accordance with the above-cited principle: that, according to the constitution and the principles of our cognitive faculty, we can think nature, in its purposive arrangements that have become known to us, in no other way than as the product of an understanding to which it is subjected. But whether this understanding may have had a final aim with the whole of it and the production of it (which would then not lie in the nature of the world of sense): that theoretical inquiry into nature can never disclose to us; rather it remains, with all acquaintance with nature, undecided whether that supreme cause is the original ground of it at all according to a final end, and not rather through an understanding determined, by the mere necessity of its nature, to the production of certain forms {{{ 442 }}} (by analogy with what we call, in the case of animals, the artistic instinct): without its being necessary, on that account, to ascribe to it even so much as wisdom, much less the highest wisdom combined with all the other properties required for the perfection of its product.

Thus physico-theology is a misunderstood physical teleology, {{ 410 }} usable only as a preparation (propaedeutic) for theology, and sufficient for this aim only through the addition of some other principle on which it can support itself, but not in itself, as its name would indicate.
