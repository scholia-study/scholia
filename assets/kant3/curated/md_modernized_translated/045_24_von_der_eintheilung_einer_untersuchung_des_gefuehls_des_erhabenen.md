## {{{ 247 }}} {{ 79 }} § 24. On the division of an investigation of the feeling of the sublime

As concerns how the moments of the aesthetic judging of objects fall to be divided in their bearing on the feeling of the sublime, the analytic is able to run its course according to the same principle as was followed in the dissection of judgments of taste. For, being a judgment of the aesthetic reflecting power of judgment, the satisfaction in the sublime, quite as much as that in the beautiful, must present itself as universally valid in respect of ***quantity***, as free from all interest in respect of ***quality***, as subjective purposiveness in respect of ***relation***, and, in respect of ***modality***, this last as necessary. In this the method will accordingly not part company with the one in the earlier section: unless one chooses to make something of the fact that there, where the aesthetic judgment bore upon the form of the object, we set out from the examination of quality; whereas here, in view of the formlessness that can attach to whatever we term sublime, we shall set out from quantity, as the first moment of the aesthetic judgment upon the sublime: though the ground of this may be gathered from the preceding §.

But the analysis of the sublime calls for a division that the analysis of the beautiful has no need of, namely its partition into the ***mathematically-*** and the ***dynamically-sublime***.

For, since the feeling of the sublime carries within itself, as its defining trait, an ***agitation*** of the mind coupled with the judging of the {{ 80 }} object, whereas taste for the beautiful presupposes and keeps the mind in ***restful*** contemplation; and since this agitation is nonetheless to be judged subjectively purposive (for the sublime does please): it is, through the imagination, referred either to the ***faculty of cognition*** or to the ***faculty of desire***, though in each kind of reference the purposiveness of the given representation is to be judged only in view of these ***faculties*** (without any end or interest): so that the former is credited to the object as a ***mathematical***, the latter as a ***dynamical*** disposition of the imagination, and the object is on that ground represented as sublime in the aforesaid twofold way.
