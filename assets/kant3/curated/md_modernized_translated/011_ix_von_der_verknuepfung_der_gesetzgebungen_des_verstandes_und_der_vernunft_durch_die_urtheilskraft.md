## {{{ 195 }}} IX. On the connection of the legislations of the understanding and of reason through the power of judgment

It is for nature, taken as an object of the senses, that the understanding gives the law _a priori_, so as to yield a theoretical cognition of nature within a possible experience. Reason, in turn, legislates _a priori_ for freedom and for freedom's own causality, as the supersensible in the subject, with an unconditionally practical cognition in view. The domain that falls under the concept of nature by virtue of the first legislation, and the domain that falls under the concept of freedom by virtue of the second, are wholly insulated from every reciprocal influence they might otherwise exert on one another in their own right (each according to its fundamental laws), by the immense gulf that divides the supersensible from appearances. Nothing is settled by the concept of freedom as regards a theoretical cognition of nature, and just as little is settled by the concept of nature as regards the practical laws of freedom: to this extent it remains impossible {{ LIV }} to throw a bridge across from the one domain over to the other. — Yet although the determining grounds of the causality that accords with the concept of freedom (together with the practical rule it holds) have no place in nature, and although what is sensible cannot determine the supersensible within the subject, the converse is nonetheless possible (not, admittedly, as regards the cognition of nature, but certainly as regards the consequences that flow from the former into the latter) and is already implicit in the concept of a causality operating through freedom, whose ***effect*** is to occur in the world in conformity with that causality's own formal laws — even though the word ***cause***, when predicated of the supersensible, denotes only the ***ground*** that determines the causality of natural things toward an effect in keeping with their own natural laws, yet determines it at the same time in agreement with the formal principle of reason's own laws, a possibility that admittedly cannot be grasped, though the objection that alleges a purported contradiction lodged in it can be sufficiently refuted[^*]. — The effect that follows upon the concept of freedom is that {{ LV }} {{{ 196 }}} final end which (or whose appearance in the sensible world) ought to exist, and for which the condition of its possibility is presupposed as lying within nature (that is, in the subject's own nature as a sensible being, namely as a human being). The power of judgment, which presupposes this _a priori_ and with no eye to the practical, puts into our hands, within the concept of a ***purposiveness*** of nature, that mediating concept standing between nature's concepts and the concept of freedom — the concept that opens the way from the pure theoretical over to the pure practical, from conformity to law according to the former to the final end according to the latter; for through it there is cognized the possibility of the final end, a possibility that can be realized nowhere but in nature and in accord with nature's laws.

By the very possibility of the laws it prescribes _a priori_ to nature, the understanding supplies a proof that nature is cognized by us merely as appearance {{ LVI }}, and so at the same time gives an indication of a supersensible substratum underlying it, while leaving that substratum entirely ***undetermined***. The power of judgment, by means of its _a priori_ principle for judging nature according to its possible particular laws, secures for that supersensible substratum (within us as much as outside us) ***determinability through the intellectual faculty***. Reason, however, bestows upon this same substratum the ***determination*** through its practical law _a priori_; and thus it is the power of judgment that makes possible the passage out of the domain of the concept of nature and into that of the concept of freedom.

Taking the powers of the soul at large, insofar as they are considered as higher powers — that is, as powers that contain an autonomy of their own — the ***faculty of cognition*** (the theoretical faculty, directed at nature) finds in the understanding that power which holds the ***constitutive*** principles _a priori_; the ***feeling of pleasure and displeasure*** finds this in the power of judgment, taken apart from such concepts and sensations as bear on determining the faculty of desire {{{ 197 }}} and could thereby be practical without mediation; and the ***faculty of desire*** finds it in reason, practical as reason is without any pleasure intervening, from whatever quarter that pleasure might come, and determining for this faculty, in its capacity as higher power, the final end that brings along with it the pure intellectual satisfaction taken in the object. — The power of judgment's concept of a purposiveness of nature is still one that belongs to the concepts of nature {{ LVII }}, serving there merely as a regulative principle for the faculty of cognition, even though the aesthetic judgment about certain objects (whether of nature or of art), which occasions that concept, is a constitutive principle as far as the feeling of pleasure or displeasure is concerned. The spontaneity that is at work in the interplay of the cognitive powers, whose mutual accord furnishes the ground of this pleasure, is what makes the concept in question fit to mediate the linkage of the domains of the concept of nature with that of freedom in respect of their consequences, since this spontaneity at once fosters the mind's receptivity to moral feeling. — The following table may make it easier to survey all the higher powers in their systematic unity[^**].

[^*]: One among the various imagined contradictions in this thoroughgoing distinction of natural causality from causality through freedom is the following reproach directed at it: that when I speak of ***obstacles*** which nature sets in the path of causality according to laws of freedom (the moral ones), or of nature's ***promotion*** of such causality, I am after all conceding to the former an ***influence*** upon the latter. But if one is merely willing to grasp what has been said, this misconstrual is quite easy to guard against. The resistance, or the furtherance, obtains not between nature and freedom as such, but between nature as appearance and the ***effects*** of freedom as appearances in the world of sense; and even the ***causality*** of freedom (that of pure and practical reason) is itself the ***causality*** of one of those natural causes subordinate to it (of the subject regarded as a human being and hence as appearance), of whose ***determination*** the intelligible that is thought under freedom contains the ground in a way that is, for the rest, as inexplicable as is that very thing which makes up the supersensible substratum of nature.
[^**]: It has been regarded as a cause for concern that my divisions in pure philosophy turn out to be threefold almost every time. But that lies in the very nature of the subject matter. If a division is to be carried out _a priori_, it will be either ***analytic***, in accordance with the principle of contradiction, in which case it is invariably twofold (_quodlibet ens est aut A aut non A_). Or else it is ***synthetic***; and if in that event it is to be conducted from ***concepts*** _a priori_ (and not, as happens in mathematics, from the intuition that corresponds _a priori_ to the concept), then, following what is demanded for synthetic unity as such — namely 1) a condition, 2) something conditioned, and 3) the concept that springs from uniting the conditioned with its condition — the division must of necessity be a trichotomy.
