## {{{ 195 }}} IX. On the connection of the legislations of the understanding and of reason through the power of judgment

The understanding is legislative _a priori_ for nature, taken as an object of the senses, toward a theoretical cognition of it in a possible experience. Reason, for its part, is legislative _a priori_ for freedom and for its own causality, as the supersensible within the subject, toward an unconditionally practical cognition. The domain that falls under the concept of nature by virtue of the first legislation, and the domain that falls under the concept of freedom by virtue of the second, are wholly insulated from every reciprocal influence they might otherwise exert on one another in their own right (each according to its fundamental laws), by the immense gulf that divides the supersensible from appearances. The concept of freedom determines nothing with regard to the theoretical cognition of nature; the concept of nature equally determines nothing with regard to the practical laws of freedom: and to that extent it is impossible {{ LIV }} to build a bridge across from the one domain into the other. — Yet although the determining grounds of the causality that accords with the concept of freedom (together with the practical rule it holds) have no place in nature, and although what is sensible cannot determine the supersensible within the subject, the converse is nonetheless possible (not, admittedly, as regards the cognition of nature, but certainly as regards the consequences that flow from the former into the latter) and is already implicit in the concept of a causality operating through freedom, whose ***effect*** is to occur in the world in conformity with that causality's own formal laws — even though the word ***cause***, when predicated of the supersensible, denotes only the ***ground*** that determines the causality of natural things toward an effect in keeping with their own natural laws, yet determines it at the same time in agreement with the formal principle of reason's own laws, a possibility that admittedly cannot be grasped, though the objection that alleges a purported contradiction lodged in it can be sufficiently refuted[^*]. — The effect that follows upon the concept of freedom is that {{ LV }} {{{ 196 }}} final end which (or whose appearance in the sensible world) ought to exist, and for which the condition of its possibility is presupposed as lying within nature (that is, in the subject's own nature as a sensible being, namely as a human being). That which presupposes this condition _a priori_ and without any regard to the practical — namely the power of judgment — provides the mediating concept that lies between the concepts of nature and the concept of freedom, a concept that renders possible the passage from the pure theoretical to the pure practical, from conformity to law under the former over to the final end under the latter, and it does so in the concept of nature's ***purposiveness***; for it is thereby that the possibility of the final end, which can become actual solely in nature and in concord with nature's laws, comes to be cognized.

Through the possibility of its _a priori_ laws for nature, the understanding furnishes a proof that we cognize nature merely as appearance {{ LVI }}, and thereby likewise points to a supersensible substratum of it, though it leaves that substratum wholly ***undetermined***. The power of judgment, by means of its _a priori_ principle for judging nature according to its possible particular laws, secures for that supersensible substratum (within us as much as outside us) ***determinability through the intellectual faculty***. Reason, by contrast, confers upon this very same substratum, through its practical law _a priori_, the ***determination***; and in this way the power of judgment makes the transition from the domain of the concept of nature over into that of the concept of freedom possible.

With respect to the powers of the soul generally, insofar as they are regarded as higher powers, that is, as ones harboring an autonomy, it is the understanding that, for the ***faculty of cognition*** (the theoretical power concerned with nature), contains _a priori_ the ***constitutive*** principles; for the ***feeling of pleasure and displeasure*** it is the power of judgment, taken independently of concepts and sensations that bear on the determining of the faculty of desire {{{ 197 }}} and might on that account become practical without mediation; for the ***faculty of desire***, reason, which is practical without the intervention of any pleasure whatsoever, from wherever it may spring, and which, as the higher power, determines for it the final end that at once brings with it the pure intellectual satisfaction taken in the object. — The concept that the power of judgment forms of a purposiveness of nature still belongs among the concepts of nature {{ LVII }}, yet only as a regulative principle of the faculty of cognition, notwithstanding that the aesthetic judgment upon certain objects (whether of nature or of art), which prompts that concept, is a constitutive principle with respect to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure. The spontaneity that is at work in the interplay of the cognitive powers, whose mutual accord furnishes the ground of this pleasure, is what makes the concept in question fit to mediate the linkage of the domains of the concept of nature with that of freedom in respect of their consequences, since this spontaneity at once fosters the mind's receptivity to moral feeling. — The following table may make it easier to survey all the higher powers in their systematic unity[^**].

[^*]: One among the various imagined contradictions in this thoroughgoing distinction of natural causality from causality through freedom is the following reproach directed at it: that when I speak of ***obstacles*** which nature sets in the path of causality according to laws of freedom (the moral ones), or of nature's ***promotion*** of such causality, I am after all conceding to the former an ***influence*** upon the latter. But if one is merely willing to grasp what has been said, this misconstrual is quite easy to guard against. The resistance, or the furtherance, obtains not between nature and freedom as such, but between nature as appearance and the ***effects*** of freedom as appearances in the world of sense; and even the ***causality*** of freedom (that of pure and practical reason) is itself the ***causality*** of one of those natural causes subordinate to it (of the subject regarded as a human being and hence as appearance), of whose ***determination*** the intelligible that is thought under freedom contains the ground in a way that is, for the rest, as inexplicable as is that very thing which makes up the supersensible substratum of nature.
[^**]: It has been regarded as a cause for concern that my divisions in pure philosophy turn out to be threefold almost every time. But that lies in the very nature of the subject matter. If a division is to be carried out _a priori_, it will be either ***analytic***, in accordance with the principle of contradiction, in which case it is invariably twofold (_quodlibet ens est aut A aut non A_). Or else it is ***synthetic***; and if in that event it is to be conducted from ***concepts*** _a priori_ (and not, as happens in mathematics, from the intuition that corresponds _a priori_ to the concept), then, following what is demanded for synthetic unity as such — namely 1) a condition, 2) something conditioned, and 3) the concept that springs from uniting the conditioned with its condition — the division must of necessity be a trichotomy.
