## {{{ 195 }}} IX. On the Combination of the Legislations of Understanding and Reason through the Power of Judgment

The understanding is _a priori_ legislative for nature, as object of the senses, for a theoretical cognition of it in a possible experience. Reason is _a priori_ legislative for freedom and its own causality, as the supersensible in the subject, for an unconditionally practical cognition. The domain of the concept of nature under the one legislation and that of the concept of freedom under the other are entirely cut off from all mutual influence that they could have on each other for themselves (each according to its fundamental laws) by the great gulf that separates the supersensible from the appearances. The concept of freedom determines nothing with regard to the theoretical cognition of nature; the concept of nature likewise nothing with regard to the practical laws of freedom: and to that extent it is not possible {{ LIV }} to throw a bridge from the one domain over to the other. — But although the determining grounds of causality in accordance with the concept of freedom (and the practical rule that it contains) are not situated in nature, and the sensible cannot determine the supersensible in the subject: this is nevertheless conversely possible (not indeed with regard to the cognition of nature, but yet with regard to the consequences of the former upon the latter) and is already contained in the concept of a causality through freedom, whose ***effect*** is to take place in the world in accordance with these its formal laws, although the word ***cause***, used of the supersensible, signifies only the ***ground*** for determining the causality of the things of nature to an effect in accordance with their own natural laws, yet at the same time also in unison with the formal principle of the laws of reason, the possibility of which cannot indeed be comprehended, but the objection of a supposed contradiction that might be found in it can be sufficiently refuted[^*]. — The effect in accordance with the concept of freedom is the {{ LV }} {{{ 196 }}} final end, which (or its appearance in the world of sense) is to exist, for which the condition of its possibility in nature (of the subject as a being of sense, namely as a human being) is presupposed. That which presupposes this _a priori_ and without regard to the practical, namely the power of judgment, provides the mediating concept between the concepts of nature and the concept of freedom, which makes possible the transition from the pure theoretical to the pure practical, from lawfulness in accordance with the former to the final end in accordance with the latter, in the concept of a ***purposiveness*** of nature; for through this the possibility of the final end, which can become actual only in nature and in agreement with its laws, is cognized.

The understanding, through the possibility of its laws _a priori_ for nature, gives a proof that nature is cognized by us only as appearance, {{ LVI }} and hence at the same time an indication of a supersensible substratum of it, but leaves this entirely ***undetermined***. The power of judgment, through its principle _a priori_ for the appraisal of nature in accordance with possible particular laws of it, provides for its supersensible substratum (within us as well as outside us) ***determinability through the intellectual faculty***. But reason gives to this same substratum, through its practical law _a priori_, the ***determination***; and thus the power of judgment makes possible the transition from the domain of the concept of nature to that of the concept of freedom.

With regard to the powers of the soul in general, insofar as they are considered as higher ones, i.e., as such as contain an autonomy, for the ***faculty of cognition*** (the theoretical cognition of nature) it is the understanding that is the one which contains the ***constitutive*** principles _a priori_; for the ***feeling of pleasure and displeasure*** it is the power of judgment, independent of concepts and sensations that relate to the determination of the faculty of desire {{{ 197 }}} and could thereby be immediately practical; for the ***faculty of desire*** it is reason, which is practical without the mediation of any pleasure whatever, from whatever source it may come, and which determines for it, as the higher faculty, the final end, which at the same time carries with it the pure intellectual satisfaction in the object. — The concept of the power of judgment concerning a purposiveness of nature still belongs to the concepts of nature, {{ LVII }} but only as a regulative principle of the faculty of cognition, although the aesthetic judgment about certain objects (of nature or of art) which occasions it is, with regard to the feeling of pleasure or displeasure, a constitutive principle. The spontaneity in the play of the faculties of cognition, whose accord contains the ground of this pleasure, makes the concept in question suitable for mediating the combination of the domains of the concept of nature with the concept of freedom in their consequences, in that this accord at the same time promotes the receptivity of the mind for the moral feeling. — The following table can facilitate the overview of all the higher faculties according to their systematic unity[^**].

[^*]: One of the various supposed contradictions in this complete distinction of the causality of nature from that through freedom is the one in which one makes the reproach against it: that, if I speak of ***hindrances*** that nature places in the way of causality in accordance with laws of freedom (the moral ones), or of its ***promotion*** by the same, I am after all conceding an ***influence*** of the former upon the latter. But if one is only willing to understand what has been said, this misinterpretation is very easy to prevent. The resistance or the promotion is not between nature and freedom, but between the former as appearance and the ***effects*** of the latter as appearances in the world of sense; and even the ***causality*** of freedom (of pure and practical reason) is the ***causality*** of one of those subordinate natural causes (the subject, considered as a human being and consequently as appearance), of whose ***determination*** the intelligible that is thought under freedom contains the ground in a way that is otherwise (just as in the case of that very thing which constitutes the supersensible substratum of nature) inexplicable.
[^**]: It has been found problematic that my divisions in pure philosophy almost always turn out to be tripartite. But that lies in the nature of the matter. If a division is to be made _a priori_, then it will either be ***analytic***, in accordance with the principle of contradiction; and then it is always bipartite (_quodlibet ens est aut A aut non A_). Or it is ***synthetic***; and if in this case it is to be conducted from ***concepts*** _a priori_ (not, as in mathematics, from the intuition corresponding _a priori_ to the concept), then, in accordance with what is required for synthetic unity in general, namely 1) condition, 2) something conditioned, 3) the concept that arises from the union of the conditioned with its condition, the division must necessarily be a trichotomy.
