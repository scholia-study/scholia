## {{ XXIX }} V. The Principle of the Formal Purposiveness of Nature is a Transcendental Principle of the Power of Judgment

A transcendental principle is that through which the universal condition _a priori_ is represented under which alone things can become objects of our cognition in general. By contrast, a principle is called metaphysical if it represents the condition _a priori_ under which alone objects whose concept must be given empirically can be further determined _a priori_. Thus the principle of the cognition of bodies as substances and as alterable substances is transcendental if it is thereby said that their alteration must have a cause; but it is metaphysical if it is thereby said that their alteration must have an ***external*** cause: because in the former case the body need only be thought through ontological predicates (pure concepts of the understanding), e.g., as substance, in order to cognize the proposition _a priori_; whereas in the second the empirical concept of a body (as a movable thing in space) must be laid at the basis of this proposition, but then it can be seen completely _a priori_ that the latter predicate (of motion only through an external cause) belongs to the body. — Thus, as I shall presently show, the principle of the purposiveness of nature (in the manifoldness of its empirical {{ XXX }} laws) is a transcendental principle. For the concept of the objects, insofar as they are thought as standing under this principle, is only the pure concept of objects of possible cognition of experience {{{ 182 }}} in general and contains nothing empirical. By contrast, the principle of the practical purposiveness that must be thought in the idea of the ***determination*** of a free ***will*** would be a metaphysical principle: because the concept of a faculty of desire as a will must nevertheless be given empirically (does not belong to the transcendental predicates). But both principles are nevertheless not empirical, but principles _a priori_: because for the combination of the predicate with the empirical concept of the subject of their judgments no further experience is required, but rather the former can be seen completely _a priori_.

That the concept of a purposiveness of nature belongs among the transcendental principles can be sufficiently seen from the maxims of the power of judgment that are laid at the basis of the investigation of nature _a priori_, and that nevertheless concern nothing but the possibility of experience, hence of the cognition of nature, but not merely as nature in general, but as nature determined through a manifoldness of particular laws. — They occur, as sentences of metaphysical wisdom, on the occasion of various rules whose necessity {{ XXXI }} one cannot demonstrate from concepts, often enough in the course of this science, though only scattered. "Nature takes the shortest way (_lex parsimoniae_); it nevertheless makes no leap, neither in the sequence of its alterations nor in the juxtaposition of specifically different forms (_lex continui in natura_); its great manifoldness in empirical laws is nevertheless unity under few principles (_principia praeter necessitatem non sunt multiplicanda_)"; and the like.

But if one thinks to specify the origin of these fundamental propositions and attempts it by the psychological route, then this is entirely contrary to their sense. For they do not say what happens, i.e., according to which rule our powers of cognition actually carry on their play, and how it is judged, but rather how it ought to be judged; and this logical objective necessity does not emerge if the principles are merely empirical. Thus the purposiveness of nature for our faculties of cognition and their use, which evidently shines forth from them, is a transcendental principle of judgments and therefore also requires a transcendental deduction, by means of which the ground for judging in this way must be sought in the sources of cognition _a priori_.

We find, namely, in the grounds of the possibility of an experience {{{ 183 }}} first of all, to be sure, something necessary, namely the universal {{ XXXII }} laws without which nature in general (as object of the senses) cannot be thought; and these rest on the categories, applied to the formal conditions of all intuition possible for us, insofar as it is likewise given _a priori_. Under these laws now the power of judgment is determining; for it has nothing to do but to subsume under given laws. E.g., the understanding says: All alteration has its cause (universal law of nature); the transcendental power of judgment now has nothing further to do than to specify the condition of subsumption under the presented concept of the understanding _a priori_: and that is the succession of the determinations of one and the same thing. For nature in general now (as object of possible experience) that law is cognized as absolutely necessary. — But now the objects of empirical cognition are determined in many ways besides that formal time-condition, or, as far as one can judge _a priori_, are determinable, so that specifically different natures, besides what they have in common as belonging to nature in general, can still be causes in infinitely manifold ways; and each of these kinds must (according to the concept of a cause in general) have its rule, which is law, hence carries necessity with it: even though, according to the constitution and limits of our faculties of cognition, we do not at all have insight into this necessity. Thus {{ XXXIII }} we must, with regard to its merely empirical laws, think in nature a possibility of infinitely manifold empirical laws that are nevertheless contingent for our insight (cannot be cognized _a priori_); and with regard to these we appraise the unity of nature according to empirical laws and the possibility of the unity of experience (as a system according to empirical laws) as contingent. But because such a unity must nevertheless necessarily be presupposed and assumed, since otherwise no thoroughgoing connection of empirical cognitions into a whole of experience would take place, in that the universal laws of nature do indeed furnish such a connection among things in accordance with their genus, as things of nature in general, but not specifically, as such particular beings of nature: so the power of judgment must assume it for its own use as a principle _a priori_ that what is contingent for human insight in the particular (empirical) laws of nature nevertheless contains a lawful unity, not to be fathomed by us, to be sure, but yet {{{ 184 }}} thinkable, in the combination of its manifold into an experience possible in itself. Consequently, because the lawful unity in a combination that we cognize, to be sure, in accordance with a necessary aim (a need of the understanding), but at the same time as contingent in itself, is represented as purposiveness of the objects (here of nature): {{ XXXIV }} so the power of judgment, which with regard to things under possible (still to be discovered) empirical laws is merely reflecting, must think nature with regard to the latter according to a ***principle of purposiveness*** for our faculty of cognition, which is then expressed in the above maxims of the power of judgment. This transcendental concept of a purposiveness of nature is now neither a concept of nature nor a concept of freedom, because it attributes nothing at all to the object (nature), but rather represents only the single way in which we must proceed in reflection on the objects of nature with a view to a thoroughgoingly connected experience, consequently a subjective principle (maxim) of the power of judgment; hence we are also gladdened (properly relieved of a need), just as if it were a happy chance favoring our aim, when we encounter such a systematic unity under merely empirical laws: even though we had necessarily to assume that there is such a unity, without being able, however, to have insight into it and to prove it.

In order to convince oneself of the correctness of this deduction of the concept at hand and of the necessity of assuming it as a transcendental principle of cognition, let one consider only the magnitude of the task: to make a connected {{ XXXV }} experience out of given perceptions of a nature containing an at all events infinite manifoldness of empirical laws, a task that lies _a priori_ in our understanding. The understanding is, to be sure, _a priori_ in possession of universal laws of nature, without which nature could not be an object of an experience at all: but it still also requires in addition a certain order of nature in its particular rules, which can become known to it only empirically, and which with regard to it are contingent. These rules, without which no progress from the universal analogy of a possible experience in general to the particular would take place, it must think for itself as laws (i.e., as necessary): because they would otherwise constitute no order of nature, even though it does not cognize their necessity, or could ever have insight into it. Thus although with regard to these {{{ 185 }}} (objects) it can determine nothing _a priori_, it must nevertheless, in order to pursue these so-called empirical laws, lay at the basis of all reflection on it a principle _a priori_, namely that a cognizable order of nature is possible according to them, a principle of which sort the following propositions express: that there is in it a subordination of genera and species comprehensible for us; that the former approach one another in turn according to a common principle, so that a transition from one to the other and thereby to a higher genus is possible; that, since for the specific difference of the effects of nature it initially seems unavoidable to our understanding that just as many different kinds of causality {{ XXXVI }} must be assumed, they may nevertheless stand under a small number of principles with the seeking out of which we have to occupy ourselves, etc. This accord of nature with our faculty of cognition is presupposed _a priori_ by the power of judgment on behalf of its reflection on it according to its empirical laws, while the understanding at the same time objectively acknowledges it as contingent, and only the power of judgment attributes it to nature as transcendental purposiveness (in relation to the faculty of cognition of the subject): because without presupposing this we would have no order of nature according to empirical laws, hence no guiding thread for an experience and investigation of it to be undertaken with these in all their manifoldness.

For it is quite conceivable: that notwithstanding all the uniformity of the things of nature according to the universal laws, without which the form of a cognition of experience in general would not take place at all, the specific difference of the empirical laws of nature together with their effects could nevertheless be so great that it would be impossible for our understanding to discover in it a comprehensible order, to divide its products into genera and species, in order to use the principles of the explanation and the understanding of the one also for the explanation and comprehension of the other, and to make a connected experience out of a material so confused for us {{ XXXVII }} (properly only infinitely manifold, not commensurate with our faculty of comprehension).

The power of judgment thus has in itself a principle _a priori_ for the possibility of nature too, but only in a subjective respect, through which it prescribes a law, not to nature (as autonomy), but to itself (as heautonomy) {{{ 186 }}} for reflection on nature, which one could call the ***law of the specification of nature*** with regard to its empirical laws, which it does not cognize in nature _a priori_, but rather assumes on behalf of an order of nature cognizable for our understanding in the division that it makes of its universal laws, when it wants to subordinate to these a manifoldness of the particular ones. Thus if one says: nature specifies its universal laws according to the principle of purposiveness for our faculty of cognition, i.e., to the suitability with the human understanding in its necessary business of finding the universal for the particular which perception offers it and connection in the unity of the principle in turn for the different (which is, to be sure, universal for each species): then one thereby neither prescribes a law to nature nor learns one from it by observation (although that principle can be confirmed by the latter). For it is not a principle of the determining {{ XXXVIII }} but merely of the reflecting power of judgment; one wants only that, however nature may be arranged according to its universal laws, one must absolutely track down its empirical laws in accordance with that principle and the maxims grounded on it, because only so far as that takes place can we make headway with the use of our understanding in experience and acquire cognition.
