## {{ XXIX }} V. The principle of nature's formal purposiveness is a transcendental principle of the power of judgment

We call a principle transcendental when it represents that universal condition, holding _a priori_, apart from which nothing whatever could come to be an object of our cognition. A principle counts as metaphysical, on the other hand, when the _a priori_ condition it sets out is one under which objects — objects whose very concept has to be supplied by experience — admit of still further _a priori_ determination. Take, for example, the principle by which we cognize bodies as substances, and indeed as substances subject to alteration: it is transcendental insofar as it states that any alteration of theirs must have some cause; it turns metaphysical, however, once it states that such alteration requires an ***external*** cause — the reason being that, in the one case, the body need only be conceived through ontological predicates (that is, pure concepts of the understanding), say as a substance, if the proposition is to be cognized _a priori_, whereas in the other the empirical concept of a body (namely, of a thing movable in space) has to be laid under the proposition, though even then it can be grasped wholly _a priori_ that this latter predicate — motion arising solely from an outer cause — pertains to the body. — And the principle of nature's purposiveness (amid the multiplicity of its empirical {{ XXX }} laws) is, as I shall show in a moment, transcendental in just this way. For once objects are thought of as falling under this principle, the concept of them is nothing more than the pure concept of things that can be objects of experiential cognition {{{ 182 }}} at all, a concept holding nothing empirical within it. The principle of a practical purposiveness stands in contrast here: since it has to be conceived by way of the idea whereby a free ***will*** receives its ***determination***, it would be a metaphysical principle — for the concept of a faculty of desire, viewed as a will, is bound after all to be given empirically (and thus falls outside the transcendental predicates). Yet neither principle is on that account empirical; both are principles _a priori_ — for joining the predicate to the empirical concept that serves as the subject of their respective judgments calls for no additional experience, that linkage being discernible entirely _a priori_.

One can see well enough that a concept of nature's purposiveness ranks among the transcendental principles by looking to the maxims of the power of judgment — maxims placed _a priori_ beneath our inquiry into nature, which nonetheless aim at nothing beyond the possibility of experience, and so of cognizing nature, yet nature not merely at large but as fixed through a multiplicity of particular laws. — As pronouncements of a metaphysical wisdom, they surface often enough in the course of this science, though only here and there, prompted by various rules whose necessity {{ XXXI }} no derivation from concepts can exhibit. »Nature chooses the shortest route (_lex parsimoniae_); all the same it takes no leap, neither across the succession of its changes nor in setting side by side forms that differ in kind (_lex continui in natura_); and for all the vast variety of its empirical laws, it still forms a unity resting on but few principles (_principia praeter necessitatem non sunt multiplicanda_)«; and so on.

Should one nevertheless set out to pin down where these principles originate, and try to do so along psychological lines, that undertaking runs flatly against everything they mean. For they do not report what in fact takes place — that is, the rule by which our powers of cognition really conduct their play, and how judging actually proceeds — but lay down how one is meant to judge; and were the principles no more than empirical, this logical, objective necessity would never come to light. Nature's purposiveness for our cognitive powers and their employment — a purposiveness that plainly shines forth from them — is therefore a transcendental principle of judgments, and as such it calls for a transcendental deduction too, one whereby the ground for judging in this fashion has to be traced back _a priori_ to the wellsprings of cognition.

Among the grounds that make an experience possible {{{ 183 }}} we do, admittedly, meet with something necessary at the outset — namely those universal {{ XXXII }} laws in whose absence nature at all (as an object of the senses) could not so much as be thought; and these laws rest upon the categories, brought to bear on the formal conditions of every intuition available to us, insofar as that intuition too is furnished _a priori_. Beneath these laws the power of judgment operates determinatively, for its whole task is simply to bring particular cases under laws already given. For example, the understanding declares that every alteration has its cause (a universal law of nature), and the transcendental power of judgment need then do no more than state _a priori_ the condition under which something may be subsumed beneath the concept the understanding has put forward — that condition being the succession of the determinations of one and the very same thing. For nature taken quite generally, then (as a possible object of experience), that law stands cognized as unconditionally necessary. — But over and above that formal condition of time, the objects of empirical cognition are further determined in numerous respects, or at least determinable so far as _a priori_ judgment reaches, so that natures differing in kind, beyond whatever they share as belonging to nature at large, may act as causes in endlessly varied ways; and each such kind, given the concept of a cause as such, has to possess its own rule, which is a law and therefore brings necessity with it — even if, owing to how our cognitive faculties are constituted and bounded, we gain no insight whatever into that necessity. So {{ XXXIII }} within nature, as regards its merely empirical laws, we are obliged to conceive the possibility of an infinite variety of empirical laws that remain, for our insight, contingent all the same (incapable of being cognized _a priori_); and with these in view we appraise nature's unity under empirical laws — together with the very possibility that experience should form a unity (a system governed by empirical laws) — as something contingent. But since a unity of that sort must nonetheless be presupposed and taken for granted as necessary — for otherwise no continuous linkage of empirical cognitions into one whole of experience could arise, seeing that the universal laws of nature do supply such a linkage among things according to their genus, as natural things generally, yet not according to their species, as the particular natural beings they are — the power of judgment is compelled, for its own use, to lay down as an _a priori_ principle that what for human insight is contingent in the particular (empirical) laws of nature does nonetheless harbor a lawful unity — one we cannot fathom, and yet {{{ 184 }}} one that can be thought — in the way their manifold is bound together into an experience possible in itself. It follows that, because this lawful unity — belonging to a combination that we recognize as answering, to be sure, to a necessary aim (a requirement of the understanding), while all the while intrinsically contingent — comes to be represented as a purposiveness of the objects (here, of nature) {{ XXXIV }}, the power of judgment, being merely reflective with respect to things that fall under possible empirical laws still awaiting discovery, has to think nature, as regards those latter laws, under a ***principle of purposiveness*** answering to our faculty of cognition — the very principle voiced in the maxims of the power of judgment set out above. Now this transcendental concept of a purposiveness in nature is neither a concept drawn from nature nor one drawn from freedom, since it attributes nothing at all to the object (to nature); it sets forth only the single manner in which, when we reflect on natural objects with an eye to a thoroughly coherent experience, we are bound to proceed — and so it is a subjective principle (a maxim) of the power of judgment; which is why we too feel gladdened, as though by a fortunate accident that has smiled on our aim (relieved, strictly speaking, of a need), whenever we light upon such a systematic unity among laws that are merely empirical — even though we had of necessity to presuppose that such a unity obtains, without our being in a position either to see into it or to demonstrate it.

To satisfy oneself that this deduction of the concept at hand is sound, and that it must be adopted as a transcendental principle of cognition, one has only to weigh how large the task is: to fashion, out of perceptions given to us, a connected {{ XXXV }} experience of a nature that may well hold an unlimited variety of empirical laws — a task lodged _a priori_ in our understanding. True, the understanding possesses _a priori_ the general laws of nature, and without them nature could be no object of experience at all; yet beyond these it stands in need, besides, of a certain orderliness in nature's more particular rules — rules that can reach it only by way of experience and that, from its own standpoint, are contingent. These rules — barring which there would be no passage at all from the broad analogy of a possible experience as such to the particular case — it must represent to itself as laws (that is, as necessary), since otherwise they would compose no order of nature, even though it neither cognizes their necessity nor could ever come to see it. Hence, although it can settle nothing _a priori_ regarding these {{{ 185 }}} (objects), still, in order to trace out these so-called empirical laws, it must set beneath all reflection upon nature an _a priori_ principle — that a cognizable order of nature is possible in keeping with them — a principle that the following propositions put into words: that nature contains a subordination of genera and species graspable by us; that the genera in turn draw near one another under some shared principle, allowing a passage from one to the next, and thereby up to a higher genus; that, although our understanding is struck at first by the seeming inevitability of having {{ XXXVI }} to assume just as many distinct kinds of causality as there are specific differences among natural effects, these may nonetheless fall under some modest number of principles, whose discovery is to be our concern, and so forth. This concord between nature and our faculty of cognition is what the power of judgment presupposes _a priori_ for the sake of reflecting on nature by its empirical laws, whereas the understanding grants the same concord to be objectively contingent, and it is the power of judgment alone that credits it to nature as a transcendental purposiveness (bearing on the subject's faculty of cognition) — for without presupposing it we should have no order of nature under empirical laws, and hence no clue to guide the experience and inquiry we mean to conduct with those laws across the whole of their variety.

For it is entirely thinkable that, for all the uniformity that natural things display under the universal laws — the laws apart from which the very form of experiential cognition could gain no foothold whatever — the specific diversity of nature's empirical laws, along with their effects, might yet run so deep that our understanding would find it impossible to detect any graspable order there, to sort nature's products into genera and species, and so to apply the principles by which the one is explained and understood to the explaining and grasping of another, and to build a connected experience out of a stuff so bewildering to us {{ XXXVII }} (in truth merely infinite in its variety and out of scale with our power of comprehension).

The power of judgment thus carries within itself an _a priori_ principle for nature's possibility as well, though only in a subjective regard — a principle by which it prescribes a law not to nature (which would be autonomy) but to its own self (which is heautonomy) {{{ 186 }}} for the purpose of reflecting on nature; and this law one might name the ***law of the specification of nature*** with respect to its empirical laws — a law it does not read off nature _a priori_, but adopts on behalf of an order of nature that our understanding can cognize, an order lying in the division it draws among nature's universal laws when it wishes to rank a manifold of particular laws beneath them. So when one says that nature specifies its universal laws according to the principle of purposiveness for our faculty of cognition — that is, so as to suit the human understanding in its necessary business of locating, for the particular that perception offers it, the universal, and of finding, for what is diverse (though universal within each species), a connection once more in the unity of the principle — one is thereby neither dictating a law to nature nor gleaning one from it through observation (however much observation may confirm that principle). For the principle in play is not one belonging to the determining {{ XXXVIII }} power of judgment but solely to the reflecting power of judgment; all that is intended is this: that, however nature may turn out to be arranged under its universal laws, one is bound at every turn to hunt down its empirical laws by that principle and the maxims resting upon it, since only just so far as it holds can we advance, in experience, in the exercise of our understanding and win any cognition.
