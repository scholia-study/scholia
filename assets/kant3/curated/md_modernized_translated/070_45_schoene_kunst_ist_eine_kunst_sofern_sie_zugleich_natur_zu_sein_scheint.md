## § 45. Fine art is an art insofar as it at the same time seems to be nature

In a product of fine art one must become aware that it is art and not nature; and yet the purposiveness in its form must seem so free of all compulsion of arbitrary rules as though it were a product of nothing but nature. That pleasure — uniquely capable of being shared with everyone, and this without resting on any concept — has its basis in the sense of liberty we feel as our cognitive powers play, a play that must at the same time still be purposive. Nature was beautiful when it at the same time looked like art; and art can be called beautiful only when we are conscious that it is art and it yet looks to us like nature.

For we may state quite generally — whether the point at issue be natural beauty or artistic beauty {{ 180 }} — that ***beautiful is whatever, in the mere act of judging*** (not in sensory sensation, nor by means of a concept) ***gives pleasure***. Now art always has a determinate intention of bringing something about. But if this were mere sensation (something merely subjective), which was meant to be accompanied by pleasure, then this product would please in the judging only by means of the feeling of sense. Were the intention directed at the bringing about of a determinate object, then, if it is attained through the art, the object would please only through concepts. Yet in either case the art would give its pleasure not ***in the mere act of judging***, that is to say, it would please as a mechanical art and not as a beautiful one.

The purposiveness residing in a work of fine art, therefore, though it {{{ 307 }}} is admittedly deliberate, must all the same not look deliberate; fine art, in other words, has to be capable of being ***regarded*** as nature, notwithstanding that we remain aware of it as art. A product of art, however, appears as nature by virtue of this: that all ***punctiliousness*** is indeed met with in its conformity to the rules by which alone the product can turn out to be what it is meant to be, yet without ***painful exactitude***, without the academic form showing through, that is, without betraying a trace that the rule hovered before the artist's eyes and laid fetters upon his powers of mind.
