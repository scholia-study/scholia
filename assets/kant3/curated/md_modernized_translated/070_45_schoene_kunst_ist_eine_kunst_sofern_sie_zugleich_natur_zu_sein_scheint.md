## § 45. Fine art is an art insofar as it at the same time seems to be nature

In a product of fine art one must become aware that it is art and not nature; and yet the purposiveness in its form must seem so free of all compulsion of arbitrary rules as though it were a product of nothing but nature. That pleasure which alone is universally communicable, yet without being grounded on concepts, rests upon this feeling of freedom in the play of our cognitive powers, a play that must nonetheless at the same time be purposive. Nature was beautiful when it at the same time looked like art; and art can be called beautiful only when we are conscious that it is art and it yet looks to us like nature.

For we can say quite generally, whether it be the beauty of nature or of art {{ 180 }} that is at issue: ***that is beautiful which, in the mere act of judging*** (not in the sensation of the senses, nor through a concept) ***pleases***. Now art always has a determinate intention of bringing something about. But if this were mere sensation (something merely subjective), which was meant to be accompanied by pleasure, then this product would please in the judging only by means of the feeling of sense. Were the intention directed at the bringing about of a determinate object, then, if it is attained through the art, the object would please only through concepts. In both cases, however, the art would please not ***in the mere act of judging***, that is, not as beautiful but as mechanical art.

Thus the purposiveness in the product of fine art, although it {{{ 307 }}} is indeed intentional, must nonetheless not appear intentional; that is, fine art must be such that it can be ***looked upon*** as nature, even though one is indeed conscious of it as art. A product of art, however, appears as nature by virtue of this: that all ***punctiliousness*** is indeed met with in its conformity to the rules by which alone the product can turn out to be what it is meant to be, yet without ***painful exactitude***, without the academic form showing through, that is, without betraying a trace that the rule hovered before the artist's eyes and laid fetters upon his powers of mind.
