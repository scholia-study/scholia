## § 45. Beautiful Art is an Art, Insofar as It Seems at the Same Time to Be Nature

In a product of beautiful art one must become conscious that it is art and not nature; but yet the purposiveness in its form must seem as free from all constraint of arbitrary rules as if it were a product of mere nature. On this feeling of freedom in the play of our cognitive faculties, which must yet at the same time be purposive, rests that pleasure which alone is universally communicable, without however founding itself on concepts. Nature was beautiful if it at the same time looked like art; and art can only be called beautiful if we are conscious of its being art and it yet looks to us like nature.

For we can say in general, whether it concerns natural or artistic beauty {{ 180 }}: ***beautiful is that which pleases in the mere judging*** (not in sensation of the senses, nor through a concept). Now art always has a determinate intention to produce something. But if this were mere sensation (something merely subjective) which was to be accompanied by pleasure, then this product would please in judging only by means of sensory feeling. If the intention were directed toward the production of a determinate object, then, if it is attained through art, the object would please only through concepts. But in both cases art would not please ***in the mere judging***, i.e., not as beautiful, but as mechanical art.

Thus the purposiveness in the product of beautiful art, although {{{ 307 }}} it is intentional, must yet not seem intentional; i.e., beautiful art must be ***regarded*** as nature, although one is conscious of it as art. A product of art appears as nature, however, by the fact that although all ***punctiliousness*** is found in its agreement with rules according to which alone the product can become what it ought to be, it is yet without ***laboriousness***, without the academic form showing through, i.e., without showing a trace that the rule hovered before the artist's eyes and put fetters on his mental powers.
