## § 66. On the principle for judging the inner purposiveness in organized beings

This principle, which is at once also the definition of such purposiveness, may be stated thus: ***An organized product of nature is one in which everything {{ 296 }} is an end and reciprocally also a means.*** Nothing in it is in vain, purposeless, or attributable to a blind natural mechanism.

As regards what occasions it, this principle is admittedly to be derived from experience — namely, from the kind that is conducted methodically and is called observation; yet on account of the universality and necessity that it asserts of such a purposiveness, it cannot be grounded merely in grounds of experience, but must rest on some _a priori_ principle, even if this were merely regulative and those ends lay solely in the idea of the one who judges and nowhere in an operative cause. One may therefore call the aforementioned principle a ***maxim*** for judging the inner purposiveness of organized beings.

It is well known that the anatomists of plants and animals, so as to explore their structure and to be able to discern the reasons why and to what end such parts, why such a situation and connection of the parts and precisely this inner form have been given to them, take as inescapably necessary the maxim that within such a creature nothing is ***in vain***, and make it hold good just as much as the principle of general natural science: that ***nothing happens by chance***. Indeed, they can renounce this teleological principle just as little as the general physical one, because, just as abandoning the latter would leave no experience whatsoever, {{ 297 }} so abandoning the former principle would leave behind no guiding thread for observing a species of natural thing that we have once thought teleologically under the concept of natural ends.

{{{ 377 }}} For this concept leads reason into an entirely different order of things than the order of a mere natural mechanism, which here will no longer satisfy us. An idea is supposed to lie at the basis of the possibility of the natural product. But because this idea is an absolute unity of the representation, whereas matter is a plurality of things that of itself can furnish no determinate unity of composition: then, if that unity of the idea is even to serve as the _a priori_ determining ground for a natural law governing the causality behind such a form of the composite, the end of nature must be extended to ***everything*** that lies in its product. For once we refer such an effect ***as a whole*** to a supersensible determining ground lying beyond nature's blind mechanism, we must also judge it entirely according to this principle; and there is no reason to assume the form of such a thing still to be in part dependent on the latter, since in that case, with the mixing of heterogeneous principles, no secure rule of judging would remain at all.

It may well be that, e.g., in an animal body some {{ 298 }} parts could be comprehended as concretions according to merely mechanical laws (such as skin, bones, hair). Yet the cause that procures the matter suited to this, modifies it in this way, forms it, and deposits it in its appropriate places, must always be judged teleologically, so that everything in it must be regarded as organized, and everything, in a certain relation to the thing itself, is in turn an organ as well.
