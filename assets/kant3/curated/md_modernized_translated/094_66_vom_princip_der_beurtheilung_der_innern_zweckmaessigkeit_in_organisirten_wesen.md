## § 66. On the principle for judging the inner purposiveness in organized beings

This principle, which is at once also the definition of such purposiveness, may be stated thus: ***An organized product of nature is one in which everything {{ 296 }} is an end and reciprocally also a means.*** Nothing in it is in vain, purposeless, or attributable to a blind natural mechanism.

True, this principle is, in respect of what prompts it, to be drawn from experience — specifically from the sort that proceeds methodically and goes by the name of observation; but because of the universal and necessary character it predicates of a purposiveness of this kind, it cannot rest on empirical grounds alone, and must instead have some _a priori_ principle as its foundation, even were that principle merely regulative and even were those ends situated solely within the idea of the one judging and nowhere within any operative cause. Accordingly, one may call the aforesaid principle a ***maxim*** for appraising, in organized beings, their inner purposiveness.

It is a familiar fact that those who dissect plants and animals — seeking to explore how such beings are built and to comprehend the grounds why and to what end particular parts, why a particular placement and joining of the parts and precisely this inner form, were bestowed on them — hold the maxim that nothing in such a creature is ***in vain*** to be inescapably necessary, and press it into service just as much as the principle of general natural science, namely that ***nothing happens by chance***. Indeed, they can renounce this teleological principle just as little as the general physical one, because, just as abandoning the latter would leave no experience whatsoever, {{ 297 }} so abandoning the former principle would leave behind no guiding thread for observing a species of natural thing that we have once thought teleologically under the concept of natural ends.

{{{ 377 }}} For this concept leads reason into an entirely different order of things than the order of a mere natural mechanism, which here will no longer satisfy us. An idea is supposed to lie at the basis of the possibility of the natural product. But since this idea constitutes an absolute unity of the representation, while matter, by contrast, is a manifold of things that on its own can supply no determinate unity of composition: then, should that unity belonging to the idea serve even as the _a priori_ determining ground of a natural law that governs how such a composite form is causally brought about, nature's end has to be extended to ***everything*** contained in its product. For once we refer such an effect ***as a whole*** to a supersensible determining ground lying beyond nature's blind mechanism, we must also judge it entirely according to this principle; and there is no reason to assume the form of such a thing still to be in part dependent on the latter, since in that case, with the mixing of heterogeneous principles, no secure rule of judging would remain at all.

It may well be that, e.g., in an animal body some {{ 298 }} parts could be comprehended as concretions according to merely mechanical laws (such as skin, bones, hair). Yet the cause that supplies the matter fit for this, that so modifies and shapes it and sets it down in its proper places, must always be appraised teleologically, so that everything within it has to be viewed as organized, and everything, taken in a certain respect toward the thing itself, is once again an organ too.
