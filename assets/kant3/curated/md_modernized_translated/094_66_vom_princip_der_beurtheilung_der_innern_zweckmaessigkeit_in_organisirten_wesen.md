## § 66. On the Principle of the Judging of the Internal Purposiveness in Organized Beings

This principle, which is at the same time the definition of the same, states: ***An organized product of nature is that in which everything {{ 296 }} is an end and, reciprocally, also a means.*** Nothing in it is in vain, purposeless, or to be ascribed to a blind mechanism of nature.

This principle is indeed, as regards its occasion, to be derived from experience, namely from that which is conducted methodically and is called observation; but on account of the universality and necessity which it predicates of such a purposiveness, it cannot rest merely on empirical grounds, but must have as its basis some principle _a priori_, even if it were merely regulative and those ends lay solely in the idea of the one judging and nowhere in an efficient cause. One can therefore call the above-mentioned principle a ***maxim*** of the judging of the internal purposiveness of organized beings.

It is well known that anatomists of plants and animals, in order to investigate their structure and to be able to see the reasons why and to what end such parts, why such a position and combination of the parts, and precisely this internal form were given to them, assume as indispensably necessary that maxim: that nothing in such a creature is ***in vain***, and assert it just as they do the principle of general physics: that ***nothing happens by chance***. In fact, they can no more abandon this teleological principle than they can the general physical one, because, just as upon the abandonment of the latter no experience in general {{ 297 }} would remain, so upon the abandonment of the former principle no guide would remain for the observation of a kind of natural things that we have once teleologically conceived under the concept of natural ends.

{{{ 377 }}} For this concept leads reason into an entirely different order of things than that of a mere mechanism of nature, which can no longer satisfy us here. An idea must lie at the basis of the possibility of the product of nature. But because this idea is an absolute unity of representation, whereas matter is a multiplicity of things that by itself can provide no determinate unity of composition: so, if that unity of the idea is to serve as the determining ground _a priori_ of a natural law of the causality of such a form of the composite, the end of nature must be extended to ***everything*** that lies in its product. For if we once refer such an effect ***as a whole*** to a supersensible determining ground beyond the blind mechanism of nature, we must also judge it entirely according to this principle; and there is no ground for still assuming the form of such a thing to be in part dependent on the latter, since in that case, with the mixing of heterogeneous principles, no reliable rule of judgment would remain.

It may well be that, e.g., in an animal body some {{ 298 }} parts could be conceived as concretions according to merely mechanical laws (such as skins, bones, hair). Yet the cause which procures the appropriate matter for this, modifies and shapes it in this way, and deposits it in its proper places, must always be judged teleologically, so that everything in it must be considered as organized, and everything is also, in a certain relation to the thing itself, in turn an organ.
