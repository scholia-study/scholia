## {{{ 221 }}} § 11. The judgment of taste has nothing but the form of the purposiveness of an object (or of its way of being represented) as its ground

Every end, if it is regarded as the ground of satisfaction, always carries an interest with it, as the determining ground of the judgment about the object of pleasure. Thus no subjective end can lie at the ground of the judgment of taste. But also no representation of an objective end, i.e., of the possibility of the object itself according to principles of the connection of ends, hence no concept of the good, can determine the judgment of taste: because it is an aesthetic and not a cognitive judgment, which therefore concerns no ***concept*** of the constitution and inner or outer possibility of the object through this or that cause, but merely the relation of the powers of representation to one another, insofar as they are determined by a representation.

Now this relation in the determination of an object {{ 35 }} as a beautiful one is connected with the feeling of a pleasure that is at the same time declared by the judgment of taste to be valid for everyone; consequently an agreeableness accompanying the representation can just as little contain the determining ground as the representation of the perfection of the object and the concept of the good. Thus nothing other than the subjective purposiveness in the representation of an object without any end (neither objective nor subjective), consequently the mere form of purposiveness in the representation through which an object is ***given*** to us, insofar as we are conscious of it, can constitute the satisfaction that we judge without a concept to be universally communicable, hence the determining ground of the judgment of taste.
