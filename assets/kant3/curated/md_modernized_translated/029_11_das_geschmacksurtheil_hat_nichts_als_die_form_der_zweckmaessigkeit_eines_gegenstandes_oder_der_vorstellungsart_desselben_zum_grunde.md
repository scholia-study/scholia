## {{{ 221 }}} § 11. The judgment of taste rests on nothing but the form of purposiveness in an object (or in the manner of representing it)

Any end, once it is viewed as the ground of the satisfaction, invariably brings an interest along with it, serving as the determining ground of the judgment upon the object of the pleasure. No subjective end, therefore, can serve as the basis of a judgment of taste. But neither can any representation of an objective end, that is, of how the object itself is possible in accordance with principles that link ends together, nor consequently any concept of the good, determine the judgment of taste: since it is aesthetic rather than cognitive, it concerns no ***concept*** of the object's constitution and inner or outer possibility through this or that cause, but solely how the representational powers stand toward one another insofar as some representation determines them.

Now, in the determining of an object {{ 35 }} as beautiful, this relation is tied to a feeling of pleasure which the judgment of taste at once pronounces valid for everybody; and so an agreeableness that goes along with the representation is no more able to supply the determining ground than are the representation of the object's perfection and the concept of the good. Accordingly, the satisfaction that we appraise as universally communicable without any concept — and, along with it, the determining ground of the judgment of taste — can be constituted solely by the subjective purposiveness carried by the representation of an object in the absence of any end (objective or subjective alike), and thus by the mere form of purposiveness in that representation whereby we are ***given*** an object, insofar as we are conscious of it.
