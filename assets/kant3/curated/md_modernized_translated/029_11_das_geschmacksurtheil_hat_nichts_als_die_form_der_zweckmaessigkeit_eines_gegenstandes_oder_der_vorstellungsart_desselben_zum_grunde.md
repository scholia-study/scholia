## {{{ 221 }}} § 11. The judgment of taste rests on nothing but the form of purposiveness in an object (or in the manner of representing it)

Any end, once it is viewed as the ground of the satisfaction, invariably brings an interest along with it, serving as the determining ground of the judgment upon the object of the pleasure. No subjective end, therefore, can serve as the basis of a judgment of taste. But neither can any representation of an objective end, that is, of how the object itself is possible in accordance with principles that link ends together, nor consequently any concept of the good, determine the judgment of taste: since it is aesthetic rather than cognitive, it has to do with no ***concept*** of the constitution and the inward or outward possibility of the object through one cause or another, but solely with how the representational powers stand in relation to each other insofar as some representation determines them.

Now, in the determining of an object {{ 35 }} as beautiful, this relation is tied to a feeling of pleasure which the judgment of taste at once pronounces valid for everybody; and so an agreeableness that goes along with the representation is no more able to supply the determining ground than are the representation of the object's perfection and the concept of the good. It follows that nothing but the subjective purposiveness in the representation of an object, devoid of any end whatever (whether objective or subjective), consequently the mere form of purposiveness in the representation whereby an object is ***given*** to us, provided we are conscious of this form, can make up the satisfaction that we appraise, apart from any concept, as universally communicable, and hence can make up the determining ground of the judgment of taste.
