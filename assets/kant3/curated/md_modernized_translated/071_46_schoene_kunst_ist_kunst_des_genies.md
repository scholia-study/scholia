## {{ 181 }} § 46. Beautiful Art is Art of Genius

***Genius*** is the talent (natural endowment) which gives the rule to art. Since talent, as an innate productive faculty of the artist, itself belongs to nature, one could also express it thus: ***Genius*** is the innate mental disposition (_ingenium_) ***through which*** nature gives the rule to art.

Whatever the case may be with this definition, and whether it be merely arbitrary, or adequate, or not, to the concept which one is accustomed to combine with the word ***genius*** (which is to be discussed in the following section): yet one can already prove in advance that, according to the meaning of the word assumed here, beautiful arts must necessarily be considered as arts of ***genius***.

For every art presupposes rules, through whose laying of the foundation a product, if it is to be called artistic, is first of all represented as possible. But the concept of beautiful art does not permit the judgment about the beauty of its product to be derived from any rule that has a ***concept*** as its determining ground, and consequently lays down a concept of how it is possible. Thus beautiful art cannot itself devise the rule according to which it is to bring its product {{ 182 }} about. Since, however, without a preceding rule a product can never be called art, nature in the subject (and through the disposition of its faculties) must give the rule to art, i.e., beautiful art is possible only as a product of genius.

One sees from this that genius is 1) a ***talent*** for producing that for which no definite rule can be given, not a disposition of skill for that which can be learned according to some rule; {{{ 308 }}} consequently, that ***originality*** must be its first property. 2) That, since there can also be original nonsense, its products must at the same time be models, i.e., ***exemplary***; consequently, though not themselves arisen through imitation, they must yet serve others for that purpose, i.e., as a standard or rule of estimation. 3) That it cannot itself describe, or scientifically indicate, how it brings its product about, but that it gives the rule as ***nature***; and therefore the author of a product which he owes to his genius does not himself know how the ideas for it find their way into him, nor does he have it in his power to devise the like at pleasure or systematically, and to communicate it to others in such precepts as would put them in a position to produce similar products. (Hence, too, the word genius is presumably derived from _genius_, the peculiar protecting and guiding spirit given to a person at birth, {{ 183 }} from whose inspiration those original ideas proceeded.) 4) That nature through genius prescribes the rule not to science but to art, and even this only in so far as the latter is to be beautiful art.
