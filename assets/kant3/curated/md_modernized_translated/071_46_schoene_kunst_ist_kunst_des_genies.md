## {{ 181 }} § 46. Fine art is the art of genius

***Genius*** is the talent (the gift of nature) that gives art the rule. Since talent, as the artist's innate productive faculty, itself belongs within nature, one could equally frame it thus: ***genius*** is the inborn cast of mind (_ingenium_) ***through which*** nature hands art its rule.

However this definition may stand, and regardless of whether it is purely arbitrary or, on the contrary, suited to the concept that people habitually connect with the word ***genius*** (a question to be taken up in the following §): even so, it can be shown beforehand that, given the sense of the word assumed here, the fine arts must of necessity be viewed as arts of ***genius***.

For every art presupposes rules, through the laying down of which a product is in the first place represented as possible if it is to be called a product of art. The concept of fine art, however, does not permit that the judgment upon its product's beauty be traced back to some rule resting on a ***concept*** as its determining ground, one that would therefore ground the product in a concept of how it might come to be possible. Fine art therefore cannot itself think up the rule by which it is to bring its product {{ 182 }} into being. Yet since, in the absence of a rule going before it, a product can never bear the name of art, it is nature in the subject (and by way of the attunement of that subject's faculties) that must give art its rule; in other words, fine art proves possible solely as a product of genius.

One gathers from this that genius is 1) a ***talent*** for producing something to which no determinate rule can be assigned: not a disposition of skill toward what may be learned according to some rule; {{{ 308 }}} consequently, that ***originality*** must be its foremost characteristic. 2) That, because originality can also yield nonsense, its products must be at once patterns, that is, ***exemplary***; and so, while not arising through imitation on their own part, they must nonetheless serve others precisely to this end, namely as a measure or rule for appraisal. 3) That it can neither describe for itself, nor indicate scientifically, the way in which it brings its product to completion, but instead furnishes the rule in the guise of ***nature***; so that the author of a product he has to thank his genius for remains himself unaware of how the ideas for it take shape within him, and likewise lacks the power to devise the like deliberately or by plan at his pleasure and to impart it to others in such prescriptions as would put them in a position to bring forth products of equal kind. (This is presumably also why the word genius is derived from _genius_, the peculiar spirit allotted to a person at birth, {{ 183 }} the protecting and guiding one, from whose prompting those original ideas were held to arise.) 4) That nature, through genius, prescribes the rule not to science but to art, and this too only insofar as the latter is to be fine art.
