## {{ 181 }} § 46. Fine art is the art of genius

***Genius*** is the talent (the gift of nature) that gives art the rule. Since talent, as the artist's innate productive faculty, itself belongs within nature, one could equally frame it thus: ***genius*** is the inborn cast of mind (_ingenium_) ***through which*** nature hands art its rule.

Whatever may be the case with this definition, and whether it is merely arbitrary or instead fits the concept that one is accustomed to attach to the word ***genius*** or not (which is to be examined in the following §): still, one can already establish in advance that, on the meaning of the word adopted here, fine arts must necessarily be regarded as arts of ***genius***.

For every art presupposes rules, through the laying down of which a product is in the first place represented as possible if it is to be called a product of art. But the concept of fine art does not allow the judgment about the beauty of its product to be derived from any rule that has a ***concept*** for its determining ground, and thus takes as its basis a concept of the manner in which the product is possible. Fine art therefore cannot itself think up the rule by which it is to bring its product {{ 182 }} into being. Since, all the same, no product can ever be called art without a rule that precedes it, nature within the subject (and through the attunement of the subject's faculties) has to supply art with its rule; in other words, fine art is possible only as a product of genius.

From this one sees that genius is 1) a ***talent*** for bringing forth that for which no determinate rule can be given: not an aptitude of skill for what can be learned by following some rule; {{{ 308 }}} it follows that ***originality*** must be its first property. 2) That, since there can also be original nonsense, its products must at the same time be models, that is, ***exemplary***; hence, though not themselves sprung from imitation, they must all the same serve others toward that end, that is, as a standard or rule for judging. 3) That it cannot itself describe, or point out scientifically, how it brings its product about, but rather that it gives the rule as ***nature***; and hence the originator of a product that he owes to his genius does not himself know how the ideas for it come to be present in him, nor does he have it in his power to think such things up at will or by design and to convey them to others in precepts that would enable them to bring forth matching products. (This is presumably also why the word genius is derived from _genius_, the peculiar spirit allotted to a person at birth, {{ 183 }} the protecting and guiding one, from whose prompting those original ideas were held to arise.) 4) That nature, through genius, prescribes the rule not to science but to art, and this too only insofar as the latter is to be fine art.
