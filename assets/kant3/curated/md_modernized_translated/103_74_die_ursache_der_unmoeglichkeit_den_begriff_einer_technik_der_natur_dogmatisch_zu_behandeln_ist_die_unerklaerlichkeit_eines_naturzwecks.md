## § 74. The reason it is impossible to treat the concept of a technic of nature dogmatically is the inexplicability of a natural end

We proceed dogmatically with a concept (even should it be empirically conditioned) when we take it to fall under another concept of the object—one that constitutes a principle of reason—and determine it in accordance with that concept. By contrast, we handle it merely critically when we view it solely in reference to our faculty of cognition—and thus to the subjective conditions under which we think it—while making no attempt to settle anything regarding its object. The dogmatic procedure with a concept is thus the one that is lawful for the determining power of judgment, the critical the one lawful merely for the reflecting.

{{{ 396 }}} Now the concept of a thing viewed as a natural end is a concept {{ 330 }} that subsumes nature under a causality thinkable only through reason, in order to judge—by this principle—what experience presents to us of the object. Yet if it is to serve dogmatically for the determining power of judgment, we would first need assurance of this concept's objective reality, since otherwise no natural thing could be subsumed under it. The concept of a natural end, however, though empirically conditioned—that is, possible only under certain conditions furnished in experience—is nonetheless not one to be abstracted from experience, but one possible solely by a principle of reason in the judging of the object. As such a principle, then, it can in no way be grasped and dogmatically established as regards its objective reality (that is, that an object conforming to it be possible); and it remains unknown to us whether it is a merely sophistical and objectively vacuous concept (_conceptus ratiocinans_), or a concept of reason that grounds cognition and is ratified by reason (_conceptus ratiocinatus_). It cannot, therefore, be treated dogmatically for the determining power of judgment: that is, not only can it not be made out whether things of nature, regarded as natural ends, demand for their production a causality of an altogether special kind (the one directed by intentions) or not; the question cannot so much as be posed, since the concept of a {{ 331 }} natural end is, as regards its objective reality, in no way provable through reason (that is to say, it is not constitutive for the determining power of judgment, but for the reflecting merely regulative).

That it is not such, however, is clear from this: that, as the concept of a ***natural product***, it comprehends within itself natural necessity and, at the same time, in that very same thing regarded as an end, a contingency in the object's form (with respect to mere natural laws); consequently, if contradiction is to be avoided here, the concept must include both a basis for the thing's possibility within nature and, in addition, a basis for the possibility of this very nature itself, together with its relation to something that is not empirically knowable nature (the supersensible), and hence not cognizable by us at all, so that it may be judged under a species of causality other than the mechanism of nature, should one seek to make out its possibility. Since, then, the concept of a thing taken as a natural end is ***for the determining power of judgment*** extravagant when one considers the object through reason (even though for the reflecting power of judgment it may well be immanent with regard {{{ 397 }}} to the objects of experience), and hence objective reality cannot be procured for it for determining judgments: it becomes thereby intelligible how all the systems one might ever devise for treating dogmatically the concept of natural ends and of nature, as {{ 332 }} a whole cohering through final causes, can decide nothing whatever, whether by objective affirmation or objective denial; for when things are subsumed under a concept that is merely problematic, its synthetic predicates (for example here: whether the end of nature that we think for the production of things is intended or unintended) must likewise deliver judgments of the same (problematic) sort about the object, be they affirmative or negative, since one does not know whether one is judging about something or about nothing. The concept of a causality operating through ends (that of art) certainly possesses objective reality, as does the concept of a causality proceeding by nature's mechanism. The concept of a natural causality following the rule of ends, however—and still more that of a being of a kind that can never be given to us in experience, namely such a being as the original ground of nature—can indeed be thought without contradiction, yet remains unfit for dogmatic determinations: for since it can neither be drawn from experience nor is needed for the possibility of experience, its objective reality can be secured by nothing whatever. And even were this to occur, how could I reckon things expressly declared to be products of divine art still among the products of nature, whose incapacity to bring forth such things by their own {{ 333 }} laws was precisely what made the appeal to a cause distinct from it necessary?
