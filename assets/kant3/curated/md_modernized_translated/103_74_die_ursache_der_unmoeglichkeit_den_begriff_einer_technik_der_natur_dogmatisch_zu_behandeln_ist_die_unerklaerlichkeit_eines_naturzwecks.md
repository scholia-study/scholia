## § 74. The reason it is impossible to treat the concept of a technic of nature dogmatically is the inexplicability of a natural end

We treat a concept dogmatically (even if it should happen to be empirically conditioned) whenever we regard it as contained under some other concept of the object—one that forms a principle of reason—and specify it to accord with that higher one. By contrast, we handle it merely critically when we view it solely in reference to our faculty of cognition—and thus to the subjective conditions under which we think it—while making no attempt to settle anything regarding its object. The dogmatic procedure with a concept is thus the one that is lawful for the determining power of judgment, the critical the one lawful merely for the reflecting.

{{{ 396 }}} Now the concept of a thing understood as a natural end is one {{ 330 }} that brings nature under a causality conceivable solely by reason, so as to appraise, on that principle, whatever is given of the object in experience. But to employ it dogmatically on behalf of the determining power of judgment, we should have to be assured beforehand of this concept's objective reality, for otherwise we could subsume no natural thing under it. The concept of a natural end, however, though empirically conditioned—that is, possible only under certain conditions furnished in experience—is nonetheless not one to be abstracted from experience, but one possible solely by a principle of reason in the judging of the object. As such a principle, then, it can in no way be grasped and dogmatically established as regards its objective reality (that is, that an object conforming to it be possible); and it remains unknown to us whether it is a merely sophistical and objectively vacuous concept (_conceptus ratiocinans_), or a concept of reason—one that founds a cognition and is confirmed by reason—(_conceptus ratiocinatus_). It cannot, therefore, be treated dogmatically for the determining power of judgment: that is, not only can it not be made out whether things of nature, regarded as natural ends, demand for their production a causality of an altogether special kind (the one directed by intentions) or not; the question cannot so much as be posed, since the concept of a {{ 331 }} natural end is, as regards its objective reality, in no way provable through reason (that is to say, it is not constitutive for the determining power of judgment, but for the reflecting merely regulative).

That it is not such, however, is clear from this: that, as the concept of a ***natural product***, it comprehends within itself natural necessity and, at the same time, in that very same thing regarded as an end, a contingency in the object's form (with respect to mere natural laws); consequently, if contradiction is to be avoided here, the concept must include both a basis for the thing's possibility within nature and, in addition, a basis for the possibility of this very nature itself, together with its relation to something that is not empirically knowable nature (the supersensible), and hence not cognizable by us at all, so that it may be judged under a species of causality other than the mechanism of nature, should one seek to make out its possibility. Since, therefore, the concept of a thing as a natural end is ***for the determining power of judgment*** overreaching when one regards the object by means of reason (although it may well be immanent for the reflecting power of judgment in view {{{ 397 }}} of the objects of experience), so that its objective reality cannot be furnished to it for determining judgments: from this it becomes conceivable why none of the systems that one might ever sketch for handling dogmatically the concept of natural ends and of nature—taken as {{ 332 }} a whole bound together by final causes—can decide anything whatever, neither by objective affirmation nor by objective denial; for once things get subsumed beneath a concept that is no more than problematic, that concept's synthetic predicates (for instance here: whether the natural end we conceive for the production of the things is intended or not) must yield judgments of precisely that same (problematic) kind about the object, whether they turn out affirmative or negative, given that one cannot tell whether one's judgment bears on something or on nothing. The concept of a causality operating through ends (that of art) certainly possesses objective reality, as does the concept of a causality proceeding by nature's mechanism. The concept of a natural causality following the rule of ends, however—and still more that of a being of a kind that can never be given to us in experience, namely such a being as the original ground of nature—can indeed be thought without contradiction, yet remains unfit for dogmatic determinations: for since it can neither be drawn from experience nor is needed for the possibility of experience, its objective reality can be secured by nothing whatever. And even were this to occur, how could I reckon things expressly declared to be products of divine art still among the products of nature, whose incapacity to bring forth such things by their own {{ 333 }} laws was precisely what made the appeal to a cause distinct from it necessary?
