## § 74. The Cause of the Impossibility of Treating the Concept of a Technic of Nature Dogmatically is the Inexplicability of a Natural End

We proceed dogmatically with a concept (even if it should be empirically conditioned) if we consider it as contained under another concept of the object which constitutes a principle of reason, and determine it in accordance with this. But we proceed with it merely critically if we consider it only in relation to our cognitive faculty, and consequently to the subjective conditions of thinking it, without undertaking to decide anything about its object. The dogmatic procedure with a concept is therefore that which is lawful for the determining, and the critical procedure is that which is lawful merely for the reflecting power of judgment.

{{{ 396 }}} Now the concept of a thing as a natural end is a concept {{ 330 }} that subsumes nature under a causality that is thinkable only through reason, in order to judge, according to this principle, about what is given of the object in experience. But in order to use it dogmatically for the determining power of judgment, we would first have to be assured of the objective reality of this concept, because otherwise we could subsume no natural thing under it. The concept of a thing as a natural end, however, is indeed an empirically conditioned concept, i.e., possible only under certain conditions given in experience, but yet not to be abstracted from it, but rather possible only according to a principle of reason in the judging of the object. It can therefore, as such a principle, not be insightfully understood at all as regards its objective reality (i.e., that an object is possible in accordance with it) and be dogmatically established; and we do not know whether it is merely a ratiocinating and objectively empty concept (_conceptus ratiocinans_) or a concept of reason, one that grounds cognition and is confirmed by reason (_conceptus ratiocinatus_). Hence, it cannot be treated dogmatically for the determining power of judgment: i.e., not only can it not be determined whether things of nature, considered as natural ends, require for their generation a causality of an entirely special kind (that according to intentions) or not; but it cannot even be asked about, because the concept of a {{ 331 }} natural end as regards its objective reality is not provable by reason at all (i.e., it is not constitutive for the determining power of judgment, but merely regulative for the reflecting power of judgment).

That it is not so, however, is clear from this, that as a concept of a ***natural product*** it contains within itself natural necessity and yet at the same time a contingency of the form of the object (in relation to mere laws of nature) in the very same thing as an end; consequently, if there is to be no contradiction here, it must contain a ground for the possibility of the thing in nature and yet also a ground of the possibility of this nature itself and of its relation to something that is not empirically cognizable nature (supersensible), and thus is not cognizable for us at all, in order to be judged according to another kind of causality than that of the mechanism of nature, if one wishes to establish its possibility. Since, therefore, the concept of a thing as a natural end is transcendent ***for the determining power of judgment*** if one considers the object through reason (although it may indeed be immanent for the reflecting power of judgment in regard {{{ 397 }}} to objects of experience), and consequently objective reality cannot be procured for it for determining judgments: it is comprehensible from this how all systems which one may ever design for the dogmatic treatment of the concept of natural ends and of nature as {{ 332 }} a whole connected through final causes, can decide nothing either objectively affirmatively or objectively negatively; because, when things are subsumed under a concept which is merely problematic, its synthetic predicates (e.g., here: whether the end of nature which we think for the generation of things is intentional or unintentional) must yield precisely such (problematic) judgments about the object, whether they be affirmative or negative, since one does not know whether one is judging about something or about nothing. The concept of a causality through ends (of art) has indeed objective reality, as has just as much that of a causality according to the mechanism of nature. But the concept of a causality of nature according to the rule of ends, and still more that of a being the like of which cannot be given to us in experience at all, namely of such a being as the original ground of nature, can indeed be thought without contradiction, but is yet of no use for dogmatic determinations: because, since it cannot be drawn from experience, nor is required for the possibility of the latter, its objective reality can be secured by nothing. But even if this were to happen, how can I still count things, which are determinately stated to be products of divine art, among the products of nature, whose inability to produce the like according to its {{ 333 }} laws is precisely what made the appeal to a cause distinct from it necessary?
