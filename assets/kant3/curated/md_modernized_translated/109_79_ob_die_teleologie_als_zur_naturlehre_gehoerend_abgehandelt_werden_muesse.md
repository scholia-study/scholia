## § 79. Whether teleology must be treated as belonging to the doctrine of nature

Within the encyclopedia of all the sciences, each and every science must occupy a determinate place of its own. Should it be a philosophical science, then it must be assigned its place within either the theoretical or the practical part thereof; and, if its place lies in the former, then either within the doctrine of nature—so far as this weighs whatever can become an object of experience (hence within the theory of corporeal things, the theory of the soul, and general cosmology)—or within the doctrine of God (dealing with the world's primordial ground, taken as the sum-total of all objects of experience).

The question, then, arises: which place is due to teleology? Does it fall under natural science (properly so called), or under theology? It must be the one or the other; for the passage from the one into the other can accommodate no science at all, seeing that such a transition means nothing more than the system's articulation or organization and affords no standing place within it.

That teleology constitutes no part of theology, even though {{ 365 }} within theology the weightiest use may be made of it, is clear in and of itself. For its object is the productions of nature and the cause of these; and although it does point out beyond, to that cause as a ground lying outside of and above nature (a divine author), it nevertheless does so not for the sake of the determining power of judgment, but only (so that the judging of things in the world may be guided, through an idea of this sort, by a regulative principle adapted to the human understanding) for the sake of the reflecting power of judgment as it contemplates nature.

{{{ 417 }}} Yet it seems, by the same token, to have no more place in natural science either, which requires principles that are determining and not merely reflecting if it is to supply objective grounds for the workings of nature. Indeed, neither for the theory of nature nor for the mechanical explanation of its phenomena by way of their efficient causes is anything whatever won by considering them according to how ends stand related to one another. To exhibit nature's ends in its products, so far as these constitute a system in accordance with teleological concepts, really pertains only to a description of nature composed along a special guiding thread—one where reason does carry out a splendid business, instructive and in various respects practically purposive, yet yields no {{ 366 }} disclosure at all about the origination and inner possibility of these forms, which is, after all, the very thing that theoretical natural science is really about.

Teleology, taken as a science, thus belongs to no doctrine whatever, but solely to critique—and specifically to the critique of one particular faculty of cognition, that is to say, of the power of judgment. But in so far as it contains principles _a priori_, it can and must lay down the method by which nature is to be judged following the principle of final causes; and in this way its doctrine of method exercises at least a negative influence on the procedure of theoretical natural science, and also on the relation which the latter may have, within metaphysics, to theology as its propaedeutic.
