## § 79. Whether teleology must be treated as belonging to the doctrine of nature

Within the encyclopedia of all the sciences, each and every science must occupy a determinate place of its own. If it is a philosophical science, then its place must be assigned to it within either the theoretical or the practical part of philosophy, and, should it fall within the former, then either within the doctrine of nature, insofar as it weighs whatever can be an object of experience (and consequently the theory of corporeal things, the theory of the soul, and general cosmology), or within the doctrine of God (concerning the primordial ground of the world as the sum-total of all objects of experience).

Now one may ask: what place properly belongs to teleology? Is it to be counted a part of natural science (in the strict sense of that word), or of theology? One of the two it must be; for no science whatever can belong to the transition from the one to the other, since such a transition signifies merely the articulation or organization of the system and no place within it.

That teleology constitutes no part of theology, even though {{ 365 }} within theology the weightiest use may be made of it, is clear in and of itself. For its object is the productions of nature and the cause of these; and although it does point out beyond, to that cause as a ground lying outside of and above nature (a divine author), it nevertheless does so not for the sake of the determining power of judgment, but only (so that the judging of things in the world may be guided, through an idea of this sort, by a regulative principle adapted to the human understanding) for the sake of the reflecting power of judgment as it contemplates nature.

{{{ 417 }}} Yet just as little does it seem to belong to natural science either, which stands in need of determining and not merely reflecting principles in order to furnish objective grounds for the effects of nature. Indeed, nothing at all is thereby gained for the theory of nature, or for the mechanical explanation of its phenomena through their efficient causes, by regarding them in terms of the relation of ends to one another. The setting-forth of the ends of nature in its products, insofar as these make up a system under teleological concepts, pertains properly only to the description of nature that is drawn up along a particular guiding thread: here reason does indeed carry out a splendid task, instructive and, for practical aims of many kinds, purposive, yet it gives no {{ 366 }} disclosure whatsoever about the coming-to-be and the inner possibility that such forms have, which is nonetheless precisely what theoretical natural science is really concerned with.

Teleology as a science therefore belongs to no doctrine at all, but rather only to critique, and indeed to the critique of a particular faculty of cognition, namely the power of judgment. But to the extent that it embraces principles _a priori_, it is able—indeed obliged—to specify the method according to which nature must be judged in conformity with the principle of final causes; and thus its doctrine of method has at least a negative bearing upon the procedure of theoretical natural science, and likewise upon the relation that the latter, within metaphysics, may bear to theology as its propaedeutic.
