## § 79. Whether Teleology Must Be Treated as Belonging to the Doctrine of Nature

Every science must have its determinate place in the encyclopedia of all sciences. If it is a philosophical science, its place must be assigned in either the theoretical or the practical part of the same, and, if it has its place in the former, either in the doctrine of nature, insofar as it considers that which can be an object of experience (consequently, the doctrine of bodies, the doctrine of the soul, and cosmology), or in the doctrine of God (concerning the original ground of the world as the sum total of all objects of experience).

Now the question is: Which place belongs to teleology? Does it belong to natural science (properly so called), or to theology? It must be one of the two; for no science can belong to the transition from one to the other, because this means only the articulation or organization of the system, and not a place within it.

That it does not belong to theology as a part of the same, although {{ 365 }} the most important use can be made of it therein, is clear of itself. For it has natural products and their cause as its object; and although it points beyond to the latter, as a ground situated outside of and above nature (a divine author), it does so not for the determining power of judgment, but only (in order to guide the judging of things in the world by such an idea as a regulative principle in a manner appropriate to human understanding) merely for the reflecting power of judgment in the consideration of nature.

{{{ 417 }}} Just as little, however, does it seem to belong to natural science, which requires determining and not merely reflecting principles in order to assign objective grounds for natural effects. In fact, nothing is gained for the theory of nature, or the mechanical explanation of its phenomena through their efficient causes, by considering them according to the relation of ends to one another. The presentation of the ends of nature in its products, insofar as they constitute a system according to teleological concepts, belongs properly only to the description of nature, which is drawn up according to a special guide: where reason indeed performs a magnificent, instructive, and, in various respects, practically useful task, but gives no {{ 366 }} explanation at all of the generation and the inner possibility of these forms, which is what theoretical natural science is actually concerned with.

Teleology as a science therefore belongs to no doctrine at all, but only to critique, and indeed of a special cognitive faculty, namely the power of judgment. But insofar as it contains principles _a priori_, it can and must state the method of how nature must be judged in accordance with the principle of final causes; and thus its doctrine of method has at least a negative influence on the procedure in theoretical natural science, and also on the relation which the latter, in metaphysics, can have to theology as its propaedeutic.
