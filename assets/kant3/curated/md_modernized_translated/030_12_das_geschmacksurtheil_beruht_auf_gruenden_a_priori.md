## § 12. The judgment of taste rests on grounds _a priori_

To make out _a priori_ the connection of the feeling of a pleasure or displeasure as an effect with some representation (sensation or concept) as its cause is absolutely impossible; for that would be a causal relation which (among objects of experience) can {{{ 222 }}} only ever be cognized _a posteriori_ and by means of experience itself {{ 36 }}. To be sure, in the Critique of Practical Reason we actually did derive _a priori_ the feeling of respect (as a special and peculiar modification of this feeling, which will not properly coincide either with the pleasure or the displeasure that we get from empirical objects) from general moral concepts. But there we could also overstep the boundaries of experience and call upon a causality that rested on a supersensible constitution of the subject, namely that of freedom. Yet even there we did not properly derive this ***feeling*** from the idea of the moral as a cause, but merely the determination of the will was derived from it. But the state of mind of a will determined in some way or other is in itself already a feeling of pleasure and identical with it, hence does not follow from it as an effect: the latter would have to be assumed only if the concept of the moral as a good preceded the determination of the will through the law; for then the pleasure that would be connected with the concept would be derived in vain from this as a mere cognition.

Now it stands in a similar way with the pleasure in the aesthetic judgment: only that here it is merely contemplative, and without effecting an interest in the object, whereas in the moral judgment it is practical. The consciousness of the merely formal purposiveness in the play of the powers of cognition {{ 37 }} of the subject upon a representation through which an object is given is the pleasure itself, because it contains a determining ground of the activity of the subject with respect to the animation of its powers of cognition, hence an inner causality (which is purposive) with respect to cognition in general, but without being restricted to a determinate cognition, hence a mere form of the subjective purposiveness of a representation, in an aesthetic judgment. This pleasure is also in no way practical, neither like that from the pathological ground of agreeableness, nor like that from the intellectual ground of the represented good. Yet it does have causality in itself, namely ***to maintain*** the state of the representation itself and the occupation of the powers of cognition without any further aim. We ***linger*** over the contemplation of the beautiful, because this contemplation strengthens and reproduces itself: which is analogous to (yet not the same as) that lingering in which a charm in the representation of the object repeatedly arouses the attention, while the mind is passive.
