## § 12. The judgment of taste rests on grounds _a priori_

To establish _a priori_ the connection of the feeling of a pleasure or displeasure, as an effect, with some representation (sensation or concept), as its cause, is flatly impossible; for that would be a causal relation which (among objects of experience) can only {{{ 222 }}} ever be recognized _a posteriori_ and by means of experience itself {{ 36 }}. It is true that in the Critique of Practical Reason we did indeed derive the feeling of respect (as a particular and distinctive modification of this feeling, one that will not quite square either with the pleasure or the displeasure that we obtain from empirical objects) _a priori_ from universal ethical concepts. But there we were also able to overstep the bounds of experience and to summon a causality resting on a supersensible property of the subject, namely that of freedom. Yet even there we did not, properly speaking, derive this ***feeling*** from the idea of the ethical as its cause; rather, only the determination of the will was derived from it. The state of mind of a will determined in some way or other, however, is in itself already a feeling of pleasure and one identical with it, and hence does not follow from it as an effect: the latter would have to be assumed only if the concept of the ethical, as a good, preceded the determination of the will by the law; for then the pleasure that would be bound up with the concept would be derived in vain from it as a mere cognition.

Now with the pleasure in the aesthetic judgment the case stands in a similar way: only that here it is merely contemplative, and brings about no interest in the object, whereas in the moral judgment it is practical. The consciousness of the merely formal purposiveness in the play of the subject's cognitive powers {{ 37 }} accompanying a representation by which an object is given just is the pleasure, for what it holds within an aesthetic judgment is a ground determining the subject's activity with regard to the quickening of those same cognitive powers — that is, an inner causality (a purposive one) bearing on cognition as such, though not tied down to any particular cognition, and so nothing but a form of the subjective purposiveness belonging to a representation. This pleasure is likewise in no way practical, neither in the manner of that stemming from the pathological ground of agreeableness, nor of that stemming from the intellectual ground of the good as represented. It does, however, carry a causality within itself, namely that of ***preserving*** the state of the representation itself and the engagement of the cognitive powers without any further aim. We ***dwell*** upon the beholding of the beautiful, because this beholding reinforces and reproduces itself: which is analogous to (though yet not the same as) that lingering in which a charm in the representation of the object repeatedly rouses the attention, while the mind remains passive.
