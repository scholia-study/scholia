## § 53. Comparison of the aesthetic value of the fine arts with one another

Among them all, ***the art of poetry*** (which owes its origin almost entirely to genius and least of all wishes to be guided by precept or by examples) claims the highest rank. It broadens the mind in that it releases the imagination into freedom and, within the bounds of a given concept and among the unlimited variety of possible forms consonant with it, brings forward the one form that unites the presentation of that concept with a fullness of thought which no linguistic expression ever wholly matches, and so raises itself aesthetically to ideas. It fortifies the mind by letting it feel its free, self-active power, independent of any determination by nature, to contemplate and judge nature as appearance according to views that nature does not of itself present in experience, whether to sense or to the understanding, and hence to employ it on behalf of, and as it were as the schema of, the supersensible. It plays with {{{ 327 }}} semblance, which it produces at will, yet without thereby deceiving; for it declares its own occupation to be mere play, which nonetheless {{ 216 }} can be put to purposive use by the understanding and for the understanding's business. — Oratory, insofar as one understands by it the art of persuasion, that is, of deceiving by means of beautiful semblance (as _ars oratoria_), and not mere fine speech (eloquence and style), is a dialectic that borrows from the art of poetry only as much as is needed to win minds over to the orator, to his advantage, before they form their judgment, and to deprive them of their freedom; it can therefore be recommended neither for the courtroom nor for the pulpit. For when it is a matter of civil laws, of the rights of individual persons, or of the lasting instruction and orientation of minds toward correct knowledge and conscientious observance of their duty, it is beneath the dignity of so important a business to let even a trace of luxuriance of wit and imagination be shown, still less any of the art of persuading and of biasing people to someone's advantage. For even though it may on occasion be applied to purposes that are in themselves legitimate and praiseworthy, it is nonetheless rendered reprehensible by the fact that in this way maxims and dispositions are subjectively corrupted, even if the deed is objectively lawful: since it is not enough to do what is right, but one must also perform it solely on the ground that it is right. Moreover, the merely distinct concept of these kinds of human affairs, combined with a {{ 217 }} lively presentation in examples and without any offense against the rules of euphony of language or of the propriety of expression for ideas of reason (which together make up fine speech), already has of itself sufficient influence on human minds that there is no need to bring in besides the machinery of persuasion; which, since it can just as well be used to gloss over or conceal vice and error, cannot wholly dispel the secret suspicion of an artful trickery. In the art of poetry everything proceeds honestly and sincerely. It avows that it means to conduct a merely diverting play with the imagination, one whose form accords with the laws of the understanding; and it makes no claim to creep up on the understanding and ensnare it by means of sensible presentation.[^*]

{{{ 328 }}} After the art of poetry I would place, ***if it is a matter of charm and movement {{ 218 }} of the mind***, the one that comes closest to it among the arts of speech and that can also very naturally be united with it, namely ***the art of tone***. For although it speaks through nothing but sensations without concepts, and hence, unlike poetry, leaves nothing over for reflection, it nonetheless moves the mind in a more manifold way and, although only transiently, yet more inwardly; it is admittedly more enjoyment than culture (the play of thought that is incidentally aroused by it is merely the effect of an as it were mechanical association); and, judged by reason, it has less worth than any other of the fine arts. Hence, like every enjoyment, it demands frequent change and does not bear repeated reiteration without producing weariness. Its charm, which admits of being so universally communicated, seems to {{ 219 }} rest on this: that every expression of language has, in its context, a tone appropriate to its meaning; that this tone more or less designates an affect of the speaker and reciprocally produces one in the listener as well, who then, conversely, has aroused in him the idea that is expressed in language with such a tone; and that, in the manner in which modulation constitutes a sort of universal language of sensations comprehensible to every human being, the art of tone exercises this language entirely on its own, in its full emphasis, namely as the language of the affects, and thereby, following the law of association, communicates universally the aesthetic ideas naturally connected with it; but that, because those {{{ 329 }}} aesthetic ideas are not concepts and determinate thoughts, the form of the combination of these sensations (harmony and melody) serves only in place of the form of a language, by means of a proportioned attunement of them (which, since with tones it rests on the relation of the number of vibrations of the air in the same span of time, insofar as the tones are combined simultaneously or also successively, can be brought mathematically under certain rules), to express the aesthetic idea of a coherent whole of an unnameable fullness of thought, in accordance with a certain theme that constitutes the affect prevailing in the piece. On this mathematical form, although not represented by determinate {{ 220 }} concepts, alone depends the satisfaction that the mere reflection upon such a multitude of sensations accompanying or following one another connects with this play of them as a condition of its beauty valid for everyone; and it is this form alone by which taste may presume to claim in advance a right over everyone's judgment.

Yet mathematics certainly plays not the least part in the charm and the emotional stirring that music brings about; it is merely the unavoidable prerequisite (_conditio sine qua non_) for the proportion among these impressions, both as they are joined together and as they succeed one another, which makes it possible to gather them into a unity, keeping them from cancelling each other out and letting them instead coalesce, through affects that resonate with them, into an uninterrupted stirring and animation of the mind, and so into an agreeable enjoyment of oneself.

If, on the other hand, one estimates the value of the fine arts by the culture they procure for the mind, and takes as one's measure the expansion of the powers that must come together in the power of judgment for the sake of cognition, then music has, in this respect, the lowest place among the fine arts (as, among those that are esteemed at the same time according to their agreeableness, it has perhaps the highest), because it merely plays with sensations. The formative arts thus far surpass it in this {{ 221 }} respect; for, in setting the imagination into a free play that is nonetheless at the same time suited to the understanding, they at the same time carry on a business, in that they bring about a product that serves the concepts of the understanding as a durable vehicle, one that recommends itself to them, so as to further the joining of these concepts with sensibility and, in this way, as it were, the urbanity of the higher cognitive faculties. {{{ 330 }}} The two kinds of art take an entirely different course: the first goes from sensations to indeterminate ideas; the second kind, however, from determinate ideas to sensations. The latter are of a ***lasting***, the former only of a ***transitory*** impression. The imagination can recall the latter and pleasantly entertain itself with them; the former, however, either fade away entirely, or, when they are involuntarily repeated by the imagination, are irksome to us rather than pleasant. Besides this, a certain lack of urbanity attaches to music, in that, chiefly on account of the character of its instruments, it extends its influence farther than one wants (over the neighborhood) and thus, as it were, forces itself upon one, thereby infringing on the freedom of others outside the musical company; which the arts that speak to the eyes do not do, since one need only turn one's eyes away if one does not wish to admit their impression. It is almost the same here as with the {{ 222 }} gratification afforded by a widely spreading scent. The person who draws his perfumed handkerchief from his pocket treats everyone around and beside him against their will and compels them, if they want to breathe, to partake at the same time; which is also why it has gone out of fashion.[^**] — Among the formative arts I would give the preference to ***painting***: partly because, as the art of drawing, it underlies all the other formative arts; partly because it can penetrate far more deeply into the region of ideas and can also, in accordance with them, expand the field of intuition more than is permitted to the others.

[^*]: I must confess: that a fine poem has always afforded me an unmixed pleasure, whereas the reading of the best speech of a Roman popular orator, or of a present-day parliamentary or pulpit orator, has always been mingled with the disagreeable feeling of disapproval of an underhanded art that knows how to move human beings, like machines, to a judgment on important matters which, in calm reflection, must lose all weight with them. Eloquence and fine speech (together, rhetoric) belong to fine art; but the orator's art (_ars oratoria_), as the art of using human weaknesses for one's own purposes (however well-meant these purposes may always be, or even actually good), is worthy of no ***esteem*** whatever. Moreover, it rose to its highest level, both in Athens and in Rome, only in an age when the commonwealth was rushing toward its downfall and every genuinely patriotic cast of mind had been extinguished. Whoever, with clear insight into things, has language in his power in respect of its richness and purity, and who, with a fertile imagination fit for the presentation of his ideas, takes a lively heartfelt interest in the true good, is the _vir bonus dicendi peritus_, the orator without art but full of force, such as ***Cicero*** would have him, though without himself having always remained faithful to this ideal.
[^**]: Those who have recommended, for domestic devotional exercises, the singing of hymns as well, did not consider that by such ***noisy*** (and for that very reason commonly pharisaical) devotion they impose a great burden on the public, in that they compel the neighborhood either to sing along or to lay aside their train of thought.
