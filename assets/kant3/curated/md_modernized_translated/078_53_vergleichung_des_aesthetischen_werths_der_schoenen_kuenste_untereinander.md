## § 53. Comparison of the Aesthetic Value of the Beautiful Arts with One Another

Among them all, ***poetry*** (which owes its origin almost entirely to genius and is least willing to be guided by rules or examples) asserts the highest rank. It expands the mind by setting the imagination at liberty and by presenting, within the limits of a given concept, among the unlimited multiplicity of possible forms that agree with it, that which connects the presentation of this concept with a wealth of thought to which no linguistic expression is fully adequate, and which thus raises itself aesthetically to ideas. It strengthens the mind by letting it feel its free, self-active power, independent of natural determination, to contemplate and judge nature as appearance according to aspects that nature does not offer of itself in experience for either sense or understanding, and thus to use it for the behoof and, as it were, as the schema of the supersensible. It plays with {{{ 327 }}} the illusion which it produces at will, without yet deceiving thereby; for it declares its occupation itself to be a mere play which can nonetheless {{ 216 }} be used purposively by the understanding and for its business. — Oratory, so far as by this is understood the art of persuading, i.e., of deceiving by means of a beautiful illusion (as _ars oratoria_), and not mere eloquence (eloquence and style), is a dialectic which borrows from poetry only as much as is necessary to win minds over to the orator's advantage before judgment is passed, and to deprive them of their freedom; it can therefore be recommended neither for the bar nor for the pulpit. For when it is a matter of civil laws, of the rights of individual persons, or of lasting instruction and determination of minds to the correct knowledge and conscientious performance of their duty: then it is beneath the dignity of so important a business to let even a trace of the luxuriance of wit and imagination show, and still more so of the art of persuading and of captivating to someone's advantage. For even though it may sometimes be applied to intentions that are in themselves lawful and praiseworthy, it yet becomes reprehensible in that in this way maxims and dispositions are subjectively corrupted, even if the action is objectively lawful: since it is not enough to do that which is right, but it must also be performed solely on the ground that it is right. Moreover, the mere distinct concept of these kinds of human affairs, connected with a {{ 217 }} lively presentation in examples and without violating the rules of the harmony of language or the propriety of expression for ideas of reason (which together constitute eloquence), already has in itself sufficient influence on human minds, so that it is not necessary to employ the machinery of persuasion here; which, since they can just as well be used to palliate or cover up vice and error, cannot entirely eliminate the secret suspicion of a cunning deception. In poetry everything proceeds honestly and sincerely. It declares itself to intend only to play an entertaining game with the imagination, and that in a form in accordance with the laws of the understanding; and does not demand to catch the understanding off guard and entangle it by means of sensory presentation.[^*]

{{{ 328 }}} After poetry, I would place that art which, ***if we are concerned with charm and the movement {{ 218 }} of the mind***, comes closest to it among the speaking arts and can also be very naturally combined with it, namely, the ***art of tone***. For although it speaks by means of mere sensations without concepts, and thus does not, like poetry, leave something behind for reflection, it yet moves the mind in a more manifold and, though only temporarily, yet more intimate manner; it is, however, to be sure, more enjoyment than culture (the play of thoughts which is incidentally excited thereby is merely the effect of an, as it were, mechanical association); and, judged by reason, it has less value than any other of the beautiful arts. Hence, like every enjoyment, it requires frequent change and does not bear repeated repetition without producing weariness. The charm of this art, which can be so universally communicated, seems to rest on this: {{ 219 }} that every expression of language in a given context has a tone that is appropriate to its sense; that this tone indicates more or less an affect of the speaker and in turn produces the same in the hearer, which then conversely excites in the latter the same idea that is expressed in language with such a tone; and that, just as modulation is, as it were, a universal language of sensations comprehensible to every human being, so the art of tone employs this language for itself alone in its full emphasis, namely as the language of affects, and thus according to the law of association universally communicates the aesthetic ideas naturally connected therewith; but that, because those {{{ 329 }}} aesthetic ideas are not concepts and determinate thoughts, the form of the composition of these sensations (harmony and melody) serves only, instead of the form of a language, by means of a proportionate tuning of them (which, because in the case of tones it rests on the ratio of the number of vibrations of the air in the same time, insofar as the tones are combined simultaneously or successively, can be brought mathematically under certain rules) to express the aesthetic idea of a coherent whole of an unspeakable wealth of thought in accordance with a certain theme which constitutes the ruling affect in the piece. On this mathematical form, although not represented by determinate {{ 220 }} concepts, alone depends the satisfaction which mere reflection on such a multitude of accompanying or succeeding sensations connects with this play of them as a condition of its beauty valid for everyone; and it is this form alone according to which taste may venture to pronounce a right beforehand concerning the judgment of everyone.

But in the charm and the movement of the mind which music produces, mathematics certainly has not the least share; rather, it is only the indispensable condition (_conditio sine qua non_) of that proportion of impressions in their combination as well as their alternation which makes it possible to grasp them together and to prevent them from destroying one another, but rather to make them harmonize toward a continuous movement and animation of the mind by means of affects consonant therewith, and thus toward a comfortable self-enjoyment.

If, on the other hand, we estimate the value of the beautiful arts according to the culture they afford the mind, and take as a standard the expansion of the faculties which must come together in the power of judgment for cognition: then music has, among the beautiful arts, the lowest place (just as it has perhaps the highest among those which are estimated at the same time according to their agreeableness), because it merely plays with sensations. The formative arts therefore far precede it in this {{ 221 }} respect; for by placing the imagination in a free and yet at the same time appropriate play for the understanding, they at the same time carry on a business, in that they bring about a product which serves as a lasting vehicle, recommending itself to them, for the concepts of the understanding, to promote their union with sensibility and thus, as it were, the urbanity of the higher cognitive powers. {{{ 330 }}} Both kinds of arts take a completely different path: the former from sensations to indeterminate ideas; the second kind, however, from determinate ideas to sensations. The latter are of a ***lasting***, the former only of a ***transitory*** impression. The imagination can recall the former and entertain itself agreeably with them; the latter, however, are either entirely extinguished, or, if they are involuntarily repeated by the imagination, they are rather troublesome to us than agreeable. Besides, a certain lack of urbanity attaches to music, in that, primarily because of the character of its instruments, it extends its influence further than is desired (upon the neighborhood) and thus, as it were, obtrudes itself, consequently detracting from the freedom of others outside the musical party; which the arts that speak to the eyes do not do, since one has only to turn one's eyes away if one does not wish to admit their impression. It is with this almost as {{ 222 }} with the entertainment afforded by a far-spreading odor. He who pulls his perfumed handkerchief out of his pocket treats all around and next to him against their will, and forces them, if they wish to breathe, to enjoy it at the same time; hence it has also gone out of fashion.[^**] — Among the formative arts, I would give the preference to ***painting***: partly because, as the art of drawing, it lies at the foundation of all other formative arts; partly because it can penetrate much further into the region of ideas, and also expand the field of intuition more in accordance with them than is permitted to the others.

[^*]: I must confess: that a beautiful poem has always given me a pure pleasure, whereas the reading of the best speech of a Roman popular orator or of a contemporary parliamentary or pulpit orator was at all times mixed with the unpleasant feeling of disapproval of an insidious art, which knows how to move human beings like machines in important matters to a judgment that must lose all weight with them upon calm reflection. Eloquence and well-speaking (together rhetoric) belong to beautiful art; but the orator's art (_ars oratoria_), as the art of using the weaknesses of human beings for one's own purposes (let these be ever so well-meant or even actually good as they please), is worthy of no ***respect*** at all. Moreover, it rose to the highest level both in Athens and in Rome only at a time when the state was hastening to its ruin and true patriotic way of thinking was extinct. He who with clear insight into matters has language in his power in accordance with its wealth and purity, and with a fertile imagination capable of presenting his ideas takes a lively interest of heart in the truly good, is the _vir bonus dicendi peritus_, the orator without art but full of emphasis, as ***Cicero*** would have him, without, however, always remaining true to this ideal himself.
[^**]: Those who have recommended the singing of spiritual songs as part of domestic devotion did not consider that they impose a great nuisance upon the public by such a ***noisy*** (and for that very reason usually pharisaical) devotion, since they force the neighborhood either to join in singing or to lay aside their thought-work.
