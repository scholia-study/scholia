## § 53. Comparison of the aesthetic value of the fine arts with one another

Of them all, ***the art of poetry***—indebted for its origin almost wholly to genius, and least willing of any to be steered by precept or by example—lays claim to the foremost rank. It broadens the mind in that it releases the imagination into freedom and, within the bounds of a given concept and among the unlimited variety of possible forms consonant with it, brings forward the one form that unites the presentation of that concept with a fullness of thought which no linguistic expression ever wholly matches, and so raises itself aesthetically to ideas. It strengthens the mind by letting it feel its own capacity—free, spontaneously active, and independent of any determination through nature—to survey and appraise nature as appearance under aspects that experience does not of its own accord offer, either to sense or to the understanding, and so to enlist it for the purposes of the supersensible and, as it were, as its schema. It toys with the {{{ 327 }}} semblance it calls forth at its own pleasure, and yet deceives no one in doing so; for it openly declares its occupation to be mere play, which all the same {{ 216 }} the understanding can turn to purposive account for its own business. — Eloquent oratory—taken as the art of persuasion, in other words of misleading through beautiful semblance (as _ars oratoria_), rather than as mere skill in speaking well (eloquence and style)—amounts to a dialectic that draws upon poetry only so far as it must, so as to sway minds toward the speaker, for his own benefit, before any judgment is reached, and to strip them of their freedom; hence it can be commended neither at the bar nor from the pulpit. For where civil laws are at stake, or the rights of particular individuals, or the enduring edification of minds and their direction toward a proper acquaintance with their duty and its scrupulous discharge, it falls below the dignity of so weighty an undertaking to betray even the faintest hint of exuberance in wit and imagination—and less still any trace of the art of talking people round and winning them over to someone's benefit. For granted that it may now and then be put to ends that are, in themselves, rightful and worthy of praise, it becomes reprehensible all the same, because in this fashion the maxims and dispositions are corrupted at the subjective level, however lawful the deed may be objectively: for merely doing what is right does not suffice; one must also carry it out on that ground alone—namely, because it is right. Besides, the plain distinct concept of such kinds of human business—joined to a {{ 217 }} vivid illustration through examples, and committing no breach of the rules governing the euphony of language or the seemliness of expression suited to ideas of reason (which taken together constitute fine speech)—already carries in itself influence enough upon human minds that there is no call to enlist, over and above it, the machinery of persuasion; and this machinery, precisely because it lends itself equally to varnishing over or masking vice and error, can never quite root out the covert suspicion of a contrived deception. In the art of poetry everything proceeds honestly and sincerely. It avows that it means to conduct a merely diverting play with the imagination, one whose form accords with the laws of the understanding; and it makes no claim to creep up on the understanding and ensnare it by means of sensible presentation.[^*]

{{{ 328 }}} Next after poetry I would rank—***where what is at issue is charm and the stirring {{ 218 }} of the mind***—that art which, of all the speaking arts, stands nearest to it and blends with it, moreover, quite naturally, namely ***the art of tone***. For though it does indeed speak by means of sensations alone, devoid of concepts, and thus, unlike poetry, leaves nothing behind for one to ponder, it nevertheless stirs the mind in more varied fashion and—transient though this be—more intimately; yet it is, to be sure, enjoyment rather than culture (the byplay of thought that it rouses along the way is nothing but the upshot of an association that is, so to speak, mechanical); and, weighed by reason, it ranks below every other of the fine arts in worth. Hence, like every enjoyment, it demands frequent change and does not bear repeated reiteration without producing weariness. The charm it exerts, being so universally communicable, appears to {{ 219 }} rest upon the following: that each expression of a language carries, within its connected use, a tone fitted to that expression's sense; that such a tone marks, to a greater or lesser degree, an affect in the one speaking and calls forth the same, reciprocally, in the one listening, in whom it in turn kindles the very idea that language voices by means of such a tone; and that, just as modulation amounts to a kind of universal language of sensations intelligible to every human being, so the art of tone wields this language by itself alone, in its whole force, that is, as the language of the affects, and in this way, obeying the law of association, imparts to all the aesthetic ideas that are by nature bound up with it; but that, since those {{{ 329 }}} aesthetic ideas are neither concepts nor determinate thoughts, the manner in which these sensations are put together (harmony and melody) takes the place merely of the form of a language, serving—through a proportioned attunement of those sensations (which, seeing that with tones it depends on the ratio between the number of vibrations of the air within one and the same span of time, insofar as the tones are joined whether simultaneously or in succession, can be set mathematically under certain rules)—to express the aesthetic idea of a coherent whole of an unnameable fullness of thought, answering to a certain theme that makes up the affect reigning in the piece. It is upon this mathematical form—even though determinate {{ 220 }} concepts do not represent it—that the whole of the satisfaction depends, the satisfaction which mere reflection upon so great a throng of sensations, whether these accompany or succeed one another, attaches to their interplay as a condition, holding for all alike, of its beauty; and this form alone is that by which taste may venture to lay claim, ahead of time, to a right over the judgment of every person.

Yet mathematics certainly plays not the least part in the charm and the emotional stirring that music brings about; it is merely the unavoidable prerequisite (_conditio sine qua non_) for the proportion among these impressions, both as they are joined together and as they succeed one another, which makes it possible to gather them into a unity, keeping them from cancelling each other out and letting them instead coalesce, through affects that resonate with them, into an uninterrupted stirring and animation of the mind, and so into an agreeable enjoyment of oneself.

If, by contrast, one appraises the worth of the fine arts according to the culture they impart to the mind, taking as one's yardstick the enlargement of those powers that have to converge, within the power of judgment, toward cognition, then music occupies, in that regard, the lowest rank among the fine arts (whereas among those prized at once for their agreeableness it stands perhaps at the top), since it does no more than play with sensations. The formative arts, accordingly, far outstrip it in this {{ 221 }} regard; for by setting the imagination into a play that is free and yet, at the same time, answerable to the understanding, they carry on a business alongside, since what they bring forth is a product that supplies the concepts of the understanding with a lasting vehicle, one commending itself to those very concepts, so as to advance their union with sensibility and thereby, so to speak, the urbanity of the higher powers of cognition. {{{ 330 }}} Arts of the two kinds follow wholly divergent paths: the one proceeds from sensations up to indeterminate ideas, whereas the other sets out from determinate ideas and moves toward sensations. An impression of the ***lasting*** sort belongs to the latter, whereas the former leave only a ***transitory*** one. The imagination can recall the latter and pleasantly entertain itself with them; the former, however, either fade away entirely, or, when they are involuntarily repeated by the imagination, are irksome to us rather than pleasant. Music, moreover, is dogged by a certain want of urbanity, in that—owing chiefly to the make of its instruments—it spreads its influence wider than is asked of it (into the neighborhood) and so, as it were, obtrudes itself, and thereby curtails the freedom of others who stand outside the musical gathering; the arts that address themselves to the eyes commit no such trespass, for one has merely to look away whenever one would rather not let their impression in. The case here is much like that {{ 222 }} of the pleasure one takes in a scent that diffuses itself far and wide. The person who draws his perfumed handkerchief from his pocket treats everyone around and beside him against their will and compels them, if they want to breathe, to partake at the same time; which is also why it has gone out of fashion.[^**] — Of the formative arts my own preference would go to ***painting***: in part because, being the art of drawing, it lies at the foundation of all the rest of them; in part because it can press far deeper into the region of ideas and, conformably to these, can widen the field of intuition to a greater extent than the others are allowed.

[^*]: I must confess: that a fine poem has always afforded me an unmixed pleasure, whereas the reading of the best speech of a Roman popular orator, or of a present-day parliamentary or pulpit orator, has always been mingled with the disagreeable feeling of disapproval of an underhanded art that knows how to move human beings, like machines, to a judgment on important matters which, in calm reflection, must lose all weight with them. Eloquence and fine speech (together, rhetoric) belong to fine art; but the orator's art (_ars oratoria_), as the art of using human weaknesses for one's own purposes (however well-meant these purposes may always be, or even actually good), is worthy of no ***esteem*** whatever. Moreover, it rose to its highest level, both in Athens and in Rome, only in an age when the commonwealth was rushing toward its downfall and every genuinely patriotic cast of mind had been extinguished. Whoever, with clear insight into things, has language in his power in respect of its richness and purity, and who, with a fertile imagination fit for the presentation of his ideas, takes a lively heartfelt interest in the true good, is the _vir bonus dicendi peritus_, the orator without art but full of force, such as ***Cicero*** would have him, though without himself having always remained faithful to this ideal.
[^**]: Those who have recommended, for domestic devotional exercises, the singing of hymns as well, did not consider that by such ***noisy*** (and for that very reason commonly pharisaical) devotion they impose a great burden on the public, in that they compel the neighborhood either to sing along or to lay aside their train of thought.
