## § 18. What the modality of a judgment of taste is

Of every representation I can say: at least that it is ***possible*** that it (as cognition) be connected with a pleasure. Of that which I call ***agreeable*** I say that it ***actually*** produces pleasure in me. But of the ***beautiful*** one thinks that it has a necessary relation to satisfaction. This necessity, now, is of a special kind: not a theoretical objective necessity, where it can be cognized _a priori_ {{{ 237 }}} that everyone ***will feel*** this satisfaction in the object that I call beautiful; nor a practical one, where, through concepts of a pure will of reason serving as a rule for freely acting beings, this satisfaction is the necessary consequence of an objective law and signifies nothing other than that one absolutely (without any further aim) ought to act in a certain way. Rather, as a necessity that is thought in an aesthetic judgment, it can only be called ***exemplary***, i.e., a necessity of the assent of ***all*** to a judgment that is regarded as an example of a universal {{ 63 }} rule that one cannot specify. Since an aesthetic judgment is not an objective and cognitive judgment, this necessity cannot be derived from determinate concepts and is thus not apodictic. Much less can it be inferred from the universality of experience (from a thoroughgoing unanimity of judgments about the beauty of a certain object). For not only would experience hardly furnish sufficiently many proofs for this, but no concept of the necessity of these judgments can be grounded on empirical judgments.
