## § 18. What the modality of a judgment of taste is

Of any representation whatever I can say at least this much: that it is ***possible*** for it, as cognition, to carry a pleasure along with it. Concerning what I term ***agreeable***, I assert that it ***actually*** does arouse pleasure within me. The ***beautiful***, however, we conceive as standing in a necessary connection with satisfaction. This necessity is, then, of a special sort: not a theoretical and objective one, by which it can be cognized _a priori_ {{{ 237 }}} that everyone ***is going to feel*** this satisfaction toward the object I have termed beautiful; and equally not a practical necessity, in which — through concepts of a pure will of reason, one that furnishes freely acting beings with their rule — this satisfaction is the necessary consequence of an objective law and signifies nothing more than that one is unconditionally required, apart from any further intention, to proceed in a certain manner. Instead, as a necessity conceived within an aesthetic judgment, it can be called only ***exemplary*** — that is, a necessity that ***all*** assent to a judgment which is looked upon as an example of a universal {{ 63 }} rule that one is unable to specify. Because an aesthetic judgment is neither objective nor a judgment of cognition, this necessity admits of no derivation from determinate concepts, and so it is not apodictic. Far less can it be inferred from the generality of experience (that is, from some complete accord among people's judgments regarding the beauty of a given object). For, quite apart from the fact that experience could hardly muster instances enough to serve here, no concept of the necessity of such judgments is capable of resting on empirical judgments.
