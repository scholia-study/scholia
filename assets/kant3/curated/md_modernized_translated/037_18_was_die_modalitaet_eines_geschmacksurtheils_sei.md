## § 18. What the modality of a judgment of taste is

Of any representation whatever I can say at least this much: that it is ***possible*** for it, as cognition, to carry a pleasure along with it. Concerning what I term ***agreeable***, I assert that it ***actually*** does arouse pleasure within me. The ***beautiful***, however, we conceive as standing in a necessary connection with satisfaction. This necessity, however, is of a peculiar kind: not a theoretical objective necessity, under which it would be cognized _a priori_ {{{ 237 }}} that this satisfaction ***will be felt*** by everyone in the object that I call beautiful; nor a practical one, where this satisfaction, by way of concepts of a pure will of reason that serves freely acting beings as a rule, follows from an objective law as its necessary result and means nothing other than that one absolutely ought (without any further aim) to act in a certain way. Rather, the necessity that is thought in an aesthetic judgment can be termed only ***exemplary*** — that is, a necessity of the assent of ***everyone*** to a judgment regarded as an instance of a universal {{ 63 }} rule that cannot be stated. Because an aesthetic judgment is neither objective nor a judgment of cognition, this necessity admits of no derivation from determinate concepts, and so it is not apodictic. Far less can it be inferred from the generality of experience (that is, from some complete accord among people's judgments regarding the beauty of a given object). For, quite apart from the fact that experience could hardly muster instances enough to serve here, no concept of the necessity of such judgments is capable of resting on empirical judgments.
