## § 86. On ethicotheology

It is a judgment that not even the commonest understanding can dismiss when it ponders the being of the things found in the world, and the world's own existence: namely, that all the manifold creatures, however great the artful arrangement and however manifold the purposively interrelated connection in which they may stand, indeed even the whole of so many systems of them, which we improperly call worlds, would be there for nothing were there not human beings (rational beings in general) within them; that is, that without the human being the whole of creation would amount to a mere wasteland, pointless and devoid of any final end. But it is also not the cognitive faculty of the human being (theoretical reason) in relation to which the being of everything else in the world first acquires its worth, as if in order that someone might be there who could ***contemplate*** the world. For if this contemplation of the world nonetheless presented to him nothing but things without a final end, then {{ 411 }} no worth could accrue to the being of these things from their being cognized; and one must already presuppose a final end of them in relation to which the very contemplation of the world would have a worth. Nor is it the feeling of pleasure and the sum of it in relation to which we think a final end of creation as given, that is, not well-being, enjoyment (be it bodily or spiritual), in a word happiness, by which we estimate that absolute worth. For: that the human being, once he exists, makes this his own final aim yields no concept of why he then exists at all, and what worth he then himself has, so as to make his existence agreeable to him. {{{ 443 }}} He must therefore already be presupposed as the final end of creation in order to have a ground of reason for why nature must harmonize toward his happiness when it is regarded as an absolute totality in accordance with principles of ends. — Hence it is only the faculty of desire: yet not the one that makes him dependent on nature (through sensible impulses), not that in respect of which the worth of his existence rests on what he receives and enjoys, but rather the worth that he alone can give himself and that consists in what he does, in how and according to what principles he acts not as a member of nature but in the ***freedom*** of his faculty of desire; that is, a {{ 412 }} good will is that whereby his existence alone can have an absolute worth and in relation to which the existence of the world can have a ***final end***.

The commonest judgment of sound human reason likewise accords completely with this: namely, that the human being can be a final end of creation only as a moral being, provided one directs the judging solely to this question and prompts it to make the attempt. What good is it, one will say, that this human being has so much talent, that he is even very active with it and thereby exercises a useful influence on the commonwealth, and so has a great worth in relation both to his circumstances of fortune and to the benefit of others, if he possesses no good will? He is a contemptible object when one regards him according to his inner self; and if creation is not to be everywhere without a final end, then he, who as a human being also belongs to it, must nonetheless, as an evil human being in a world under moral laws, in accordance with these forfeit his subjective end (happiness), as the sole condition under which his existence can coexist with the final end.

Now if we encounter arrangements of ends in the world and, as reason unavoidably demands, subordinate the ends that are only conditioned to an unconditioned supreme one, that is, to a final end, then one first easily sees that in that case it is not a question of an end of nature (within {{ 413 }} nature), insofar as it exists, but of the end of its existence together with all its arrangements, and hence of the last ***end of creation***, and in this really also of the supreme condition under which alone a final end (that is, the determining ground of a highest understanding for the production of worldly beings) can take place.

{{{ 444 }}} Since we now recognize the human being as the end of creation only as a moral being, we possess, to begin with, a ground—or at any rate the chief condition—for viewing the world as a whole cohering in accordance with ends and as a system of final causes; but above all, for that relation of natural ends to an intelligent world-cause which the constitution of our reason makes necessary for us, we possess ***a principle*** for thinking the nature and the attributes of this first cause as the supreme ground within the realm of ends, and so for determining the concept of it: something physical teleology could not accomplish, since it was able to prompt only indeterminate concepts of that cause, concepts that were for just this reason unfit alike for theoretical and for practical use.

Proceeding from this thus-determined principle of the original being's causality, we shall be obliged to conceive it not only as an intelligence legislating for nature, but likewise as the sovereign lawgiver within a moral realm of ends. With a view to the ***highest*** ***good*** that is possible under its governance {{ 414 }} alone—namely, the existence of rational beings subject to moral laws—we shall conceive this original being as ***omniscient***, in order that even the inmost of their dispositions (wherein the genuine moral worth of the deeds of rational worldly beings resides) not lie concealed from it; as ***omnipotent***, in order that it be able to render all of nature adequate to this supreme end; as ***all-good*** and, together with this, ***just***, since these two attributes (which, conjoined, yield ***wisdom***) make up the conditions upon which rests the causality of a highest world-cause regarded as a supreme good under moral laws; and, in the same manner, we shall have to ascribe to it every further transcendental attribute besides, such as ***eternity***, ***omnipresence***, and so on. (for goodness and justice are moral attributes), which are presupposed with a view to such a final end. — In this way ***moral*** teleology supplements the deficiency of ***physical*** teleology and for the first time grounds a ***theology***; whereas the latter, if it did not borrow unnoticed from the former but were to proceed consistently, could by itself ground nothing but a ***demonology***, which is incapable of any determinate concept.

But the principle of relating the world—on account of the moral determination of ends of certain beings within it—to a supreme cause, {{ 415 }} as a deity, does this not merely by supplementing the physico-teleological ground of proof and thus necessarily taking the latter as its basis; rather, it is also ***by itself*** sufficient for this, and it directs the attention {{{ 445 }}} to the ends of nature and the investigation of the incomprehensibly great art that lies hidden behind their forms, in order to give the ideas that pure practical reason supplies an incidental confirmation from the ends of nature. For the concept of worldly beings standing under moral laws is an _a priori_ principle by which the human being is necessarily bound to judge himself. That, furthermore, if there is anywhere a world-cause acting intentionally and directed toward an end, that moral relation must be the condition of the possibility of a creation just as necessarily as the one according to physical laws (namely, if that intelligent cause also has a final end): this too reason regards _a priori_ as a principle necessary for it in the teleological judging of the existence of things. Now everything depends solely on this: whether we have any ground sufficient for reason (be it speculative or practical) to attribute a ***final end*** to the supreme cause that acts according to ends. For that in that case this end, given the subjective constitution of our reason, and even however we may ever think the reason of other beings, could be no other than ***the {{ 416 }} human being under moral laws***: can hold for us as certain _a priori_; whereas, by contrast, the ends of nature in the physical order can in no way be cognized _a priori_, and above all it can in no way be seen that a nature could not exist without such ends.

Remark.

Place a human being in the moments when his mind is attuned to moral feeling! When, surrounded by a beautiful nature, he finds himself in a calm, serene enjoyment of his existence, he feels within himself a need to be grateful to someone for it. Or let him find himself another time in the same frame of mind amid a press of duties that he can and will satisfy only through voluntary sacrifice; then he feels within himself a need to have thereby at the same time carried out something commanded and obeyed a supreme lord. Or suppose he has, perhaps thoughtlessly, transgressed against his duty, though without thereby becoming answerable to any human being; then the stern self-reproaches will nonetheless speak a language within him, as if they were the voice of a judge to whom he had to render {{{ 446 }}} an account of it. In a word: he needs a moral intelligence, so that for the end for which he exists he may have a being which, in accordance with this end, is the cause of him and of the world. To contrive incentives behind these feelings is futile; for they are immediately bound up with the purest moral disposition, because ***gratitude***, ***obedience***, and ***humbling*** (submission to {{ 417 }} deserved chastisement) are particular attunements of mind toward duty, and the mind inclined toward the enlargement of its moral disposition here voluntarily thinks for itself merely an object that is not in the world, so as, if possible, to prove its duty even toward such a being. It is therefore at least possible, and the ground for it lies in the moral way of thinking, to represent to oneself a pure moral need for the existence of a being under which either our morality gains more strength or also (at least according to our representation) more scope, namely a new object for its exercise; that is, to assume a morally legislative being outside the world, without any regard to theoretical proof, still less to selfish interest, on a pure moral ground free of all extraneous influence (though admittedly only a subjective one), upon the mere commendation of a pure practical reason that legislates entirely on its own. And although such an attunement of mind occurs seldom, or also does not last long, but passes by fleetingly and without lasting effect, or also passes by without any reflection on the object represented in such a shadow-image and without any effort to bring it under distinct concepts: still the ground for it—the moral predisposition in us, as a subjective principle, not to content itself, in contemplating the world, with its purposiveness through natural causes, but to lay at its basis a supreme cause governing nature according to moral principles—is unmistakable. — To which is further added that we feel ourselves impelled by the moral law to strive after a universal highest end, yet feel ourselves, and the whole of nature as well, incapable of attaining it; that only insofar as we strive after it may we judge ourselves to be in conformity with the final end of an intelligent world-cause (if {{ 418 }} there were such a one); and thus practical reason harbors a purely moral basis for positing this cause (which may be done without any contradiction), if for nothing more, then at least so that we not run the risk of taking that striving to be wholly idle in its effects and thereby letting it flag.

{{{ 447 }}} With all this only so much is to be said here: that ***fear*** was indeed able first to produce ***gods*** (demons), but ***reason***, by means of its moral principles, was able first to produce the concept of ***God*** (even when one was, as is usual, very ignorant in the teleology of nature, or also, because of the difficulty of reconciling by a sufficiently established principle the appearances that here contradict one another, very doubtful); and that the inner ***moral*** determination of ends of his existence supplemented what was lacking in the knowledge of nature, by directing us, for the final end of the existence of all things—for which no principle satisfies reason except an ***ethical*** one—to think the supreme cause with properties by which it is capable of subjecting the whole of nature to that single aim (to which nature is merely an instrument), (that is, as a ***deity***).
