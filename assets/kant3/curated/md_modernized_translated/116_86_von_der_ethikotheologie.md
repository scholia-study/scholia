## § 86. On ethicotheology

Here is a verdict from which even the most ordinary understanding cannot free itself, once it reflects on the being of the things in the world and on the existence of the world itself: namely, that all these manifold creatures—however great the artful contrivance and however varied the purposive interconnection binding them to one another may be, nay, even the entire totality of so many such systems, which we wrongly term worlds—would exist to no purpose if there were no human beings (rational beings as such) among them; in other words, that apart from the human being the whole creation would be nothing but a barren waste, in vain and without any final end. But it is also not the cognitive faculty of the human being (theoretical reason) in relation to which the being of everything else in the world first acquires its worth, as if in order that someone might be there who could ***contemplate*** the world. For if this contemplation of the world nonetheless presented to him nothing but things without a final end, then {{ 411 }} no worth could accrue to the being of these things from their being cognized; and one must already presuppose a final end of them in relation to which the very contemplation of the world would have a worth. Nor is it the feeling of pleasure and the sum of it in relation to which we think a final end of creation as given, that is, not well-being, enjoyment (be it bodily or spiritual), in a word happiness, by which we estimate that absolute worth. For: that the human being, once he exists, makes this his own final aim yields no concept of why he then exists at all, and what worth he then himself has, so as to make his existence agreeable to him. {{{ 443 }}} He must therefore already be presupposed as the final end of creation in order to have a ground of reason for why nature must harmonize toward his happiness when it is regarded as an absolute totality in accordance with principles of ends. — It is, then, solely the faculty of desire that is at issue here—though not the faculty by which sensible impulses render him beholden to nature, nor the one with regard to which the worth of his being would depend on what he takes in and relishes, but rather the worth he can confer on himself and himself alone, a worth lying in what he does, in the manner and the principles by which he acts, no longer as a link in nature but from the ***freedom*** of his faculty of desire; in short, a {{ 412 }} good will is what alone lends his existence an absolute worth, and it is in reference to such a will that the world's existence can attain a ***final end***.

With this, too, the most ordinary verdict of healthy human reason agrees perfectly: namely, that only as a moral being is the human being capable of being a final end of creation, provided one steers the appraisal to this question alone and induces it to make the trial. Of what use is it, someone will object, that this human being is endowed with so much talent, that he is even highly industrious in employing it and so brings a beneficial influence to bear upon the community, thereby possessing great worth alike as regards his own fortunate circumstances and the advantage of others, if he has no good will? Viewed with respect to his inner self, he is an object worthy of contempt; and should creation not be everywhere destitute of a final end, then this human being—who, as a human being, likewise belongs to it—must, being an evil one in a world under moral laws, in conformity with those laws lose his subjective end (namely happiness), that being the only condition upon which his existence is able to stand together with the final end.

Suppose now that we come upon orderings of ends in the world and, as reason inescapably requires, place the ends that are merely conditioned beneath an unconditioned and supreme one, that is, beneath a final end: then it is readily apparent at the outset that what is then in question is not an end of nature (within {{ 413 }} nature), insofar as nature exists, but rather the end of its existence along with all its arrangements, and thus the ultimate ***end of creation***, and, within that, properly the supreme condition under which alone a final end can occur—that is, the ground on which a highest understanding is determined to bring forth the beings of the world.

{{{ 444 }}} Now, inasmuch as we acknowledge the human being to be the aim of creation solely in his character as a moral being, we have, in the first place, a ground—or at least the principal condition—for regarding the world as a totality whose members cohere according to ends, and likewise as a system of final causes; but chiefly, with respect to that connection of natural ends to an intelligent world-cause which is made necessary for us by the constitution of our reason, we have ***a principle*** by which to conceive the nature and properties of this first cause as the highest ground in the realm of ends, and thereby to fix its concept: which physical teleology was unable to do, since it could give rise only to indeterminate concepts of that cause—concepts that were, for precisely this reason, useless alike for theoretical and for practical employment.

Proceeding from this thus-determined principle of the original being's causality, we shall be obliged to conceive it not only as an intelligence legislating for nature, but likewise as the sovereign lawgiver within a moral realm of ends. With a view to the ***highest*** ***good*** that is possible under its governance {{ 414 }} alone—namely, the existence of rational beings subject to moral laws—we shall conceive this original being as ***omniscient***, in order that even the inmost of their dispositions (wherein the genuine moral worth of the deeds of rational worldly beings resides) not lie concealed from it; as ***omnipotent***, in order that it be able to render all of nature adequate to this supreme end; as ***all-good*** and, together with this, ***just***, since these two attributes (which, conjoined, yield ***wisdom***) make up the conditions upon which rests the causality of a highest world-cause regarded as a supreme good under moral laws; and, in the same manner, we shall have to ascribe to it every further transcendental attribute besides, such as ***eternity***, ***omnipresence***, and so on. (for goodness and justice are moral attributes), which are presupposed with a view to such a final end. — In this way ***moral*** teleology supplements the deficiency of ***physical*** teleology and for the first time grounds a ***theology***; whereas the latter, if it did not borrow unnoticed from the former but were to proceed consistently, could by itself ground nothing but a ***demonology***, which is incapable of any determinate concept.

But the principle of relating the world—on account of the moral determination of ends of certain beings within it—to a supreme cause, {{ 415 }} as a deity, does this not merely by supplementing the physico-teleological ground of proof and thus necessarily taking the latter as its basis; rather, it is also ***by itself*** sufficient for this, and it directs the attention {{{ 445 }}} to the ends of nature and the investigation of the incomprehensibly great art that lies hidden behind their forms, in order to give the ideas that pure practical reason supplies an incidental confirmation from the ends of nature. For the concept of worldly beings standing under moral laws is an _a priori_ principle by which the human being is necessarily bound to judge himself. That, furthermore, if there is anywhere a world-cause acting intentionally and directed toward an end, that moral relation must be the condition of the possibility of a creation just as necessarily as the one according to physical laws (namely, if that intelligent cause also has a final end): this too reason regards _a priori_ as a principle necessary for it in the teleological judging of the existence of things. At this point the whole matter turns on a single question: whether, for reason (be it the speculative or the practical), we possess any sufficient ground for ascribing a ***final end*** to that supreme cause which operates according to ends. For that this end, in that case, could be none other than ***the {{ 416 }} human being under moral laws***—given the subjective constitution of our reason, and indeed however we might ever conceive the reason of other beings—may count for us as certain _a priori_; the ends of nature within the physical order, on the other hand, admit of no _a priori_ cognition whatever, and least of all can it be discerned that a nature devoid of such ends could not exist.

Remark.

Place a human being in the moments when his mind is attuned to moral feeling! When, surrounded by a beautiful nature, he finds himself in a calm, serene enjoyment of his existence, he feels within himself a need to be grateful to someone for it. Or let him find himself another time in the same frame of mind amid a press of duties that he can and will satisfy only through voluntary sacrifice; then he feels within himself a need to have thereby at the same time carried out something commanded and obeyed a supreme lord. Or suppose he has, perhaps thoughtlessly, transgressed against his duty, though without thereby becoming answerable to any human being; then the stern self-reproaches will nonetheless speak a language within him, as if they were the voice of a judge to whom he had to render {{{ 446 }}} an account of it. In a word: he needs a moral intelligence, so that for the end for which he exists he may have a being which, in accordance with this end, is the cause of him and of the world. To contrive incentives behind these feelings is futile; for they are immediately bound up with the purest moral disposition, because ***gratitude***, ***obedience***, and ***humbling*** (submission to {{ 417 }} deserved chastisement) are particular attunements of mind toward duty, and the mind inclined toward the enlargement of its moral disposition here voluntarily thinks for itself merely an object that is not in the world, so as, if possible, to prove its duty even toward such a being. It is therefore at least possible, and the ground for it lies in the moral way of thinking, to represent to oneself a pure moral need for the existence of a being under which either our morality gains more strength or also (at least according to our representation) more scope, namely a new object for its exercise; that is, to assume a morally legislative being outside the world, without any regard to theoretical proof, still less to selfish interest, on a pure moral ground free of all extraneous influence (though admittedly only a subjective one), upon the mere commendation of a pure practical reason that legislates entirely on its own. And even though a disposition of mind of this sort should arise but rarely, or should fail to take firm hold, passing off instead in fleeting fashion and leaving no abiding effect, or should slip away without one's giving any thought to the object that such a shadow-image sets before us and without any endeavor to bring that object under distinct concepts—the ground of it is nonetheless not to be mistaken: namely, the moral predisposition within us, which, as a subjective principle, refuses, in surveying the world, to rest satisfied with its purposiveness as issuing from natural causes, and instead lays beneath it a supreme cause that governs nature according to moral principles. — To this it must be added that the moral law drives us to pursue a universal highest end, while we nonetheless sense that we ourselves, together with the whole of nature, are powerless to reach it; and that only in the measure that we strive toward it are we permitted to judge ourselves as answering to the final end of an intelligent world-cause (supposing {{ 418 }} such a one existed); and thus practical reason has at its disposal a pure moral ground on which to posit such a cause (an assumption that involves no contradiction)—if nothing further, then at least so that we do not run the risk of viewing that endeavor as wholly fruitless in its effects and thereby allowing it to slacken.

{{{ 447 }}} By all of this no more is meant to be said here than this: that ***fear*** was, to be sure, the first thing able to bring forth ***gods*** (demons), whereas it was ***reason***, through the mediation of its moral principles, that first proved able to bring forth the concept of ***God*** (and this even where one was, as commonly happens, quite ignorant in the teleology of nature, or, owing to the difficulty of squaring by some adequately tested principle the appearances that mutually conflict here, quite in doubt); and that the inner ***moral*** determination of the ends of his existence made good what the knowledge of nature had lacked, in that it directed us—regarding that final end for the existence of all things, an end for which no principle satisfies reason save an ***ethical*** one—to conceive the supreme cause as endowed with properties enabling it to subject the whole of nature to that one aim (of which nature is but the instrument), (that is, as a ***deity***).
