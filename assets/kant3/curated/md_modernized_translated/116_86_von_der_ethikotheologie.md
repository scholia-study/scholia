## § 86. On ethico-theology

It is a judgment of which even the commonest understanding cannot rid itself, when it reflects on the existence of the things in the world and the existence of the world itself: namely that all the manifold creatures, of however great an artistic arrangement and however manifold a purposively interrelated connection they may be, indeed even the whole of so many systems of them, which we incorrectly call worlds, would be there for nothing, if there were not human beings (rational beings in general) in them; i.e., that without the human being the whole creation would be a mere wasteland, in vain and without a final end. But it is also not the cognitive faculty of the human being (theoretical reason) in relation to which the existence of everything else in the world first gets its value, say, so that there might be someone there who could ***contemplate*** the world. For if this contemplation of the world made present to him after all nothing but things without a final end, then {{ 411 }} no value can accrue to the existence of the world from the fact that it is cognized; and one must already presuppose a final end of it in relation to which the contemplation of the world itself has a value. Nor is it the feeling of pleasure and the sum of it, in relation to which we think a final end of creation as given, i.e., not well-being, enjoyment (whether bodily or spiritual), in a word happiness, by which we estimate that absolute value. For the fact that, when the human being is there, he makes this into his own final aim, gives no concept of what he is then there for at all and what value he himself then has, so as to make his existence agreeable to him. {{{ 443 }}} He must therefore already be presupposed as the final end of creation, in order to have a rational ground why nature must harmonize with his happiness, when it is considered as an absolute whole according to principles of ends. — Thus it is only the faculty of desire: but not that which makes him dependent on nature (through sensible impulses), not that with regard to which the value of his existence rests on what he receives and enjoys: but rather the value which he alone can give to himself, and which consists in what he does, how and according to what principles he acts not as a member of nature, but in the ***freedom*** of his faculty of desire; i.e., a {{ 412 }} good will is that through which his existence alone can have an absolute value and in relation to which the existence of the world can have a ***final end***.

The commonest judgment of healthy human reason also agrees perfectly with this: namely that the human being can be a final end of creation only as a moral being, if one directs the judging only to this question and occasions it to make the attempt. What does it help, one will say, that this human being has so much talent, that he is even very active with it and thereby exerts a useful influence on the commonwealth and thus, in relation both to his circumstances of fortune and to the benefit of others, has a great value, if he possesses no good will? He is a contemptible object, if one considers him according to his inner being; and if creation is not to be everywhere without a final end, then he, who as a human being also belongs to it, must yet, as an evil human being in a world under moral laws, in accordance with these forfeit his subjective end (happiness), as the sole condition under which his existence can coexist with the final end.

If we now encounter arrangements of ends in the world and, as reason unavoidably demands, subordinate the ends, which are only conditioned, to an unconditioned supreme one, i.e., to a final end: then one sees first easily that there is then talk not of an end of nature (within {{ 413 }} it), insofar as it exists, but rather of the end of its existence with all its arrangements, hence of the ultimate ***end of creation***, and in this also properly of the supreme condition under which alone a final end (i.e., the determining ground of a highest understanding for the production of the world-beings) can take place.

{{{ 444 }}} Since now we recognize the human being only as a moral being as the end of creation: we have first a ground, at least the principal condition, for regarding the world as a whole connected according to ends and as a system of final causes; but chiefly, for the relation of the natural ends to an intelligent world-cause that is for us necessary according to the constitution of our reason, ***a principle*** for thinking the nature and properties of this first cause as the supreme ground in the realm of ends, and so for determining the concept of it: which physical teleology was not able to do, which could occasion only indeterminate and, precisely on that account, useless concepts of it for theoretical as well as for practical use.

From this so determined principle of the causality of the original being we shall have to think it not merely as an intelligence and as legislating for nature, but also as legislative chief in a moral realm of ends. In relation to the ***highest*** ***good*** possible alone under its dominion, {{ 414 }} namely the existence of rational beings under moral laws, we shall think this original being as ***omniscient***: so that even the innermost of the dispositions (which constitutes the proper moral value of the actions of rational world-beings) is not hidden from it; as ***omnipotent***: so that it can make the whole of nature adequate to this highest end; as ***all-good*** and at the same time ***just***: because these two properties (united, ***wisdom***) constitute the conditions of the causality of a supreme cause of the world as the highest good under moral laws; and so too we shall have to think in it all the remaining transcendental properties, such as ***eternity***, ***omnipresence***, etc. (for goodness and justice are moral properties), which are presupposed in relation to such a final end. — In such a way ***moral*** teleology supplements the lack of the ***physical*** and first grounds a ***theology***: since the latter, if it did not unnoticed borrow from the former, but were to proceed consistently, could ground, by itself alone, nothing but a ***demonology***, which is capable of no determinate concept.

But the principle of the relation of the world, on account of the moral determination of ends of certain beings in it, to a supreme cause, {{ 415 }} as deity, does this not merely by supplementing the physico-teleological ground of proof and so necessarily laying this at the basis; rather it is also sufficient for this ***by itself*** and drives the attention {{{ 445 }}} to the ends of nature and the investigation of the incomprehensibly great art lying hidden behind their forms, in order to give to the ideas which pure practical reason provides incidental confirmation in the natural ends. For the concept of world-beings under moral laws is a principle _a priori_ in accordance with which the human being must necessarily judge himself. That, furthermore, if there is at all an intentionally acting world-cause directed toward an end, that moral relation must just as necessarily be the condition of the possibility of a creation as that according to physical laws (if namely that intelligent cause also has a final end): reason also regards _a priori_ as a principle necessary for it for the teleological judging of the existence of things. Now it only comes down to this: whether we have any ground sufficient for reason (whether the speculative or the practical) to ascribe a ***final end*** to the supreme cause acting according to ends. For that then this, according to the subjective constitution of our reason, and even however we may always think the reason of other beings, can be none other than ***the {{ 416 }} human being under moral laws***: can hold as certain _a priori_ for us; whereas, on the contrary, the ends of nature in the physical order cannot at all be cognized _a priori_, and chiefly that a nature without such ends could not exist can in no way be seen.

Remark.

Place a human being in those moments of the attunement of his mind to moral sensation! If he finds himself, surrounded by a beautiful nature, in a calm, serene enjoyment of his existence, then he feels in himself a need to be grateful to someone for it. Or let him another time see himself, in the same disposition of mind, in the press of duties to which he can and will give satisfaction only through voluntary self-sacrifice; then he feels in himself a need to have hereby at the same time carried out something commanded and obeyed a supreme lord. Or let him have perhaps, in a thoughtless way, transgressed against his duty, whereby, however, he has precisely not become answerable to human beings; then the stern self-reproaches will nevertheless conduct a language in him, as if they were the voice of a judge to whom he had {{{ 446 }}} to render account for it. In a word: he needs a moral intelligence, in order to have, for the end for which he exists, a being which, in accordance with this, is the cause of him and of the world. To contrive incentives behind these feelings is futile; for they are connected immediately with the purest moral disposition, because ***gratitude***, ***obedience***, and ***humbling*** (submission to {{ 417 }} deserved chastisement) are special attunements of mind toward duty, and the mind inclined to the enlargement of its moral disposition here only voluntarily thinks an object that is not in the world, in order, where possible, to prove its duty even toward such a one. It is therefore at least possible, and the ground for it lies also in the moral manner of thinking, to represent to oneself a pure moral need for the existence of a being under which either our morality gains more strength or also (at least according to our representation) more extent, namely a new object for its exercise; i.e., to assume a morally legislative being outside the world, without any regard to theoretical proof, still less to selfish interest, on a pure moral ground, free of all alien influence (though indeed only subjective), upon the mere commendation of a pure practical reason legislating by itself alone. And although such an attunement of the mind may occur rarely, or also may not last long, but passes away fleetingly and without lasting effect, or even without any reflection on the object represented in such a shadow-image and without any effort to bring it under distinct concepts: yet the ground for it, the moral predisposition in us, as a subjective principle, not to content itself in the contemplation of the world with its purposiveness through natural causes, but rather to lay at the basis of it a supreme cause governing nature according to moral principles, is unmistakable. — To which there is still to be added, that we feel ourselves pressed by the moral law to strive after a universal highest end, but yet feel ourselves and the whole of nature incapable of attaining it; that we may judge ourselves to be in accordance with the final end of an intelligent world-cause (if {{ 418 }} there were such a one) only insofar as we strive after it; and so there is at hand a pure moral ground of practical reason for assuming this cause (since it can happen without contradiction), if for nothing more, yet so that we do not run the danger of regarding that endeavor in its effects as entirely vain and thereby letting it grow faint.

{{{ 447 }}} By all this only this much is to be said here: that ***fear*** indeed was first able to bring forth ***gods*** (demons), but that ***reason***, by means of its moral principles, was first able to bring forth the concept of ***God*** (even when one was, as is usual, very ignorant in the teleology of nature, or also, on account of the difficulty of reconciling the appearances which contradict one another in this through a sufficiently proven principle, very doubtful); and that the inner ***moral*** determination of ends of his existence supplemented that which the acquaintance with nature lacked, in that it namely directed us, for the final end of the existence of all things, for which the principle is satisfying to reason in no other way than ***ethically***, to think the supreme cause with properties by which it is capable of subjecting the whole of nature to that single intention (to which nature is merely an instrument), (i.e., as a ***deity***).
