## General remark on the first section of the Analytic

If one draws the result from the above analyses, it turns out that everything comes down to the concept of taste: that it is a faculty for judging an object in relation to the {{ 69 }} ***free lawfulness*** of the imagination. If now in the judgment of taste the imagination must be considered in its freedom, then it is, first, taken not as reproductive, as it is subject to the laws of association, but as productive and self-active (as the author of voluntary forms of possible intuitions); and although in the apprehension of a given object of the senses it is indeed bound to a determinate form of this object and to that extent has no free play (as in poetic invention), yet it can well be conceived: that the object can offer it precisely such a form as contains a {{{ 241 }}} composition of the manifold such as the imagination, if it were left to itself freely, would design in accord with the ***lawfulness of the understanding*** in general. But that the ***imagination*** be ***free*** and yet ***of itself lawful***, i.e., that it carry an autonomy with it, is a contradiction. The understanding alone gives the law. But if the imagination is constrained to proceed according to a determinate law, then its product is determined as to form by concepts, as it ought to be; but then the satisfaction, as shown above, is not that in the beautiful but in the good (perfection, at most merely formal), and the judgment is not a judgment through taste. Thus a lawfulness without a law and a subjective agreement of the imagination with the understanding without an objective one — since the representation is related to a determinate concept of an object — can alone subsist together with the free lawfulness of the understanding (which has also been called purposiveness without an end) and with the peculiarity of a judgment of taste.

Now geometrically regular shapes, a circular figure, a {{ 70 }} square, a cube, etc., are commonly adduced by critics of taste as the simplest and most indubitable examples of beauty; and yet they are called regular precisely because one cannot represent them otherwise than so that they are regarded as mere presentations of a determinate concept that prescribes the rule for that shape (according to which alone it is possible). One of the two must therefore be erroneous: either that judgment of the critics in ascribing beauty to the said shapes; or ours, which finds purposiveness without a concept necessary for beauty.

No one will easily find it necessary that a person of taste have more satisfaction in a circular shape than in a scrawled outline, more in an equilateral and equiangular quadrilateral than in a slanted, unequal-sided, as it were crippled one; for that requires only common understanding and no taste at all. Where an intention is perceived, e.g., to judge the magnitude of a place, or to make comprehensible the relation of the parts to one another and to the whole in a division: there regular shapes, and indeed those of the simplest kind, are necessary; and the satisfaction rests not immediately on the sight of the shape, but on its usefulness {{{ 242 }}} for all sorts of possible intentions. A room whose walls make slanted angles, a garden plot of such a kind, indeed every violation of symmetry, both in the shape of animals (e.g., being one-eyed) and of buildings or of flower beds, displeases because it is contrary to purpose, not only practically with respect to a determinate use of these things, but also for judging in all sorts of possible intentions; which is not the case in the judgment of taste, which, when it is pure, immediately connects satisfaction {{ 71 }} or dissatisfaction, without regard to use or an end, with the mere ***contemplation*** of the object.

The regularity that leads to the concept of an object is indeed the indispensable condition (_conditio sine qua non_) for grasping the object in a single representation and determining the manifold in its form. This determination is an end with respect to cognition; and in relation to this it is also always connected with satisfaction (which accompanies the bringing about of every even merely problematic intention). But it is then merely the approval of the solution that satisfies a problem, and not a free and indeterminately purposive entertainment of the powers of mind with what we call beautiful, and in which the understanding is in the service of the imagination and not the latter in the service of the former.

In a thing that is possible only through an intention, a building, even an animal, the regularity that consists in symmetry must express the unity of intuition that accompanies the concept of the end, and belongs to cognition. But where only a free play of the powers of representation is to be entertained (yet under the condition that the understanding suffer no offense thereby), in pleasure gardens, room decoration, all sorts of tasteful furnishings, and the like, regularity that announces itself as constraint is avoided as much as possible; hence the English taste in gardens, the baroque taste in furniture drives the freedom of the imagination even to the point of approaching the grotesque, and in this separation from all constraint of the rule sets precisely the case where taste can show its {{ 72 }} greatest perfection in projects of the imagination.

Everything stiff-regular (which comes close to mathematical regularity) has the quality of being contrary to taste: that it grants no long entertainment in the contemplation of it, but rather, insofar as it does not {{{ 243 }}} expressly have cognition or a determinate practical end as its intention, produces boredom. By contrast, that with which the imagination can play unstudiedly and purposively is always new to us, and one does not become weary of its sight. ***Marsden***, in his description of Sumatra, makes the remark that the free beauties of nature there surround the spectator everywhere and hence have little that is attractive for him any longer: by contrast, a pepper garden, where the poles on which this plant climbs form avenues between them in parallel lines, when he came upon it in the midst of a forest, had much charm for him; and he concludes from this that wild, apparently irregular beauty pleases, only for variety, only the one who has seen his fill of the regular. But he needed only make the experiment of staying for a day at his pepper garden in order to become aware that, once the understanding has, through the regularity, put itself into the disposition for order that it everywhere requires, the object no longer entertains him, but rather imposes a burdensome constraint on the imagination: whereas the nature there, prodigal in manifoldness to the point of luxuriance, subjected to no constraint of artificial rules, can give constant nourishment to his taste. — Even the song of birds, which we can bring under no musical rule, seems to contain more freedom and therefore more for taste than even a human song that is conducted according to all the rules of the art of music: because one becomes far sooner weary {{ 73 }} of the latter, when it is repeated often and for a long time. But here we presumably exchange our sympathy with the merriment of a small beloved little animal for the beauty of its song, which, when it is imitated quite exactly by a human being (as sometimes happens with the warbling of the nightingale), seems to our ear to be entirely tasteless.

Beautiful objects are further to be distinguished from beautiful views of objects (which often, on account of the distance, can no longer be distinctly recognized). In the latter, taste seems to attach itself not so much to what the imagination ***apprehends*** in this field, as rather to what it thereby gets occasion to ***invent*** poetically, i.e., to the proper fantasies with which the mind entertains itself while it is continually aroused by the manifoldness upon which the eye strikes; just as, for instance, at the sight of the changing shapes of a fire in the fireplace or of a rippling brook, both of which are no {{{ 244 }}} beauties, but yet carry a charm with them for the imagination, because they sustain its free play.
