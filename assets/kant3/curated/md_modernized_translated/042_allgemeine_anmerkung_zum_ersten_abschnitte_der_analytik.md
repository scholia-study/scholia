## General observation on the first section of the Analytic

Gathering together the upshot of the dissections above, one finds that everything reduces to the concept of taste: to wit, that taste amounts to a power of judging an object with reference to the {{ 69 }} ***free lawfulness*** of the imagination. Now if, in the judgment of taste, the imagination must be regarded in its freedom, then it is assumed, first of all, to be not reproductive, as when it stands under the laws of association, but rather productive and self-active (as the author of chosen forms of possible intuitions); and even though, in taking up a given object of sense, it is tied to a determinate form of that object and to this extent enjoys no free play (as it does in poetic invention), one can still readily grasp how the object might hand it just such a form as holds a {{{ 241 }}} composition of the manifold of the kind that the imagination, if left to its own freedom, would sketch in agreement with the ***lawfulness of the understanding*** as such. Yet for the ***imagination to be free*** and at the same time ***lawful of its own accord***, i.e. to carry an autonomy about with it, is contradictory. It is the understanding, and it alone, that supplies the law. Should the imagination, however, be constrained to operate by some determinate law, then what it produces is, in point of form, fixed by concepts as to how it should turn out; but then the satisfaction, as was shown above, is not satisfaction in the beautiful but in the good (in perfection, or at best in merely formal perfection), and the judgment ceases to be one of taste. Hence a lawfulness without any law, together with a merely subjective agreement of the imagination with the understanding that lacks an objective one — the objective sort obtaining where the representation is tied to a determinate concept of an object — is the only thing that can hold together with the free lawfulness of the understanding (which has likewise been styled purposiveness without an end) and with what is distinctive in a judgment of taste.

Geometrically regular figures — a circular figure, a {{ 70 }} square, a cube, and the like — are, to be sure, routinely adduced by critics of taste as the plainest and least questionable instances of beauty; and yet the very ground on which they count as regular is that one can picture them in no other way than as bare presentations of a determinate concept which lays down the rule for that figure (the rule apart from which it would not be possible at all). One of these two judgments must therefore be in error: either that of the critics, who confer beauty on the figures in question, or our own, which takes purposiveness without a concept to be indispensable to beauty.

Hardly anyone will find a person of taste to be required in order to take greater satisfaction in a circular figure than in a scrawled contour, or greater in a four-sided figure with equal sides and equal corners than in one that is skewed, of unequal sides, and as it were stunted; for this demands nothing beyond ordinary understanding and no taste of any kind. Wherever some aim is discerned — say, to estimate the size of a public square, or to make graspable the proportion of the parts among themselves and to the whole in some subdivision — there regular figures, and indeed those of the simplest sort, become necessary; and the satisfaction does not settle directly upon the sight of the figure but upon its serviceability {{{ 242 }}} for aims of every possible kind. A room whose walls meet at oblique angles, a garden laid out in such a fashion, indeed any breach of symmetry — whether in the figure of animals (say, being one-eyed), or of edifices, or of beds of flowers — gives displeasure, because it runs counter to purpose, not merely in practice, with a view to some determinate use of these things, but also for judging them with respect to aims of every possible kind; and this is precisely what does not hold in the judgment of taste, which, so long as it is pure, links satisfaction {{ 71 }} or displeasure immediately to the sheer ***contemplation*** of the object, with no regard to use or to any end whatever.

That regularity which conducts us toward an object's concept is, to be sure, the condition we cannot dispense with (_conditio sine qua non_) if the object is to be gathered into a single representation and the manifold within its form is to be fixed. This fixing is an end in regard to cognition, and, related to cognition, it is at all times conjoined with satisfaction (the sort that attends the achievement of any aim, even a merely problematic one). In that case, however, what we have is merely our endorsement of the solution that meets a task, not a free and indeterminately purposive occupation of the mental powers with what we name beautiful, wherein the understanding waits upon the imagination rather than the imagination upon the understanding.

In a thing that owes its possibility solely to a purpose — a building, or even an animal — the regularity residing in symmetry has to express that unity of intuition which attends the concept of the end, and forms a part of cognition. But where the sole aim is to keep up a free play of the representational powers (though always on the proviso that the understanding take no offense in the process) — in pleasure gardens, in the ornamenting of rooms, in tasteful utensils of every sort, and suchlike — regularity, wherever it gives notice of itself as compulsion, is shunned as far as can be; and this is why the English taste in gardens, and the baroque taste in furnishings, drive the imagination's freedom well toward the brink of the grotesque, and, in this divorce from every compulsion of rule, establish the very case in which taste can exhibit its {{ 72 }} utmost perfection in the designs of the imagination.

Everything stiffly regular (whatever comes near to mathematical regularity) carries within it what is repugnant to taste: that it grants no protracted occupation to its contemplation, but rather, so far as it does not {{{ 243 }}} expressly have cognition, or some determinate practical end, for its aim, breeds tedium. What the imagination can play with unbidden and purposively, by contrast, strikes us as forever new, and one never tires of beholding it. ***Marsden***, in his account of Sumatra, offers the observation that nature's free beauties there surround the onlooker on every side and for that reason have little that still attracts him: a pepper garden, on the other hand, where the stakes round which this plant climbs form avenues in parallel rows between them, held great charm for him when he chanced on it deep within a forest; and from this he infers that a wild beauty, ruleless in appearance, gratifies only the person who, sated with the regular kind, welcomes it by way of variety. Yet he need only have tried the experiment of lingering a whole day at his pepper garden to realize that, once regularity has set the understanding into the disposition toward order that it requires on all sides, the object entertains him no further but instead lays a wearisome compulsion on the imagination; whereas the nature there, lavish in its variety to the pitch of exuberance and bound by no compulsion of contrived rules, could furnish his taste with nourishment that never fails. — Even birdsong, which we are unable to bring under any rule of music, seems to hold more freedom, and on that account more for taste, than even a human song performed in strict conformity with every rule of the musical art: for one {{ 73 }} wearies far more quickly of the latter once it is repeated frequently and at length. But here we are presumably trading our fellow-feeling in the gaiety of a small, much-loved little creature for the beauty of its song, which, once a human being reproduces it with full exactness (as now and then occurs with the warbling of the nightingale), seems to our ear altogether devoid of taste.

Beautiful objects, further, are to be kept distinct from beautiful prospects onto objects (which, owing to their remoteness, can often no longer be made out with any clarity). With the latter, taste seems to fasten not so much on what the imagination ***apprehends*** in this field, but rather on what it is thereby prompted to ***invent***, i.e. on the very reveries in which the mind occupies itself, all the while being kept continually astir by the manifold on which the eye lights; much as in the sight of the ever-changing shapes of a hearth-fire or of a purling stream, neither of which counts as a {{{ 244 }}} beauty, though each holds an allure for the imagination in that it keeps its free play going.
