## § 1. The judgment of taste is aesthetic

To settle whether something is beautiful or not, we do not refer the representation to the object for cognition by means of the understanding; instead we refer it, by means of the imagination (perhaps conjoined with the understanding), {{ 4 }} to the subject and to its feeling of pleasure or displeasure. Accordingly, a judgment of taste is not a judgment of cognition and so is not logical but aesthetic — where by the latter is meant one whose determining ground can be ***nothing but*** ***subjective***. Any relation among representations, even a relation among sensations, admits of being objective (in which case it denotes the real of an empirical representation) — with one exception: the relation to {{{ 204 }}} the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, by which nothing at all gets marked out in the object, and in which, rather, the subject feels its own self as it is affected by the representation.

Apprehending a regular and purposive building with one's cognitive faculty (be the mode of representation distinct or confused) is quite another matter than being aware of that same representation as attended by the sensation of satisfaction. In this latter case the representation is referred wholly to the subject — specifically to the subject's feeling of life, under the heading of the feeling of pleasure or displeasure — and this founds an entirely peculiar faculty of discrimination and judging that adds nothing to cognition, but only sets the given representation within the subject {{ 5 }} over against the entire faculty of representations, of which the mind grows aware in feeling its own condition. The representations given within a judgment may be empirical, and thus aesthetic; but the judgment reached by their means is logical so long as they are referred, within that judgment, merely to the object. The reverse also holds: should the given representations be rational outright, yet within a judgment be referred solely to the subject (to its feeling), they are, just so far, invariably aesthetic.
