## § 1. The Judgment of Taste is Aesthetic

In order to distinguish whether something is beautiful or not, we relate the representation not through the understanding to the object for cognition, but rather through the imagination (perhaps combined with the understanding) {{ 4 }} to the subject and its feeling of pleasure or displeasure. The judgment of taste is therefore not a cognitive judgment, hence not logical, but aesthetic, by which is understood that whose determining ground ***can be no other*** than ***subjective***. All relation of representations, even that of sensations, can however be objective (and then it signifies the real of an empirical representation); only not the relation to {{{ 204 }}} the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, through which nothing at all in the object is designated, but in which the subject feels itself, as it is affected by the representation.

To grasp a regular, purposive building with one's faculty of cognition (whether in a distinct or a confused manner of representation) is something entirely different from being conscious of this representation with the sensation of satisfaction. Here the representation is related entirely to the subject, and indeed to its feeling of life under the name of the feeling of pleasure or displeasure: which grounds an entirely special faculty for distinguishing and appraising that contributes nothing to cognition, but only holds the given representation in the subject {{ 5 }} up against the whole faculty of representations, of which the mind becomes conscious in the feeling of its state. Given representations in a judgment can be empirical (hence aesthetic); but the judgment that is made through them is logical, if only they are related in the judgment to the object. Conversely, however, if the given representations were even rational, but were related in a judgment solely to the subject (its feeling), then they are to that extent always aesthetic.
