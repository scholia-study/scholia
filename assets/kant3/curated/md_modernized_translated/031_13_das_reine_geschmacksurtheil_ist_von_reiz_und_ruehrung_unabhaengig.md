## {{{ 223 }}} § 13. The pure judgment of taste is independent of charm and emotion

Every interest spoils the judgment of taste and takes from it its impartiality, especially when it does not, like the interest of reason, {{ 38 }} place the purposiveness before the feeling of pleasure but rather grounds it on this; which latter always happens in the aesthetic judgment about something insofar as it gratifies or pains. Hence judgments that are affected in this way can make either no claim at all to a universally valid satisfaction, or so much the less a claim as there are sensations of the kind in question among the determining grounds of taste. Taste is always still barbaric where it needs the admixture of ***charms*** and ***emotions*** for satisfaction, indeed even makes these the standard of its approval.

Yet charms are often not only counted toward beauty (which should really concern merely the form) as a contribution to the aesthetic universal satisfaction, but are even passed off as beauties in themselves, and hence the matter of the satisfaction is passed off for the form: a misunderstanding which, like many another that nevertheless still has something true at its ground, can be removed through careful determination of these concepts.

A judgment of taste on which charm and emotion have no influence (although they may be combined with the satisfaction in the beautiful), which thus has merely the purposiveness of the form as its determining ground, is a ***pure judgment of taste***.
