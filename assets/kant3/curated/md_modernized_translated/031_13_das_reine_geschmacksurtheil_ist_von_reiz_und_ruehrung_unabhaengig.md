## {{{ 223 }}} § 13. The pure judgment of taste is independent of charm and emotion

Any interest whatever spoils the judgment of taste, robbing it of its impartiality, most especially when, in contrast to the interest of reason {{ 38 }}, it fails to place purposiveness before the feeling of pleasure and instead founds that purposiveness on the feeling; and this second alternative invariably occurs whenever an aesthetic judgment is passed on something to the degree that the thing pleases or pains. Hence judgments affected in this way can lay either no claim at all to universally valid satisfaction, or so much less of one as there are sensations of the kind just described among the determining grounds of taste. A taste still remains barbaric in every instance where it requires ***charms*** and ***emotions*** to be mixed into the satisfaction, indeed where it goes so far as to make these the standard of its approval.

Meanwhile charms are frequently not only reckoned to beauty (which properly should have to do with the form alone) as contributing to the universal aesthetic satisfaction, but are indeed proclaimed to be beauties in themselves, the matter of the satisfaction thus being presented as its form: a misunderstanding which, like many another that still has some truth lying at its root, can be dispelled by defining these concepts with care.

A judgment of taste on which neither charm nor emotion exerts any influence (although these can indeed be combined with the satisfaction in the beautiful), one whose determining ground is thus solely the purposiveness of the form, is a ***pure judgment of taste***.
