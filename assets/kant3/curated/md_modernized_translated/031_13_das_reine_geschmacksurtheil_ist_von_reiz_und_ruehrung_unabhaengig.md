## {{{ 223 }}} § 13. The pure judgment of taste is independent of charm and emotion

Every interest corrupts the judgment of taste and deprives it of its impartiality, above all when, unlike the interest of reason {{ 38 }}, it does not send purposiveness ahead of the feeling of pleasure but instead grounds that purposiveness upon this feeling; and this latter case always happens in the aesthetic judgment about something insofar as it gratifies or pains. Hence judgments affected in this way can lay either no claim at all to universally valid satisfaction, or so much less of one as there are sensations of the kind just described among the determining grounds of taste. Taste is at all times still barbaric wherever it requires the admixture of ***charms*** and ***emotions*** to satisfaction, and indeed where it even makes these into the measure of its approval.

Yet charms are quite often not merely counted toward beauty (which really ought to concern nothing but the form) as a contribution to the general aesthetic satisfaction, but are even passed off as beauties in their own right, so that the matter of the satisfaction is given out for its form: a misunderstanding that, like many another which nonetheless still has something true at its base, can be cleared up by a careful determination of these concepts.

A judgment of taste upon which charm and emotion have no influence (even though they may well let themselves be joined with the satisfaction in the beautiful), and which therefore has nothing but the purposiveness of the form as its determining ground, is a ***pure judgment of taste***.
