## § 23. Transition from the faculty for judging the beautiful to that for judging the sublime

The beautiful agrees with the sublime in this, that both please for their own sake. A further point of agreement is that each of them presupposes no judgment of sense, and likewise none that determines logically, but rather a judgment of reflection: the satisfaction, accordingly, hangs neither upon a sensation, in the manner of the agreeable, nor upon any determinate concept, in the manner of the satisfaction in the good, and yet it is still brought into relation with concepts, however indeterminate these may be; thus it attaches to the bare presentation, or to the faculty for it, and by this attachment the faculty of presentation, that is, the imagination, comes to be viewed, in the presence of a given intuition, as harmonizing with the understanding's or reason's ***faculty of concepts***, and as promoting the latter. Hence both kinds of judgment are ***singular*** judgments that nevertheless announce themselves as universally valid with respect to every subject, even though their claim reaches only as far as the feeling of pleasure, and to no cognition of the object.

Yet there are also notable differences between the two that strike the {{ 75 }} eye. Natural beauty has to do with the form of the object, a form consisting in its limitation; the sublime, on the other hand, may be met with even in an object without form, so far as ***unboundedness*** is represented either in it or through its prompting, and a totality of that unboundedness is yet added in thought: whence the beautiful appears to be taken for the presentation of an understanding's concept that is indeterminate, while the sublime is taken for that of such a concept of reason. Accordingly the satisfaction is in the one case connected with the representation of ***quality***, but in the other with that of ***quantity***. In its very kind, moreover, this latter satisfaction stands quite far apart from the former: for the one (the beautiful) carries with it, straightaway, a feeling that life is furthered, and thus goes together with charms and with an {{{ 245 }}} imagination at play; the other, however (the feeling of the sublime), is a pleasure springing up only in a roundabout way, in that it comes about through the feeling of a momentary checking of the vital forces followed at once by an outpouring of them that is all the stronger, so that, as a stirring of the mind, it seems to be no play but rather earnestness in how the imagination is engaged. For this reason, too, it cannot be reconciled with charms, and, inasmuch as the object does not merely attract the mind but by turns repels it over and over, the satisfaction in the sublime holds within it not positive pleasure so much as admiration {{ 76 }} or respect, and thus earns the name of a negative pleasure.

The weightiest and inmost difference, however, that sets the sublime apart from the beautiful is presumably this: that if, as is proper, we take into consideration here at the outset only the sublime as it appears in objects of nature (the sublime of art being always held within the conditions of its agreement with nature), then the beauty of nature (the self-subsistent sort) carries within its own form a purposiveness, by which the object seems, as it were, marked out in advance to suit our power of judgment, and hence amounts on its own to an object of satisfaction; whereas whatever stirs in us, with no need of subtle reasoning, the feeling of the sublime through mere apprehension may well look, in respect of its form, ill-adapted in purpose to our power of judgment, unsuited to our faculty of presentation, and violent, so to speak, toward the imagination, and yet is judged only the more sublime on that account.

From this, however, one sees at once that we express ourselves incorrectly on the whole when we call some ***object of nature*** sublime, even though we may with full correctness term very many of them beautiful; for how can that which is apprehended in itself as contrary to purpose be designated by an expression of approval? Nothing more can be asserted than that the object is fit for presenting a sublimity such as may be found within the mind; for the properly sublime {{ 77 }} can be housed in no sensible form whatever, and pertains solely to ideas of reason: these, though no presentation befitting them is possible, are stirred up and summoned into the mind by this very unfittingness, which does admit of being presented in sensible fashion. So the broad ocean, whipped into rage by storms, cannot be termed sublime. The look of it is ghastly; and the mind must beforehand have been stocked with ideas of many sorts {{{ 246 }}} if such an intuition is to tune it toward a feeling that is sublime in its own right, since the mind is thereby spurred to forsake sensibility and to busy itself with ideas harboring a higher purposiveness.

Self-subsistent natural beauty reveals to us a technique of nature that presents it as a system governed by laws whose principle we do not encounter anywhere in our whole faculty of understanding, namely the principle of a purposiveness relative to the use of the power of judgment with respect to appearances, so that these must be judged as belonging not merely to nature under its aimless mechanism, but also to an analogy with art. It thus does not really extend our cognition of natural objects, to be sure, yet it does extend our concept of nature, namely from that of a mere mechanism to the concept of the very same nature as art: which invites deep investigations into the possibility of a form of this kind. Yet in whatever we tend to call {{ 78 }} sublime within nature there lies so entirely nothing that would point toward particular objective principles and toward natural forms conforming to them, that nature instead, amid its chaos or amid its wildest and most unruly disorder and devastation, so long as magnitude and might come into view, is what most arouses the ideas of the sublime. We gather from this that the concept of the sublime in nature falls far short, both in importance and in the wealth of its consequences, of the concept of the beautiful in nature; and that it points to nothing purposive within nature itself, but solely within the possible ***use*** of the intuitions we have of it, in order to render felt in us a purposiveness altogether independent of nature. As for the beautiful in nature, we have to look for a ground beyond ourselves, whereas for the sublime we look merely inside ourselves and in the way of thinking that brings sublimity into the representation of the former; this is a highly needful preliminary observation, one that severs the ideas of the sublime entirely from any purposiveness ascribed to ***nature***, and reduces the theory of the sublime to a mere appendix to the aesthetic appraisal of the purposiveness of nature, seeing that by this no particular form is represented in nature, while what gets developed is merely a purposive employment which the imagination makes of its own representation.
