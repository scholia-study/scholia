## § 23. Transition from the faculty for judging the beautiful to that for judging the sublime

The beautiful agrees with the sublime in that both please for themselves. Further, in that both presuppose neither a judgment of sense nor a logically determining judgment, but rather a judgment of reflection: consequently the satisfaction depends neither on a sensation, like that of the agreeable, nor on a determinate concept, like the satisfaction in the good, but is nevertheless still related to concepts, although indeterminate which ones; hence the satisfaction is connected with the mere presentation or the faculty for it, through which the faculty of presentation or the imagination is considered, upon a given intuition, to be in accord with the ***faculty of concepts*** of the understanding or of reason, as a promotion of the latter. Hence both kinds of judgments are also ***singular*** judgments and yet ones that announce themselves as universally valid with respect to every subject, although they lay claim merely to the feeling of pleasure and not to any cognition of the object.

But there are also notable differences between the two that strike the {{ 75 }} eye. The beautiful in nature concerns the form of the object, which consists in its being bounded; the sublime, by contrast, is to be found also in a formless object, insofar as ***unboundedness*** is represented in it or by its occasion, and yet a totality of it is thought in addition: so that the beautiful seems to be taken for the presentation of an indeterminate concept of the understanding, but the sublime for that of a like concept of reason. Thus the satisfaction is there connected with the representation of ***quality***, but here with that of ***quantity***. Also, the latter satisfaction is, as to its kind, very greatly distinguished from the former: in that this one (the beautiful) directly carries with it a feeling of the promotion of life and is therefore compatible with charms and a {{{ 245 }}} playing imagination; but that one (the feeling of the sublime) is a pleasure that arises only indirectly, namely so that it is produced through the feeling of a momentary inhibition of the vital powers and the immediately following all the stronger outpouring of them, hence as an emotion seems to be no play but seriousness in the occupation of the imagination. Hence it is also incompatible with charms, and, since the mind is not merely attracted by the object but also alternately ever again repelled, the satisfaction in the sublime contains not so much a positive pleasure as rather admiration {{ 76 }} or respect, i.e., it deserves to be called negative pleasure.

But the most important and inner difference of the sublime from the beautiful is probably this: that, if we here, as is fitting, first of all take into consideration only the sublime in objects of nature (that of art is namely always restricted to the conditions of agreement with nature), natural beauty (the self-subsisting) carries with it a purposiveness in its form, through which the object seems to be as it were predetermined for our power of judgment, and so constitutes in itself an object of satisfaction; whereas that which, without rationalizing, merely in the apprehension excites in us the feeling of the sublime may as to its form indeed appear contrary to purpose for our power of judgment, unsuited to our faculty of presentation, and as it were doing violence to the imagination, but is nevertheless judged to be all the more sublime for that.

But one sees from this at once that we express ourselves entirely incorrectly when we call any ***object of nature*** sublime, although we can quite correctly call very many of them beautiful; for how can that be designated with an expression of approval which is apprehended as in itself contrary to purpose? We can say no more than that the object is suitable for the presentation of a sublimity that can be encountered in the mind; for the properly sublime {{ 77 }} can be contained in no sensible form, but concerns only ideas of reason: which, although no presentation adequate to them is possible, are aroused and called into the mind precisely through this inadequacy, which can be presented sensibly. Thus the wide ocean, enraged by storms, cannot be called sublime. Its sight is horrible; and one must already have filled the mind with all sorts of ideas {{{ 246 }}} if it is to be attuned by such an intuition to a feeling that is itself sublime, in that the mind is incited to abandon sensibility and to occupy itself with ideas that contain a higher purposiveness.

Self-subsisting natural beauty discloses to us a technique of nature that makes it representable as a system according to laws whose principle we do not encounter in our entire faculty of understanding, namely that of a purposiveness with respect to the use of the power of judgment in regard to appearances, so that these must be judged not merely as belonging to nature in its purposeless mechanism, but also as belonging to the analogy with art. It thus actually broadens, not indeed our cognition of objects of nature, but yet our concept of nature, namely as mere mechanism, into the concept of the very same as art: which invites deep investigations into the possibility of such a form. But in that which we are accustomed to call sublime in it {{ 78 }} there is so utterly nothing that leads to particular objective principles and forms of nature conformable to them, that nature, rather in its chaos or in its wildest, most irregular disorder and devastation, provided only that magnitude and might let themselves be seen, most excites the ideas of the sublime. From this we see that the concept of the sublime in nature is by far not so important and rich in consequences as that of the beautiful in it; and that in general it indicates nothing purposive in nature itself, but only in the possible ***use*** of its intuitions, in order to make perceptible in ourselves a purposiveness entirely independent of nature. For the beautiful in nature we must seek a ground outside ourselves, but for the sublime merely in ourselves and in the way of thinking that introduces sublimity into the representation of the former; a very necessary preliminary remark, which entirely separates the ideas of the sublime from that of a purposiveness of ***nature*** and makes of the theory of it a mere appendix to the aesthetic judging of the purposiveness of nature, because through it no particular form is represented in nature, but only a purposive use that the imagination makes of its representation is developed.
