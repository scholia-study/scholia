## § 23. Transition from the faculty for judging the beautiful to that for judging the sublime

The beautiful agrees with the sublime in this, that both please for their own sake. They agree further in this, that both presuppose neither a judgment of sense nor a logically determining one, but rather a judgment of reflection: consequently the satisfaction depends neither on a sensation, as with the agreeable, nor on a determinate concept, as does the satisfaction in the good, yet it is nonetheless referred to concepts, though it remains undetermined which; hence the satisfaction is bound up with the mere presentation, or with the faculty of presentation, whereby the faculty of presentation, or the imagination, is regarded, given an intuition, as being in accord with the ***faculty of concepts*** of the understanding or of reason, as furthering the latter. Hence both kinds of judgment are ***singular*** judgments that nevertheless announce themselves as universally valid with respect to every subject, even though their claim reaches only as far as the feeling of pleasure, and to no cognition of the object.

Yet there are also notable differences between the two that strike the {{ 75 }} eye. The beautiful in nature concerns the object's form, which lies in its being bounded; the sublime, by contrast, is to be found even in a formless object, insofar as ***unboundedness*** is represented in it, or on its occasion, while a totality of that unboundedness is nonetheless thought along with it: so that the beautiful seems to be taken as the presentation of an indeterminate concept of the understanding, but the sublime as that of a similar concept belonging to reason. Accordingly the satisfaction is in the one case connected with the representation of ***quality***, but in the other with that of ***quantity***. The latter satisfaction, moreover, differs greatly in kind from the former: for the one (the beautiful) directly brings with it a feeling that life is being advanced, and is therefore compatible with charms and with a {{{ 245 }}} playful imagination; whereas the other (the feeling of the sublime) is a pleasure that arises only indirectly, namely in such a way that it is produced through the feeling of a momentary inhibition of the vital powers followed immediately by an all the stronger outpouring of them, and hence, as an emotion, seems to be not play but earnestness in the activity of the imagination. Hence it is also incompatible with charms, and, since the mind is not simply drawn toward the object but alternately thrust back from it again and again, the satisfaction in the sublime contains not so much positive pleasure as rather admiration {{ 76 }} or respect, that is, it deserves to be called a negative pleasure.

But the most important and intrinsic difference of the sublime from the beautiful is surely this: that if, as is fitting, we here consider first of all only the sublime in objects of nature (that of art being always restricted to the conditions under which it accords with nature), natural beauty (the self-subsistent kind) carries with it a purposiveness in its form, through which the object seems as it were to be predetermined for our power of judgment, and so constitutes in itself an object of satisfaction; whereas that which, without any subtle reasoning, arouses in us the feeling of the sublime merely in our apprehension of it may indeed appear, as regards its form, contrary to purpose for our power of judgment, ill-suited to our faculty of presentation, and as it were violent to the imagination, yet is judged all the more sublime for that.

From this, however, one sees at once that we express ourselves incorrectly on the whole when we call some ***object of nature*** sublime, even though we may with full correctness term very many of them beautiful; for how can that which is apprehended in itself as contrary to purpose be designated by an expression of approval? We can say no more than that the object is suitable for the presentation of a sublimity that can be encountered in the mind; for the sublime proper {{ 77 }} can be contained in no sensible form but concerns only ideas of reason: which, although no presentation adequate to them is possible, are precisely by this inadequacy, which does allow of sensible presentation, roused and called forth into the mind. So the broad ocean, whipped into rage by storms, cannot be termed sublime. The sight of it is dreadful; and one must already have filled the mind with a variety of ideas {{{ 246 }}} if it is to be attuned by such an intuition to a feeling that is itself sublime, in that the mind is incited to abandon sensibility and to occupy itself with ideas that contain a higher purposiveness.

Self-subsistent natural beauty reveals to us a technique of nature that presents it as a system governed by laws whose principle we do not encounter anywhere in our whole faculty of understanding, namely the principle of a purposiveness relative to the use of the power of judgment with respect to appearances, so that these must be judged as belonging not merely to nature under its aimless mechanism, but also to an analogy with art. It thus does not really extend our cognition of natural objects, to be sure, yet it does extend our concept of nature, namely from that of a mere mechanism to the concept of the very same nature as art: which invites deep investigations into the possibility of a form of this kind. But in that which we are accustomed to call {{ 78 }} sublime in nature there is so utterly nothing leading to particular objective principles and to forms of nature answering to them, that nature is rather, in its chaos, or in its most savage and lawless disarray and havoc, provided only that magnitude and might are shown, what most excites the ideas of the sublime. From this we recognize that the concept of the sublime in nature is by far not so important, nor so rich in consequences, as that of the beautiful in it; and that it indicates nothing purposive in nature itself at all, but only in the possible ***use*** of our intuitions of it, so as to make palpable in ourselves a purposiveness wholly independent of nature. For the beautiful in nature we must seek a ground outside ourselves, but for the sublime merely within ourselves and in the cast of mind that introduces sublimity into the representation of the former; a very necessary preliminary remark, which detaches the ideas of the sublime altogether from any purposiveness belonging to ***nature***, and turns the theory of it into a mere appendix to the aesthetic judging of the purposiveness of nature, since thereby no special form in nature is presented, but only a purposive use that the imagination makes of its representation is developed.
