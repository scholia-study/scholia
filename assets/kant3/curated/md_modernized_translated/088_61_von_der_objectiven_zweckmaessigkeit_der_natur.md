## {{{ 359 }}} {{ 267 }} § 61. On the Objective Purposiveness of Nature

One has, according to transcendental principles, good reason to assume a subjective purposiveness of nature in its particular laws for its comprehensibility by the human power of judgment and the possibility of connecting particular experiences into a system of the same; wherein, then, among its many products, such can also be expected as possible which, as if they were quite properly designed for our power of judgment, contain such specific forms adequate to it as serve, through their manifoldness and unity, as it were, to strengthen and entertain the mental powers (which are in play in the use of this faculty), and to which one therefore assigns the name of ***beautiful*** forms.

But that things of nature serve one another as means to ends, and that their possibility itself is sufficiently intelligible only through this kind of causality, for this we have no ground at all in the general idea of nature as the sum total of the objects of the senses. For in the above {{ 268 }} case, the representation of things, because it is something in us, could quite well also be thought _a priori_ as fit and suitable for the internally purposeful disposition of our cognitive faculties; but how ends which are not ours, and which also do not belong to nature (which we do not assume to be an intelligent being), can or should yet constitute a special kind of causality, or at least a quite peculiar lawfulness of the same, cannot be presumed _a priori_ with any ground at all. What is more, experience itself cannot prove to us the reality of these; unless a rationalization had preceded which only imports the concept of end into {{{ 360 }}} the nature of things, but does not derive it from objects and their empirical cognition, thus using it more to make nature comprehensible according to the analogy with a subjective ground of the connection of representations in us, than to cognize it from objective grounds.

Moreover, objective purposiveness, as a principle of the possibility of things of nature, is so far from being ***necessarily*** connected with the concept of the same, that it is rather precisely that to which one primarily appeals in order to prove from it the contingency of nature and of its form. For if one adduces, e.g., the structure of a bird, the hollows in its bones, the position of its wings {{ 269 }} for motion and of its tail for steering, etc., one says that all of this is contingent in the highest degree according to the mere _nexus effectivus_ in nature, without yet taking to aid a special kind of causality, namely that of ends (_nexus finalis_); i.e., that nature, considered as a mere mechanism, could have formed itself in a thousand different ways without hitting precisely upon the unity according to such a principle, and one must therefore hope to find the least ground for this _a priori_ only outside the concept of nature, not in it.

Nonetheless, teleological estimation is, at least problematically, with right drawn into the investigation of nature; but only in order to bring it, according to the ***analogy*** with causality according to ends, under principles of observation and inquiry, without presuming to ***explain*** it thereby. It therefore belongs to the reflecting, not the determining power of judgment. The concept of connections and forms of nature according to ends is yet at least ***one more principle*** for bringing its appearances under rules, where the laws of causality according to the mere mechanism of the same do not suffice. For we adduce a teleological ground where we attribute causality in regard to an object to a concept of the object, as if it were situated in nature (not in us), or rather represent to ourselves the possibility of the object according to the analogy of such a causality {{ 270 }} (the like of which we find in ourselves), consequently thinking of nature as ***technical*** through its own faculty; whereas, if we do not ascribe such a mode of action to it, its causality would have to be represented as a blind mechanism. If, on the contrary, we were to subject nature to ***intentionally*** active causes, consequently laying at the basis of {{{ 361 }}} teleology not merely a ***regulative*** principle for the mere ***estimation*** of appearances, to which nature according to its particular laws can be thought of as subject, but thereby also a ***constitutive*** principle of the ***derivation*** of its products from their causes: then the concept of a natural end would no longer belong to the reflecting but to the determining power of judgment; but in that case it would indeed not belong peculiarly to the power of judgment at all (as does the concept of beauty as formal subjective purposiveness), but would as a concept of reason introduce a new causality into natural science, which we nevertheless only borrow from ourselves and attribute to other beings, without yet wanting to assume them to be of the same kind as ourselves.
