## {{{ 359 }}} {{ 267 }} § 61. On the objective purposiveness of nature

Following transcendental principles, we have good reason to assume a subjective purposiveness of nature in its particular laws, one directed at its comprehensibility for the human power of judgment and at the possibility of combining its particular experiences into a system of these; and there, amongst nature's many products, some may likewise be looked for as possible which — as if they had been arranged quite expressly for our power of judgment — contain forms so specifically suited to it that, by their manifoldness and unity, they serve, so to speak, to fortify and sustain the mental powers (those set in play in the use of this faculty), and on which one accordingly bestows the name of ***beautiful*** forms.

But that things of nature mutually serve as means toward ends, and that their possibility itself should prove adequately intelligible solely through a causality of this kind — for that we have not the slightest ground in the general idea of nature, taken as the totality of the objects of the senses. For in the above {{ 268 }} case the representation of things, since it is something within us, could very well be thought even _a priori_ as fit and suited to the inwardly purposive attunement of our cognitive faculties; but how ends that are not our own, and that also do not belong to nature (which we do not assume to be an intelligent being), could or should nevertheless constitute a special kind of causality, or at least a lawfulness altogether peculiar to it, cannot in the least be presumed _a priori_ on any ground. What is more, even experience cannot prove to us the reality of such causality; there would have to have been some sophistical reasoning beforehand that merely reads the concept of an end into {{{ 360 }}} the nature of things, yet does not draw it from the objects and their empirical cognition, and thus uses that concept more to render nature intelligible by analogy with a subjective ground for connecting representations within us than to cognize nature from objective grounds.

Besides, objective purposiveness, taken as a principle by which the things of nature are made possible, stands so far from being ***necessarily*** tied to the concept of nature that it is instead precisely what one chiefly invokes in order to demonstrate, on that very basis, how contingent nature and its form are. For if one adduces, for instance, the structure of a bird, the hollowness in its bones, the way its wings {{ 269 }} are set for motion and its tail for steering, and so on, one is thereby asserting that all of this, taken merely according to the _nexus effectivus_ in nature and without calling upon a special variety of causality — namely that of ends (_nexus finalis_) — is contingent to the highest degree; in other words, that nature, viewed as mere mechanism, could have shaped itself in a thousand different ways without hitting precisely upon unity in accordance with such a principle, so that one may not hope to encounter the slightest ground for it _a priori_ anywhere except outside the concept of nature, and never within it.

Still, teleological judging is, at least problematically, justly drawn into natural research; yet only so as to bring it under principles of observation and investigation by ***analogy*** with causality in accordance with ends, without presuming to ***explain*** it thereby. It therefore pertains to the reflecting power of judgment, not the determining one. The concept of nature's connections and forms according to ends is, nonetheless, at the very least ***one more principle*** by which to subsume its appearances under rules in cases where the laws of causality, resting on its mere mechanism, prove insufficient. For we adduce a teleological ground whenever, to a concept of an object — as though that concept were located in nature (and not in us) — we assign a causality with respect to an object, or rather picture to ourselves the possibility of the thing on the analogy of such a causality {{ 270 }} (of the kind we come upon within ourselves), thereby thinking nature as ***technical*** through a capacity of its own; against which, should we not ascribe to it such a mode of operation, its causality would have to be envisaged as a blind mechanism. Should we instead place beneath nature causes that act ***intentionally***, and so lay as the foundation of {{{ 361 }}} teleology not solely a ***regulative*** principle meant merely for the ***judging*** of appearances — which nature, in keeping with its particular laws, might be thought to be subject to — but also a ***constitutive*** principle for the ***derivation*** of its products out of their causes: then a natural end, taken as a concept, would cease to belong to the reflecting power of judgment and would fall instead to the determining one; yet in that case it would in truth not be peculiar to the power of judgment at all (like the concept of beauty, which is a formal subjective purposiveness), but would, as a concept of reason, introduce into natural science a new causality — one that we in fact merely borrow from ourselves and attribute to other beings, without, however, being willing to assume them to be of the same kind as us.
