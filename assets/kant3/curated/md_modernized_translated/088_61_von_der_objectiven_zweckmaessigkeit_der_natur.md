## {{{ 359 }}} {{ 267 }} § 61. On the objective purposiveness of nature

On grounds of transcendental principles there is good reason to posit a subjective purposiveness of nature, in its particular laws, with respect to its graspability by the human power of judgment and the possibility of linking the particular experiences into a system of them; and here, among the many products of nature, some may also be expected as possible which, as though they had been laid out quite specifically for our power of judgment, contain such specific forms suited to it that, through their manifoldness and unity, they serve as it were to strengthen and sustain the powers of the mind (which are in play in the exercise of this faculty), and to which one therefore attaches the name of ***beautiful*** forms.

That things of nature, however, should serve one another as means to ends, and that their very possibility should become sufficiently intelligible only by way of a causality of this sort — of that we find no ground at all in the general idea of nature as the sum total of the objects of sense. For in the above {{ 268 }} case the representation of things, since it is something within us, could very well be thought even _a priori_ as fit and suited to the inwardly purposive attunement of our cognitive faculties; but how ends that are not our own, and that also do not belong to nature (which we do not assume to be an intelligent being), could or should nevertheless constitute a special kind of causality, or at least a lawfulness altogether peculiar to it, cannot in the least be presumed _a priori_ on any ground. What is more, even experience cannot prove to us the reality of such causality; there would have to have been some sophistical reasoning beforehand that merely reads the concept of an end into {{{ 360 }}} the nature of things, yet does not draw it from the objects and their empirical cognition, and thus uses that concept more to render nature intelligible by analogy with a subjective ground for connecting representations within us than to cognize nature from objective grounds.

Besides, objective purposiveness, taken as a principle by which the things of nature are made possible, stands so far from being ***necessarily*** tied to the concept of nature that it is instead precisely what one chiefly invokes in order to demonstrate, on that very basis, how contingent nature and its form are. For when one cites, say, how a bird is built, the cavities within its bones, the disposition of its wings {{ 269 }} for movement and of its tail for steering, and so on, then one is claiming that all of this, judged by the mere _nexus effectivus_ in nature and without yet enlisting a special kind of causality, namely that of ends (_nexus finalis_), is contingent in the highest degree; that is, that nature, regarded as sheer mechanism, might have formed itself in a thousand other ways without ever striking upon unity of this kind under such a principle, so that one may hope to find the least ground for it _a priori_ nowhere within the concept of nature but only outside it.

Nevertheless, teleological judging is rightly drawn into the investigation of nature, at least problematically; but only in order to bring it, by ***analogy*** with causality according to ends, under principles of observation and inquiry, without presuming thereby to ***explain*** it. It belongs, then, to the reflecting and not to the determining power of judgment. The concept of connections and forms of nature in accordance with ends is at least ***one more principle*** for bringing its appearances under rules where the laws of causality according to nature's mere mechanism do not suffice. For we advance a teleological ground wherever we ascribe to a concept of the object — as if it were present in nature (not in us) — a causality regarding an object, or rather represent to ourselves the possibility of the object by analogy with a causality of that kind {{ 270 }} (such as we meet with within ourselves), and thus conceive nature as ***technical*** through a power of its own; whereas, were we not to attribute such a manner of operating to it, its causality would have to be represented as a blind mechanism. If, on the contrary, we were to set ***intentionally***-acting causes beneath nature, and were thereby to make {{{ 361 }}} teleology rest not merely on a ***regulative*** principle serving only for the ***judging*** of the appearances, to which nature could be thought subject under its particular laws, but on a ***constitutive*** principle as well, one for the ***derivation*** of its products from the causes that produce them: then the concept of a natural end would no longer fall to the reflecting but to the determining power of judgment; in that event, however, it would in fact not belong to the power of judgment as its own at all (as the concept of beauty does, being a formal subjective purposiveness), and would instead, as a concept of reason, bring a new causality into natural science — a causality that we in truth merely take over from ourselves and lend to other beings, though without meaning to assume them to be of one kind with us.
