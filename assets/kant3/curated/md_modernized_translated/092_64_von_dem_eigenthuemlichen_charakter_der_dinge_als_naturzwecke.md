## § 64. On the Peculiar Character of Things as Natural Ends

In order to see that a thing is possible only as an end, i.e., that we must seek the causality of its origin not in the mechanism of nature, but in a cause whose power to act is determined by concepts, {{{ 370 }}} it is required: that its form is not possible according to mere laws of nature, i.e., such as can be cognized by us through the understanding alone, applied to objects of the senses; but that even its empirical cognition, as regards its cause and effect, {{ 285 }} presupposes concepts of reason. This ***contingency*** of its form under all empirical laws of nature in relation to reason, since reason, which must cognize also the necessity of every form of a natural product if it wishes to understand even only the conditions connected with its generation, can nevertheless not assume this necessity in that given form, is itself a ground for assuming its causality as if for this very reason it were possible only through reason; but the latter is then the faculty of acting according to ends (a will); and the object, which is represented as possible only from this, would be represented as possible only as an end.

If someone, in a country that appeared to him to be uninhabited, were to perceive a geometrical figure, say a regular hexagon, drawn in the sand: his reflection, working on a concept of it, would, through reason, become aware, even if obscurely, of the unity of the principle of its generation, and would accordingly not judge the sand, the neighboring sea, the winds, or even animals with their footprints, which he knows, or any other non-rational cause, to be a ground of the possibility of such a shape: because the contingency of coinciding with such a concept, which is possible only in reason, {{ 286 }} would seem so infinitely great to him that it would be just as if there were no law of nature for it at all, so that consequently no cause in merely mechanically acting nature, but only the concept of such an object as a concept, which only reason can give and with which it can compare the object, contains the causality for such an effect, and hence this can be regarded entirely as an end, but not a natural end, i.e., as a product of ***art*** (_vestigium hominis video_).

But in order to judge something which one cognizes as a natural product nevertheless also as an end, and hence as a ***natural end***: for this, if indeed there is not a contradiction in it, more is already required. I would say provisionally: a thing exists as a natural end, ***if it is*** (though in a twofold sense) ***both cause and effect of itself***; {{{ 371 }}} for in this lies a causality the like of which cannot be connected with the mere concept of a nature without underlying it with an end, but which even then can indeed be thought without contradiction, but cannot be comprehended. We will first illustrate the determination of this idea of a natural end by means of an example before we explain it fully.

In the first place, a tree generates another tree according to a known law of nature. But the tree which it generates is of the same genus; {{ 287 }} and thus it generates itself as regards the ***genus***, in which, on the one hand as effect, on the other hand as cause, it is incessantly produced by itself and likewise frequently produces itself, thus continuously preserving itself as a genus.

Secondly, a tree also generates itself as an ***individual***. This kind of effect we indeed call only growth; but this is to be taken in such a sense that it is entirely distinguished from every other increase in size according to mechanical laws, and is to be esteemed as equal to generation, although under another name. The matter which it adds to itself is first processed by this plant into a specifically peculiar quality, which the mechanism of nature outside of it cannot supply, and it develops itself further by means of a material which, as regards its composition, is its own product. For although, as regards the constituents which it receives from nature outside of it, it must be regarded only as an educt: yet in the separation and new combination of this raw material there is to be found such an originality of the faculty of separation and formation of this kind of natural being, that all art remains infinitely far removed from it when it attempts to reconstruct those products of the vegetable kingdom from the elements which it obtains through their analysis, or even from the material which nature supplies for their nourishment.

***Thirdly***, a part of this creature also generates itself in such a way: that {{ 288 }} the preservation of the one reciprocally depends on the preservation of the other. The eye on a tree leaf, grafted into the branch of another, produces on a foreign stock a plant of its own kind, and likewise the graft on another trunk. Hence, on the same tree, one can also regard each branch or leaf as merely grafted or inoculated upon it, and consequently as a tree existing for itself, which only attaches itself to another and {{{ 372 }}} feeds parasitically. At the same time, the leaves are indeed products of the tree, but yet they also reciprocally preserve it; for repeated defoliation would kill it, and its growth depends on their effect on the trunk. The self-help of nature in these creatures in case of injury, where the lack of a part that belonged to the preservation of the neighboring ones is compensated by the rest; the malformations or monstrosities in growth, where certain parts, because of occurring defects or obstacles, form themselves in an entirely new way in order to preserve what is there and to produce an anomalous creature: I will mention here only in passing, although they belong to the most wondrous properties of organized creatures.
