## § 64. On the distinctive character of things as natural ends

To grasp that a thing can be possible solely as an end—that is, to have to seek the causality behind its origin not within nature's mechanism but instead in a cause whose power to produce effects is settled by concepts—{{{ 370 }}} the following is demanded: that its form not admit of being possible under mere natural laws, namely those which we, applying the understanding by itself to objects of the senses, are able to cognize; but rather that even its empirical cognition, in respect of its cause and effect, {{ 285 }} should presuppose concepts of reason. This ***contingency*** of its form, in relation to reason and across all empirical natural laws—for reason, obliged to recognize in each form of a natural product likewise the necessity belonging to it, if it would grasp even merely the conditions tied to that product's generation, is nonetheless unable to accept this necessity in the given form—already furnishes a ground for taking the causality of that thing to be such that, on that very account, it could be possible only through reason; and reason is in that case the capacity for acting according to ends (a will); and the object, which one represents to oneself as possible only as something proceeding from this, would in that case be represented as possible only as an end.

Suppose that, in a country seeming to him uninhabited, a person noticed a geometrical figure—perhaps a regular hexagon—traced in the sand: his reflection, in working out a concept of it, would then apprehend, however dimly, through the mediation of reason, the unity in the principle of its production, and, guided by this, would decline to judge the sand, the sea nearby, the winds, or even animals whose footprints he is familiar with, or any other cause lacking reason, as a ground for the possibility of such a shape: for the sheer contingency involved in its coinciding with a concept of this sort, one that only reason {{ 286 }} can render possible, would strike him as so boundlessly great that it would come to the same thing as if no natural law governed it whatsoever, so that no cause within nature operating merely mechanically, but solely the concept of such an object—as a concept that reason alone can furnish and by which alone reason can measure the object—could equally contain the causality for such an effect, and hence this could be regarded outright as an end, though not as a natural end, that is, as a product of ***art*** (_vestigium hominis video_).

But to judge something that one recognizes as a natural product nonetheless also as an end—hence as a ***natural end***—for this, provided there is not perhaps some contradiction lurking here, still more is required. I would say provisionally: a thing exists as a natural end ***when it is, of itself*** (though in a twofold sense) ***both cause and effect***; {{{ 371 }}} for what lies in this is a causality that cannot be bound up with the bare concept of a nature so long as no end is laid beneath it, and which, though it may then at least be thought without contradiction, still cannot be comprehended. Before expounding this idea of a natural end in full, let us begin by clarifying its determination through an example.

A tree, in the first place, generates another tree according to a known law of nature. But the tree that it produces is of the same species; {{ 287 }} and thus it produces itself as regards its ***species***, within which, being on the one hand effect and on the other hand cause, ceaselessly brought forth by itself and likewise often bringing itself forth, it perpetually preserves itself as a species.

Secondly, a tree also produces itself as an ***individual***. This kind of effect we call, to be sure, merely growth; but growth is to be taken in such a sense that it is wholly distinct from any other increase in magnitude according to mechanical laws and is to be reckoned as equivalent to a generation, albeit under a different name. The matter that it adds to itself this growing thing first works up into a specifically distinctive quality that the mechanism of nature outside it cannot supply, and it develops itself further by means of a material that, in its composition, is its own product. For although, as regards the constituents that it receives from the nature outside it, it must be regarded merely as an educt: still, in the separation and fresh recombination of this raw material there is to be found such an originality in the separative and formative capacity of this kind of natural being that all art remains infinitely far removed from it when it attempts to reconstitute those products of the vegetable kingdom out of the elements it obtains by decomposing them, or out of the material that nature supplies for their nourishment.

***Thirdly***, one part of this creature likewise brings itself forth in such a fashion: that {{ 288 }} the maintenance of the one hangs reciprocally upon the maintenance of the other. The bud on the leaf of one tree, inoculated into the branch of another, brings forth on an alien stock a growth of its own kind, and likewise does the graft-scion upon another trunk. Hence one may also regard, on one and the same tree, every branch or leaf as merely grafted or budded onto it, and thus as a tree existing for itself, one that merely attaches itself to another and feeds parasitically {{{ 372 }}} upon it. At the same time the leaves are indeed products of the tree, yet they in turn also sustain it; for were it stripped of its foliage again and again this would kill it, and its growth rests on the effect they exert upon the trunk. The self-help of nature in these creatures upon their injury, where the lack of a part that belonged to the preservation of the neighboring ones is made good by the rest; the malformations or deformities in growth, where certain parts, on account of deficiencies or obstacles that arise, shape themselves in an entirely new way so as to preserve what is there and to bring forth an anomalous creature: these I will mention here only in passing, notwithstanding that they belong among the most marvelous properties of organized creatures.
