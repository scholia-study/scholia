## § 64. On the distinctive character of things as natural ends

To recognize that a thing is possible only as an end—that is, that the causality of its origin must be sought not in the mechanism of nature but in a cause whose capacity to act is determined through concepts—{{{ 370 }}} what is required is this: that its form not be possible according to mere laws of nature, that is, such laws as we can cognize through the understanding alone as applied to objects of the senses; but rather that even the empirical cognition of it, with respect to its cause and effect, {{ 285 }} presuppose concepts of reason. This ***contingency*** of its form with respect to all empirical laws of nature in relation to reason—since reason, which must discern in every form of a natural product also the necessity of that form whenever it seeks to grasp even just the conditions bound up with its generation, nevertheless cannot assume this necessity in that given form—is itself a ground for assuming the causality of such a thing to be as though, for precisely that reason, it were possible only through reason; but reason is then the faculty of acting in accordance with ends (a will); and the object that is represented as possible only as arising from this would be represented as possible only as an end.

If someone were to perceive, in a land that appeared to him uninhabited, a geometrical figure—say, a regular hexagon—drawn in the sand: then his reflection, as it labors upon a concept of that figure, would become aware, if only obscurely, by means of reason, of the unity of the principle of its generation, and accordingly would not judge the sand, the neighboring sea, the winds, or even animals with their footprints that he knows, or any other cause devoid of reason, to be a ground of the possibility of such a shape: because the contingency of its coinciding with such a concept, one possible only in reason, {{ 286 }} would seem to him so infinitely great that it would be just as though there were no law of nature for it at all, and hence that no cause in merely mechanically operating nature, but only the concept of such an object as a concept that reason alone can supply and against which reason alone can compare the object, could likewise contain the causality for such an effect, so that it could be regarded thoroughly as an end, though not a natural end—that is, as a product of ***art*** (_vestigium hominis video_).

But to judge something that one recognizes as a natural product nonetheless also as an end—hence as a ***natural end***—for this, provided there is not perhaps some contradiction lurking here, still more is required. I would say provisionally: a thing exists as a natural end ***when it is, of itself*** (though in a twofold sense) ***both cause and effect***; {{{ 371 }}} for herein lies a causality of a kind that cannot be connected with the mere concept of a nature unless one attributes an end to it, and one that, even then, can indeed be thought without contradiction yet cannot be comprehended. Let us first illustrate the determination of this idea of a natural end by means of an example before we set it out completely.

A tree, in the first place, generates another tree according to a known law of nature. But the tree that it produces is of the same species; {{ 287 }} and thus it produces itself as regards its ***species***, within which, being on the one hand effect and on the other hand cause, ceaselessly brought forth by itself and likewise often bringing itself forth, it perpetually preserves itself as a species.

Secondly, a tree also produces itself as an ***individual***. This kind of effect we call, to be sure, merely growth; but growth is to be taken in such a sense that it is wholly distinct from any other increase in magnitude according to mechanical laws and is to be reckoned as equivalent to a generation, albeit under a different name. The matter that it adds to itself this growing thing first works up into a specifically distinctive quality that the mechanism of nature outside it cannot supply, and it develops itself further by means of a material that, in its composition, is its own product. For although, as regards the constituents that it receives from the nature outside it, it must be regarded merely as an educt: still, in the separation and fresh recombination of this raw material there is to be found such an originality in the separative and formative capacity of this kind of natural being that all art remains infinitely far removed from it when it attempts to reconstitute those products of the vegetable kingdom out of the elements it obtains by decomposing them, or out of the material that nature supplies for their nourishment.

***Thirdly***, one part of this creature also produces itself in such a way: that {{ 288 }} the preservation of the one depends reciprocally on the preservation of the others. The bud on the leaf of one tree, inoculated into the branch of another, brings forth on an alien stock a growth of its own kind, and likewise does the graft-scion upon another trunk. Hence one may also regard, on one and the same tree, every branch or leaf as merely grafted or budded onto it, and thus as a tree existing for itself, one that merely attaches itself to another and feeds parasitically {{{ 372 }}} upon it. At the same time the leaves are indeed products of the tree, yet they in turn also sustain it; for repeated defoliation would kill it, and its growth depends on their action upon the trunk. The self-help of nature in these creatures upon their injury, where the lack of a part that belonged to the preservation of the neighboring ones is made good by the rest; the malformations or deformities in growth, where certain parts, on account of deficiencies or obstacles that arise, shape themselves in an entirely new way so as to preserve what is there and to bring forth an anomalous creature: these I will mention here only in passing, notwithstanding that they belong among the most marvelous properties of organized creatures.
