## {{ 143 }} § 34. No objective principle of taste is possible

By a principle of taste one would understand a fundamental proposition under whose condition one could subsume the concept of an object and then, by means of an inference, conclude that it is beautiful. But this is absolutely impossible. For I must feel the pleasure immediately in the representation of it, and it cannot be talked into me by any grounds of proof. Although critics, therefore, as ***Hume*** says, can reason more plausibly than cooks, they still share one and the same fate with them. They cannot expect the determining ground of their judgment from the force of proofs, but only from the {{{ 286 }}} reflection of the subject on its own state (of pleasure or displeasure) while rejecting all precepts and rules.

But what critics can and should nevertheless reason about, so that it may contribute to the correction and extension of our judgments of taste, is not to present the determining ground of this kind of aesthetic judgment in a universally applicable formula, which is impossible; but rather to investigate the cognitive faculties and their offices in these judgments, and to expound in examples the mutual subjective purposiveness, {{ 144 }} of which it was shown above that its form in a given representation is the beauty of its object. Thus the critique of taste is itself only subjective in respect of the representation by which an object is given to us: namely, it is the art or science of bringing the mutual relation of the understanding and the imagination to each other in the given representation (without relation to a preceding sensation or concept), and consequently their agreement or disagreement, under rules, and determining them in respect of their conditions. It is ***art*** if it shows this only in examples; it is ***science*** if it derives the possibility of such a judging from the nature of these faculties as cognitive faculties in general. With the latter, as a transcendental critique, we have to do here alone and throughout. It is to develop and justify the subjective principle of taste as an _a priori_ principle of the power of judgment. Critique as art seeks merely to apply the physiological (here psychological), and consequently empirical, rules according to which taste actually proceeds (without thinking about their possibility) to the judging of its objects, and criticizes the products of beautiful art; just as ***the former*** criticizes the faculty itself of judging them.
