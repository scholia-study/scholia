## § 87. On the moral proof of the existence of God

A ***physical teleology*** exists, one that supplies our theoretically reflecting power of judgment with a ground of proof adequate for supposing that an intelligent world-cause exists. Yet we {{ 419 }} likewise discover within ourselves — and even more so in the concept, quite generally, of a rational being endowed with freedom (with a causality of its own) — a ***moral teleology*** as well; but since the relation to ends within us is settled _a priori_ along with its law, and so admits of being cognized as necessary, this teleology has no need, for the present purpose, of an intelligent cause outside us to account for that inner lawfulness: no more than, upon finding something purposive in the geometrical properties of figures (serviceable for every sort of possible artistic practice), we are entitled to look past them to a highest understanding that bestows this upon them. Still, this moral teleology does bear upon us insofar as we are beings of the world and hence beings bound up with other things within it: things toward which — whether as ends, or as objects with respect to which we ourselves are the final end — the very same moral laws enjoin us to direct our judging. Now out of this moral teleology — which concerns the relation of our own causality to ends, indeed to a final end that we are bound to intend in the world, and equally the reciprocal relation of the world to that ethical end together with the {{{ 448 }}} outer possibility of carrying it out (a matter on which no physical teleology can instruct us) — the necessary question arises: whether it obliges our rational judging to pass beyond the world {{ 420 }} and to seek, for that relation of nature to the moral within us, an intelligent supreme principle, so that nature may be represented by us as purposive in respect of the inner moral legislation and its possible execution as well. There is, then, indeed a moral teleology; and it hangs together, just as necessarily, with the ***nomothetics*** of freedom on the one side and that of nature on the other, as civil legislation hangs together with the question where the executive power is to be sought, and as, in general, a connection obtains in everything wherein reason is to supply a principle for the reality of some lawful order of things possible only in accordance with ideas. — We shall begin by laying out how reason advances from that moral teleology, and from its bearing upon the physical one, up to ***theology***, and only afterward take up considerations concerning the possibility and conclusiveness of this mode of inference.

Suppose one takes the existence of certain things — or merely of certain forms of things — to be contingent, and thus possible only through something else acting as their cause: then, for this causality, one may look for the supreme ground, and so, for what is conditioned, the unconditioned ground, either within the physical or within the teleological order (following the _nexu effectivo_ or the _finali_). That is to say, one may ask: which is the supreme productive cause? or what is the supreme (utterly unconditioned) end {{ 421 }} of these things — that is, the final end of their production, whether of this product or of all of them in general? whereby, of course, it is then presupposed that this cause is capable of a representation of ends, and hence is an intelligent being, or must at any rate be thought by us as acting in accordance with the laws of such a being.

Now, if one follows out the latter order, there is a ***principle*** to which even the commonest human reason feels itself at once constrained to give its assent: namely, that if a ***final end*** is to obtain anywhere at all — one that reason must specify _a priori_ — this end can be nothing other than ***the human being*** (any rational worldly being whatever) ***under moral laws***.[^*] For (thus does everyone judge): were the world made up {{ 422 }} {{{ 449 }}} entirely of lifeless things, or even, in part, of living yet reasonless beings, the existence of such a world would carry no worth at all, since within it no being would exist that possessed the slightest concept of a worth. Were there, by contrast, rational beings too, but ones whose reason could locate the worth of things' existence solely in nature's relation to them (in their well-being), and were unable to procure such a worth originally {{ 423 }} (in freedom) for itself: then there would be, to be sure, (relative) ends in the world, yet no (absolute) final end, since the being of such rational creatures would forever remain devoid of purpose. The moral laws, however, are of that peculiar character whereby they prescribe something to reason as an end unconditionally — precisely, that is, in the manner the concept of a final end demands: and the existence of such a reason, one that in the relation to ends can be its own supreme law — put otherwise, the being of rational creatures under moral laws — is the sole thing conceivable as the final end {{{ 450 }}} of a world's existence. Should matters not stand thus, on the other hand, then either no end whatever underlies the existence of these beings in their cause, or ends without any final end lie at their basis.

As the formal rational condition upon our exercise of freedom, the moral law binds us of itself alone, without hanging upon any end as a material condition; but it does, besides, determine for us — and that _a priori_ — a final end, which it makes obligatory for us to strive after: and this final end is the ***highest*** ***good in the world*** attainable through freedom.

The condition, subjective in kind, under which a human being (and, by all our concepts, every finite rational being as well) can posit a final end for itself under the law stated above, is happiness. Hence, {{ 424 }} the supreme physical good realizable in the world, the good to be advanced as a final end so far as it lies with us, is ***happiness***: subject to the objective condition that the human being accord with the law of ***morality***, this constituting the worthiness to be happy.

These two requirements of the final end laid upon us by the moral law, however, we find it impossible — by any of our rational faculties — to represent to ourselves as ***linked*** together through mere natural causes and as fitting the idea of the final end so conceived. Thus the concept of such an end's ***practical necessity***, arising through the deployment of our powers, does not square with the theoretical concept of its ***physical possibility***, the possibility of its being effected, provided we tie to our freedom no causality (of a means) beyond that of nature.

It follows that we are obliged to posit a moral cause of the world (a world-author) in order to propose to ourselves a final end conformable to the moral law; and just so far as the latter is necessary, just so far (that is, to the same degree and on the same ground) is the former also to be posited as necessary: namely, that a God exists.[^**]

This proof, to which the form of logical precision may readily be {{ 425 }} imparted, does not intend to say: that assuming the existence of God {{{ 451 }}} is exactly as necessary as acknowledging the validity of the moral law; and hence that whoever cannot bring himself to be convinced of the former might judge himself released from the obligations flowing from the latter. No! only the ***intention*** of realizing, through obedience to the latter, the final end in the world — a happiness of rational beings coinciding harmoniously with obedience to moral laws, as the highest good of the world — would in that case have to be relinquished. Every rational being would still have to acknowledge himself as strictly bound by the precept of morals; since the laws of morals are formal, commanding unconditionally, with no regard for ends (as the matter of willing). But the one requirement of the final end, as practical reason prescribes it to worldly beings, is an irresistible end implanted in them by their nature (as {{ 426 }} finite beings) — an end that reason wishes only to subordinate to the moral law ***as*** an inviolable ***condition***, or else to see universalized in keeping with it, thereby making the promotion of happiness in accord with morality into the final end. This end, now — so far (as regards the former) as lies within our capacity — the moral law commands us to promote; whatever the upshot such striving may have, let it be what it will. Duty is discharged in the form taken by an earnest will, not in the mediating causes of one's succeeding.

Let us grant, then: that a man talks himself — swayed partly by the feebleness of all those so extravagantly praised speculative arguments, partly by sundry irregularities meeting him in nature and in the moral world — into the proposition: that there is no God; even so, he would in his own eyes be a worthless creature were he, on that account, to treat the laws of duty as merely fancied, void, and non-obligatory, and to make up his mind to transgress them without qualm. Such a man, even later on — should he manage in the end to become convinced of what he had at first called into doubt — would, with that cast of mind, still remain a worthless creature all the same: even though he discharged his duty, but out of fear, or from a reward-seeking motive, without {{{ 452 }}} any duty-revering disposition, as exactly, in point of effect, as could ever {{ 427 }} be demanded of him. Conversely, were he as a believer to follow it, by his own conviction, sincerely and without self-interest, and yet, each time he set up the case by way of trial that he might one day be convinced there is no God, straightaway to deem himself free of every moral obligation: then things must indeed stand but poorly with the inner moral disposition within him.

We may, accordingly, take an upright man (Spinoza, say) who remains firmly convinced within himself: that no God exists, and (since, as regards the object of morality, it comes out to the same result) that there is likewise no future life; how, then, will he judge his own inner destination of ends by way of the moral law, which he actively reveres? For himself he asks no advantage out of obeying it, neither in this world nor in any other; disinterestedly he wills, rather, simply to bring about the good toward which that holy law directs all his powers. But his endeavor is bounded; and from nature he may indeed look for an occasional, chance concurrence here and there, yet never for a lawful concord, arriving by steady rules (as his maxims inwardly are and must be), with the end that he nonetheless feels himself bound and driven to effect. Fraud, violence, and envy will forever be in full swing about him, even {{ 428 }} though he himself is honest, peaceable, and kindly disposed; and the righteous men he yet encounters beyond himself will, for all their worthiness to be happy, nonetheless be delivered up by nature — which takes no notice of this — to every ill of privation, sickness, and untimely death, no less than the other animals of the earth, and will remain so ever after, until one wide grave engulfs them one and all (upright or not, it counts for the same here) and flings them, who could believe themselves the final end of creation, back down into the gulf of the aimless chaos of matter out of which they had been drawn. — The end, then, that this well-meaning man had, and was bound to have, before his eyes in obeying the moral laws, he would assuredly have to surrender as impossible; or, if he means here too to stay faithful to the summons of his ethical inner destination, and not to let the respect which the ethical law directly breathes into him toward obedience be enfeebled by the nullity of the sole ideal final end answering to its lofty demand (which cannot come about without inflicting some damage on the moral disposition): {{{ 453 }}} then he must — as indeed he very well may, since the thing is at least not self-contradictory — for practical purposes, that is, so as at least to frame for himself a concept of the possibility of the final end morally prescribed to him, posit the existence of a ***moral*** world-author, that is, {{ 429 }} of God.

[^*]: I say advisedly: ***under*** moral laws. It is not the human being ***according to*** moral laws — that is, one whose conduct conforms to them — who is the final end of creation. For by the latter expression we would assert more than we know: namely, that it lies within a world-author's power to bring it about that the human being always conduct himself in a manner adequate to the moral laws; something which presupposes a concept of freedom and of nature (the latter being the only one of the two for which an external author can be thought) that would have to embrace an insight into nature's supersensible substratum, and into the sameness of this substratum with that which renders causality through freedom possible in the world — an insight reaching far beyond anything our reason can attain. Only of the ***human being under moral laws*** are we able to say, without transgressing the limits of our insight: that his existence makes up the final end of the world. This, moreover, tallies entirely with the verdict of human reason when it reflects, in moral terms, upon how the world runs its course. We take ourselves to catch the traces of a wise relation to ends even in what is evil, if only we observe that the wanton malefactor does not die until he has borne the richly merited penalty for his misdeeds. On our concepts of free causality, good or bad behavior rests with us; the supreme wisdom of the world's governance, however, we locate in this, that for the former the occasion, but for both the outcome, is meted out according to moral laws. It is in this latter that the honor of God properly consists — a thing therefore not inaptly termed by theologians the ultimate end of creation. — It should further be remarked that, in employing the word creation, we understand by it nothing beyond what has here been stated, namely the cause of the ***existence*** of a ***world***, or of the things (the substances) within it; as the very concept of this word indeed entails (_actuatio substantiae est creatio_): which accordingly does not yet carry with it the presupposition of a freely acting, and therefore intelligent, cause (whose existence is the very thing we mean first to prove).
[^**]: This moral argument is meant to furnish no ***objectively*** valid proof of God's existence, not to demonstrate to the doubting believer that a God exists; but rather that, if he wishes to think in a morally consistent way, he ***must incorporate*** the acceptance of this proposition among the maxims of his practical reason. — Nor is it thereby being claimed: that it is necessary ***for morality*** to presuppose a happiness of all rational worldly beings proportioned to their morality; rather: that it is necessary ***through it***. Hence it is a ***subjectively***, for moral beings, sufficient argument.
