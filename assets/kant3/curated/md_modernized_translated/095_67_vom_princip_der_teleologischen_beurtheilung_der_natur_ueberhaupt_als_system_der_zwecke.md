## § 67. On the Principle of the Teleological Judging of Nature in General as a System of Ends

We have said above of the ***outer*** purposiveness of natural things: that it provides no sufficient authorization to use them at the same time as ends of nature for the explanatory grounds of their existence and to use their accidentally purposive effects in the idea as grounds of their existence according to the principle of final causes. Thus one cannot immediately hold the ***rivers*** to be natural ends because they promote communication among peoples in the interior of lands, nor the ***mountains*** because they contain the sources for these rivers and, for their preservation, the snow reserve for rainless times, nor likewise the ***slope*** of the land which drains these waters and allows the land {{{ 378 }}} to become dry: because, although {{ 299 }} this shape of the earth's surface was very necessary for the origin and preservation of the vegetable and animal kingdoms, it yet has nothing in itself to the possibility of which one would see oneself forced to assume a causality according to ends. The very same applies to plants which man uses for his necessity or delight: to animals, the camel, the cattle, the horse, dogs, etc., which he can use so diversely partly for his nourishment, partly for his service, and for the most part cannot do without at all. Of things, none of which one has cause to regard as an end in itself, the outer relation can be judged as purposive only hypothetically.

To judge a thing to be a natural end in virtue of its inner form is something quite different from holding the existence of this thing to be an end of nature. For the latter assertion we require not merely the concept of a possible end, but the cognition of the ultimate end (_scopus_) of nature, which requires a relation of the same to something supersensible that far transcends all of our teleological cognition of nature; for the end of the existence of nature itself must be sought beyond nature. The inner form of a mere blade of grass can sufficiently prove its origin, as possible only according to the rule of ends, for our human faculty of judging. But if one departs from this and looks only at the use that other natural beings make of it, {{ 300 }} thus leaving the contemplation of the inner organization and looking only at outer purposive relations, such as how the grass is necessary for the cattle, and how the latter is necessary for man as a means to his existence; and if one does not see why it is then necessary that human beings exist (which, if one has in mind, say, the New Hollanders or the Fuegians, might not be so easy to answer): then one arrives at no categorical end, but all of this purposive relation rests on a condition to be projected ever further, which, as unconditioned (the existence of a thing as an ultimate end), lies entirely outside the physico-teleological contemplation of the world. But then such a thing is also not a natural end; for it (or its entire genus) is not to be regarded as a natural product.

It is only matter, so far as it is organized, which necessarily carries with it the concept of itself as a natural end, because this specific form of its is at the same time a product of nature. But this concept {{{ 379 }}} now necessarily leads to the idea of the whole of nature as a system according to the rule of ends, to which idea all mechanism of nature must now be subordinated according to principles of reason (at least in order to test natural appearance thereby). The principle of reason belongs to it as merely subjective, i.e., as a maxim: Everything in the world is good for something or other; nothing in it is in vain; and one is {{ 301 }} authorized, indeed called upon, by the example that nature gives in its organic products, to expect nothing of it and its laws but what is purposive on the whole.

It is understood that this is not a principle for the determining, but only for the reflecting power of judgment, that it is regulative and not constitutive, and that we thereby obtain only a guide to contemplate natural things, in relation to a determining ground that is already given, according to a new lawful order, and to extend natural science according to another principle, namely that of final causes, yet without prejudice to that of the mechanism of their causality. Moreover, it is by no means decided thereby whether anything that we judge according to this principle is ***intentionally*** an end of nature: whether the grass is there for the cattle or sheep, and whether these and the other natural things are there for man. It is good to contemplate from this side even those things that are unpleasant to us and contrary to purpose in particular relations. Thus one could say, e.g.: that the vermin which plague humans in their clothes, hair, or beds are, according to a wise arrangement of nature, an incentive to cleanliness, which in itself is already an important means for the preservation of health. Or that the mosquito and other stinging insects, which make the deserts of America {{ 302 }} so troublesome to the savages, are so many goads of activity for these developing human beings, in order to drain the swamps and to make the dense forests that block the draft of air light, and thereby, likewise through the cultivation of the soil, to make their residence at the same time healthier. Even what seems contrary to nature in man's inner organization, when treated in this way, gives an entertaining, at times also instructive, prospect of a teleological order of things to which, without such a principle, mere physical contemplation alone would not lead us. Just as some judge the tapeworm to be given to the human being or animal which it inhabits, as it were as a compensation {{{ 380 }}} for a certain defect in its vital organs: so I would ask whether dreams (without which sleep never is, even if one only rarely remembers them) might not be a purposive arrangement of nature, namely, in that during the relaxation of all bodily moving forces they serve, by means of the imagination and its great activity (which in this state for the most part rises to the point of affect), to move the vital organs most inwardly; just as during an overloaded stomach, where this movement is so much more necessary, it commonly plays with so much more liveliness in night sleep; so that consequently, without this internally moving force and fatiguing restlessness of which we accuse dreams (which {{ 303 }} yet in fact are perhaps remedies), sleep itself, even in a healthy state, would probably be a complete extinction of life.

Natural beauty too, i.e., its agreement with the free play of our cognitive faculties in the apprehension and judging of its appearance, can in this way be regarded as an objective purposiveness of nature in its whole, as a system of which man is a member: once the teleological judging of the same through natural ends, which the organized beings put into our hands, has authorized us to the idea of a great system of the ends of nature. We can regard it as a favor[^*], that nature has had for us, that over and above the useful she has still distributed beauty and charms so richly, and love her on that account, just as we contemplate her with respect because of her immensity, and {{ 304 }} feel ourselves ennobled in this contemplation: just as if nature had quite specifically in this intention set up and decorated her magnificent stage.

We wish to say nothing else in this section than that, once we have discovered in nature a faculty of producing products that can be thought by us only according to the concept of final causes, we may go further and judge even those which (or their, although purposive, {{{ 381 }}} relation) do not indeed make it necessary to seek another principle for their possibility beyond the mechanism of blindly acting causes, nonetheless as belonging to a system of ends: because the former idea already, as regards its ground, leads us beyond the sensible world; since then the unity of the supersensible principle must be regarded as valid in the same way not merely for certain species of natural beings, but for the whole of nature as a system.

[^*]: In the aesthetic part it was said: ***we look upon beautiful nature with favor***, in that we have a completely free (uninterested) liking for its form. For in this mere judgment of taste, no regard at all is paid to the end for which these natural beauties exist: whether to arouse a pleasure in us, or without any relation to us as ends. In a teleological judgment, however, we do pay attention to this relation; and there we can ***regard it as a favor of nature*** that she has wished to be conducive to our culture by setting up so many beautiful shapes.
