## § 67. On the principle for the teleological judging of nature in general, considered as a system of ends

We remarked above concerning the ***external*** purposiveness of natural things: that it yields no sufficient entitlement to employ them, at the same time as ends of nature, as explanatory grounds of their existence, and to use their contingently purposive effects, held in the idea, as grounds of their existence according to the principle of final causes. Thus the ***rivers***, because they promote intercourse among peoples in the interior of countries, the ***mountains***, because they contain the sources for these rivers and, for the maintenance of the same, the store of snow for rainless seasons, and likewise the ***slope*** of the land, which carries off these waters and lets the ground {{{ 378 }}} become dry, are on that account not straightaway to be taken for natural ends: because, although {{ 299 }} this configuration of the earth's surface was highly necessary for the origination and maintenance of the vegetable and animal kingdoms, it nevertheless has nothing in itself for whose possibility one would find oneself compelled to assume a causality according to ends. The very same holds of plants, which the human being uses for his needs or his enjoyment: of animals, the camel, the ox, the horse, the dog, etc., which he can employ in such manifold ways partly for his nourishment, partly for his service, and for the most part cannot do without at all. In the case of things of which one has no cause to regard any as an end in itself, the external relation can be judged to be purposive only hypothetically.

To judge a thing as a natural end on account of its inner form is something wholly different from holding the existence of this thing to be an end of nature. For this latter claim we need not merely a concept of some possible end, but rather cognition of the final end (_scopus_) of nature, which demands a relation of the same to something supersensible that far outstrips all our teleological cognition of nature; for the end of nature's own existence must be sought beyond nature. The inner form of a mere blade of grass can sufficiently establish, for our human faculty of judging, that its origin is possible solely under the rule of ends. But if one departs from this and looks only at the use that other natural beings make of it, {{ 300 }} thus abandoning the consideration of the inner organization and attending only to external purposive relations—how the grass is needed by cattle, how these are needed by the human being as a means to his existence—and one does not see why it should be necessary that human beings exist (which, if one has in mind, say, the New Hollanders or the Fuegians, might not so easily be answered): then one arrives at no categorical end, but rather all this purposive relation rests on a condition that must always be set ever further back, a condition that, as unconditioned (the existence of a thing as final end), lies wholly outside the physico-teleological consideration of the world. In that case, however, such a thing is also not a natural end; for it (or its entire species) is not to be regarded as a product of nature.

It is thus only matter, insofar as it is organized, that necessarily carries with it the concept of itself as a natural end, because this specific form of it is at once itself a product of nature. This concept, however, {{{ 379 }}} now necessarily leads to the idea of the whole of nature as a system according to the rule of ends, an idea to which the entire mechanism of nature must now be subordinated according to principles of reason (at least so as to put the appearance of nature to the test against it). Reason's principle pertains to it only as something subjective, i.e., as a maxim: everything in the world is good for something or other; nothing in it is in vain; and one is {{ 301 }} entitled, indeed called upon, by the example that nature gives in its organic products, to expect from it and its laws nothing but what is purposive on the whole.

It goes without saying that this is a principle not for the determining but only for the reflecting power of judgment, that it is regulative and not constitutive, and that through it we obtain merely a guiding thread for considering natural things in relation to a determining ground that is already given, according to a new lawful order, and for extending natural science according to another principle, namely that of final causes, yet without detriment to the principle of the mechanism of its causality. For the rest, this in no way settles whether anything that we judge according to this principle is ***intentionally*** an end of nature: whether the grasses exist for the sake of the ox or the sheep, and whether these and the remaining natural things exist for the sake of the human being. It is good to consider even those things that are disagreeable to us and contrary to purpose in particular relations from this side as well. Thus one could say, e.g.: the vermin that plague human beings in their clothes, their hair, or their bedsteads are, by a wise arrangement of nature, an incentive to cleanliness, which of itself already is an important means of preserving health. Or the mosquitoes and other stinging insects that make the deserts of America {{ 302 }} so troublesome to the savages are so many goads to activity for these nascent human beings, prompting them to drain the marshes and to thin out the dense forests that block the movement of the air, and thereby, together with the cultivation of the soil, to make their dwelling-place at the same time more healthful. Even what appears to run counter to nature in the human being's inner organization, when handled in this manner, affords an entertaining and at times also instructive glimpse into a teleological ordering of things—one to which mere physical observation, lacking such a principle, would not by itself lead us. Just as some judge the tapeworm to have been given to the human being or the animal in which it dwells as it were to compensate for a certain deficiency of its vital organs, {{{ 380 }}} so I would ask whether dreams (without which sleep never occurs, even though we but seldom remember them) might not be a purposive arrangement of nature, in that, namely, during the relaxation of all the bodily moving forces, they serve to move the vital organs most inwardly by means of the imagination and its great busyness (which in this state mostly rises to the level of affect); just as they also, when the stomach is overfilled, where this movement is all the more necessary, commonly play in the night's sleep with all the more liveliness; so that consequently, without this inwardly moving force and wearying unrest, of which we accuse dreams (which {{ 303 }} are yet in fact perhaps remedies), sleep even in a healthy condition would probably be a complete extinction of life.

The beauty of nature too, i.e., its accord with the free play of our powers of cognition in the apprehension and judging of its appearance, can in this way be regarded as an objective purposiveness of nature in its whole, as a system in which the human being is a member: once the teleological judging of it through the natural ends that the organized beings put at our disposal has entitled us to the idea of a great system of the ends of nature. We can regard it as a favor[^*] that nature has shown us that, over and above the useful, it dispensed beauty and charms so abundantly, and can love it on that account, just as we can regard it with respect on account of its immeasurability and, in this contemplation, {{ 304 }} feel ourselves ennobled: exactly as if nature had, quite properly with this intention, set up and adorned its magnificent stage.

We mean to say nothing else in this § than that, once we have discovered in nature a capacity to bring forth products that can be thought by us only according to the concept of final causes, we may go further and, even in the case of those which (or whose relation, purposive though it be {{{ 381 }}}) do not exactly make it necessary to seek, beyond the mechanism of blindly operating causes, another principle for their possibility, may nonetheless judge them as belonging to a system of ends: because the former idea already, so far as its ground is concerned, leads us beyond the world of sense; for the unity of the supersensible principle must then, in precisely this fashion, be held to hold good not for particular kinds of natural being alone, but for nature taken as a whole, considered as a system.

[^*]: In the aesthetic part it was said: ***that we regard beautiful nature with favor***, inasmuch as we take an entirely free (disinterested) satisfaction in its form. For in this bare judgment of taste no account whatever is taken of the end for which these natural beauties exist: whether in order to arouse a pleasure in us, or without any relation to us as ends. In a teleological judgment, however, we also attend to this relation; and there we can ***regard it as a favor of nature*** that, by presenting so many beautiful shapes, it has wished to be conducive to our culture.
