## § 67. On the principle for the teleological judging of nature in general, considered as a system of ends

Concerning the ***external*** purposiveness of natural things we stated earlier that it affords no adequate warrant for pressing them into service, as ends of nature at once, to explain why they exist, nor for taking their accidentally purposive effects, as conceived in the idea, as reasons for their existence under the principle of final causes. Thus the ***rivers***, because they promote intercourse among peoples in the interior of countries, the ***mountains***, because they contain the sources for these rivers and, for the maintenance of the same, the store of snow for rainless seasons, and likewise the ***slope*** of the land, which carries off these waters and lets the ground {{{ 378 }}} become dry, are on that account not straightaway to be taken for natural ends: because, although {{ 299 }} this configuration of the earth's surface was highly necessary for the origination and maintenance of the vegetable and animal kingdoms, it nevertheless has nothing in itself for whose possibility one would find oneself compelled to assume a causality according to ends. The very same holds of plants, which the human being uses for his needs or his enjoyment: of animals, the camel, the ox, the horse, the dog, etc., which he can employ in such manifold ways partly for his nourishment, partly for his service, and for the most part cannot do without at all. In the case of things of which one has no cause to regard any as an end in itself, the external relation can be judged to be purposive only hypothetically.

To judge a thing as a natural end on account of its inner form is something wholly different from holding the existence of this thing to be an end of nature. For this latter claim we need not merely a concept of some possible end, but rather cognition of the final end (_scopus_) of nature, which demands a relation of the same to something supersensible that far outstrips all our teleological cognition of nature; for the end of nature's own existence must be sought beyond nature. The inner form of a mere blade of grass can sufficiently establish, for our human faculty of judging, that its origin is possible solely under the rule of ends. But if one departs from this and looks only at the use that other natural beings make of it, {{ 300 }} thus abandoning the consideration of the inner organization and attending only to external purposive relations—how the grass is needed by cattle, how these are needed by the human being as a means to his existence—and one does not see why it should be necessary that human beings exist (which, if one has in mind, say, the New Hollanders or the Fuegians, might not so easily be answered): then one arrives at no categorical end, but rather all this purposive relation rests on a condition that must always be set ever further back, a condition that, as unconditioned (the existence of a thing as final end), lies wholly outside the physico-teleological consideration of the world. But then such a thing fails to be a natural end as well; for it (or indeed its entire species) cannot count as a product of nature.

Therefore nothing but matter, so far as it is organized, of necessity brings the concept of itself as a natural end in its train, since this, its specific form, is at once nature's own product. This concept, however, {{{ 379 }}} now carries us necessarily onward to the idea of nature in its totality as a single system built on the rule of ends—an idea beneath which nature's entire mechanism must from now on be subordinated in accordance with principles of reason (at least so as to test the appearance of nature against it). Reason's principle pertains to it only as something subjective, i.e., as a maxim: everything in the world is good for something or other; nothing in it is in vain; and one is {{ 301 }} entitled, indeed called upon, by the example that nature gives in its organic products, to expect from it and its laws nothing but what is purposive on the whole.

It is self-evident that this serves as a principle belonging not to the determining but solely to the reflecting power of judgment, that its role is regulative rather than constitutive, and that it thereby furnishes us with nothing more than a guiding clue for viewing natural things—in their bearing on a ground of determination already at hand—under a new order of law, and for broadening natural science by a further principle, namely that of final causes, though this leaves wholly intact the principle of its causal mechanism. For the rest, this in no way settles whether anything that we judge according to this principle is ***intentionally*** an end of nature: whether the grasses exist for the sake of the ox or the sheep, and whether these and the remaining natural things exist for the sake of the human being. It is good to consider even those things that are disagreeable to us and contrary to purpose in particular relations from this side as well. One might say, for instance: the vermin that torment people in their clothing, their hair, or their bedsteads are, according to a wise provision of nature, a spur toward cleanliness, which on its own already counts as an important means whereby health is preserved. Or, again, the mosquitoes together with other stinging insects, which render the deserts of America {{ 302 }} so burdensome to the savages, count as so many prods to industry for these fledgling human beings, driving them to drain the swamps, to clear the dense forests that shut out the current of air, and thereby—along with the tilling of the soil—to render their dwelling-place healthier at once. Even what appears to run counter to nature in the human being's inner organization, when handled in this manner, affords an entertaining and at times also instructive glimpse into a teleological ordering of things—one to which mere physical observation, lacking such a principle, would not by itself lead us. Just as some judge the tapeworm to have been given to the human being or the animal in which it dwells as it were to compensate for a certain deficiency of its vital organs, {{{ 380 }}} so I would ask whether dreams (without which sleep never occurs, even though we but seldom remember them) might not be a purposive arrangement of nature, in that, namely, during the relaxation of all the bodily moving forces, they serve to move the vital organs most inwardly by means of the imagination and its great busyness (which in this state mostly rises to the level of affect); just as they also, when the stomach is overfilled, where this movement is all the more necessary, commonly play in the night's sleep with all the more liveliness; so that consequently, without this inwardly moving force and wearying unrest, of which we accuse dreams (which {{ 303 }} are yet in fact perhaps remedies), sleep even in a healthy condition would probably be a complete extinction of life.

Beauty in nature, too—i.e., its agreement, in the apprehension and appraisal of its appearance, with the free play of our powers of cognition—can in this fashion be viewed as an objective purposiveness belonging to nature as one whole, a system wherein the human being stands as a member: provided the teleological judging of nature, through those natural ends which organized beings put into our hands, has once warranted for us the idea of one great system embracing nature's ends. We can regard it as a favor[^*] that nature has shown us that, over and above the useful, it dispensed beauty and charms so abundantly, and can love it on that account, just as we can regard it with respect on account of its immeasurability and, in this contemplation, {{ 304 }} feel ourselves ennobled: exactly as if nature had, quite properly with this intention, set up and adorned its magnificent stage.

In this § we intend to assert nothing beyond this: that, having once detected in nature a power to bring forth products conceivable by us solely under the concept of final causes, we then press on and are entitled to count as belonging to a system of ends even those products which—or whose relation, purposive though it be {{{ 381 }}}—do not strictly oblige us to look, over and above the mechanism of blindly acting causes, for some further principle of their possibility: for that first idea already, as regards its ground, carries us out past the sensible world; since the unity of the supersensible principle must count as valid in one and the same way not only for certain species of natural being, but equally for the entirety of nature as a system.

[^*]: In the aesthetic portion it was remarked: ***that we behold beautiful nature with favor***, in that we find in its form a wholly free (disinterested) satisfaction. For in this judgment of taste, taken merely as such, no consideration at all is given to what end these beauties of nature exist for: whether it be to awaken a pleasure within us, or with no reference whatever to us as ends. In a teleological judgment, by contrast, we do pay heed to this relation as well; and here we may ***regard it as a favor of nature*** that, through the display of so many beautiful forms, it has meant to further our culture.
