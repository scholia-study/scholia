## § 32. First peculiarity of the judgment of taste

In determining its object as regards satisfaction (as beauty), the judgment of taste lays claim to ***everyone's*** assent, just as if it were objective.

To say: this flower is beautiful amounts to nothing more than ascribing to it its own {{{ 282 }}} claim upon everyone's satisfaction. Through the agreeableness of its scent it has no claims whatever. This scent delights the one, while to the other it makes the head swim. What, then, could one surmise from this, other than that beauty must be taken to be a property of the flower itself, one that does not conform to the diversity of minds and of so many senses, but to which these must conform if they wish to pass judgment upon it? And yet this is not how matters stand. For the judgment of taste consists precisely in this, that it calls a thing beautiful only according to that constitution by which the thing accommodates itself to our manner of taking it in.

Moreover, of every judgment that is meant to prove the subject's taste it is required that the subject judge on its own, without needing {{ 137 }} to grope its way through experience among the judgments of others and to inform itself beforehand of their satisfaction or dissatisfaction in the same object, and hence that it pronounce its judgment not as imitation, on the grounds that a thing does, as it happens, really please universally, but rather _a priori_. Yet one might expect that an _a priori_ judgment would have to include a concept of the object for the cognition of which it supplies the principle; the judgment of taste, however, rests on no concepts whatsoever and is nowhere cognition at all, but merely an aesthetic judgment.

For that reason a young poet is not to be turned from his conviction that his poem is beautiful, neither by the public's judgment nor by that of his friends; and if he does grant them a hearing, it happens not because his estimate of the matter has now changed, but because — even supposing (at least as concerns him) that the whole public were of a false taste — he still, in his hunger for applause, finds cause to conform himself to the common delusion (even against his own judgment). Only later, once his power of judgment has been sharpened further through practice, does he freely depart from his earlier judgment; just as he also handles those of his judgments that rest wholly upon reason. Taste lays claim to autonomy alone. Were one to let the judgments of others serve as the determining ground of one's own, that would be heteronomy.

That one rightly extols the works of the ancients as models and {{ 138 }} calls their authors classical, like a certain nobility among writers who by their precedent give laws to the people, seems to point to sources of taste that are _a posteriori_ and to refute its autonomy in every subject. Yet with equal justice one could {{{ 283 }}} say that the ancient mathematicians, who even now pass for models — scarcely to be dispensed with — of the utmost thoroughness and elegance in the synthetic method, no less gave proof of an imitative reason on our side and of its inability to generate, out of itself, rigorous proofs endowed with the greatest intuition by means of the construction of concepts. No employment of our powers, however free it might be — and this holds even of reason, which is obliged to derive all its judgments _a priori_ from one common source — would keep clear of faulty attempts, if every subject had always to begin wholly from the raw endowment of its native temper and no others had gone before it with their own; and that precedence was aimed not at turning those who come after into mere imitators, but rather, by the way they proceeded, at putting others onto the trail, so that these might search out the principles in their own selves and thereby follow their own, and often better, course. Even in religion, where surely each one must take the rule of his conduct from himself, since he also remains answerable for it and cannot shift the blame for his transgressions {{ 139 }} onto others as teachers or predecessors, still nothing is ever achieved by general precepts, whether one has received them from priests or philosophers or drawn them from oneself, so much as by an example of virtue or holiness, which, set forth in history, neither renders dispensable the autonomy of virtue drawn from the proper and original idea of morality (_a priori_), nor transforms it into a mechanism of imitation. ***Following after***, which relates to a precedent, and not imitation, is the correct expression for every influence that the products of an exemplary author can have upon others; which signifies no more than this: to draw from the same sources from which that author himself drew, and to learn from one's predecessor merely the manner of conducting oneself therein. Yet of all our faculties and talents it is taste, above all, that — since its judgment admits of no determination by concepts or rules — has the greatest need of examples of whatever, in the onward march of culture, has held its place longest in approval, lest it should soon lapse back into coarseness and sink to the rawness of those first attempts.
