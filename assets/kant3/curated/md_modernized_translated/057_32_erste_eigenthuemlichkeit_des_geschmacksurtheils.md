## § 32. First Peculiarity of the Judgment of Taste

The judgment of taste determines its object in respect of liking (as beauty) with a claim to ***everyone's*** assent, as if it were objective.

To say: this flower is beautiful, means just as much as merely repeating its own {{{ 282 }}} claim to everyone's liking. Through the agreeableness of its scent, it has no claims at all. This scent delights one person, while it makes another's head swim. What else should one suspect from this, than that beauty must be held to be a property of the flower itself, which does not adapt to the diversity of heads and of so many senses, but according to which these must adapt if they wish to judge of it? And yet it is not so. For the judgment of taste consists precisely in this: that it calls a thing beautiful only in virtue of that constitution in which it adapts to our way of receiving it.

Moreover, it is required of every judgment that is to prove the taste of the subject: that the subject should judge for itself, without having {{ 137 }} to grope about by means of experience among the judgments of others and to inform itself beforehand of their liking or disliking of the same object, and hence should pronounce its judgment not as an imitation, because a thing perhaps actually pleases universally, but _a priori_. But one would think that an _a priori_ judgment must contain a concept of the object, for the cognition of which it contains the principle; yet the judgment of taste is not grounded on concepts at all, and is not a cognition at all, but only an aesthetic judgment.

Hence a young poet does not let himself be dissuaded from the persuasion that his poem is beautiful, neither by the judgment of the public nor by that of his friends; and if he lends them an ear, this does not happen because he now judges it differently, but because, even if (at least in respect of himself) the entire public had a false taste, he still finds reason, in his desire for applause, to accommodate himself (even contrary to his own judgment) to the common illusion. Only later, when his power of judgment has been more sharpened by practice, does he voluntarily depart from his former judgment; just as he does with his judgments that rest entirely on reason. Taste claims only autonomy. To make the judgments of others the determining ground of one's own would be heteronomy.

That one rightly praises the works of the ancients as models, and {{ 138 }} calls their authors classical, like a certain nobility among writers who give laws to the people by their precedence, seems to indicate _a posteriori_ sources of taste and to refute the autonomy of taste in each subject. Yet one could just as well {{{ 283 }}} say that the ancient mathematicians, who to this day are held to be indispensable models of the highest thoroughness and elegance of the synthetic method, also proved an imitating reason on our part, and an inability of the same to produce from itself strict proofs with the greatest intuition through construction of concepts. There is no use of our powers at all, however free it may be, and even of reason (which must draw all of its judgments from the common source _a priori_), which, if each subject always had to begin entirely from the raw predisposition of its natural state, would not fall into faulty attempts, had not others preceded it with theirs, not in order to make followers into mere imitators, but rather through their procedure to put others on the track of seeking the principles in themselves and thus of taking their own, often better, path. Even in religion, where certainly everyone must derive the rule of his conduct from himself, because he also remains personally responsible for it and cannot shift the blame for his transgressions {{ 139 }} onto others as teachers or predecessors, never will so much be accomplished through general rules that one may have obtained either from priests or from philosophers, or even taken from oneself, as through an example of virtue or holiness, which, set up in history, does not make the autonomy of virtue from one's own and original idea of morality (_a priori_) dispensable, or transform it into a mechanism of imitation. ***Following***, which refers to a precedent, not imitation, is the right expression for any influence that the products of an exemplary author can have on others; which means only as much as: drawing from the same sources from which that author himself drew, and learning from one's predecessor only the manner of conducting oneself in doing so. But among all faculties and talents, taste is precisely that which, because its judgment is not determinable by concepts and rules, stands in greatest need of examples of what has longest preserved approval in the progress of culture, so as not to become uncouth again quickly and fall back into the crudeness of the first attempts.
