## § 32. First peculiarity of the judgment of taste

The judgment of taste determines its object with regard to satisfaction (as beauty) with a claim to ***everyone's*** assent, as though it were objective.

To say: this flower is beautiful amounts to nothing more than ascribing to it its own {{{ 282 }}} claim upon everyone's satisfaction. Through the agreeableness of its scent it has no claims whatever. This scent delights the one, while to the other it makes the head swim. What, then, could one surmise from this, other than that beauty must be taken to be a property of the flower itself, one that does not conform to the diversity of minds and of so many senses, but to which these must conform if they wish to pass judgment upon it? And yet this is not how matters stand. For the judgment of taste consists precisely in this, that it calls a thing beautiful only according to that constitution by which the thing accommodates itself to our manner of taking it in.

Moreover, of every judgment that is meant to prove the subject's taste it is required that the subject judge on its own, without needing {{ 137 }} to grope its way through experience among the judgments of others and to inform itself beforehand of their satisfaction or dissatisfaction in the same object, and hence that it pronounce its judgment not as imitation, on the grounds that a thing does, as it happens, really please universally, but rather _a priori_. One would suppose, however, that a judgment _a priori_ must contain a concept of the object, for whose cognition it holds the principle; the judgment of taste, though, is not grounded on concepts at all and is in no respect cognition, but only an aesthetic judgment.

Hence a young poet will not be dissuaded from the persuasion that his poem is beautiful, neither by the judgment of the public nor by that of his friends; and if he does lend them his ear, this happens not because he now judges the matter otherwise, but because, even though (at least as far as he is concerned) the whole public may have a false taste, he nonetheless finds cause, in his craving for applause, to accommodate himself (even against his own judgment) to the common delusion. Only later, once his power of judgment has been sharpened further through practice, does he freely depart from his earlier judgment; just as he also handles those of his judgments that rest wholly upon reason. Taste lays claim to autonomy alone. To make the judgments of others into the determining ground of one's own would be heteronomy.

That one rightly extols the works of the ancients as models and {{ 138 }} calls their authors classical, like a certain nobility among writers who by their precedent give laws to the people, seems to point to sources of taste that are _a posteriori_ and to refute its autonomy in every subject. Yet one might just as well {{{ 283 }}} say that the ancient mathematicians, who to this day are held to be well-nigh indispensable models of the highest thoroughness and elegance of the synthetic method, likewise proved an imitative reason on our part and an incapacity of that reason to bring forth from itself strict proofs with the greatest intuition through the construction of concepts. There is no use of our powers whatever, however free it may be, and even of reason (which must draw all its judgments from the common source _a priori_), which would not fall into faulty attempts, were every subject always to begin entirely from the raw disposition of its temperament, had not others gone before it with theirs — not so as to make those who follow into mere imitators, but rather so as, through their procedure, to set others on the track of seeking the principles within themselves and thus of taking their own, often better, course. Even in religion, where surely each one must take the rule of his conduct from himself, since he also remains answerable for it and cannot shift the blame for his transgressions {{ 139 }} onto others as teachers or predecessors, still nothing is ever achieved by general precepts, whether one has received them from priests or philosophers or drawn them from oneself, so much as by an example of virtue or holiness, which, set forth in history, neither renders dispensable the autonomy of virtue drawn from the proper and original idea of morality (_a priori_), nor transforms it into a mechanism of imitation. ***Following after***, which relates to a precedent, and not imitation, is the correct expression for every influence that the products of an exemplary author can have upon others; which signifies no more than this: to draw from the same sources from which that author himself drew, and to learn from one's predecessor merely the manner of conducting oneself therein. But among all the faculties and talents, taste is precisely the one which, because its judgment cannot be determined by concepts and precepts, stands most in need of examples of that which has maintained itself longest in approval in the advance of culture, so as not soon to grow uncouth again and to relapse into the crudeness of the first attempts.
