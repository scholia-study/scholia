## § 68. On the Principle of Teleology as an Inner Principle of Natural Science

The principles of a science are either internal to it and are called domestic (_principia domestica_); or they are grounded on concepts that can find a place only outside of it and are ***foreign*** principles (_peregrina_). Sciences that contain the latter {{ 305 }} lay borrowed propositions (_lemmata_) at the basis of their doctrines; i.e., they borrow some concept and with it a ground of arrangement from another science.

Every science is in itself a system; and it is not enough to build in it according to principles and thus to proceed technically, but one must also go to work with it architectonically, as a building existing for itself, and treat it not as an annex and as a part of another building, but as a whole for itself, although one can afterwards establish a transition from this into that or reciprocally.

If one therefore introduces the concept of God into natural science and into its context, in order to make the purposiveness in nature comprehensible to oneself, and afterwards uses this purposiveness in turn to prove that there is a God: then there is internal stability in neither of the two sciences; and a deceptive diallelus brings each into uncertainty, because they allow their boundaries to run into one another.

The expression of a purpose of nature already sufficiently prevents this confusion, so as not to mix natural science and the occasion it gives for the ***teleological*** estimation of its objects with the contemplation of God and thus with a ***theological*** derivation; and one must not regard it as insignificant whether one confuses that expression {{ 306 }} {{{ 382 }}} with that of a divine purpose in the arrangement of nature, or even passes off the latter as more proper and more suitable for a pious soul, because in the end it must come to deriving those purposive forms in nature from a wise Author of the world; but rather one must carefully and modestly restrict oneself to the expression that says precisely only as much as we know, namely, a purpose of nature. For before we even ask about the cause of nature itself, we find in nature and the course of its generation such products as are generated in it according to known laws of experience, according to which natural science must estimate its objects, and consequently must seek their causality in nature itself according to the rule of purposes. Hence it must not overstep its boundary in order to draw into itself, as a domestic principle, that to whose concept no experience can ever be adequate, and which one is authorized to venture upon only after the completion of natural science.

Properties of nature that can be demonstrated _a priori_ and thus, according to their possibility, understood from general principles without any contribution of experience, cannot—even if they carry a technical purposiveness with them—be counted at all as belonging to the teleology of nature, as a method belonging to physics for solving its questions, because they are absolutely necessary. Arithmetical and {{ 307 }} geometrical analogies, likewise general mechanical laws—however much the unification of different rules, apparently completely independent of one another, in a single principle may seem strange and admirable to us in them—therefore contain no claim to be teleological grounds of explanation in physics; and even if they deserve to be taken into consideration in the general theory of the purposiveness of the things of nature in general, this would nevertheless belong elsewhere, namely into metaphysics, and would not constitute an inner principle of natural science: just as, indeed, with the empirical laws of natural purposes in organized beings, it is not only permitted but even unavoidable to use the teleological ***mode of estimation*** as a principle of the doctrine of nature with respect to a peculiar class of its objects.

In order, then, that physics may keep strictly within its boundaries, it completely abstracts from the question of whether the natural purposes are ***intentional*** or ***unintentional***; for that would be meddling in foreign business {{{ 383 }}} (namely that of metaphysics). It is enough that they are objects that, according to laws of nature which we can think of only under the idea of purposes as a principle, are solely ***explainable*** and merely in this way, in accordance with their inner form, {{ 308 }} even only internally ***cognizable***. Hence, in order not to make oneself suspect of even the least presumption, as if one wanted to mix something that does not belong to physics at all, namely a supernatural cause, among our grounds of cognition, one speaks indeed in teleology of nature as if the purposiveness in it were intentional, but at the same time in such a way that one ascribes this intention to nature, i.e., to matter; whereby (since no misunderstanding can take place on this point, as no one of himself will ever ascribe intention in the proper sense of the word to a lifeless matter) one wishes to indicate that this word here signifies only a principle of the reflecting, not of the determining power of judgment, and thus is not intended to introduce any special ground of causality, but only to add for the use of reason another kind of investigation than that according to mechanical laws, in order to supplement the inadequacy of the latter even for the empirical search for all particular laws of nature. Therefore, in teleology, insofar as it is drawn into physics, one speaks quite rightly of the wisdom, the economy, the foresight, and the beneficence of nature, without thereby making of it an intelligent being (for that would be absurd); but also without daring to want to set another, intelligent being over it as its master builder, because this would be presumptuous[^*] {{ 309 }}: rather, only a kind of causality of nature according to an analogy with our own in the technical use of reason is thereby to be designated, in order to have before our eyes the rule according to which certain products of nature must be investigated.

Why, then, does teleology usually not constitute a proper part of theoretical natural science, but is drawn to theology as a propedeutic or transition? This is done in order to

{{{ 384 }}}

keep the study of nature, according to its mechanism, firmly to that which we can subject to our observation or to experiments in such a way that we could produce it ourselves like nature, at least according to the similarity of laws; for one understands completely only as much as one can make and bring about oneself according to concepts. But organization, as an internal purpose of nature, infinitely transcends all capacity for a similar representation through art; and as regards external nature-arrangements {{ 310 }} held to be purposive (e.g., winds, rain, and the like), physics indeed considers their mechanism; but their relation to purposes, insofar as this is supposed to be a condition necessarily belonging to the cause, physics cannot represent at all, because this necessity of connection concerns entirely the combination of our concepts and not the constitution of things.

[^*]: The German word ***vermessen*** is a good, meaningful word. A judgment in which one forgets to estimate the linear measure of one's powers (of the understanding) can sometimes sound very humble and yet make great claims and is nevertheless very presumptuous. Of this kind are most of those judgments by which one pretends to elevate divine wisdom, by attributing to it intentions in the works of creation and preservation that are actually intended to do honor to the ratiocinator's own wisdom.
