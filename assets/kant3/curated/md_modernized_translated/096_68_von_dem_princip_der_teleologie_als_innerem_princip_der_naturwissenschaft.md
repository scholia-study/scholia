## § 68. On the principle of teleology as an internal principle of natural science

A science's principles are either intrinsic to it, in which case they are termed native (_principia domestica_), or else they are grounded in concepts that can have their place only outside it, and are ***foreign*** principles (_peregrina_). Those sciences which contain principles of the latter kind {{ 305 }} set lemmas (_Lemmata_) at the foundation of their teachings; that is, from another science they take on loan some concept, and along with it a ground for ordering their material.

Each science is, taken on its own, a system, and it does not suffice to build within it according to principles and so to proceed technically; rather, one must go to work architectonically with it too, as with an edifice standing on its own, treating it not as an annex and a portion of some other building, but as a whole in its own right, even if afterward one can establish a passage from the one into the other, or reciprocally.

If, then, for the sake of natural science and within its context one imports the concept of God, so as to render the purposiveness in nature explicable to oneself, and then afterward has recourse to this very purposiveness in order to prove that a God exists, then neither of the two sciences possesses any inner stability, and a deceptive diallelon casts each into uncertainty by letting their respective boundaries run together into one another.

The expression "an end of nature" already guards well enough against this confusion, keeping natural science, and the occasion it affords for the ***teleological*** judging of its objects, from being blended with the contemplation of God and thus with a ***theological*** derivation; and one ought not to treat it as a matter of indifference whether one exchanges that expression {{ 306 }} {{{ 382 }}} for that of a divine end in the arrangement of nature, or indeed passes the latter off as more seemly and better befitting a devout soul, on the ground that in the end it must after all come down to tracing those purposive forms of nature back to a wise originator of the world; one must instead confine oneself carefully and modestly to the expression that states precisely no more than what we know, namely an end of nature. For even before we inquire into the cause of nature itself, we already come upon, within nature and in the course of its production, products of the sort that arise in it under familiar empirical laws—laws under which natural science is to judge its objects, and consequently must also seek their causality, following the rule of ends, within nature itself. It must therefore not overleap its boundary so as to draw into itself, as a native principle, that for whose concept no experience whatever could be adequate, and upon which one is warranted in venturing only once natural science has been brought to completion.

Natural constitutions that admit of _a priori_ demonstration, and whose possibility can therefore be grasped from universal principles without any recourse to experience, cannot—although they carry a technical purposiveness with them—for that very reason, because they are wholly necessary, be reckoned at all as part of the teleology of nature, taken as a method, proper to physics, for resolving the latter's questions. Arithmetical {{ 307 }} and geometrical analogies, and likewise universal mechanical laws—however strange and admirable we may find in them the union, within a single principle, of various rules that on the face of it are entirely independent of one another—advance on that account no claim to serve as teleological grounds of explanation within physics; and even where they merit being drawn into consideration in the general theory of the purposiveness of natural things at large, that theory would nonetheless have its home elsewhere, namely in metaphysics, and would form no internal principle of natural science: whereas, with the empirical laws of natural ends in organized beings, it is not merely admissible but positively unavoidable to put the teleological ***manner of judging*** to work as a principle of natural doctrine in regard to a particular class of its objects.

Now, in order that physics may hold itself precisely within its boundaries, it abstracts wholly from the question whether the ends of nature are ***intentional*** or ***unintentional***; for that would amount to intruding upon an alien task {{{ 383 }}} (namely that of metaphysics). Suffice it that they are objects which, solely and exclusively under natural laws that we can think, as a principle, only under the idea of ends, are ***explicable***, and which, in this way alone and in respect of their inner form, {{ 308 }} are even only inwardly ***knowable***. Accordingly, so as not to lay oneself open to the slightest presumption—as if one meant to smuggle in among our grounds of cognition something that has no place at all in physics, namely a supernatural cause—one does, to be sure, in teleology speak of nature as though the purposiveness in it were intentional, yet at the same time in such a manner that one attributes this intention to nature, that is, to matter; whereby one means to signal (since here no misunderstanding can occur, given that no one, of his own accord, would attribute intention in the strict sense of the word to a lifeless stuff) that this word here denotes merely a principle of the reflecting, and not of the determining power of judgment, and hence is not to introduce any special ground of causality, but only, for reason's use, to append a kind of investigation distinct from that carried on by mechanical laws, so as to make good the shortfall of the latter even in the empirical search for all the particular laws of nature. This is why, in teleology, insofar as it is enlisted for physics, one may speak quite properly of the wisdom, the frugality, the providence, the benevolence of nature, without thereby turning it into an intelligent being (for that would be preposterous), yet also without making bold to want to install some other, intelligent being above it as its master builder, since to do so would be presumptuous[^*] {{ 309 }}; rather, what is thereby designated is merely a kind of causality of nature, by analogy with our own in the technical employment of reason, so as to keep in view the rule by which certain of nature's products are to be pursued in inquiry.

But why, then, does teleology ordinarily not form a distinct part of theoretical natural science, and is instead annexed to theology as a propaedeutic or a transition? This comes about in order to keep the

study of nature according to its mechanism tied to whatever we can so submit to our observation or to experiments that we could ourselves bring it about, in the manner of nature, at least in the resemblance of the laws; for one gains complete insight only into so much as one can oneself fashion and accomplish according to concepts. Organization, however, as an inner end of nature, infinitely exceeds all capacity for a comparable presentation through art; and as concerns external arrangements of nature reckoned to be {{ 310 }} purposive (for example, winds, rain, and suchlike), physics does indeed examine their mechanism, but their bearing upon ends—insofar as this is supposed to be a condition necessarily attaching to the cause—it cannot exhibit at all, because this necessity of the linkage bears entirely on the connection of our concepts and not on the constitution of the things.

[^*]: The German word ***vermessen*** is a good and telling word. A judgment in which one neglects to reckon the reach of one's own powers (of the understanding) can at times sound quite humble, and yet advance great claims, and be exceedingly presumptuous for all that. Most of the judgments by which one professes to magnify the divine wisdom are precisely of this stamp, inasmuch as one imputes to it, in the works of creation and of preservation, aims that in truth are meant to redound to the credit of the reasoner's own wisdom.
