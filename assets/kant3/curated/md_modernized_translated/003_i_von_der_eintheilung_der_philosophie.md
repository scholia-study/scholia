## I. On the Division of Philosophy

If we divide philosophy, insofar as it contains principles of the rational cognition of things through concepts (not merely, like logic, principles of the form of thought in general without distinction of objects), as is customary, into ***theoretical*** and ***practical***: we proceed quite correctly. But then the concepts which assign their object to the principles of this rational cognition must also be specifically different, since they would otherwise not justify any division, which always presupposes an opposition of the principles of the rational cognition belonging to the different parts of a science.

But there are only two kinds of concepts which allow of just as many different principles of the possibility of their objects: namely, the ***concepts of nature*** and the ***concept of freedom***. Since the former make a ***theoretical*** cognition possible according to principles _a priori_, while the {{ XII }} second, in regard to these, carries with it in its concept only a negative principle (of mere opposition), but on the contrary establishes for the determination of the will extending principles, which are therefore called practical: philosophy is justly divided into two parts completely different in their principles, namely, into the theoretical as ***philosophy of nature*** and the practical as ***moral philosophy*** (for thus is the practical legislation of reason according to the concept of freedom called). Hitherto, however, a great abuse has prevailed with these expressions for the division of the different principles, and with them also of philosophy: in that the practical according to concepts of nature was taken as identical with the practical according to the concept of freedom, and thus, {{{ 172 }}} under the same names of a theoretical and practical philosophy, a division was made through which (since both parts could have identical principles) in fact nothing was divided.

The will, as the faculty of desire, is namely one of the various natural causes in the world, specifically that which acts according to concepts; and everything that is represented as possible (or necessary) through a will is called practically possible (or necessary): in distinction from the physical possibility or necessity of an effect, the cause of which is determined to causality not through concepts (but, as in the case of lifeless {{ XIII }} matter, through mechanism, and in animals, through instinct). — Here, then, in regard to what is practical, it is left undetermined whether the concept that gives the rule to the causality of the will is a concept of nature or a concept of freedom.

The latter distinction, however, is essential. For if the concept determining the causality is a concept of nature, then the principles are ***technically practical***; but if it is a concept of freedom, then these are ***morally practical***: and because in the division of a science of reason everything depends on that difference of objects whose cognition requires different principles, the former will belong to theoretical philosophy (as the doctrine of nature), while the latter alone will constitute the second part, namely (as the doctrine of morals) practical philosophy.

All technically practical rules (i.e., those of art and skill in general, or also of prudence, as a skill in having influence over men and their will), insofar as their principles rest on concepts, must be counted only as corollaries to theoretical philosophy. For they concern only the possibility of things according to concepts of nature, to which belongs not only the means to be found in nature for this purpose, but even the will itself (as the faculty of desire, hence as a natural faculty), insofar as it can be determined by natural incentives in accordance with those rules {{ XIV }}. Yet such practical rules are not called laws (such as physical ones), but only precepts: and indeed for this reason, because the will stands not merely under the concept of nature, but also under the concept of freedom, in relation to which its principles are called laws and, with their consequences, alone constitute the second, namely the practical part of philosophy.

Just as little, then, as the solution of the problems of pure geometry belongs to {{{ 173 }}} a special part of it, or the art of land surveying deserves the name of a practical geometry in distinction from pure geometry as a second part of geometry in general: so, and even less, may the mechanical or chemical art of experiments or of observations be counted as a practical part of the doctrine of nature, nor, finally, domestic, agricultural, or political economy, the art of social intercourse, the precept of dietetics, or even the general doctrine of happiness, indeed not even the control of inclinations and the taming of affects for the sake of the latter, be counted as practical philosophy, or the latter even constitute the second part of philosophy in general; because they all contain only rules of skill, which are therefore only technically practical, in order to produce an effect that is possible according to natural concepts of causes and effects, which, since they belong to theoretical philosophy, are subject to those precepts as mere {{ XV }} corollaries from the same (natural science) and thus cannot demand a place in a special philosophy called practical. In contrast, the morally practical precepts, which ground themselves entirely on the concept of freedom to the complete exclusion of determining grounds of the will from nature, constitute a completely special kind of precepts: which also, like the rules obeyed by nature, are called laws absolutely, but do not, like them, rest on sensible conditions, but on a supersensible principle and, alongside the theoretical part of philosophy, demand for themselves quite alone another part under the name of practical philosophy.

One sees from this that a sum total of practical precepts which philosophy gives does not constitute a special part of it placed alongside the theoretical one merely because they are practical; for they could be so even if their principles were taken entirely from the theoretical cognition of nature (as technically practical rules); but rather, because and if their principle is not borrowed at all from the concept of nature, which is always sensibly conditioned, and thus rests on the supersensible, which the concept of freedom alone makes cognizable through formal laws, and they are therefore morally practical, i.e., not merely {{ XVI }} precepts and rules for this or that purpose, but are laws without any preceding reference to ends and purposes.
