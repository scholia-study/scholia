## I. On the division of philosophy

Whenever philosophy—so far as it holds within itself, by means of concepts, principles for the rational cognition of things (and not, as logic does, merely the principles governing the form of thought as such, with no distinction drawn among objects)—is partitioned in the usual manner into a ***theoretical*** and a ***practical*** part: one is doing entirely the right thing. But then the concepts too, which assign their object to the principles of this rational cognition, must be specifically distinct, since otherwise they would authorize no division—and a division at all times presupposes a contrast between the principles of that rational cognition which belongs to the several parts of a science.

Yet only two sorts of concept exist, ones that permit exactly as many differing principles for how their objects are possible: namely, the ***concepts of nature*** and the ***concept of freedom***. Because the first of these make a ***theoretical*** cognition possible in accordance with principles _a priori_, while the {{ XII }} second—as regards those same principles—carries with it already in its very concept only a negative principle (that of sheer opposition), yet erects, for the determining of the will, expanding fundamental propositions that are for this reason called practical: philosophy comes to be rightly divided, in keeping with its principles, into two wholly distinct parts—the theoretical as ***philosophy of nature***, the practical as ***moral philosophy*** (for such is the term applied to reason's practical legislation under the concept of freedom). Up to now, however, a great abuse of these expressions—for dividing the various principles, and along with them philosophy itself—has held sway: in that one identified the practical that rests on concepts of nature with the practical that rests on the concept of freedom, and in this way {{{ 172 }}} made, under the very same names of a theoretical and a practical philosophy, a division through which (since both parts could share identical principles) nothing was in fact divided at all.

For the will, as a faculty of desire, is one among the many natural causes in the world—namely the one that operates in accordance with concepts; and everything represented as possible (or as necessary) by means of a will counts as practically possible (or necessary): in contrast to the physical possibility or necessity of an effect whose cause is determined to its causality not by concepts (but rather, as with lifeless {{ XIII }} matter through mechanism and with animals through instinct). — Here, now, it is left undetermined, as regards the practical, whether the concept that prescribes the rule for the will's causality is one of nature or one of freedom.

This latter distinction, however, is essential. For should the concept that settles the causality be one of nature, the principles will be ***technically practical***; should it, however, be one of freedom, they will be ***morally practical***: and because, in classifying a rational science, all turns upon that particular difference among objects whose cognition stands in need of differing principles, the first kind will belong to theoretical philosophy (as the doctrine of nature), while the others, wholly on their own, will make up the second part, that is (as the doctrine of morals) practical philosophy.

All technically practical rules (that is, those of art and skill in general, or likewise of prudence, as a skill in bringing influence to bear on human beings and their will), insofar as their principles rest upon concepts, must be reckoned merely as corollaries appended to theoretical philosophy. For what they concern is merely the possibility of things under concepts of nature, to which there belong not only the means to be met with in nature for the purpose, but the will itself as well (being a faculty of desire, and hence a power of nature), insofar as it can be determined, in conformity with those rules {{ XIV }}, by the incentives of nature. Yet practical rules of this kind are not termed laws (in the way physical ones are, say), but only precepts: and precisely for this reason, that the will stands not merely under the concept of nature, but likewise under the concept of freedom, with respect to which the will's own principles bear the name of laws and, taken together with what follows from them, alone make up the second part of philosophy, namely the practical one.

Thus, just as little as the resolution of problems in pure geometry belongs to {{{ 173 }}} a special part of that science, or as the art of land-surveying earns the title of a practical geometry—as opposed to the pure—being a second part of geometry as such: so too, and still less, may the mechanical or chemical art of experiments or of observations pass for a practical part of the doctrine of nature; nor, finally, may domestic, agricultural, and civic economy, the art of social dealings, the rules of dietetics, not even the general doctrine of happiness, indeed not so much as the curbing of the inclinations and the bridling of the affects on behalf of that doctrine, be reckoned to practical philosophy, nor may these last perhaps even constitute the second part of philosophy as a whole; for the reason that one and all they hold nothing but rules of skill—which are on that account only technically practical—serving to produce an effect that is possible under concepts of nature governing cause and effect, and these, inasmuch as they fall within theoretical philosophy, stand subject to those precepts as mere {{ XV }} corollaries drawn out of it (out of natural science) and can accordingly demand no place in a separate philosophy, the one termed practical. By contrast, the morally practical precepts—which are founded wholly upon the concept of freedom, to the utter exclusion of any determining grounds of the will taken from nature—make up a quite distinctive species of precept: precepts which, just like the rules that nature obeys, are called laws without qualification, yet which rest not, as those do, on sensible conditions, but on a supersensible principle, and which, beside the theoretical part of philosophy, claim entirely for themselves a further part under the title of practical philosophy.

From this one perceives that a body of practical precepts which philosophy furnishes does not, on the ground that they are practical, make up a special part of it placed at the side of the theoretical part; for they could well be that even if their principles were drawn entirely out of nature's theoretical cognition (as technically practical rules); rather, it does so because and when their principle is in no way borrowed from the concept of nature, which is invariably conditioned by the senses, and rests therefore on the supersensible, which the concept of freedom alone renders knowable through formal laws, and they are on that account morally practical—that is, not merely {{ XVI }} precepts and rules directed to this or that intention, but laws that hold without any antecedent reference to ends and intentions.
