## {{{ 467 }}} § 91. On the Kind of Assent by a Practical Faith

If we look merely at the way in which something can be ***for us*** (according to the subjective character of our cognitive powers) an object of cognition (_res cognoscibilis_): then concepts are compared not with objects, but merely with our cognitive faculties and the use which these can make of the given representation (in a theoretical or practical respect); and the question of whether something is a cognizable being or not is not a question that concerns the possibility of things themselves, but rather our cognition of them.

***Cognizable*** things are now of three kinds: ***matters of opinion*** (_opinabile_), ***matters of fact*** (_scibile_) and ***matters of faith*** (_mere credibile_).

1) Objects of mere ideas of reason, which cannot be represented at all in any possible experience for theoretical cognition, are to that extent not ***cognizable*** things at all, and consequently, in respect of them, one cannot even ***opine***; just as to opine _a priori_ {{ 455 }} is already in itself absurd and the straight path to mere figments of the brain. Either our proposition _a priori_ is therefore certain, or it contains nothing at all for assent. Thus ***matters of opinion*** are always objects of an at least in itself possible empirical cognition (objects of the world of sense), which, however, according to the mere degree of this power that we possess, is impossible ***for us***. Thus the ether of modern physicists—an elastic fluid penetrating all other matters (most intimately mixed with them)—is a mere matter of opinion, yet still of such a kind that, if our external senses were sharpened to the highest degree, it could be perceived; but it can never be represented in any observation or experiment. To assume rational inhabitants of other planets is a matter of opinion; for if we could come closer to them, which is possible in itself, we would determine by experience whether they exist or do not exist; but we will never come so close to them, and so it remains a matter of opining. But to opine that there are pure, thinking spirits without bodies in the material universe (if, as is reasonable, we reject certain actual appearances that are passed off as such) is called inventing and is not {{{ 468 }}} a matter of opinion at all, but a mere idea which remains when one takes away everything material from a thinking being and yet leaves it with thinking. But whether in that case the latter (which {{ 456 }} we know only in man, i.e., in connection with a body) would remain, we cannot determine. Such a thing is a ***ratiocinated being of reason*** (_ens rationis ratiocinantis_), not a ***being of reason*** (_ens rationis ratiocinatae_); of which latter, however, it is possible to demonstrate the objective reality of its concept sufficiently at least for the practical use of reason, because this use, which has its peculiar and apodictically certain principles _a priori_, even demands (postulates) it.

2) Objects for concepts whose objective reality can be proven (whether by pure reason or by experience, and in the former case from theoretical or practical data of the same, but in all cases by means of an intuition corresponding to them) are (_res facti_) ***matters of fact***[^*]. Of this kind are the mathematical properties of magnitudes (in geometry), because they are capable of an _a priori_ ***exhibition*** for the theoretical use of reason. Furthermore, {{ 457 }} things or their properties that can be demonstrated through experience (one's own or that of others by means of testimonies) are likewise matters of fact. — What is, however, very remarkable is that even an idea of reason (which in itself is capable of no exhibition in intuition, and consequently of no theoretical proof of its possibility) is found among the matters of fact; and that is the idea of ***freedom***, whose reality as a special kind of causality (of which the concept in a theoretical respect would be transcendent) can be demonstrated through practical laws of pure reason and, in accordance with these, in actual actions, and consequently in experience. — The only one among all the ideas of pure reason whose object is a matter of fact and must be counted among the _scibilia_.

{{{ 469 }}} 3) Objects which, in relation to the duty-conforming use of pure practical reason (be it as consequences or as grounds), must be thought _a priori_, but which are transcendent for its theoretical use, are mere ***matters of faith***. Of this kind is the ***highest good*** in the world to be promoted through freedom, whose concept cannot be sufficiently proven in its objective reality in any experience possible for us, and thus for the theoretical use of reason, but whose use for the best possible promotion of that end is nevertheless commanded by pure practical reason and must consequently be assumed as {{ 458 }} possible. This commanded effect, ***together with the only conditions of its possibility thinkable for us***, namely, the existence of God and the immortality of the soul, are ***matters of faith*** (_res fidei_), and indeed the only ones among all objects that can be so called[^**]. For although what we can only learn from the experience of others through ***testimony*** must be believed by us, it is not therefore in itself a matter of faith; for to ***one*** of those witnesses it was still his own experience and a matter of fact, or is presupposed as such. Moreover, it must be possible to arrive at knowledge through this path (of historical faith); and the objects of history and geography, as indeed everything which it is at least possible to know according to the character of our cognitive powers, belong not to matters of faith, but to matters of fact. Only objects of pure reason can at most be matters of faith {{ 459 }}, but not as objects of mere pure speculative reason; for there they cannot even with certainty be counted among things, i.e., objects of that cognition which is possible for us. They are ideas, i.e., concepts whose objective reality cannot be theoretically secured. On the other hand, the highest final end to be promoted by us, that by which alone we can become worthy of being ourselves the final end of a creation, is an idea that has objective reality for us in a practical relation, and is a matter; but because we cannot procure this reality for this concept in {{{ 470 }}} a theoretical respect, it is a mere matter of faith of pure reason, and with it, at the same time, God and immortality as the conditions under which alone we, according to the character of our (human) reason, can think the possibility of that effect of the lawful use of our freedom. But assent in matters of faith is an assent in a purely practical respect, i.e., a moral faith, which proves nothing for theoretical cognition, but merely for practical cognition of pure reason directed toward the fulfillment of its duties, and does not expand speculation or the practical rules of prudence according to the principle of self-love at all. If the supreme principle of all moral laws is a postulate, then at the same time the possibility of their highest object, and consequently also the condition {{ 460 }} under which alone we can think this possibility, is thereby postulated at the same time. Thereby the cognition of the latter becomes neither knowledge nor opinion of the existence and character of these conditions as a theoretical mode of cognition, but merely an assumption in a practical and, moreover, commanded respect for the moral use of our reason.

Even if we could seemingly ground a ***determinate*** concept of an intelligent cause of the world on the ends of nature which physical teleology presents to us in such rich measure, the existence of this being would still not be a matter of faith. For since this is assumed not for the sake of the fulfillment of my duty, but only for the explanation of nature, it would be merely the opinion and hypothesis most appropriate to our reason. Now that teleology by no means leads to a determinate concept of God, which, on the contrary, is found only in that of a moral author of the world, because he alone indicates the final end to which we can count ourselves only insofar as we conduct ourselves in accordance with what the moral law imposes on us as a final end, and consequently obligates us to do. Consequently, the concept of God obtains the preference in our assent to count as a matter of faith only through the relation to the object of our duty as a condition of the possibility of reaching the final end of the same; whereas this very same {{ 461 }} concept cannot make its object valid as a matter of fact: because, although the necessity of duty is indeed clear to practical reason, yet the attainment of its final end, insofar as it is not entirely in our power, is assumed only for the sake of the practical use of {{{ 471 }}} reason, and is thus not practically necessary in the same way as duty itself is[^***].

***Faith*** (as _habitus_, not as _actus_) is the moral way of thinking {{ 462 }} of reason in assent to that which is inaccessible to theoretical cognition. It is therefore the constant principle of the mind to assume as true, because of the obligation to it, that which is necessary to presuppose as a condition for the possibility of the highest moral final end[^****]; although its possibility, {{{ 472 }}} but just as well its impossibility, cannot be insightfully comprehended {{ 463 }} by us. Faith (simply so called) is a trust in the attainment of an aim, the promotion of which is a duty, but the possibility of its execution is not ***insightfully comprehensible*** to us (consequently neither is that of the only conditions thinkable by us). The faith, therefore, that refers to special objects which are not objects of possible knowledge or opinion (in which latter case, especially in historical matters, it would have to be called credulity and not faith), is entirely moral. It is a free assent, not to that for which dogmatic proofs are to be found for the theoretically determining power of judgment, nor to that which we hold ourselves bound to, but to that which we assume for the sake of an aim according to laws of freedom; yet not like an opinion without sufficient ground, but as grounded in reason (although only in respect of its practical use), {{ 464 }} ***sufficiently for its aim***: for without it, when encountering the challenge of theoretical reason for proof (of the possibility of the object of morality), the moral way of thinking has no firm constancy, but vacillates between practical commands and theoretical doubts. To be ***incredulous*** means to follow the maxim of not believing testimonies at all; but he is ***unbelieving*** who, because those ideas of reason lack a ***theoretical*** justification of their reality, therefore denies them all validity. He therefore judges dogmatically. A dogmatic ***unbelief***, however, cannot coexist with a moral maxim ruling in one's way of thinking (for reason cannot command us to pursue an end that is recognized as nothing but a figment of the brain); but a ***skeptical faith*** can indeed do so, to which the lack of conviction through grounds of speculative reason is merely an obstacle, from which a critical insight into the limits of the latter can remove its influence on conduct, and {{{ 473 }}} place in its stead a predominant practical assent as compensation.

When one wishes to introduce another principle in place of certain failed attempts in philosophy and to procure influence for it, it is a source of great satisfaction to see how those attempts and why {{ 465 }} they had to fail.

***God***, ***freedom*** and ***the immortality of the soul*** are those problems to whose solution all the preparations of metaphysics are directed, as their ultimate and sole end. Now it was believed that the doctrine of freedom was necessary only as a negative condition for practical philosophy, whereas the doctrine of God and of the nature of the soul, belonging to theoretical philosophy, had to be demonstrated for themselves and separately, in order afterward to combine both with what the moral law (which is possible only under the condition of freedom) commands, and thus to bring about a religion. But one can soon see that these attempts had to fail. For from mere ontological concepts of things in general, or of the existence of a necessary being, it is absolutely impossible to make a concept of an original being that is determinate through predicates which can be given in experience and could thus serve for cognition; on the other hand, the concept which was grounded on experience of the physical purposiveness of nature could in turn yield no proof sufficient for morality, and consequently for the cognition of a God. Just as little could the knowledge of the soul through experience (which we conduct only in this life) procure a concept of its spiritual, immortal nature sufficient for morality. ***Theology*** and ***pneumatology***, as {{ 466 }} problems for the behoof of the sciences of a speculative reason, because their concept is transcendent for all our cognitive faculties, can be brought about by no empirical data and predicates. — The determination of both concepts, of God as well as of the soul (in respect of its immortality), can happen only through predicates which, although they themselves are possible only from a supersensible ground, must nevertheless demonstrate their reality in experience: for only thus can they make possible a cognition of entirely supersensible beings. — Of this kind is now the sole concept of the freedom of man under moral laws to be found in human reason, {{{ 474 }}} together with the final end which the former prescribes through the latter, of which the first are fit to attribute to the author of nature, and the second to man, those properties which contain the necessary condition for the possibility of both: so that precisely from this idea the existence and character of those beings, otherwise entirely hidden from us, can be inferred.

Therefore, the ground of the failed intention to prove God and immortality by the merely theoretical path lies in this: that no cognition at all of the supersensible is possible by this path (of the concepts of nature). That, on the contrary, it succeeds by the moral path (of the concept of freedom) {{ 467 }} has this ground: that here the supersensible which lies at its basis (freedom), through a determinate law of causality which arises from it, not only procures material for the cognition of the other supersensible (of the moral final end and the conditions of its practicability), but also demonstrates its reality as a matter of fact in actions, but precisely for this reason can yield no other than a valid ground of proof in a practical respect (which is also the only one that religion requires).

It always remains very remarkable in this: that among the three pure ideas of reason—***God***, ***freedom*** and ***immortality***—that of freedom is the only concept of the supersensible which demonstrates its objective reality (by means of the causality thought in it) in nature through its possible effect in the latter, and precisely thereby makes possible the connection of the other two with nature, and of all three with one another into a religion; and that we thus have in us a principle which is capable of determining the idea of the supersensible in us, and thereby also that of the same outside us, into a cognition, although possible only in a practical respect, of which merely speculative philosophy (which could give of freedom only a merely negative concept) had to despair: consequently, the concept of freedom (as the fundamental concept {{ 468 }} of all unconditionally practical laws) can extend reason beyond those limits within which every concept of nature (theoretical) would have to remain hopelessly restricted.

[^*]: I here extend, with right as it seems to me, the concept of a matter of fact beyond the usual meaning of this word. For it is not necessary, indeed not even feasible, to restrict this expression merely to actual experience when the relation of things to our cognitive powers is under discussion, since a merely possible experience is already sufficient for speaking of them merely as objects of a determinate mode of cognition.
[^**]: Matters of faith are not, however, on that account ***articles of faith***, if by the latter one understands those matters of faith to whose ***confession*** (internal or external) one can be obligated: which natural theology therefore does not contain. For since, as matters of faith, they cannot be grounded (like matters of fact) on theoretical proofs: it is a free assent, and only as such is it compatible with the morality of the subject.
[^***]: The final end which the moral law imposes on us to promote is not the ground of duty; for this ground lies in the moral law, which as a formal practical principle directs categorically, without regard to the objects of the faculty of desire (the matter of volition), and consequently of any end. This formal character of my actions (their subordination under the principle of universal validity), in which alone their inner moral worth consists, is entirely in our power; and I can very well abstract from the possibility or impracticability of the ends which I am obligated to promote in accordance with that law (because in them consists only the outer value of my actions) as from something which is never fully in my power, in order to look only at what is of my own doing. Nevertheless, the intention to promote the final end of all rational beings (happiness, insofar as it is possible in agreement with duty) is indeed imposed by the law of duty. But speculative reason does not comprehend at all the practicability of the same (neither on the part of our own physical power nor of the cooperation of nature); rather, it must, as far as we can judge reasonably from such causes, consider such a result of our good conduct from mere nature (in us and outside us), without assuming God and immortality, to be an unfounded and null, although well-meaning, expectation, and, if it could have complete certainty of this judgment, regard the moral law itself as a mere delusion of our reason in a practical respect. But since speculative reason convinces itself completely that the latter can never happen, whereas, on the contrary, those ideas whose object lies beyond nature can be thought without contradiction: it will have to acknowledge those ideas as real for its own practical law and the task imposed thereby, and thus in a moral respect, in order not to come into contradiction with itself.
[^****]: It is a trust in the promise of the moral law; yet not as one contained in it, but as one which I introduce into it, and indeed on morally sufficient ground. For a final end cannot be commanded by any law of reason without reason's simultaneously promising its reachability, even if it be uncertain, and hereby also justifying assent to the only conditions under which alone our reason can think this reachability. The word _fides_ already expresses this; and it can only seem questionable how this expression and this particular idea enter into moral philosophy, since they were introduced only with Christianity, and the adoption of them might perhaps seem to be only a flattering imitation of its language. But this is not the only case in which this wondrous religion, in the greatest simplicity of its presentation, has enriched philosophy with far more determinate and purer concepts of morality than philosophy had until then been able to deliver, which concepts, however, once they are there, are ***freely*** approved by reason and accepted as such, although reason could and should well have arrived at them and introduced them on its own.
