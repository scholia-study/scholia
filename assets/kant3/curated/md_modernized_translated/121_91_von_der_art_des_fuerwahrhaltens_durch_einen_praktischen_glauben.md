## {{{ 467 }}} § 91. On the kind of holding-to-be-true through a practical faith

If we look solely to the manner in which something can be, ***for us*** (in keeping with the subjective make-up of our representational powers), an object of cognition (_res cognoscibilis_): then the concepts get held up not against the objects, but only against our faculties of cognition and the use these are able to make of a given representation (whether with a theoretical or a practical intent); and the question whether some thing is a cognizable being or not bears not on the possibility of the things themselves, but on our cognition of them.

***Cognizable*** things fall, then, into three sorts: ***matters of opinion*** (_opinabile_), ***matters of fact*** (_scibile_), and ***matters of faith*** (_mere credibile_).

1) Objects of mere rational ideas, incapable of being presented for theoretical cognition in any possible experience whatever, are to that same extent not ***cognizable*** things either, so that with respect to them one cannot so much as ***form an opinion***; for opining _a priori_ {{ 455 }} is of itself already preposterous and the straight road to nothing but chimeras of the brain. Either, then, our _a priori_ proposition holds with certainty, or it carries nothing at all fit to be held true. ***Matters of opinion***, accordingly, are at all times objects of a cognition drawn from experience that is at least possible in itself (objects of the sensible world), yet a cognition which, measured by the sheer degree of this power we actually have, stays ***for us*** out of reach. The ether of more recent physicists, for example—an elastic fluid that soaks through every other kind of matter (blending with it most intimately)—counts as a bare matter of opinion, yet remains all the while of such a nature that, had the outer senses been honed to their highest pitch, it might be perceived; and it is nonetheless something no observation or experiment could ever exhibit. To posit rational dwellers on other planets is a matter of opinion; for could we but draw nearer to them, which is in itself possible, we should decide through experience whether or not they exist; but we shall never approach them so closely, and so the matter rests at opining. Yet to opine: that pure spirits, thinking apart from any body, exist within the material universe (dismissing, that is, as one properly should, certain actual appearances put forward to that effect) is to indulge in invention and constitutes {{{ 468 }}} no matter of opinion at all, but merely an idea left behind once one strips from a thinking being everything material while still leaving it its thinking. But whether that last (which {{ 456 }} we are acquainted with in the human being alone, i.e., in union with a body) does then remain behind, we are in no position to decide. A thing of that sort is a ***ratiocinated being*** (_ens rationis ratiocinantis_), not a ***being of reason*** (_ens rationis ratiocinatae_); of the latter it is at any rate feasible to make out, adequately at least for reason's practical employment, the objective reality of its concept, since that employment—furnished with its own proper and apodictically certain principles _a priori_—goes so far as to require (postulate) it.

2) Objects belonging to concepts whose objective reality admits of proof—be it by pure reason or else by way of experience, and in the first case out of that reason's own theoretical or practical data, though in every case through an intuition answering to them—are (_res facti_) ***matters of fact***[^*]. Among these belong the mathematical properties of magnitudes (in geometry), inasmuch as they lend themselves to an _a priori_ ***presentation*** on behalf of reason's theoretical use. Likewise, {{ 457 }} things, or characteristics of them, capable of being made out through experience (whether one's own or another's by way of testimony), are equally matters of fact. — What is truly noteworthy, however, is that even a rational idea (one intrinsically incapable of any presentation in intuition, and so of any theoretical proof of its possibility) turns up among the matters of fact; and this is the idea of ***freedom***, the reality of which, as a peculiar species of causality (a concept that, viewed theoretically, would overreach), lets itself be made out through the practical laws of pure reason and, conformably to them, in real deeds, and thus in experience. — The single one among all the ideas of pure reason whose object is a matter of fact and has to be numbered among the _scibilia_.

{{{ 469 }}} 3) Objects that, in bearing on the dutiful employment of pure practical reason (whether as consequences or as grounds), have to be thought _a priori_, yet overreach the theoretical employment of that same reason, are nothing but ***matters of faith***. Such is the ***highest*** ***good*** achievable in the world through freedom, a concept whose objective reality can be proven in no experience open to us, and hence not adequately for reason's theoretical use, but whose application toward the fullest possible realization of that end is all the same enjoined by pure practical reason and must therefore be taken to be {{ 458 }} possible. This enjoined effect, ***along with the sole conditions of its possibility that we are able to think***, namely the being of God and the immortality of the soul, are ***matters of faith*** (_res fidei_)—and indeed the only ones among all objects deserving of that name[^**]. For although we are bound to believe whatever we can learn solely from the experience of others by way of ***testimony***, it is not thereby a matter of faith in itself; for in the case of ***one*** of those witnesses it was, after all, personal experience and a matter of fact, or is taken to be such. Beyond this, it must be feasible to reach knowledge along this route (that of historical faith); and the objects of history and of geography, like whatever else it is at least possible to know given how our cognitive faculties are constituted, fall not under matters of faith but under matters of fact. Objects of pure reason alone can, if anything, be matters of faith, {{ 459 }} though not qua objects of merely pure speculative reason; for there they cannot so much as be counted with any certainty among the things—that is, among the objects of the cognition open to us. They are ideas, that is, concepts to which objective reality cannot be secured theoretically. The highest final end we are to bring about, on the other hand—that whereby alone we can grow worthy of being ourselves the final end of a creation—is an idea possessing, for us, objective reality in a practical bearing, and is a thing; but precisely because we cannot furnish this reality to the concept in a {{{ 470 }}} theoretical intent, it is a bare matter of faith of pure reason, and together with it, at one and the same time, God and immortality figure as the conditions under which alone, given how our (human) reason is constituted, we can conceive the possibility of that upshot of the law-governed use of our freedom. The holding-to-be-true in matters of faith, however, is a holding-to-be-true from a purely practical intent, i.e., a moral faith, which vouches for nothing on the theoretical side, but solely for the practical, duty-observing cognition of pure reason, and in no wise stretches speculation or those prudential rules of practice framed on the principle of self-love. Suppose the supreme principle of all ethical laws to be a postulate: then the possibility of its highest object, and thereby also the condition {{ 460 }} under which we are able to think that possibility, is co-postulated along with it. By this route the cognition of the latter turns into neither knowledge nor opinion regarding the existence and constitution of these conditions as a theoretical mode of cognition, but merely into an acceptance, within a practical and to that extent enjoined bearing, for the moral use of our reason.

Even could we, upon the ends of nature that physical teleology sets before us in such abundant measure, seemingly rest a ***determinate*** concept of an intelligent world-cause, the being of that cause would still be no matter of faith. For since it is posited not with a view to discharging my duty, but merely toward accounting for nature, it would amount to no more than the opinion and hypothesis best suited to our reason. Now that teleology by no means leads on to a determinate concept of God, which is met with, on the contrary, in that of a moral author of the world alone, because he alone furnishes the final end—one to which we may reckon ourselves only insofar as we conduct ourselves in accord with what the moral law lays upon us as a final end and thereby makes binding on us. The concept of God, in consequence, gains the precedence—within our holding-to-be-true—of ranking as a matter of faith solely through its bearing on the object of our duty as the condition for the possibility of reaching that duty's final end; whereas this very {{ 461 }} concept still cannot press its object as a matter of fact: for, granted that the necessity of duty stands clear enough before practical reason, the reaching of its final end, so far as that does not lie wholly within our command, is assumed only on behalf of the practical use of {{{ 471 }}} reason, and thus is not practically necessary in the way that duty itself is[^***].

***Faith*** (as a _habitus_, not an _actus_) is reason's moral cast of thought {{ 462 }} in holding true what lies beyond the reach of theoretical cognition. It is, then, the mind's enduring principle to take as true, out of the obligation binding one to it, whatever must be presupposed as the condition for the possibility of the highest moral final end[^****]; even though its possibility, {{{ 472 }}} but equally its impossibility as well, can in no way be seen into {{ 463 }} by us. Faith (so termed without qualification) is a confidence in reaching an aim the furthering of which is a duty, while the possibility of carrying it out is not something we can ***see into*** (nor, consequently, that of the sole conditions we are able to think). Faith, then, as directed toward particular objects that are not objects of possible knowing or opining (in which latter case it would have to be styled credulity—chiefly in the historical domain—rather than faith), is thoroughly moral. It is a free holding-to-be-true, not of that for which dogmatic proofs offer themselves to the theoretically determining power of judgment, nor of that to which we deem ourselves bound, but of that which we take up on behalf of an aim under laws of freedom; and yet not in the fashion of some opinion lacking sufficient ground, but as grounded in reason (albeit only as regards its practical use), {{ 464 }} ***adequately for the aim thereof***: for lacking it the moral cast of thought, upon the affront it meets in theoretical reason's summons to prove (the possibility of morality's object), possesses no firm constancy, but sways to and fro between practical injunctions and theoretical misgivings. To be ***incredulous*** is to cleave to the maxim of putting no trust in testimonies at all; ***unbelieving***, by contrast, is he who, since those rational ideas want a ***theoretical*** grounding of their reality, denies them on that score every validity. His verdict is thus a dogmatic one. A dogmatic ***unbelief***, however, cannot subsist alongside an ethical maxim that governs one's cast of thought (for reason cannot enjoin the pursuit of an end acknowledged to be sheer chimera); whereas a ***faith of doubt*** can indeed subsist, one for which the want of conviction on grounds of speculative reason is a mere hindrance—a hindrance that a critical insight into the bounds of that reason can divest of its sway over conduct, and {{{ 473 }}} in whose stead it can set up an overriding practical holding-to-be-true.

When one means to install another principle in the room of certain miscarried attempts in philosophy, and to win it currency, it makes for no small satisfaction to grasp how those attempts, and why {{ 465 }} they, could not but come to grief.

***God***, ***freedom***, and the ***immortality of the soul*** are the problems toward whose resolution the whole apparatus of metaphysics is directed as toward its final and sole end. Now the belief prevailed that the doctrine of freedom is called for merely as a negative condition of practical philosophy, whereas the doctrine of God and of the soul's make-up, as pertaining to theoretical philosophy, would need to be established on its own and in isolation, so as afterward to couple both with the biddings of the moral law (itself possible only under the condition of freedom) and thereby bring a religion into being. One may soon perceive, however, that these attempts were bound to miscarry. For out of bare ontological concepts—whether of things taken quite universally or of a necessary being's existence—there is simply no framing a concept of an original being that predicates capable of being furnished in experience, and thus of serving cognition, could render determinate; while the concept grounded rather on experience of nature's physical purposiveness could, for its part, deliver no proof adequate for morality, and hence for the cognition of a God. Just as little could acquaintance with the soul by way of experience (which we can conduct only in this present life) yield a concept of its spiritual, deathless nature adequate for morality. ***Theology*** and ***pneumatology***, taken as {{ 466 }} problems on behalf of the sciences of a speculative reason, can be reached through no empirical data or predicates whatever, seeing that their concept overreaches all our faculties of cognition. — The determination of both concepts, of God no less than of the soul (with a view to its immortality), can come about only through predicates which, though themselves possible solely from a supersensible ground, must all the same establish their reality in experience: for by that route alone can they render a cognition of wholly supersensible beings possible. — Of just this kind is the sole concept to be discovered in human reason, {{{ 474 }}} the concept of the freedom of the human being under moral laws, together with the final end that freedom, by means of these laws, prescribes—of which the former are suited to invest the author of nature, and the latter the human being, with those properties that carry within them the condition necessary to the possibility of both: so that precisely from this idea one may infer to the existence and constitution of those beings otherwise entirely concealed from us.

Thus the reason why the design of proving God and immortality miscarries along the merely theoretical route lies in this: that of the supersensible, along this route (that of the concepts of nature), no cognition whatever is possible. That it prospers, by contrast, along the moral route (that of the concept of freedom) {{ 467 }} has this reason: that here the supersensible lying at its base (freedom), by way of a determinate law of causality springing from it, not merely supplies matter for cognizing the other supersensible (the moral final end and the conditions of its practicability), but moreover, as a matter of fact, makes good its reality in deeds—yet for that very reason can supply no ground of proof valid otherwise than for a practical intent (which, too, is the only intent religion stands in need of).

One thing here stays permanently striking: that of the three pure rational ideas—***God***, ***freedom***, and ***immortality***—freedom alone is that concept of the supersensible which makes good its objective reality (by means of the causality thought within it) in nature, through the effect it can there produce, and precisely thereby renders possible the joining of the other two to nature, and of all three among themselves into a religion; and that we accordingly harbor within us a principle competent to determine the idea of the supersensible in us—and through this the idea of the supersensible outside us as well—toward a cognition, though one possible only for a practical intent, at which merely speculative philosophy (able to yield, even of freedom, no more than a negative concept) was bound to despair: so that the concept of freedom (as the fundamental concept {{ 468 }} of all unconditionally practical laws) is able to widen reason past those limits within which every concept of nature (a theoretical concept) would have to remain hemmed in without hope.

[^*]: I here broaden, as seems to me warranted, the concept of a matter of fact past the customary sense of the word. For it is neither needful nor even practicable to confine this expression to actual experience alone whenever the relation of things to our cognitive faculties is in question, since a merely possible experience already suffices for speaking of them simply as objects of a determinate mode of cognition.
[^**]: Matters of faith are not, however, on that account ***articles of faith***, if by the latter one understands such matters of faith to whose ***confession*** (whether inward or outward) one may be held bound: such, then, natural theology does not embrace. For since, as matters of faith, they cannot (in the way matters of fact do) rest upon theoretical proofs: the holding-to-be-true is a free one, and only as such does it square with the subject's morality.
[^***]: The final end whose promotion the moral law lays upon us is not the ground of duty; for that ground resides in the moral law, which, as a formal practical principle, directs categorically, heedless of the objects of the faculty of desire (the matter of volition), and thus of any end whatever. This formal character of my actions (their subsumption under the principle of universal validity), wherein alone their inner moral worth consists, lies wholly within our command; and I may perfectly well abstract from the feasibility or unrealizability of the ends it falls to me to promote in keeping with that law (since in them consists only the outward worth of my actions), as from something never fully within my command, so as to attend solely to what is my own to do. Yet the design of promoting the final end of all rational beings (happiness, so far as it is possible in concord with duty) is, all the same, laid upon us precisely by the law of duty. But speculative reason discerns not at all the practicability of this end (neither on the part of our own physical capacity nor on that of nature's cooperation); rather, from causes of that sort, so far as we can reasonably judge, it is bound to hold any such issue of our good conduct—were it presumed to spring from bare nature (within us and without) with no God and no immortality—for a groundless and idle, however well-meant, expectation, and, could it attain complete certainty in this verdict, would have to look upon the moral law itself as a mere illusion of our reason in a practical regard. But since speculative reason satisfies itself entirely that the latter can never come to pass, while those ideas, whose object lies past nature, can nonetheless be thought without contradiction: for the sake of its own practical law and the task thereby imposed—and so in a moral regard—it will have to own those ideas as real, on pain of falling into contradiction with itself.
[^****]: It is a confidence placed in the promise of the moral law; yet not as a promise lodged within that law itself, but as one I put into it, and that on morally sufficient grounds. For no final end can be enjoined by any law of reason unless that reason at the same time pledges its reachability, uncertain though it be, and thereby likewise warrants the holding-to-be-true of the sole conditions under which our reason is able to think it. The word Fides gives voice to just this; and it can seem questionable only how this term and this peculiar idea came to enter moral philosophy, given that it was ushered in only with Christianity, and its adoption might perhaps look to be no more than a fawning imitation of Christianity's idiom. But this is not the one case where this marvelous religion, amid the utmost plainness of its delivery, has endowed philosophy with far more determinate and purer concepts of morality than philosophy had thitherto been able to provide—concepts which, once they are on hand, reason ***freely*** endorses and takes up as its own, concepts at which it might well have arrived unaided and which it could and ought to have introduced.
