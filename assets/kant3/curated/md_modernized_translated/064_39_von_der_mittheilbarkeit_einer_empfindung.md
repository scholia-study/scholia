## § 39. On the communicability of a sensation

When sensation, as the real element of perception, is referred to cognition, it is called sense-sensation; and what is specific in its quality can be represented as thoroughly communicable in the same manner only if one assumes that everyone possesses a sense identical to our own: yet this simply cannot be presupposed of a sense-sensation. Thus this kind of sensation cannot be imparted to a person in whom the sense of smell is absent; and even when he does not lack it, one still cannot be sure whether he has exactly the same sensation of a flower as we have of it. But we must picture human beings as differing still more with respect to the ***agreeableness*** or ***disagreeableness*** in the sensation of one and the very same object of the senses; and it is quite impossible to demand that the pleasure in objects of this sort be granted by everyone. Since a pleasure of this {{{ 292 }}} kind reaches the mind by way of sense, so that we are thereby passive with regard to it, one may term it the pleasure of ***enjoyment***.

The satisfaction, on the other hand, that an action affords on account of its moral constitution {{ 154 }} is not a pleasure of enjoyment but one of self-activity and of the latter's agreement with the idea of one's determination. This feeling, which goes by the name of the ethical, nevertheless demands concepts and exhibits not a free but a law-governed purposiveness; it can therefore be imparted universally in no other manner than by way of reason, and—should the pleasure be of one and the same kind in everyone—through quite specific practical concepts of reason.

As a pleasure belonging to ratiocinating contemplation, the pleasure in the sublime of nature does, to be sure, also make a claim upon universal participation, and yet it already presupposes a further feeling—namely that of one's supersensible vocation—which, however dark it may be, rests upon a moral foundation. But that other human beings will take this into account and find a satisfaction in contemplating the raw magnitude of nature (a satisfaction that truly cannot be ascribed to the sight of it, which is rather forbidding) is something I am not entitled to presuppose outright. Notwithstanding this, in view of the fact that those moral predispositions ought to be taken into account on every fitting occasion, I may still require that satisfaction of everyone as well—though solely through the moral law, which on its side is once again grounded on concepts of reason.

The pleasure in the beautiful, by contrast, is not a pleasure of enjoyment, nor {{ 155 }} one of some lawful activity, and just as little one of ratiocinating contemplation according to ideas, but rather one of mere reflection. With no end or principle whatever to serve it as a guiding standard, this pleasure accompanies the ordinary apprehension of an object through the imagination, as the faculty of intuition, in reference to the understanding, as the faculty of concepts, by way of a procedure of the power of judgment that it must carry out even on behalf of the commonest experience: with the sole difference that in the present case it is constrained to this for an empirical objective concept, whereas there (in aesthetic estimation) it is constrained merely so as to apprehend how suited the representation is to the harmonious (subjectively purposive) occupation of the two cognitive powers in their freedom, that is, to sense the representational state with pleasure. This pleasure must of necessity rest upon the very same conditions in every person, since they are the subjective conditions under which any cognition whatever becomes possible, and the proportion of those {{{ 293 }}} cognitive powers demanded for taste is likewise demanded for the common and sound understanding that may be presupposed in everyone. For precisely this reason the one who judges with taste (provided only that, in this consciousness, he does not err and does not mistake the matter for the form, charm for beauty) is likewise entitled to require of every other that subjective purposiveness—that is, {{ 156 }} the satisfaction he takes in the object—and to hold his feeling to be universally communicable, and that too without any mediating concepts.
