## § 39. On the communicability of a sensation

When sensation, as the real element of perception, is referred to cognition, it is called sense-sensation; and what is specific in its quality can be represented as thoroughly communicable in the same manner only if one assumes that everyone possesses a sense identical to our own: yet this simply cannot be presupposed of a sense-sensation. Thus this kind of sensation cannot be imparted to a person in whom the sense of smell is absent; and even when he does not lack it, one still cannot be sure whether he has exactly the same sensation of a flower as we have of it. But we must picture human beings as differing still more with respect to the ***agreeableness*** or ***disagreeableness*** in the sensation of one and the very same object of the senses; and it is quite impossible to demand that the pleasure in objects of this sort be granted by everyone. The pleasure of this {{{ 292 }}} kind—because it enters the mind through the senses, and we are thus passive in it—may be called the pleasure of ***enjoyment***.

The satisfaction in an action for the sake of its moral character {{ 154 }}, by contrast, is not a pleasure of enjoyment but rather one of self-activity and of its conformity with the idea of its vocation. This feeling, which is called the moral feeling, however, requires concepts and presents not a free but a lawful purposiveness, and hence can also be communicated universally in no other way than through reason and—should the pleasure be alike in everyone—by means of highly determinate practical concepts of reason.

The pleasure in the sublime of nature, as a pleasure of ratiocinating contemplation, does indeed likewise lay claim to universal participation, yet it already presupposes another feeling, namely that of one's supersensible vocation: a feeling which, however obscure it may be, has a moral foundation. But that other human beings will take this into account and find a satisfaction in contemplating the raw magnitude of nature (a satisfaction that truly cannot be ascribed to the sight of it, which is rather forbidding) is something I am not entitled to presuppose outright. Notwithstanding this, in view of the fact that those moral predispositions ought to be taken into account on every fitting occasion, I may still require that satisfaction of everyone as well—though solely through the moral law, which on its side is once again grounded on concepts of reason.

The pleasure in the beautiful, by contrast, is not a pleasure of enjoyment, nor {{ 155 }} one of some lawful activity, and just as little one of ratiocinating contemplation according to ideas, but rather one of mere reflection. Without having any end or principle to serve as its guide, this pleasure attends the everyday apprehension of an object by the imagination, as the faculty of intuition, in its relation to the understanding, as the faculty of concepts, through a procedure of the power of judgment that it must exercise as well for the sake of the most commonplace experience: save that here it is driven to do so for the sake of an empirical objective concept, whereas there (in aesthetic judging) it is driven to do so merely so as to perceive the suitability of the representation for the harmonious (subjectively purposive) engagement of both cognitive powers in their freedom, that is, to feel the state of representation with pleasure. This pleasure must of necessity rest upon the very same conditions in every person, since they are the subjective conditions under which any cognition whatever becomes possible, and the proportion of those {{{ 293 }}} cognitive powers demanded for taste is likewise demanded for the common and sound understanding that may be presupposed in everyone. For precisely this reason the one who judges with taste (provided only that, in this consciousness, he does not err and does not mistake the matter for the form, charm for beauty) is likewise entitled to require of every other that subjective purposiveness—that is, {{ 156 }} the satisfaction he takes in the object—and to hold his feeling to be universally communicable, and that too without any mediating concepts.
