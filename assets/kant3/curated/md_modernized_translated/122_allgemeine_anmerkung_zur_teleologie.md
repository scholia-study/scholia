## {{{ 475 }}} General Remark on Teleology

If the question is what rank the moral argument, which proves the existence of God only as a matter of faith for practical pure reason, holds among the others in philosophy: then the entire possession of the latter can easily be estimated, where it then turns out that here there is no choice to be made, but that its theoretical capacity must, before an impartial critique, surrender all of its claims of its own accord.

On fact it must first of all ground all holding-for-true, if it is not to be completely groundless; and there can therefore obtain only the single difference in proving, whether on this fact a holding-for-true of the conclusion drawn from it can be grounded as ***knowing*** for theoretical cognition, or merely as ***faith*** for practical cognition. All facts belong either to the ***concept of nature***, which proves its reality on the objects of the senses given (or possible to be given) prior to all concepts of nature; or to the ***concept of freedom***, which sufficiently demonstrates its reality through the causality of reason in view of certain effects possible through it in the world of sense, which it irrefutably postulates in the moral law. The concept of nature (belonging merely to theoretical cognition) is now either metaphysical and completely _a priori_; or physical, i.e., _a posteriori_ and necessarily {{ 469 }} thinkable only through definite experience. The metaphysical concept of nature (which presupposes no definite experience) is therefore ontological.

***The ontological proof*** of the existence of God from the concept of an original being is now either that which concludes from ontological predicates, through which alone it can be thought as thoroughly determined, to absolutely necessary existence, or from the absolute necessity of the existence of any thing whatsoever to the predicates of the original being: for to the concept of an original being, so that it be not derivative, belongs the unconditional necessity of its existence and (in order to represent this) the thoroughgoing determination by its concept. Both requirements one believed to find in the concept of the ontological idea of a ***most real being***: and thus arose two metaphysical proofs.

The proof laying a merely metaphysical concept of nature at its ground (properly called ontological) concluded from the concept of the most real {{{ 476 }}} being to its absolutely necessary existence; for (it is said) if it did not exist, a reality, namely existence, would be lacking to it. — The other (which is also called the metaphysico-***cosmological*** proof) concluded from the necessity of the existence of some thing (the like of which, since an existence is given to me in self-consciousness, must by all means be admitted) to its thoroughgoing determination as the most real being: because everything existing must be thoroughly determined, but that which is absolutely necessary (namely what we are to cognize as such, and hence _a priori_) must be thoroughly determined ***by its concept***; which, however, can be found only in the concept of a most real thing. It is not necessary here to expose the sophistry in both inferences, which has already been done elsewhere {{ 470 }}; but only to remark that such proofs, even if they could be defended by all kinds of dialectical subtlety, could yet never pass beyond the school into the public world and have the least influence on mere common sense.

The proof which lays at its ground a concept of nature which can only be empirical, but which is nevertheless supposed to lead beyond the boundaries of nature as the sum total of the objects of the senses, can be no other than that from the ***purposes*** of nature: the concept of which, to be sure, cannot be given _a priori_ but only through experience, but which nevertheless promises such a concept of the original ground of nature as is, among all that we can think, uniquely suited to the supersensible, namely that of a supreme understanding as the cause of the world; which, indeed, it completely accomplishes according to principles of the reflecting power of judgment, i.e., according to the constitution of our (human) cognitive power. — But whether it is capable, from the same data, of supplying this concept of a ***supreme***, i.e., independent, intelligent being also as that of a God, i.e., an author of a world under moral laws, and hence sufficiently determined for the idea of a final purpose of the existence of the world, that is a question on which everything depends; whether we now require a theoretically sufficient concept of the original being for the sake of the whole of the cognition of nature, or a practical one for religion.

This argument, taken from physical teleology, is worthy of respect. It has the same effect for conviction on common sense as on the most subtle thinker; and a ***Reimarus*** in his {{ 471 }} not yet surpassed work, wherein he carries out this ground of proof at length with the {{{ 477 }}} thoroughness and clarity peculiar to him, has thereby earned immortal merit. — But whereby does this proof gain such a powerful influence on the mind, especially in judgment by cold reason (for the moving and elevating of the mind by the wonders of nature might be reckoned as persuasion), to a calm, entirely yielding assent? It is not the physical purposes that all point to an unfathomable understanding in the world's cause; for these are insufficient for this, because they do not satisfy the need of the questioning reason. For to what end (asks the latter) are all those artificial things of nature; to what end man himself, with whom we must stop as the last conceivable purpose of nature for us; to what end is this entire nature there, and what is the final purpose of so great and manifold an art? To be created for enjoyment, or for contemplation, observation, and admiration (which, if it stops there, is also nothing more than enjoyment of a special kind) as the ultimate final purpose for which the world and man himself exist, cannot satisfy reason: for the latter presupposes a personal value, which man alone can give to himself, as the condition under which alone he and his existence can be a final purpose. In the absence of this (which alone is capable of a determined concept), the purposes of nature do not satisfy its inquiry, especially because they can offer no ***determinate concept*** of the highest being as an all-sufficient (and for that very reason sole, properly to be called ***highest***) being and of the laws according to which its understanding is the cause of the world.

That the physico-teleological proof therefore convinces, just as if it were at the same time {{ 472 }} a theological one, does not arise from the use of the ideas of purposes of nature as so many empirical grounds of proof of a ***highest*** understanding; but there is imperceptibly mixed into the inference the moral ground of proof that dwells in every human being and moves him so deeply, according to which one attributes to the being which reveals itself so incomprehensibly artfully in the purposes of nature also a final purpose, and hence wisdom (although without being authorized to do so by the perception of the former), and thus arbitrarily supplements that argument in view of the defect that still attaches to it. In fact, therefore, only the moral ground of proof produces conviction, and even this only in a moral respect, to which everyone feels his assent {{{ 478 }}} most intimately; the physico-teleological, however, has only the merit of guiding the mind in the contemplation of the world onto the path of purposes, and thereby to an ***intelligent*** author of the world: since the moral relation to purposes and the idea of just such a legislator and author of the world, as a theological concept, although it is a pure addition, nevertheless seems to develop of itself from that ground of proof.

For the rest, one can well let the matter rest here in the ordinary ***presentation***. For it is commonly difficult for the common and healthy understanding, when the separation requires much reflection, to distinguish from one another as heterogeneous the different principles which it mixes together, and from one of which alone it really and correctly draws its conclusion. However, the moral ground of proof for the existence of God does not actually merely ***supplement***, as it were, the physico-teleological proof to make a complete proof; but it is a special proof that ***replaces*** the lack of conviction {{ 473 }} from the latter: in that this latter can in fact achieve nothing but to direct reason, in the judgment of the ground of nature and of its contingent but admirable order (which becomes known to us only through experience), to the causality of a cause that contains its ground according to purposes (which, according to the constitution of our cognitive powers, we must think as an intelligent cause), and to make it attentive, and thus more receptive to the moral proof. For that which is required for the latter concept is so essentially distinct from everything that concepts of nature can contain and teach, that it requires a special ground of proof and a proof entirely independent of the preceding ones in order to specify the concept of the original being sufficiently for a theology and to conclude to its existence. — The moral proof (which, to be sure, proves the existence of God only in a practical, yet also indispensable respect of reason) would therefore still remain in its force if we met with no material at all, or only ambiguous material, for physical teleology in the world. It is conceivable that rational beings might see themselves surrounded by such a nature as showed no clear trace of organization, but only the effects of a mere mechanism of crude matter, for the sake of which, and in the variability of some merely accidentally purposive forms and relations, there seemed to be no ground to conclude to an intelligent author; where, then, there would also be no occasion for a {{{ 479 }}} physical teleology: and yet reason, which receives no guidance here through concepts of nature, would find in the concept of freedom and in the moral ideas grounded on it a practically sufficient ground to postulate the concept of the original being adequate to these {{ 474 }}, i.e., as a deity, and nature (even our own existence) as a final purpose conformed to that being and its laws, and indeed in view of the indispensable command of practical reason. — But that in the actual world there is, for the rational beings in it, abundant material for physical teleology (which was not strictly necessary), serves as a welcome confirmation for the moral argument, in so far as nature is capable of presenting something analogous to the ideas of reason (the moral ones). For the concept of a supreme cause that has understanding (which, however, is far from being sufficient for a theology) thereby receives a reality sufficient for the reflecting power of judgment; but it is not required in order to ground the moral proof upon it: nor does the latter serve to supplement the former (which by itself points to morality not at all) through continued inference according to a single principle into ***one*** proof. Two such heterogeneous principles as nature and freedom can yield only two different kinds of proof, since the attempt to conduct the same from the former is found to be inadequate for what is to be proved.

If the physico-teleological ground of proof were sufficient for the proof sought, this would be very satisfying to speculative reason; for it would give hope of producing a theosophy (for so one would have to call the theoretical cognition of the divine nature and of its existence which would be sufficient for the explanation of the constitution of the world and at the same time for the determination of the moral laws). Likewise, if psychology were sufficient to arrive thereby at the cognition of the immortality of the soul, it would make possible a pneumatology which would be just as welcome to speculative {{ 475 }} reason. But both, however dear they may be to the vanity of the thirst for knowledge, do not fulfill the wish of reason with respect to theory, which would have to be grounded on the cognition of the nature of things. But whether the former as theology, the latter as anthropology, both grounded on the moral, i.e., the principle of freedom, and hence conformed to the practical use of reason, might not better fulfill their objective final purpose, is another question which we do not need to pursue further here.

{{{ 480 }}} But the physico-teleological ground of proof is not sufficient for theology for this reason: that it does not, and cannot, give any concept of the original being sufficiently determined for this purpose, but that one must take this concept entirely from elsewhere, or else must replace its deficiency by an arbitrary addition. You conclude from the great purposiveness of the natural forms and their relations to an intelligent cause of the world; but to what degree of this understanding? Without doubt you cannot presume to conclude to the highest possible understanding; for to that it would be required that you saw that a greater understanding than that of which you perceive proofs in the world is not thinkable: which would mean attributing omniscience to yourselves. Likewise, you conclude from the magnitude of the world to a very great power of the author; but you will limit yourselves to the admission that this has only comparative significance for your capacity of comprehension, and, since you do not cognize all that is possible in order to compare it with the world's magnitude so far as you know it, you cannot from so small a scale infer any omnipotence of the author, and so forth. Now by this means you arrive at no determinate concept of an original being suitable for a theology; for this can be found only in that of the allness of the perfections compatible with {{ 476 }} an understanding, to which merely ***empirical*** data can in no way help you: but without such a determinate concept you cannot conclude to a ***single*** intelligent original being either, but can only assume such a being (for whatever purpose it may be). — Now, to be sure, it can be quite well granted that you (since reason has nothing grounded to say against it) arbitrarily add: where so much perfection is met with, one may well assume all perfection to be united in a single cause of the world; because with such a determinate principle reason gets along better theoretically and practically. But after all you cannot praise this concept of the original being as having been proved by you, since you have assumed it only for the sake of a better use of reason. All the lamentation, therefore, or impotent anger over the alleged outrage of casting doubt on the cogency of your chain of reasoning, is vain boasting which would like to have the doubt that is freely spoken against your argument be taken for a doubting of sacred truth, in order to let its own shallow nature slip through behind this cover.

Moral teleology, on the contrary, which is no less firmly grounded {{{ 481 }}} than physical teleology, but rather deserves preference because it rests _a priori_ on principles inseparable from our reason, leads to what is required for the possibility of a theology, namely, to a determinate ***concept*** of the supreme cause as cause of the world according to moral laws, and hence of such a cause as satisfies our moral final purpose: to which end nothing less than omniscience, omnipotence, omnipresence, and so forth, are required as natural properties belonging to it, which {{ 477 }} must be thought of as connected with the moral final purpose, which is infinite, and hence as adequate to it; and it can thus, entirely by itself, supply the concept of a ***single*** author of the world who is suitable for a theology.

In such a way a theology leads also directly to ***religion***, i.e., the ***cognition of our duties as divine commands***: because the cognition of our duty and of the final purpose imposed upon us therein by reason could first produce the concept of God determinately, which concept is therefore already in its origin inseparable from obligation toward this being; whereas, if the concept of the original being could also be found determinately on the merely theoretical path (namely, of the same as a mere cause of nature), it would afterwards still be combined with great difficulty, perhaps even the impossibility of accomplishing it without arbitrary interpolation, to attribute to this being by thorough proofs a causality according to moral laws, without which, however, that allegedly theological concept can constitute no foundation for religion. Even if a religion could be founded on this theoretical path, it would, in view of disposition (in which, after all, its essence consists), be genuinely distinct from that in which the concept of God and the (practical) conviction of his existence arise from the ground-ideas of morality. For if we had to presuppose the omnipotence, omniscience, and so forth, of an author of the world as concepts given to us from elsewhere, in order afterwards merely to apply our concepts of duties to our relation to him, then these duties would have to carry with them very strongly the appearance of coercion and extorted submission; instead of which, if respect for the moral law, quite freely and according to the prescription of our own {{ 478 }} reason, represents to us the final purpose of our vocation, we admit into our moral outlooks, with the most sincere reverence—which is entirely distinct from pathological fear {{{ 482 }}}—a cause that harmonizes with this purpose and with its execution, and willingly submit ourselves to it[^*].

If one asks why we should care at all to have a theology, it is clearly evident that it is necessary, in subjective view, not for the extension or correction of our cognition of nature and of any theory whatsoever, but solely for religion, i.e., the practical, namely the moral use of reason. If it is now found that the only argument which leads to a determinate concept of the object of theology is itself moral: then it will not only not be surprising, but one will also, in view of the accessibility of holding-for-true from this ground of proof, miss nothing with respect to its final purpose, if it is admitted that such an argument sufficiently demonstrates the existence of God only for our moral vocation, i.e., in practical view, and that speculation in no way proves its strength in this, or thereby extends the scope {{ 479 }} of its domain. Also, the surprise or the alleged contradiction of a possibility of a theology asserted here with what the critique of speculative reason said of the categories—namely, that these can produce cognition only in application to objects of the senses, but in no way when applied to the supersensible—will disappear if one sees them used here for a cognition of God, yet not in a theoretical respect (according to what his nature, inscrutable to us, is in itself), but solely in a practical respect. — In order to make an end on this occasion to the misinterpretation of that very necessary doctrine of the Critique—which also, to the annoyance of the blind dogmatist, repels reason back into its boundaries—I append here the following elucidation of the same.

When I attribute to a body ***moving force***, and hence think {{{ 483 }}} of it through the category of ***causality***: I thereby at the same time ***cognize*** it, i.e., I determine its concept as an object in general through that which belongs to it for itself as an object of the senses (as the condition of the possibility of that relation). For if the moving force that I attribute to it is a repulsive one, then there belongs to it (even if I do not yet place another beside it against which it exerts this force) a place in space, furthermore an extension, i.e., space within itself, moreover the filling of this space through the repulsive forces of its parts, and finally also the law of this filling (that the ground of the repulsion of the latter must decrease in the same proportion as the extension of the body grows, and the space which it fills with the same parts through this force increases). — On the contrary, if I think of a supersensible being as the ***first mover***, and hence through the category of causality in view of the same world-determination (the motion of matter): {{ 480 }} then I must not think of it as in any place in space, nor as extended, indeed I must not even think of it as existing in time and simultaneously with others. Thus I have no determinations at all which could make the condition of the possibility of motion through this being, as its ground, intelligible to me. Consequently, I do not in the least cognize it for itself through the predicate of cause (as first mover): but I have only the representation of a something which contains the ground of the motions in the world; and its relation to these as their cause, since it otherwise offers me nothing belonging to the constitution of the thing which is the cause, leaves the concept of the latter completely empty. The ground of this is: because with predicates that find their object only in the world of sense, I can indeed proceed to the existence of something which must contain the ground of the latter, but not to the determination of its concept as a supersensible being, which expels all those predicates. Through the category of causality, therefore, when I determine it by the concept of a ***first mover***, I do not in the least cognize what God is; but perhaps it will succeed better if I take occasion from the world-order not merely to ***think*** of his causality as that of a supreme ***understanding***, but also to ***cognize*** him through this determination of the said concept: because there the burdensome condition of space and of extension is eliminated. To be sure, the great purposiveness in the world compels us to ***think*** {{{ 484 }}} of a supreme cause of the same and of its causality as if through an understanding; but we are thereby in no way authorized to ***attribute*** this to it (just as, for example, we think of the eternity of God as existence at all times, because otherwise we can form no concept at all of mere existence as a magnitude, i.e., as duration; or think of the divine omnipresence as existence {{ 481 }} in all places, in order to make comprehensible to ourselves the immediate presence for things outside one another, without nevertheless being permitted to attribute any of these determinations to God as something cognized in him). When I determine the causality of man in view of certain products which are explainable only through intentional purposiveness by thinking of it as an understanding of his, I do not need to stop there, but can attribute this predicate to him as a well-known property of his and thereby cognize him. For I know that intuitions are given to the senses of man and are brought by the understanding under a concept and hereby under a rule; that this concept contains only the common characteristic (with the omission of the particular) and is therefore discursive; that the rules for bringing given representations under a consciousness in general are given by it even prior to those intuitions, and so forth: I therefore attribute this property to man as one through which I ***cognize*** him. But if I now wish to ***think*** of a supersensible being (God) as an intelligence, this is, in a certain respect of my use of reason, not only permitted but even unavoidable; but to flatter oneself that one can attribute understanding to it and thereby ***cognize*** it through a property of the same is by no means permitted: because I must then omit all those conditions under which alone I know an understanding, so that the predicate, which serves only for the determination of man, cannot be referred to a supersensible object at all, and thus through a causality so determined, what God is cannot be cognized at all. And so it goes with all categories, which can have no meaning at all for cognition in theoretical respect unless they are applied to objects {{ 482 }} of possible experience. — But according to the analogy with an understanding, I can, indeed I must, think of a supersensible being in a certain other respect, without nevertheless wanting thereby to cognize it theoretically; namely, if this determination of its causality concerns an effect in the world which contains a morally necessary but, for beings of sense, unfeasible purpose: since then a cognition of God and of his existence (theology) is possible through properties and determinations of his causality thought of in him merely according to {{{ 485 }}} analogy, which has all required reality in a practical relation, but also ***only in respect of this*** (as a moral one). — An ethikotheology is therefore indeed possible; for morality can indeed subsist with its rule, but not with the final purpose which this very rule imposes, without theology, without leaving reason in the air in view of the latter. But a theological ethics (of pure reason) is impossible: because laws which reason does not originally give itself, and the observance of which it does not also effect as a pure practical power, cannot be moral. In the same way, a theological physics would be a nonentity, because it would present not laws of nature, but arrangements of a supreme will; whereas a physical (properly physico-teleological) theology can yet at least serve as a propaedeutic to theology proper: in that, through the contemplation of the purposes of nature, of which it offers rich material, it gives occasion to the idea of a final purpose which nature cannot present; and hence can indeed make felt the need of a theology which would determine the concept of God sufficiently for the highest practical use of reason, but cannot produce it and ground it adequately on its proofs.

[^*]: Both the admiration of beauty and the being moved by the so manifold purposes of nature, which a reflecting mind is capable of feeling even before a clear representation of a rational author of the world, have in themselves something similar to a ***religious*** feeling. They seem, therefore, to act first on the moral feeling (of gratitude and reverence toward the cause unknown to us) through a way of judging them that is analogous to the moral one, and thus to act on the mind through the excitation of moral ideas, when they inspire that admiration which is combined with far more interest than mere theoretical contemplation can produce.
