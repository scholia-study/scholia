## § 41. On the empirical interest in the beautiful

That the judgment of taste, whereby a thing is pronounced beautiful, is required to rest on no interest ***as its ground of determination***, has been established sufficiently above. Yet it in no way follows from this that no interest whatever can be bound up with the judgment once it has been put forward as a {{ 162 }} pure aesthetic one. Yet this connection can never be more than indirect, that is, taste must first be conceived in conjunction with something else, so that to the satisfaction of mere reflection upon an object there may be joined a further ***pleasure in the existence*** of that object (in which all interest consists). For here in the aesthetic judgment what holds is what is said of the cognitive judgment (concerning things in general): _a posse ad esse non valet consequentia_. This further element may, then, be of an empirical kind — an inclination belonging by nature to the human being — or else of an intellectual kind, namely a characteristic of the will whereby it can be determined _a priori_ through reason; and each of the two harbors a satisfaction in an object's existence, thereby able to furnish the basis of an interest in something that has pleased already on its own and apart from all regard to any interest whatever.

The beautiful is of empirical interest only in ***society***; and if one concedes the drive toward society to be natural to the human being, and concedes the fitness and the propensity for it, that is, ***sociability***, to be a requirement {{{ 297 }}} of the human being as a creature destined for society, and thus a property belonging to ***humanity***, then it cannot fail to come about that taste too is regarded as a faculty for judging {{ 163 }} everything through which one is able to communicate even one's ***feeling*** to everyone else, and hence as a means of furthering what the natural inclination of each demands.

Left to himself alone, a man cast away on a barren island would deck out neither his hut nor his own person, nor would he go in search of flowers, still less cultivate them in order to adorn himself with them; rather, only in company does it occur to him to be not just a human being but also, in his own manner, a refined one (the beginning of civilization): for one judges to be such a person the one who is disposed and skilled to share his pleasure with others, and whom no object satisfies unless he can feel the satisfaction in it in fellowship with others. Each person, moreover, looks for and demands from everyone else this regard for universal communication, as though on the strength of an original covenant prescribed by humanity itself; and so, to be sure, in the beginning it is merely charms that acquire importance within society and become tied to a great interest — colors, for one, with which to paint oneself (roucou among the Caribs, cinnabar among the Iroquois), or else flowers, shells, brightly hued birds' feathers, and in course of time beautiful shapes too (upon canoes, garments, and so on) that carry with them no delight of any kind, that is, no satisfaction of enjoyment: until at length a civilization that has climbed to its uttermost point {{ 164 }} makes of this well-nigh the chief business of refined inclination, and sensations come to be held worth only so much as they admit of being universally shared; at which stage, even though the pleasure that anyone takes in such an object is slight and in itself devoid of any noticeable interest, the idea of its universal communicability nonetheless enlarges its worth almost past all measure.

This interest, indirectly appended to the beautiful through the inclination toward society and thus empirical, is however of no importance to us here, we who have only to attend to whatever may stand in relation _a priori_, even if only indirectly, to the judgment of taste. For should an interest bound up with it come to light even in this form, then taste would reveal a passage of our faculty of judging from sensory gratification to moral feeling; and not merely would one be the better guided {{{ 298 }}} to occupy taste in a purposive way, but there would likewise be presented, as such, an intermediate member in the chain of human faculties _a priori_ upon which all legislation must rest. This much may well be said of the empirical interest taken in the objects of taste, and in taste itself: that, since taste therein indulges inclination, however refined the latter may be, it nonetheless gladly {{ 165 }} coalesces with all the inclinations and passions that, within society, reach their greatest diversity and highest degree, and that the interest in the beautiful, when it rests upon this, can afford but a very ambiguous passage from the agreeable to the good. But whether this passage might not, after all, be furthered by taste, when taste is taken in its purity, we have reason to examine.
