## § 41. On the empirical interest in the beautiful

That the judgment of taste, whereby a thing is pronounced beautiful, is required to rest on no interest ***as its ground of determination***, has been established sufficiently above. But it does not follow from this that, once it has been given as a {{ 162 }} pure aesthetic judgment, no interest could be connected with it. Yet this connection can never be more than indirect, that is, taste must first be conceived in conjunction with something else, so that to the satisfaction of mere reflection upon an object there may be joined a further ***pleasure in the existence*** of that object (in which all interest consists). For here in the aesthetic judgment what holds is what is said of the cognitive judgment (concerning things in general): _a posse ad esse non valet consequentia_. Now this other element may be something empirical, namely an inclination proper to human nature; or something intellectual, as a property of the will to be determinable _a priori_ by reason: both of which involve a satisfaction in the existence of an object and can in this way lay the ground for an interest in that which has already pleased of itself and without reference to any interest at all.

The beautiful is of empirical interest only in ***society***; and if one concedes the drive toward society to be natural to the human being, and concedes the fitness and the propensity for it, that is, ***sociability***, to be a requirement {{{ 297 }}} of the human being as a creature destined for society, and thus a property belonging to ***humanity***, then it cannot fail to come about that taste too is regarded as a faculty for judging {{ 163 }} everything through which one is able to communicate even one's ***feeling*** to everyone else, and hence as a means of furthering what the natural inclination of each demands.

Left to himself alone, a man cast away on a barren island would deck out neither his hut nor his own person, nor would he go in search of flowers, still less cultivate them in order to adorn himself with them; rather, only in company does it occur to him to be not just a human being but also, in his own manner, a refined one (the beginning of civilization): for one judges to be such a person the one who is disposed and skilled to share his pleasure with others, and whom no object satisfies unless he can feel the satisfaction in it in fellowship with others. Everyone, besides, expects and requires of everyone else a regard for universal communication, as if in virtue of an original compact dictated by humanity itself; and thus, indeed, at the outset only stimuli come to count for much in society and to be bound up with great interest, for instance colors for painting oneself (roucou among the Caribs and cinnabar among the Iroquois), or flowers, seashells, prettily colored bird feathers, though in time beautiful forms as well (as upon canoes, clothing, and so on) that bring with them no gratification whatever, that is, no satisfaction of enjoyment: until in the end civilization, risen to its highest point, {{ 164 }} turns this into almost the principal work of refined inclination, and sensations are prized only so far as they permit of being universally shared; whereupon, granting that the pleasure each takes in such an object is but trifling and of itself carries no appreciable interest, the idea of its universal communicability nevertheless magnifies its worth almost without limit.

This interest, indirectly appended to the beautiful through the inclination toward society and thus empirical, is however of no importance to us here, we who have only to attend to whatever may stand in relation _a priori_, even if only indirectly, to the judgment of taste. For should an interest bound up with it come to light even in this form, then taste would reveal a passage of our faculty of judging from sensory gratification to moral feeling; and not merely would one be the better guided {{{ 298 }}} to occupy taste in a purposive way, but there would likewise be presented, as such, an intermediate member in the chain of human faculties _a priori_ upon which all legislation must rest. This much may well be said of the empirical interest taken in the objects of taste, and in taste itself: that, since taste therein indulges inclination, however refined the latter may be, it nonetheless gladly {{ 165 }} coalesces with all the inclinations and passions that, within society, reach their greatest diversity and highest degree, and that the interest in the beautiful, when it rests upon this, can afford but a very ambiguous passage from the agreeable to the good. But whether this passage might not, after all, be furthered by taste, when taste is taken in its purity, we have reason to examine.
