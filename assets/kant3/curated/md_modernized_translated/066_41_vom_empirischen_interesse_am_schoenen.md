## § 41. On the Empirical Interest in the Beautiful

That the judgment of taste, by which something is declared beautiful, must have no interest ***as its determining ground***, has been sufficiently demonstrated above. But it does not follow from this that, after it has been given as a {{ 162 }} pure aesthetic judgment, no interest can be combined with it. This combination, however, can always be only indirect, i.e., taste must first of all be represented as combined with something else, in order to be able to connect with the satisfaction of mere reflection on an object a further ***pleasure in its existence*** (as that in which all interest consists). For what is said of judgments of cognition (of things in general) holds here in the aesthetic judgment: _a posse ad esse non valet consequentia_. This other can now be something empirical, namely an inclination inherent in human nature; or something intellectual, as a property of the will of being able to be determined _a priori_ by reason: both of which contain a satisfaction in the existence of an object, and can thus lay the ground for an interest in that which has already pleased for itself and without regard to any interest.

Empirically, the beautiful interests only in ***society***; and if one admits the drive to society as natural to man, but the fitness and the bent for it, i.e., ***sociability***, as a requirement {{{ 297 }}} of man as a creature destined for society, and thus as a property belonging to ***humanity***: then it cannot fail that one should also view taste as a faculty of judging {{ 163 }} everything by means of which one can even communicate one's ***feeling*** to everyone else, and consequently as a means of promoting what everyone's natural inclination demands.

By himself, an abandoned human being on a desert island would neither adorn his hut nor himself, nor seek out flowers, much less plant them in order to decorate himself with them; rather, only in society does it occur to him to be not merely a human being, but also, in his own way, a refined human being (the beginning of civilization): for we judge as such a one him who is inclined and apt to communicate his pleasure to others, and whom an object does not satisfy if he cannot feel the satisfaction in it in community with others. Also, everyone expects and demands from everyone else regard for universal communication, as if from an original contract dictated by humanity itself; and thus, to be sure, at first only charms, e.g., colors for painting oneself (roucou among the Caribs and cinnabar among the Iroquois), or flowers, mussel shells, beautifully colored bird feathers, but in time also beautiful forms (as on canoes, clothes, etc.), which carry with them no pleasure at all, i.e., satisfaction of enjoyment, become important in society and connected with great interest: until at last civilization, having reached its highest point, {{ 164 }} makes of this almost the main business of refined inclination, and sensations are valued only as much as they allow of being universally communicated; where, although the pleasure that each has in such an object is only inconsiderable and in itself without noticeable interest, yet the idea of its universal communicability increases its value almost infinitely.

This empirical interest, indirectly attached to the beautiful through inclination to society, is however of no importance for us here, who have to look only at what may have a relation _a priori_, even if only indirect, to the judgment of taste. For if a connected interest should be discovered in this form as well, then taste would reveal a transition of our faculty of judging from sensory enjoyment to moral feeling; and not only would we thereby be better guided {{{ 298 }}} in employing taste purposively, but a connecting link in the chain of human faculties _a priori_, on which all legislation must depend, would also be represented as such. So much can indeed be said of the empirical interest in objects of taste and in taste itself, that, since the latter indulges inclination, however much it may be refined, it also readily allows itself {{ 165 }} to merge with all the inclinations and passions that reach their greatest variety and highest degree in society, and that the interest in the beautiful, when based on this, can provide only a very ambiguous transition from the agreeable to the good. Whether, however, the latter might not after all be promoted by taste when it is taken in its purity, is what we have cause to investigate.
