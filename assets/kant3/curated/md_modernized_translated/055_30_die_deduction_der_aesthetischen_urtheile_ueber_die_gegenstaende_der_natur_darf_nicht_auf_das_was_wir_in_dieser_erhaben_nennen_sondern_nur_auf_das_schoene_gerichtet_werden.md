## § 30. The Deduction of Aesthetic Judgments on the Objects of Nature Must Not Be Directed to What We Call Sublime in the Latter, but Only to the Beautiful

The claim of an aesthetic judgment to universal validity for every subject requires, as a judgment that must base itself on some _a priori_ principle, a deduction (i.e., a legitimation of its presumption), which must be added to its exposition, when, namely, it concerns a liking or disliking for the ***form of the object***. Of this kind are judgments of taste concerning the beautiful in nature. For purposiveness then still has its ground in the object and its shape, even if it does not indicate the relation of the object to other objects in accordance with concepts (for a judgment of cognition); but rather concerns merely the apprehension of this form, insofar as it shows itself in the mind to be in agreement generally with the ***faculty*** both {{ 132 }} of concepts and of their presentation (which is one and the same with that of apprehension). One can therefore, even in respect of the beautiful in nature, raise various questions concerning the cause of this purposiveness of its forms: e.g., how one would explain why nature has so lavishly spread beauty everywhere, even at the bottom of the ocean, where the human eye (for which alone that beauty is, after all, purposive) only rarely reaches, and the like.

But the sublime of nature — if we pass a pure aesthetic judgment upon it that is not mixed with concepts of perfection as objective purposiveness, in which case it would be a teleological judgment — can be regarded as completely formless or misshapen, but nonetheless as an object of a pure liking, and can exhibit subjective purposiveness of the given presentation; and here the question now arises: whether for an aesthetic judgment of this kind, besides the exposition of what is thought in it, a deduction of its claim to some (subjective) _a priori_ principle can also be demanded.

{{{ 280 }}} To this the following serves as an answer: that the sublime of nature is so called only improperly, and must properly be attributed merely to the way of thinking, or rather to the ground for it in human nature. To become conscious of this, the apprehension of an {{ 133 }} otherwise formless and unpurposive object gives merely the occasion, which is in such a manner ***used*** subjectively-purposively, but is not judged as such ***in itself*** and because of its form (as it were _species finalis accepta, non data_). Hence our exposition of judgments about the sublime of nature was at the same time their deduction. For when we analyzed the reflection of the power of judgment in them, we found in them a purposive relation of the cognitive faculties, which must be laid at the basis of the faculty of ends (the will) _a priori_ and is therefore itself purposive _a priori_: which, then, immediately contains the deduction, i.e., the justification of the claim of such a judgment to universally necessary validity.

We shall therefore have to seek only the deduction of judgments of taste, i.e., of judgments about the beauty of natural things, and thus satisfy the task for the entire aesthetic power of judgment as a whole.
