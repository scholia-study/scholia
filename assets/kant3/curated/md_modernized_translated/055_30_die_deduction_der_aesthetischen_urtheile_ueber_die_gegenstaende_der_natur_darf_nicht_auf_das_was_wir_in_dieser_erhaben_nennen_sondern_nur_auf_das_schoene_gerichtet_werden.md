## § 30. The deduction of aesthetic judgments concerning nature's objects must be directed not at what we call sublime in nature, but solely at the beautiful

The claim that an aesthetic judgment makes to universal validity for every subject, being a judgment that has to be grounded on some principle _a priori_, stands in need of a deduction (that is, a legitimation of its pretension), one that has to be supplied beyond the mere exposition of that judgment — namely, whenever it concerns a satisfaction or displeasure in ***the object's form***. The judgments of taste passed upon nature's beauty are precisely such judgments. For the purposiveness then, even so, has its ground in the object and in its shape, granted that it does not point to that object's relation to other objects by way of concepts (with a view to a cognitive judgment); instead it merely concerns the apprehension of this form, insofar as that form, within the mind, proves commensurate with the ***faculty*** both {{ 132 }} of concepts and of their presentation (this latter being one and the same as the faculty of apprehension), taken quite generally. With regard to nature's beauty, accordingly, one can likewise pose a variety of questions bearing on the cause of this purposiveness of its forms: for instance, how one is to account for nature's having scattered beauty so prodigally on every side, even upon the ocean floor, which the human eye — the sole thing for which such beauty is after all purposive — but seldom reaches, and so forth.

The sublime in nature, however — if we pronounce a pure aesthetic judgment upon it, one not blended with concepts of perfection understood as objective purposiveness, since were that the case the judgment would instead be teleological — may be taken as altogether formless or shapeless and yet as something in which a pure satisfaction is found, and may display a subjective purposiveness belonging to the given representation; and so the question now presents itself: whether, for an aesthetic judgment of this sort, over and above the exposition of what gets thought within it, one may further demand a deduction of its claim to some (subjective) principle _a priori_.

{{{ 280 }}} The answer to this runs as follows: that the sublime in nature bears that name only in a loose sense, and that strictly it must be attributed merely to the manner of thinking, or rather to the foundation for it lying in human nature. The apprehension of an {{ 133 }} object that is otherwise formless and devoid of purposiveness does no more than provide the occasion for becoming conscious of this — an object that in this fashion gets ***employed*** subjectively and purposively, yet is not judged as such an object ***in its own right*** and for the sake of its form (as it were, _species finalis accepta, non data_). Our exposition of the judgments upon the sublime in nature was thus at the same time their deduction. For once we had taken apart how the power of judgment reflects within such judgments, we came upon in them a purposive relationship among the cognitive powers — one that has to be laid _a priori_ at the basis of the faculty of ends (the will) and is on that account itself purposive _a priori_: a relationship that thereupon immediately carries with it the deduction, that is, the vindication of such a judgment's claim to a universally necessary validity.

We shall accordingly have merely to look for the deduction of judgments of taste, that is, of the judgments concerning the beauty of natural things, and in this way discharge the task set for the aesthetic power of judgment in its entirety.
