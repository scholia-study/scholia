## § 5. Comparison of the three specifically distinct kinds of satisfaction

The agreeable and the good both bear a relation to the faculty of desire, and to that extent they carry with them a satisfaction—the former a pathologically conditioned one (through incentives, stimulos), the latter a pure practical one—which is determined not merely through the representation of the object but at the same time through the represented linkage of the subject with the existence of that same object. Not merely the object, but its existence as well, is what pleases. By contrast, the judgment of taste is merely ***contemplative***, i.e., a judgment which, being indifferent as regards the existence of an object, only holds its constitution together with the feeling of pleasure and displeasure. But this contemplation itself is likewise not directed at concepts; for the judgment of taste is not a cognitive judgment (neither a theoretical nor a practical one) and hence is also not ***grounded*** on concepts, nor even ***aimed*** at such.

The agreeable, the beautiful, the good thus denote three distinct relations that representations can bear to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, in {{ 15 }} {{{ 210 }}} relation to which we distinguish objects or modes of representation from one another. Moreover, the expressions suited to each, by which one designates the complacency in them, are not one and the same. ***Agreeable*** is what someone calls that which gratifies him; ***beautiful***, what merely pleases him; ***good***, what is esteemed, ***approved***, i.e., that in which an objective worth is set by him. Agreeableness holds also for non-rational animals; beauty only for human beings, i.e., animal but nonetheless rational beings, yet also not merely as such (e.g., spirits), but at the same time as animal ones; the good, however, for every rational being in general; a proposition that can receive its complete justification and explanation only in what follows. One may say that, among all three of these kinds of satisfaction, the satisfaction of taste in the beautiful is the single one that is disinterested and ***free***; for no interest, neither that of the senses nor that of reason, wrings approval from us. Hence one could say of satisfaction that in the three cases mentioned it refers to ***inclination***, or to ***favor***, or to ***respect***. For favor is the sole free satisfaction. An object of inclination, and one which a law of reason enjoins upon us for the purpose of desiring, allow us no freedom to make of anything whatsoever an object of pleasure for ourselves. All interest presupposes a need, or brings one {{ 16 }} forth; and, as a determining ground of approval, it no longer leaves the judgment about the object free.

As regards the interest that inclination takes in the agreeable, everyone says: hunger is the best cook, and to people of healthy appetite everything is palatable that is at all edible; hence such a satisfaction proves no choice governed by taste. Only once the need has been satisfied can one distinguish who among the many has taste and who does not. In the very same way there are manners (Conduite) without virtue, courtesy without benevolence, decency without honorableness, and so forth. For wherever the ethical law pronounces, there is objectively no further free choice regarding what ought to be done; and to exhibit taste in one's conduct (or in judging that of others) is something quite other than to give voice to one's moral cast of mind: for the latter contains a command and brings forth a need, whereas ethical taste, by contrast, merely toys with the objects of satisfaction without binding itself to any one of them.

Explanation of the beautiful, inferred from the first moment. {{{ 211 }}}

***Taste*** is the faculty for judging an object or a mode of representation through a satisfaction or dissatisfaction ***without any interest whatever***. The object of such a satisfaction is called ***beautiful***.
