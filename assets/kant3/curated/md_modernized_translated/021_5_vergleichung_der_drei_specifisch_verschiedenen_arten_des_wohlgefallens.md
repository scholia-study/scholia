## § 5. Comparison of the three specifically distinct kinds of satisfaction

Both the agreeable and the good stand in a relation to the faculty of desire, and on that account each brings with it a satisfaction—the one pathologically conditioned (by incentives, stimulos), the other purely practical—whose determining ground lies not in the representation of the object alone but likewise in the subject's represented connection with that object's existence. Not merely the object, but its existence as well, is what pleases. The judgment of taste, on the other hand, is purely ***contemplative***, that is, a judgment that, remaining indifferent toward whether an object exists, merely sets the object's character alongside the feeling of pleasure and displeasure. This contemplation, however, is for its own part not directed toward concepts; for a judgment of taste is no cognitive judgment (be it theoretical or practical), and accordingly it is neither ***grounded*** upon concepts nor ***aimed*** at any such.

Thus the agreeable, the beautiful, and the good designate three different relations that representations have to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, in {{ 15 }} {{{ 210 }}} regard to which we set objects or kinds of representation off from one another. Moreover, the expressions suited to each, by which one designates the complacency in them, are not one and the same. ***Agreeable*** is the name a person gives to whatever delights him; ***beautiful***, to what he simply finds pleasing; ***good***, to what he prizes, ***approves***, that is, to that in which he posits an objective worth. Agreeableness holds also for non-rational animals; beauty only for human beings, i.e., animal but nonetheless rational beings, yet also not merely as such (e.g., spirits), but at the same time as animal ones; the good, however, for every rational being in general; a proposition that can receive its complete justification and explanation only in what follows. One may observe that the satisfaction which taste takes in the beautiful stands alone among these three kinds of satisfaction as a disinterested and ***free*** one; for no interest, whether of the senses or of reason, compels the approval. Hence one could say of satisfaction that in the three cases mentioned it refers to ***inclination***, or to ***favor***, or to ***respect***. For favor is the sole free satisfaction. An object of inclination, and one which a law of reason enjoins upon us for the purpose of desiring, allow us no freedom to make of anything whatsoever an object of pleasure for ourselves. Every interest presupposes a need, or else calls one {{ 16 }} forth; and once it serves as the determining ground of approval, it leaves the judgment upon the object no longer free.

As regards the interest that inclination takes in the agreeable, everyone says: hunger is the best cook, and to people of healthy appetite everything is palatable that is at all edible; hence such a satisfaction proves no choice governed by taste. It is only when the need has been met that one can tell, among many people, who has taste and who does not. In the very same way there are manners (Conduite) without virtue, courtesy without benevolence, decency without honorableness, and so forth. For wherever the ethical law pronounces, there is objectively no further free choice regarding what ought to be done; and to exhibit taste in one's conduct (or in judging that of others) is something quite other than to give voice to one's moral cast of mind: for the latter contains a command and brings forth a need, whereas ethical taste, by contrast, merely toys with the objects of satisfaction without binding itself to any one of them.

Explanation of the beautiful, inferred from the first moment. {{{ 211 }}}

***Taste*** is the capacity for appraising an object, or a mode of representation, by means of a satisfaction or dissatisfaction ***without any interest whatever***. The object of such a satisfaction is called ***beautiful***.
