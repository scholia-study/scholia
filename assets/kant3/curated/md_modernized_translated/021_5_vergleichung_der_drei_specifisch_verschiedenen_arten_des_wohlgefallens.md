## § 5. Comparison of the Three Specifically Different Kinds of Satisfaction

The agreeable and the good both have a relation to the faculty of desire, and to that extent carry with them, the former a pathologically conditioned satisfaction (through stimuli, stimulos), the latter a pure practical satisfaction, which is determined not merely through the representation of the object but at the same time through the represented connection of the subject with the existence of the object. Not merely the object, but also its existence pleases. By contrast, the judgment of taste is merely ***contemplative***, i.e., a judgment which, indifferent with regard to the existence of an object, holds together only its constitution with the feeling of pleasure and displeasure. But this contemplation itself is also not directed at concepts; for the judgment of taste is not a cognitive judgment (neither a theoretical nor a practical one) and is therefore also not ***grounded*** on concepts, nor ***aimed*** at such.

The agreeable, the beautiful, the good thus designate three different relations of representations to the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, in {{ 15 }} {{{ 210 }}} relation to which we distinguish objects or kinds of representation from one another. Also, the expressions appropriate to each, with which one designates the complacency in them, are not the same. ***Agreeable*** is what someone calls that which gratifies him; ***beautiful***, what merely pleases him; ***good***, what is esteemed, ***approved***, i.e., that in which an objective value is set by him. Agreeableness counts also for nonrational animals; beauty only for human beings, i.e., animal but yet rational beings, though also not merely as such (e.g., spirits), but at the same time as animal beings; the good, however, for every rational being in general; a proposition that can receive its complete justification and explanation only in what follows. One can say: that among all these three kinds of satisfaction that of taste in the beautiful is alone a disinterested and ***free*** satisfaction; for no interest, neither that of the senses nor that of reason, extorts the approval. Hence one could say of satisfaction that in the three named cases it relates to ***inclination***, or ***favor***, or ***respect***. For favor is the only free satisfaction. An object of inclination, and one that is imposed upon us for desire by a law of reason, leave us no freedom to make for ourselves an object of pleasure out of anything whatever. All interest presupposes a need, or produces one; {{ 16 }} and, as a determining ground of approval, it no longer leaves the judgment about the object free.

As far as the interest of inclination in the case of the agreeable is concerned, everyone says: hunger is the best cook, and to people of healthy appetite everything tastes good that is only edible; hence such a satisfaction proves no choice in accordance with taste. Only when the need is satisfied can one distinguish who among many has taste or not. In the same way there are manners (Conduite) without virtue, politeness without benevolence, propriety without honorableness, etc. For where the moral law speaks, there is objectively no further free choice with regard to what is to be done; and to show taste in one's conduct (or in the appraisal of that of others) is something quite different from expressing one's moral way of thinking: for the latter contains a command and produces a need, whereas the moral taste merely plays with the objects of satisfaction without attaching itself to any one of them.

Explanation of the beautiful inferred from the first moment. {{{ 211 }}}

***Taste*** is the faculty for the appraisal of an object or of a kind of representation through a satisfaction or dissatisfaction ***without any interest***. The object of such a satisfaction is called ***beautiful***.
