## § 43. On art in general

1) ***Art*** is set apart from ***nature*** as doing (_facere_) is from acting or operating in general (_agere_), and the product or consequence of the former, as a ***work*** (_opus_), is set apart from that of the latter as an effect (_effectus_) {{ 174 }}.

By rights, one ought to call art only that bringing-forth through freedom, that is, through a power of choice that grounds its actions in reason. For although one is pleased to call the product of bees (their regularly built waxen combs) a work of art, this happens only on account of the analogy with the latter; namely, as soon as one reflects that they base their labor on no rational deliberation of their own, one says at once that it is a product of their nature (of instinct), and it is ascribed as art only to their creator.

When, in searching through a peat bog, one comes upon a piece of hewn wood, as has occasionally happened, one does not call it a product of nature at all, but of art; the cause that brought it forth had an end in view, to which the thing owes its shape. In other respects one is indeed inclined to recognize an art in anything whose constitution is such that, within its cause, a representation of the thing had to run ahead of its actual existence (as happens even with bees), though without the effect's needing on that account to have been genuinely ***thought*** by the cause; but whenever one simply calls something a work of art, so as to set it off from a natural effect, one invariably means by it a work of human beings.

2) ***Art*** as a human skill is also distinguished from {{ 175 }} ***science*** (***being able*** from ***knowing***), as a practical faculty from a theoretical one, as technique from theory (as the art of surveying from geometry). And here too that which one ***can*** do, as soon as one merely ***knows*** what is to be done and is thus sufficiently acquainted with the desired effect, is not exactly called art. Only that for which, even when one knows it in the most complete way, one nevertheless does not thereby at once possess the skill to make {{{ 304 }}} it, belongs to that extent to art. ***Camper*** describes very precisely how the best shoe would have to be constructed, yet he certainly could not make one[^*].

3) ***Art***, too, is distinguished from ***handicraft***; the former one terms ***free***, whereas the latter might equally be termed ***remunerative art***. One regards the first as though it could turn out purposively (succeed) only as play, that is, an occupation that is agreeable in itself; the second in such a way that, as labor, that is, an occupation that is in itself disagreeable (burdensome) and alluring only through its effect (e.g., the wages), it can accordingly be compulsorily {{ 176 }} imposed. Whether, in the ranking of the guilds, watchmakers should count as artists but smiths as craftsmen: that requires a different point of view for judging than the one we adopt here, namely the proportion of the talents that must underlie one or the other of these occupations. Whether, among the so-called seven liberal arts, some may not have been listed that are to be counted among the sciences, and several too that are to be compared with handicrafts: of this I will not speak here. But that in all free arts there is nonetheless demanded some element of compulsion or, as the name goes, a ***mechanism***, lacking which the ***spirit*** — that which has to remain ***free*** in art, and which alone breathes life into the work — would be left with no body whatever and would dissipate altogether: it is not unwise to bear this in mind (e.g., in the art of poetry, correctness and richness of language, likewise prosody and meter), since many recent educators believe they best promote a free art by taking all constraint away from it and transforming it from labor into mere play.

[^*]: In my region the common man, when one puts before him some such task as Columbus with his egg, says: ***that is no art, it is merely a science***. That is, if one knows it, then one ***can*** do it; and he says just the same of all the supposed arts of the sleight-of-hand performer. That of the tightrope dancer, on the other hand, he will by no means refuse to call art.
