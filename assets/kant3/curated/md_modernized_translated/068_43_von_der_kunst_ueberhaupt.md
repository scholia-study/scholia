## § 43. On art in general

1) ***Art*** is distinguished from ***nature*** just as doing (_facere_) differs from acting or operating at large (_agere_); and what the former yields, its product or upshot, counts as a ***work*** (_opus_), whereas what issues from the latter counts as an effect (_effectus_) {{ 174 }}.

By rights, one ought to call art only that bringing-forth through freedom, that is, through a power of choice that grounds its actions in reason. For even though we like to call the product of bees (those regularly constructed combs of wax) a work of art, we do so merely because of its analogy with the latter; for the moment it occurs to us that they found their labor upon no reasoning of their own, we promptly declare their handiwork to be an outgrowth of their nature (of instinct), and only their creator is credited with the art.

When, in searching through a peat bog, one comes upon a piece of hewn wood, as has occasionally happened, one does not call it a product of nature at all, but of art; the cause that brought it forth had an end in view, to which the thing owes its shape. In other respects one is indeed inclined to recognize an art in anything whose constitution is such that, within its cause, a representation of the thing had to run ahead of its actual existence (as happens even with bees), though without the effect's needing on that account to have been genuinely ***thought*** by the cause; but whenever one simply calls something a work of art, so as to set it off from a natural effect, one invariably means by it a work of human beings.

2) ***Art*** as a human skill is also distinguished from {{ 175 }} ***science*** (***being able*** from ***knowing***), as a practical faculty from a theoretical one, as technique from theory (as the art of surveying from geometry). And here too that which one ***can*** do, as soon as one merely ***knows*** what is to be done and is thus sufficiently acquainted with the desired effect, is not exactly called art. Only that for which, even when one knows it in the most complete way, one nevertheless does not thereby at once possess the skill to make {{{ 304 }}} it, belongs to that extent to art. ***Camper*** describes very precisely how the best shoe would have to be constructed, yet he certainly could not make one[^*].

3) ***Art***, too, is distinguished from ***handicraft***; the former one terms ***free***, whereas the latter might equally be termed ***remunerative art***. One regards the first as though it could turn out purposively (succeed) only as play, that is, an occupation that is agreeable in itself; the second in such a way that, as labor, that is, an occupation that is in itself disagreeable (burdensome) and alluring only through its effect (e.g., the wages), it can accordingly be compulsorily {{ 176 }} imposed. Whether, in the ranking of the guilds, watchmakers should count as artists but smiths as craftsmen: that requires a different point of view for judging than the one we adopt here, namely the proportion of the talents that must underlie one or the other of these occupations. Whether some of the so-called seven liberal arts that were included ought rather to be reckoned among the sciences, and certain others to be likened to handicrafts: on that question I shall not dwell here. Yet it is not unwise to recall that in every free art there is still required something of the compulsory sort — or, as it is called, a ***mechanism*** — without which the ***spirit***, which in art must be ***free*** and which alone animates the work, would possess no body at all and would evaporate entirely (for instance, in poetic composition, grammatical correctness and a wealth of language, and equally prosody and metrical measure), for a number of modern educators suppose that they most effectively advance a free art when they strip every constraint from it and turn it out of labor into sheer play.

[^*]: Where I come from, whenever someone sets an ordinary fellow a task of that kind — like Columbus with his egg — he replies: ***that is no art, it is merely a science***. In other words, once one knows the thing, one ***can*** manage it; and he passes exactly this verdict on every so-called art of the conjurer. That of the tightrope dancer, on the other hand, he will by no means refuse to call art.
