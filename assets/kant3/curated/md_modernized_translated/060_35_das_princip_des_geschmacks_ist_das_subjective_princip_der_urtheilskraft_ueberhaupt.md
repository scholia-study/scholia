## {{ 145 }} § 35. The principle of taste is the subjective principle of the power of judgment in general

What distinguishes the judgment of taste from the logical judgment is this: the latter places a representation under concepts of the object, whereas the former brings it under no concept whatever, for otherwise the necessary universal assent it demands could be compelled by proofs. All the same, it is like the logical judgment in professing a universality and {{{ 287 }}} necessity—though not one drawn from concepts of the object, and therefore only a subjective one. Since it is the concepts in a judgment that constitute its content (everything belonging to cognition of the object), and since the judgment of taste admits of no determination by concepts, it is grounded only on the subjective formal condition of judging as such. The subjective condition of all judgments is the faculty of judging itself, or the power of judgment. Brought to bear on a representation by which an object is given, this power calls for two representational faculties to be in agreement: the imagination (which supplies the intuition together with the assembling of its manifold) and the understanding (which supplies the concept whereby the unity of that assembling is represented). Here, because no concept of the object serves as the judgment's basis, the judgment can amount only to placing the imagination {{ 146 }} itself—with a representation by which an object is given—beneath the condition that understanding as such arrives from intuition at concepts. That is to say: since the imagination's freedom lies precisely in its schematizing apart from any concept, a judgment of taste has to rest upon the bare sensation of imagination in its ***freedom*** and understanding with its ***lawfulness*** reciprocally quickening one another—upon a feeling, then, that lets the object be appraised by the purposiveness of the representation (by which an object is given) for the advancement of the cognitive powers at their free play; and taste, being a subjective power of judgment, holds a principle of subsumption—not of intuitions beneath ***concepts***, but of the ***faculty*** of intuitions or presentations (that is, the imagination) beneath the faculty of concepts (that is, the understanding), in the measure that the first ***in its freedom*** agrees with the second ***in its lawfulness***.

To track down this legitimating ground through a deduction of the judgments of taste, nothing can guide us but the formal peculiarities belonging to judgments of this sort—that is, taken merely so far as their logical form comes under consideration.
