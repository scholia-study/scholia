## {{ 145 }} § 35. The principle of taste is the subjective principle of the power of judgment as such

What distinguishes the judgment of taste from the logical judgment is this: the latter places a representation under concepts of the object, whereas the former brings it under no concept whatever, for otherwise the necessary universal assent it demands would admit of being forced by proofs. Nevertheless, it is like the logical judgment in professing a universality and {{{ 287 }}} necessity—though not one drawn from concepts of the object, and therefore only a subjective one. Now because what makes up a judgment's content (all that pertains to cognition of the object) is its concepts, whereas the judgment of taste cannot be settled by any concept, this latter rests solely upon that subjective formal condition which any judgment whatever requires. Underlying every judgment as its subjective condition is the very faculty of judging, or the power of judgment. Brought to bear on a representation by which an object is given, this power calls for two representational faculties to be in agreement: the imagination (which supplies the intuition together with the assembling of its manifold) and the understanding (which supplies the concept whereby the unity of that assembling is represented). Here, because no concept of the object serves as the judgment's basis, the judgment can amount only to placing the imagination {{ 146 }} itself—with a representation whereby an object is given—beneath the condition that understanding as such arrives from intuition at concepts. That is to say: since the imagination's freedom lies precisely in its schematizing apart from any concept, a judgment of taste has to rest upon the bare sensation of imagination in its ***freedom*** and understanding with its ***lawfulness*** reciprocally quickening one another—thus on a feeling which allows the object to be judged according to the purposiveness of the representation (through which an object is given) in regard to the furthering of the cognitive powers in their free play; and a principle of subsumption is what taste, being a subjective power of judgment, harbors—though not one of intuitions under ***concepts*** but rather of the ***faculty*** of intuitions or presentations (that is, the imagination) under the faculty of concepts (in other words, the understanding), so far as the first of these ***in its freedom*** accords with the second ***in its lawfulness***.

To track down this legitimating ground through a deduction of the judgments of taste, nothing can guide us but the formal peculiarities belonging to judgments of this sort—that is, taken merely so far as their logical form comes under consideration.
