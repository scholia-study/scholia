## {{ 145 }} § 35. The Principle of Taste is the Subjective Principle of the Power of Judgment in General

The judgment of taste differs from a logical one in this: that the latter subsumes a representation under concepts of the object, whereas the former does not subsume it under a concept at all, because otherwise the necessary universal assent could be compelled by proofs. Yet it is similar to the latter in that it claims a universality and {{{ 287 }}} necessity, though not according to concepts of the object, hence a merely subjective one. Now since the concepts in a judgment constitute its content (that which belongs to the cognition of the object), but the judgment of taste is not determinable by concepts, it grounds itself only on the subjective formal condition of a judgment in general. The subjective condition of all judgments is the faculty of judging itself, or the power of judgment. This power, employed in regard to a representation whereby an object is given, requires the harmony of two faculties of representation: namely, of the imagination (for the intuition and the combination of the manifold of the same) and of the understanding (for the concept of the representation of the unity of this combination). Since, then, no concept of the object lies at the basis of the judgment here, it can consist only in the subsumption of the imagination {{ 146 }} itself (in the case of a representation whereby an object is given) under the condition that the understanding in general proceeds from intuition to concepts. That is, since the freedom of the imagination consists precisely in its schematizing without a concept: the judgment of taste must rest on a mere sensation of the mutually quickening imagination in its ***freedom*** and the understanding with its ***lawfulness***, hence on a feeling that allows the object to be judged according to the purposiveness of the representation (whereby an object is given) for the promotion of the cognitive faculties in their free play; and taste, as a subjective power of judgment, contains a principle of subsumption, not of intuitions under ***concepts***, but of the ***faculty*** of intuitions or exhibitions (i.e., of the imagination) under the faculty of concepts (i.e., the understanding), insofar as the former ***in its freedom*** harmonizes with the latter ***in its lawfulness***.

In order now to discover this ground of right by means of a deduction of judgments of taste, only the formal peculiarities of this class of judgments—hence insofar as merely their logical form is considered—can serve as our guide.
