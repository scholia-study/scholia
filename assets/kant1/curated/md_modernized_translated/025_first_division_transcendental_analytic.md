## {{{ 83 }}} {{ 89 }} First Division. Transcendental Analytic

This analytic is the dissection of the entirety of our _a priori_ cognition into the elements of the pure cognition of the understanding. It depends upon the following points: 1. That the concepts be pure and not empirical concepts. 2. That they belong not to intuition and to sensibility, but to thinking and to understanding. 3. That they be elementary concepts, and be well distinguished from those that are derived or composed from them. 4. That their table be complete, and that they entirely fill the whole field of the pure understanding. Now this completeness of a science cannot be reliably assumed on the basis of a rough estimate of an aggregate brought about merely by trials; therefore it is possible only by means of an ***idea of the whole*** of the _a priori_ cognition of the understanding, and through the division of the concepts that constitute it determined from that idea, consequently only through their ***connection in a system***. The pure understanding separates itself completely not only from everything empirical, but even from all sensibility. {{ 90 }} It is therefore a self-subsistent, self-sufficient unity, not to be increased by any additions from without. Hence the sum of its cognition will constitute a system to be comprehended and determined under one idea, the completeness and articulation of which can at the same time yield a touchstone of the correctness and genuineness of all pieces of cognition that fit into it. This entire part of transcendental logic, however, consists of two books, the one of which contains the ***concepts***, the other the ***principles*** of pure understanding.
