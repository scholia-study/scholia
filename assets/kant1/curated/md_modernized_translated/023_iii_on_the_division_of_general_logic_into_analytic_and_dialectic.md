## {{{ 79 }}} III. On the Division of General Logic into **Analytic** and **Dialectic**

That old and celebrated question by which people supposed they could back the logicians into a corner, seeking to drive them either into a wretched circular argument or into an admission of their ignorance — and thereby of the emptiness of their whole art — runs as follows: ***What is truth?*** As for the nominal definition of truth — that it consists in a cognition's agreement with its object — this is granted at the outset and presupposed; what one wants to know, rather, is the universal and reliable criterion for the truth of any cognition whatsoever.

To know what one can reasonably ask for is already a substantial and necessary proof of prudence or discernment. For if a question is inherently absurd and demands needless answers, then, apart from embarrassing the one who poses it, it sometimes has the added drawback of tempting an incautious listener into equally absurd replies, and of presenting the laughable sight of one person {{ 83 }} (as the ancients said) milking a he-goat while the other holds a sieve beneath it.

If the truth of a cognition lies in its agreement with the object it concerns, then that object must thereby be marked off from others; for a cognition is false when it does not accord with the object to which it is referred, even if it should contain something that might well hold of other objects. A universal criterion of truth, now, would have to be one valid for all cognitions alike, without regard to any difference among their objects. Yet it is evident that, since with any such criterion one leaves aside the whole content of a cognition (its relation to its object), while truth bears precisely on that content, it is downright impossible, and indeed absurd, to demand a mark of the truth of cognitions as regards this content; and thus that no sufficient and at the same time universal sign of truth can possibly be given. Since we earlier designated a cognition's content as its matter, we shall have to say this: with respect to matter, no universal sign of a cognition's truth can be demanded, since to ask for one is inherently self-contradictory.

{{{ 80 }}} But where a cognition is taken merely as to its form (with all content laid aside), it is just as evident: that a logic, insofar as it presents the {{ 84 }} universal and necessary rules of the understanding, must in these very rules set out criteria of truth. For whatever conflicts with them is false, since in such a case the understanding contravenes its own universal rules of thought, and hence itself. These criteria, however, touch only the form of truth — that is, of thinking as such — and to that degree are wholly correct, though not sufficient. For however completely a cognition might conform to logical form — that is, avoid contradicting itself — it may still, for all that, be at odds with its object. The purely logical criterion of truth, then — namely a cognition's agreement with the universal and formal laws of understanding and reason — is indeed the _conditio sine qua non_, and so the negative condition of all truth: but here logic reaches its limit, and the error that strikes not the form but the content lies beyond the reach of any logical touchstone.

General logic, then, breaks the entire formal business of the understanding and of reason down into its elements, and presents them as the principles by which all our cognition is to be logically appraised. This part of logic may accordingly be termed analytic, and it is on that very account the at least negative touchstone of truth, inasmuch as one must first check and assess every cognition as to its form against these rules before examining it {{ 85 }} as to its content, so as to make out whether, in regard to the object, it holds any positive truth. But since the mere form of a cognition, however far it may square with logical laws, comes nowhere near to establishing the material (objective) truth of that cognition, no one may venture, on the strength of logic alone, to pass judgment on objects or to assert anything, without first having gathered well-grounded information about them from a source outside logic — reserving logic merely for the subsequent task of putting that information to use, of linking it into a connected whole by logical laws, or, better yet, of testing it against them. Even so, there is something so seductive in commanding such a plausible art — that of imparting to all our cognitions the form of the understanding, however empty and impoverished one may remain as to their content — that this general logic, a mere ***canon*** for appraisal, has been employed as though it were an ***organon*** for actually bringing forth objective assertions, or at any rate {{{ 81 }}} for putting on a deceptive show of them, and so has in truth been abused in the process. General logic, taken now as a supposed organon, is called ***dialectic***.

However diverse the meanings in which the ancients applied this name to a science or an art, one may still infer with confidence, from the use they actually made of it, that in their hands it {{ 86 }} was nothing but the ***logic of illusion***. It was an art of sophistry that lent to one's ignorance — indeed to one's willful deceptions — the semblance of truth, aping the method of thoroughness that logic prescribes across the board and turning its topics to the embellishment of every hollow proceeding. Now this may be set down as a sound and useful caution: that general logic, ***regarded as an organon***, is at all times a logic of illusion — that is, dialectical. For since it instructs us in nothing at all concerning the content of a cognition, but only in the formal conditions of accord with the understanding — conditions, moreover, wholly indifferent with respect to the objects — the demand that one wield it as an instrument (organon) so as to broaden and extend one's knowledge, at least in appearance, must come to nothing more than idle talk, whereby one asserts whatever one pleases with a certain show of plausibility, or, if one prefers, contests it at will.

Instruction of that sort is in no way consonant with the dignity of philosophy. On that account, the term dialectic has instead been assigned to logic as a ***critique of dialectical illusion***, and it is in this sense that we too would have it understood here.
