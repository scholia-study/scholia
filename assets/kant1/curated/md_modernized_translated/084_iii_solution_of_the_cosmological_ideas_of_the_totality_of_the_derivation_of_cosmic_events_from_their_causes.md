## III. Solution of the cosmological ideas of the totality of the derivation of cosmic events from their causes.

With respect to what happens, only two sorts of causality are thinkable: either one conforming to ***nature*** or one arising from ***freedom***. The {{{ 363 }}} first is the tie binding one state to an earlier state within the sensory world, such that the one succeeds the other by a rule. Now, because the ***causality*** of appearances is grounded in temporal conditions, and because an antecedent state, had it subsisted for all time, could not itself have generated an effect that begins only within time, the causality belonging to the cause of whatever happens or arises has ***come to be*** as well, and, on the understanding's own principle, once again stands in need of a cause.

{{ 561 }} Freedom in the cosmological sense, by contrast, I take to mean the power to initiate a state ***of itself***, a power whose causality is accordingly not itself subordinated, under natural law, to a further cause that would fix its position in time. Understood thus, freedom is a purely transcendental idea: one that, in the first place, incorporates nothing drawn from experience, and whose object, in the second place, can never be exhibited as determinate in any experience, for it belongs to the universal law conditioning the very possibility of all experience that whatever occurs has a cause, so that the causality of that cause—which ***itself happened*** or arose—must once more possess a cause of its own; and by this the whole domain of experience, to whatever extent it may reach, gets converted into an aggregate of sheer nature. Yet since no absolute totality of conditions within the causal nexus can be reached along that route, reason contrives for itself the idea of a spontaneity capable of commencing activity on its own, with no prior cause required to determine it, in its turn, to that activity in keeping with the law of causal linkage.

It is highly striking that the practical concept of freedom should rest upon this ***transcendental*** idea of ***freedom***, and that the latter should be, within the practical concept, the genuine seat of the difficulties which have always hemmed in the question {{ 562 }} of freedom's very possibility. ***Freedom in the practical sense*** amounts to the independence of free choice from ***necessitation*** by sensibility's promptings. For a free choice counts as ***sensible*** in the measure that it is ***pathologically*** ***affected*** (through the moving grounds of sensibility); it earns the name ***animal*** (_arbitrium brutum_) where it admits of being ***pathologically necessitated***. The human power of choice is, to be sure, an _arbitrium sensitivum_, though not _brutum_ but rather _liberum_, since sensibility {{{ 364 }}} does not make its action necessary, there residing instead in the human being a power to settle himself, on his own, apart from any necessitation by sensible promptings.

It is not hard to see that, were all causality within the sensory world nothing but nature, then each event would find its determination in another within time, under necessary laws; and consequently, since appearances—so far as they determine choice—would be obliged to make every action necessary as the natural upshot of themselves, the cancellation of transcendental freedom would simultaneously annihilate all practical freedom. For practical freedom takes for granted that, even though a thing has not come about, it nonetheless ***ought*** to have come about, and that its cause among appearances was thus not so determinative as to rule out a causality lodged in our choice—one able to bring forth, independently of those natural causes and even in defiance of their might and sway, something determined within the temporal order by empirical laws, and hence to inaugurate a series of events ***quite of itself***.

What comes to light here, then, is just what one meets with generally in the conflict of a reason that hazards itself {{ 563 }} past the frontiers of possible experience: that the problem is, strictly speaking, not ***physiological*** but ***transcendental***. Thus the question of freedom's possibility does indeed press upon psychology, but, resting as it does on the dialectical arguments of merely pure reason, it—along with its resolution—must be the concern of transcendental philosophy and of it alone. To equip that discipline—which is not at liberty to refuse a satisfying answer on the matter—for the task, I must, before anything else, endeavor to pin down its procedure here somewhat more exactly by way of a remark.

Supposing appearances were things in themselves, and space and time therefore forms under which things in themselves exist, then conditions and conditioned would invariably belong, as members, to one and the same series, and out of this there would arise, in the present case too, the antinomy shared by all transcendental ideas—namely, that such a series must inescapably prove either too great or too small for the understanding. The dynamical concepts of reason, however—our concern in this number and the next—carry this peculiarity: inasmuch as they bear not on an object regarded as a magnitude but solely on its ***existence***, one may likewise leave aside the magnitude of the series of conditions {{ 564 }} and attend, in their case, only to the dynamical {{{ 365 }}} bearing of condition upon conditioned; so that in the question of nature and freedom we run up against the difficulty, at the outset, whether freedom is so much as possible anywhere, and, granting that it is, whether it can stand together with the universality of the natural law of causality; hence whether the proposition be a rightly disjunctive one—that every effect in the world must issue ***either*** from nature ***or*** from freedom—or whether, rather, ***both*** may not obtain at once, in differing regards, in one and the same event. The soundness of that principle—that all events of the sensory world hang together thoroughly under unalterable natural laws—already stands firm, qua principle of the transcendental analytic, and no harm befalls it. So the sole question is this: whether, in spite of that, freedom may also have a place in regard to the very effect that nature determines, or whether that inviolable rule shuts it out completely. And at this point the widespread yet deceiving assumption of the ***absolute reality*** of appearances promptly reveals its detrimental power to throw reason into confusion. For if appearances be things in themselves, freedom is past saving. Then nature is the entire and, of itself, adequately determining cause of each event, whose condition is on every occasion contained solely within the series of appearances that, together with their effect, fall necessarily under the law of nature. If, on the other hand, {{ 565 }} appearances are held to be nothing beyond what they actually are—not things in themselves, that is, but mere representations interlinked by empirical laws—then they must themselves have further grounds that are not appearances. Such an intelligible cause, though, is, as regards its causality, not determined by appearances, even while its effects do appear and can accordingly be determined by other appearances. Hence it lies, together with its causality, outside the series, while its effects are to be found within the series of empirical conditions. The effect may therefore be viewed as free with respect to its intelligible cause, and yet, with respect to the appearances, as a result flowing from them by nature's necessity—a distinction which, laid out in general terms and wholly in the abstract, is bound to look exceedingly subtle and obscure, but which will grow clear in the applying. My only aim here has been to note this: that, since the thoroughgoing interconnection of all appearances within one fabric of nature is a law that grants no reprieve, it would of necessity {{{ 366 }}} topple all freedom were one to insist obstinately on the reality of appearances. And this is why those who, on this head, go along with the common opinion have never managed to reach a reconciliation of nature with freedom.
