## {{{ 308 }}} {{ 472 }} Of the Antinomy of Pure Reason

## Third Conflict of the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

Causality according to laws of nature is not the sole causality from which the world's appearances, taken all together, admit of being derived. For explaining these appearances it is furthermore necessary to assume a causality through freedom.

## Proof.

Assume that no causality exists beyond that which conforms to nature's laws; in that case everything ***that happens*** has for its presupposition an antecedent state, upon which it ensues unfailingly by a rule. Now this antecedent state must itself be something that came to be (something that arose within time, not having been before); for had it subsisted from all eternity, its consequence likewise would not first have originated but would always have been. Hence the causality belonging to the cause by which something happens is itself a ***thing that has occurred***, one that under the law of nature once more requires an antecedent state along with its causality, which state in turn requires a yet earlier one, and so on. So should all events come to pass under nothing but the laws of nature, {{ 474 }} there is then, at every juncture, only a _subaltern_ start and never a first, so that on the side of the causes descending one from another the series achieves no completeness at all. Yet this is exactly wherein the law of nature consists: that nothing comes about save through a cause determined with sufficiency _a priori_. Thus the proposition that every causality is possible solely under nature's laws falls into contradiction with itself when taken in its unlimited universality, and this universality can therefore not be accepted as the only one.

{{{ 310 }}} There must, accordingly, be posited a causality whereby something comes to pass while its cause is not in turn fixed, by some further cause going before it, under necessary laws—that is to say, an ***absolute spontaneity*** on the part of the causes, a capacity to set going ***of itself*** a series of appearances proceeding by natural laws, and thus transcendental freedom, in the absence of which even within nature's course the succession of appearances on the causal side never attains completeness.

## {{{ 309 }}} {{ 473 }} Antithesis.

There is no freedom; everything in the world, on the contrary, takes place merely under laws of nature.

## Proof.

Posit that there existed, in the transcendental sense, a ***freedom*** functioning as a peculiar species of causality by which the world's events might be brought about—that is, a power to inaugurate absolutely a state, and thereby a whole series of consequences flowing from it; then it is not merely a series that would commence through this spontaneity, but the very determining of this spontaneity toward generating the series, i.e. the causality itself, that would set in absolutely, so that nothing at all precedes whereby this occurring deed is fixed under abiding laws. But any beginning of acting whatever has for its presupposition a state in which the cause is not yet acting; and a dynamically first onset of the action presupposes a state bearing no causal connection at all to the preceding state of that self-same cause, i.e. one arising in no manner out of it. Transcendental freedom, accordingly, runs counter to the law of causality, and any such {{ 475 }} binding-together of the successive states of operative causes—a linkage that permits no unity of experience and is on that account encountered in no experience—comes to nothing more than an empty entity of thought.

We are left, then, with nothing but ***nature***, and it is within nature that the coherence and ordering of the world's events must be sought by us. Freedom, understood as independence of nature's laws, is indeed an ***emancipation*** from ***compulsion***—but no less from the ***guiding thread*** of every rule. For it will not do to say that, in place of nature's laws, laws of freedom enter into the causality of the world's course; since, were the latter fixed by laws, it would be no freedom at all, but itself nothing else than nature. Nature, then, and transcendental freedom are distinguished as lawfulness from lawlessness, whereof the former does indeed burden the understanding with the {{{ 311 }}} difficulty of pursuing the descent of occurrences ever higher up the series of causes, since in them causality stands conditioned at every point, yet by way of compensation it holds out a thoroughgoing and lawlike unity of experience, whereas the delusion of freedom, while it holds out to the probing understanding a resting place amid the causal chain—conducting it toward an unconditioned causality that sets itself acting spontaneously—nonetheless, blind as it is in itself, tears loose the guiding thread of the rules on which alone a thoroughly connected experience rests.

## {{ 476 }} Comment on the Third Antinomy.

## I. Regarding the Thesis.

Far from exhausting the whole content of the psychological concept that bears this same name—a concept in large measure empirical—the transcendental idea of freedom covers only the part touching the absolute spontaneity of the action, which is the genuine ground of that action's imputability; and yet it remains the real rock of offense for philosophy, which meets with insuperable difficulties the moment it is to concede a kind of unconditioned causality of this sort. So in the matter of the will's freedom, whatever it is that has ever since kept speculative reason in such deep embarrassment is at bottom merely ***transcendental***, and comes down solely to this: whether one must assume a power to commence ***of itself*** a series of successive things or states. In what way such a thing is possible is not something we are equally bound to be able to answer; for with causality by natural laws as well we must rest satisfied with cognizing _a priori_ that a causality of the kind has to be presupposed, notwithstanding that the possibility—how, by way of one existence, the existence of another comes to be posited—escapes our comprehension entirely, so that on this point we are thrown back upon experience alone. To be sure, the way we have actually exhibited this necessity—that a series of appearances have a first beginning out of freedom—reaches only as far as is required to render an origin of the world intelligible, whereas all the states that ensue {{ 478 }} may be regarded as a sequence proceeding by mere natural laws. But since hereby the power to inaugurate a series in time {{{ 312 }}} wholly ***of itself*** stands after all demonstrated (even if not discerned), we are now at liberty as well to allow, right in the midst of the world's course, sundry series to begin of themselves as to their causality, and to endow the substances of these with a power of acting out of freedom. But here one should not let oneself be held back by a misconstrual—namely, that inasmuch as a successive series within the world admits only of a beginning that is first in a comparative sense (there being always some prior state of things in the world), no absolutely first beginning of such series could be possible in the course of the world. For our talk here concerns not a beginning absolutely first in the order of time, but one first in the order of causality. Should I at this moment (say) get up from my chair with complete freedom, and untouched by any determining compulsion from natural causes, then with this occurrence—and with its natural consequences running on to infinity—a brand-new series takes its start absolutely, notwithstanding that, temporally regarded, the occurrence is but the sequel to a series that went before. For this resolve and this act by no means lie within the train of merely natural effects, nor are they simply its prolongation; on the contrary, the determining natural causes leave off entirely, above them, in regard to this event—an event that succeeds those causes yet does not issue from them, and that consequently, though not in the order of time, must for all that, in the order of causality, be termed an utterly first beginning of a series of appearances.

How firmly reason ***needs*** to invoke, within the chain of natural causes, a first beginning grounded in freedom is borne in upon the eye with great clarity by this: that every philosopher of antiquity (the Epicurean school aside) felt driven, in accounting for the world's motions, to posit a ***first mover***, i.e. a cause acting freely that first, and from itself, started this series of states. For from nature by itself they did not dare to render a first beginning intelligible.

## {{ 477 }} II. Regarding the Antithesis.

The advocate of nature's omnipotence (transcendental ***physiocracy***), set against the doctrine of freedom, would defend his proposition, over and against the latter's sophistical reasonings, after the following fashion. ***If you assume nothing mathematically first, with respect to time, in the world, then neither have you any need to look for something dynamically first, with respect to causality.*** Who charged you with thinking up a world-state that is first in the strict sense, and thereby an absolute commencement of the gradually unwinding series of appearances, and—merely to secure your fancy a point of rest—with imposing limits upon nature's boundlessness? The world's substances have always been there—or at any rate the unity of experience renders that assumption unavoidable—and so nothing stands in the way of granting further that the succession of their states, i.e. a running series of their changes, has likewise always obtained, whence no first beginning, be it mathematical or dynamical, would be admissible to seek. That an endless lineage of this sort should be possible—one lacking any first link with reference to which everything remaining is merely consequent—cannot, as to its very possibility, be brought to comprehension. But if it is on this ground that you would cast aside these enigmas of nature, you will see yourselves constrained to disown many a synthetic fundamental constitution {{{ 313 }}} (fundamental forces) that you can just as little make sense of, {{ 479 }} and even the bare possibility of alteration as such is bound to give you offense. For were it not that experience apprises you of its actuality, you would be forever unable to contrive _a priori_ how a succession of being and non-being so unremitting could be possible.

Even granting, at the most, a transcendental power of freedom for the purpose of inaugurating the world's changes, this power would still have to lie, at the least, wholly beyond the world (albeit it stays ever a rash pretension to posit, past the entire compass of every possible intuition, some further object incapable of being given in any possible perception). But to invest the substances within the world itself with such a power can never be allowed, for in that case what one names nature—the interconnection of appearances that reciprocally and necessarily determine one another under universal laws—together with the criterion of empirical truth whereby experience is set apart from dreaming, would for the greater part evaporate. For alongside a power of freedom so lawless there is hardly any nature left to think, seeing that its laws would suffer ceaseless modification from the influences of that power, and the interplay of appearances—which, under nature alone, would run orderly and uniform—would be thrown thereby into confusion and disconnection.
