## {{{ 308 }}} {{ 472 }} Of the Antinomy of Pure Reason

## Third Conflict of the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

Causality in accordance with laws of nature is not the only one from which the appearances of the world can one and all be derived. To explain these appearances it is necessary to assume that there is also another causality, that of freedom.

## Proof.

Assume there is no other causality than in accordance with laws of nature; then everything ***that happens*** presupposes a previous state, upon which it inevitably follows according to a rule. Now, however, the previous state itself must be something that has happened (come to be in time, since it was not there before), because if it had always existed, its consequence also would not have first arisen, but would always have existed. Therefore, the causality of the cause through which something happens is itself something ***that has happened***, which in accordance with the law of nature again presupposes a previous state and its causality, and this in turn presupposes an even earlier one, and so on. If, therefore, everything happens according to mere laws of nature, {{ 474 }} there is at any time only a _subaltern_, but never a first beginning, and thus in general no completeness of the series on the side of the causes descending from one another. Now, however, the law of nature consists precisely in this: that nothing happens without a cause sufficiently determined _a priori_. Therefore the proposition, as if all causality were possible only according to laws of nature, contradicts itself in its unlimited universality, and this causality can therefore not be assumed as the only one.

{{{ 310 }}} Consequently, we must assume a causality through which something happens without its cause being further determined by another preceding cause according to necessary laws, i.e., an ***absolute spontaneity*** of causes, of beginning ***of itself*** a series of appearances which runs according to laws of nature, hence transcendental freedom, without which, even in the course of nature, the succession of appearances on the side of causes is never complete.

## {{{ 309 }}} {{ 473 }} Antithesis.

There is no freedom, but everything in the world happens solely in accordance with laws of nature.

## Proof.

Assume there is a ***freedom*** in the transcendental sense as a special kind of causality, according to which the occurrences of the world could take place, namely, a faculty of absolutely beginning a state, and consequently also a series of consequences of it; then not only will a series begin absolutely through this spontaneity, but the determination of this spontaneity itself to produce the series, i.e., its causality, will begin absolutely, so that nothing precedes it by which this occurring action is determined according to constant laws. But every beginning of action presupposes a state of the cause which is not yet active, and a dynamically first beginning of action presupposes a state which has no causal connection whatsoever with the preceding state of the very same cause, i.e., in no way follows from it. Therefore, transcendental freedom is contrary to the law of causality, and is such a {{ 475 }} connection of successive states of active causes in accordance with which no unity of experience is possible, and which is therefore not to be met with in any experience, being consequently an empty object of thought.

We have therefore nothing but ***nature***, in which we must seek the connection and order of world occurrences. Freedom (independence) from the laws of nature is indeed a ***liberation*** from ***constraint***, but also from the ***guidance*** of all rules. For one cannot say that in place of the laws of nature, laws of freedom enter into the causality of the course of the world, because if this causality were determined according to laws, it would not be freedom, but itself nothing other than nature. Nature, therefore, and transcendental freedom differ like lawfulness and lawlessness, of which the former indeed burdens the understanding with the {{{ 311 }}} difficulty of seeking the lineage of occurrences ever higher up in the series of causes, because their causality is always conditioned, but by way of compensation promises a thoroughgoing and lawful unity of experience, while on the other hand the illusion of freedom indeed promises rest to the inquiring understanding in the chain of causes by leading it to an unconditioned causality that begins to act of itself, but which, since it is itself blind, tears away the guidance of rules, by which alone a thoroughly connected experience is possible.

## {{ 476 }} Observation on the Third Antinomy.

## I. On the Thesis.

The transcendental idea of freedom is indeed far from constituting the entire content of the psychological concept of this name, which is for the most part empirical, but only that of the absolute spontaneity of action as the proper ground of its imputability; yet it is nevertheless the real stumbling block for philosophy, which finds insurmountable difficulties in admitting this kind of unconditioned causality. That which in the question of the freedom of the will has always so greatly embarrassed speculative reason is therefore properly only ***transcendental***, and relates solely to whether a faculty must be assumed of beginning ***of itself*** a series of successive things or states. How such a faculty is possible does not need to be answered with equal necessity, since in the case of causality in accordance with laws of nature we must likewise content ourselves with cognizing _a priori_ that such a causality must be presupposed, although we cannot in any way comprehend the possibility of how through a certain existence the existence of another is posited, and must therefore hold ourselves solely to experience. Now, we have indeed established this necessity of a first beginning of a series of appearances from freedom only in so far as is required to make a beginning of the world comprehensible, whereas all subsequent {{ 478 }} states can be taken as a succession according to mere laws of nature. But since the faculty of beginning a series in time entirely ***of itself*** is thereby at least proved (although not comprehended), it is now also permitted to allow different series of causality to begin of themselves in the midst of the course of the world, and to ascribe to their substances a faculty of acting from freedom. But let us not be stopped here by a misunderstanding: namely, that since a successive series in the world can have only a comparatively first beginning, because a state of things in the world always precedes it, no absolutely first beginning of series is possible during the course of the world. For we are not speaking here of an absolutely first beginning in terms of time, but in terms of causality. If I now (for instance) arise from my chair quite freely and without the necessarily determining influence of natural causes, then in this occurrence, together with its natural consequences ad infinitum, an entirely new series begins absolutely, although in terms of time this occurrence is only the continuation of a preceding series. For this resolution and act do not lie at all in the succession of mere natural effects and are not a mere continuation of them; rather, the determining natural causes entirely cease above them in respect of this event, which indeed follows upon them, but does not result from them, and therefore, though not in terms of time, yet in respect of causality, must be called an absolutely first beginning of a series of appearances.

The confirmation of the ***need*** of reason to appeal in the series of natural causes to a first beginning from freedom is very obvious from this: that (with the exception of the Epicurean school) all the philosophers of antiquity felt themselves compelled, in order to explain the movements of the world, to assume a ***first mover***, i.e., a freely acting cause, which first and of itself began this series of states. For they did not venture to make a first beginning comprehensible from mere nature.

## {{ 477 }} II. On the Antithesis.

The defender of the omnipotence of nature (transcendental ***physiocracy***) in opposition to the doctrine of freedom would assert his proposition against the sophistical arguments of the latter in the following manner. ***If you do not admit any mathematically first in the world in terms of time, you also have no need to seek for a dynamically first in terms of causality.*** Who has commanded you to devise an absolutely first state of the world, and therewith an absolute beginning of the series of appearances which gradually rolls on, and, in order that you may provide a resting place for your imagination, to set limits to unlimited nature? Since the substances in the world have always existed—at least the unity of experience makes such a presupposition necessary—there is no difficulty in also assuming that the change of their states, i.e., a series of their alterations, has always existed, and that consequently no first beginning, either mathematical or dynamical, may be sought. The possibility of such an infinite derivation without any first member, in respect of which all the rest is merely subsequent, cannot, as regards its possibility, be made comprehensible. But if you choose to reject these riddles of nature on that account, you will find yourself compelled to reject many synthetic fundamental qualities {{{ 313 }}} (fundamental forces) which you can just as little comprehend, {{ 479 }} and even the possibility of alteration in general must become offensive to you. For if you did not find through experience that it is real, you would never be able to devise _a priori_ how such an unceasing succession of being and non-being is possible.

Even if, however, a transcendental faculty of freedom is at all events conceded for beginning alterations in the world, this faculty would at least have to be only outside the world (although it always remains a bold pretension to assume, outside the sum total of all possible intuitions, an object which cannot be given in any possible perception). But to ascribe such a faculty to substances in the world itself can never be permitted, because then the connection of mutually and necessarily determining appearances according to universal laws, which we call nature, and with it the criterion of empirical truth, which distinguishes experience from dreaming, would for the most part vanish. For in the presence of such a lawless faculty of freedom, nature can hardly be thought any longer, because the laws of the latter are ceaselessly altered by the influences of the former, and the play of appearances, which according to mere nature would be regular and uniform, is thereby rendered confused and disconnected.
