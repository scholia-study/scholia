## Introduction §1

In whatever way and by whatever means a cognition may relate to objects, that through which it relates immediately to them, and at which all thought as a means is directed, is intuition. This, however, takes place only insofar as the object is given to us; but this in turn is possible for us human beings at least only through the object affecting the mind in a certain way. The capacity (receptivity) to acquire representations through the way in which we are affected by objects is called **sensibility**. Objects are therefore given to us by means of sensibility, and it alone yields us intuitions; but they are thought through the understanding, and from it arise concepts. All thought, however, must relate ultimately, whether straightaway (_directe_) or by a detour (_indirecte_), by means of certain marks, to intuitions, and thus in us to sensibility, because in no other way can an object be given to us.

{{{ 50 }}} The effect of an object upon the faculty of representation, {{ 34 }} insofar as we are affected by it, is ***sensation***. That intuition which relates to the object through sensation is called ***empirical***. The undetermined object of an empirical intuition is called ***appearance***.

In the appearance I call that which corresponds to sensation its ***matter***, but that which causes the manifold of the appearance to be able to be ordered in certain relations I call the ***form*** of the appearance. Since that within which alone sensations can be ordered and placed in a certain form cannot itself be sensation again, the matter of all appearance is only given to us _a posteriori_, but its form must lie ready for them all in the mind _a priori_, and can therefore be considered separately from all sensation.

I call all representations pure (in the transcendental sense) in which nothing is encountered that belongs to sensation. Accordingly, the pure form of sensible intuitions in general is to be encountered in the mind _a priori_, wherein all the manifold of appearances is intuited in certain relations. This pure form of {{ 35 }} sensibility will itself also be called pure intuition. Thus, if I separate from the representation of a body that which the understanding thinks about it, such as substance, force, divisibility, etc., as well as that which belongs to sensation, such as impenetrability, hardness, color, etc., there still remains for me something from this empirical intuition, namely extension and shape. These belong to pure intuition, which occurs _a priori_, even without an actual object of the senses or of sensation, as a mere form of sensibility in the mind.

A science of all principles of sensibility _a priori_ I call the ***transcendental aesthetic***.[^*] There must therefore be such a {{{ 51 }}} {{ 36 }} science, which constitutes the first part of the transcendental doctrine of elements, in contrast to that which contains the principles of pure thinking and is named transcendental logic.

In the transcendental aesthetic we will therefore first isolate sensibility, by separating off everything that the understanding thinks in it through its concepts, so that nothing remains but empirical intuition. Secondly, we will detach from this everything that belongs to sensation, so that nothing remains but pure intuition and the mere form of appearances, which is the only thing that sensibility can yield _a priori_. In this investigation it will be found that there are two pure forms of sensible intuition as principles of _a priori_ cognition, namely space and time, with the consideration of which we shall now occupy ourselves.

[^*]: The Germans are the only ones who currently make use of the word ***aesthetic*** in order to designate thereby what others call the critique of taste. Lying at the foundation here is a disappointed hope, which the excellent analyst Baumgarten conceived, of bringing the critical estimation of the beautiful under rational principles, and elevating its rules to a science. But this effort is in vain. For the aforementioned rules or criteria are merely empirical according to their foremost sources, and can thus never serve as determinate _a priori_ laws to which our judgment of taste would have to conform; rather the latter constitutes the proper touchstone of the correctness of the former. For this reason it is advisable either to let this appellation pass out of use again and preserve it for that doctrine which is true science (whereby one would also come closer to the language and the sense of the ancients, among whom the division of cognition into _aisthēta kai noēta_ was very famous), or else to share the appellation with speculative philosophy and take aesthetic partly in a transcendental sense, partly in a psychological meaning.
