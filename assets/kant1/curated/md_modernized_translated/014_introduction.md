## Introduction §1

No matter in what way or by what means a cognition may be referred to objects, intuition is that by which it is referred to them directly, and toward which all thinking, as a means, is directed. Yet intuition occurs only where an object is given to us, and this, at least for us as human beings, is in turn possible only if the object affects the mind in a certain manner. That capacity for receiving representations by means of the manner in which we are affected by objects — a receptivity — we call **sensibility**. Objects, then, are given to us through sensibility, and it alone yields us intuitions; but they are thought through the understanding, and concepts have their origin in it. All thinking, however, whether it proceeds straightforwardly (_directe_) or by a roundabout path (_indirecte_), must in the end, through certain marks, bear upon intuitions and therefore, in us, upon sensibility, since there exists no other way in which an object could be given to us.

{{{ 50 }}} ***Sensation*** is the effect produced by an object upon the faculty of representation, {{ 34 }} so far as we are affected by that object. An intuition that bears upon its object by way of sensation is termed ***empirical***. The undetermined object of an empirical intuition is what we call ***appearance***.

Within an appearance, I give the name ***matter*** to whatever answers to sensation, whereas whatever allows the manifold of the appearance to be arranged under certain relations I give the name ***form*** of the appearance. Because that in which the sensations can alone be arranged and set into a definite form cannot itself once more be a sensation, the matter of every appearance is, to be sure, furnished to us only _a posteriori_, whereas its form must already lie prepared for them all _a priori_ within the mind, and hence admits of being examined in separation from every sensation.

All representations in which nothing that belongs to sensation is encountered I call pure, taking the term in its transcendental sense. The pure form of sensible intuitions as such will accordingly be met with _a priori_ in the mind, and within it the entire manifold of appearances is intuited under certain relations. This pure form of {{ 35 }} sensibility will, in its own right, likewise be termed pure intuition. So, for instance, if from my representation of a body I take away all that the understanding thinks in it — substance, force, divisibility, and the like — together with all that belongs in it to sensation — impenetrability, hardness, color, and so forth — there is still left over to me, out of this empirical intuition, something more, namely extension and figure. These latter belong to pure intuition, which has its place _a priori_ in the mind as a mere form of sensibility, even in the absence of any actual object of sense or sensation.

I give the name ***transcendental aesthetic*** to a science of all the _a priori_ principles of sensibility.[^*] Such a science, then, must exist, {{{ 51 }}} {{ 36 }} forming the first division of the transcendental doctrine of elements, as opposed to the one that holds the principles of pure thinking and goes under the name of transcendental logic.

Within transcendental aesthetic, accordingly, we shall in the first place set sensibility apart on its own, stripping away all that the understanding contributes there by means of its concepts, so that empirical intuition alone is left behind. In the second place, we shall then remove from this whatever pertains to sensation, so that there is left over nothing save pure intuition together with the bare form of the appearances — the sole item that sensibility is able to furnish _a priori_. This inquiry will bring to light two pure forms of sensible intuition that serve as principles of _a priori_ cognition, namely space and time, and it is with weighing these that we shall now busy ourselves.

[^*]: At present it is the Germans alone who employ the word ***aesthetic*** for what others name the critique of taste. Underlying their usage is a hope, one that came to nothing, entertained by that outstanding analyst Baumgarten — the hope of subjecting the critical estimation of the beautiful to rational principles and of raising its rules to the rank of a science. Yet this effort is in vain. For the said rules or criteria are, in respect of their principal sources, nothing but empirical, and can therefore never furnish fixed _a priori_ laws by which our judgment of taste would be obliged to steer; on the contrary, it is this judgment that supplies the genuine test of whether they are correct. On this account the wiser course is either to drop the term altogether once again and keep it in reserve for the doctrine that constitutes a genuine science (by which one would, moreover, draw closer to the idiom and understanding of the ancients, with whom the partition of cognition into _aisthēta kai noēta_ enjoyed great renown), or else to divide the term with speculative philosophy, employing aesthetic now in a transcendental sense, now with a psychological import.
