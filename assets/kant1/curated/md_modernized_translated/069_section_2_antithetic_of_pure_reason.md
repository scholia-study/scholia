## Section 2. Antithetic of pure reason

If a thetic is any aggregate of dogmatic doctrines, then by antithetic I understand not dogmatic assertions of the contrary, but rather the conflict of cognitions that are dogmatic in appearance (_thesin cum antithesi_), without conceding to any one of them a superior claim to assent over another. The antithetic thus concerns itself not at all with one-sided assertions, but instead considers universal cognitions of reason solely with respect to their mutual conflict and the causes of that conflict. The transcendental antithetic is an investigation concerning pure reason's antinomy, together with its causes and its outcome. If we use our reason not merely for the application of the principles of the understanding {{ 449 }} to objects of experience, but venture to extend those principles beyond the boundary of the latter, then ***rationalizing*** doctrines arise that can look for no confirmation in experience and need dread no refutation from it, and each of which is not only free from contradiction in itself but even finds in the nature of reason conditions of its necessity—only that, unfortunately, the opposite has grounds for its assertion on its own side that are just as valid and necessary.

The questions that naturally present themselves in such a dialectic of pure reason are therefore these: 1. In the case of which propositions pure reason is in fact unavoidably subject to an antinomy. 2. On what causes this antinomy rests. 3. Whether, and in what manner, a path to certainty nonetheless remains open to reason under this contradiction.

A dialectical doctrine of pure reason must accordingly have about it this feature, distinguishing it from all sophistical propositions, that it does not concern an arbitrary question raised only for some chosen {{{ 291 }}} purpose, but rather one which every human reason must necessarily encounter in its progress; and, secondly, that together with its opposite it carries with it not merely a contrived illusion, which disappears at once when one sees through it, but a natural and unavoidable illusion {{ 450 }} that, even when one is no longer taken in by it, still continues to delude, though not to cheat, and so can indeed be rendered harmless but never eradicated.

A dialectical doctrine of this kind will bear not on the understanding's unity within concepts of experience, but on reason's unity within mere ideas—and the conditions of that unity, given that they must first, as a rule-governed synthesis, be congruent with the understanding while yet, as the absolute unity of this same synthesis, being congruent with reason, will turn out too large for the understanding whenever they are adequate to reason's unity, and too small for reason whenever they fit the understanding; whence a conflict is bound to spring that admits of no avoidance, begin the matter as one may.

These rationalizing assertions accordingly throw open a dialectical arena of combat, in which the upper hand stays with whichever party is allowed to make the attack, while the one forced to proceed by mere defense is bound to lose. Hence even doughty knights, whether they pledge themselves to the good cause or the bad, are sure to carry off the victor's wreath, provided only that they see to it that theirs is the prerogative of striking the final blow, with no obligation to endure a renewed assault from their opponent. One can easily imagine that this arena has from time immemorial been entered often enough, that many {{ 451 }} victories were won on both sides, but that, as regards the last one, which decided the matter, care was always taken so that the champion of the good cause alone kept the field, by forbidding his opponent to take up arms any further. As impartial umpires we must set entirely aside the question whether the cause over which the disputants do battle be the good one or the bad, and let them first thresh out their quarrel among themselves. Perhaps, after they have wearied one another more than they have done any harm, they will of themselves come to see the nullity of their quarrel and go their separate ways as good friends.

This method of watching a dispute among assertions play out, or {{{ 292 }}} rather of prompting it oneself—not in order to decide finally in favor of one side or the other, but in order to investigate whether its object may not perhaps be a mere phantom that each grasps at in vain and from which none can gain anything even were it not resisted at all: this procedure, I say, may be called the ***skeptical method***. It is wholly different from skepticism, that principle of a contrived and scientific ignorance which saps the very groundwork of all cognition in order, so far as it can, to leave nowhere any dependability or security in cognition. For the skeptical procedure is directed at certainty: in a controversy meant honestly by both sides and {{ 452 }} conducted with insight, it looks to locate the point of misunderstanding, so that, like sagacious lawgivers, it may draw for itself, from the perplexity of judges in legal suits, a lesson about what is deficient and not precisely fixed in their statutes. The antinomy that comes to light in applying the laws is, with our restricted wisdom, the best trial of legislative craft, since it thereby alerts reason—which in abstract speculation does not easily catch its own false steps—to the factors at work in fixing its principles.

Yet this skeptical method belongs, in its essence, to transcendental philosophy and to it alone; in every other domain of investigation it may perhaps be done without, but not here. Within mathematics an employment of it would be nonsensical, for there no false claims can hide themselves and slip out of sight, seeing that the demonstrations must always advance along the thread of pure intuition, and that by way of a synthesis at every point evident. In experimental philosophy a doubt that postpones judgment may indeed be useful, yet at least no misunderstanding is possible that could not readily be removed, and the ultimate means of deciding the dispute must in the end lie in experience, whether they come to be found early or late. {{ 453 }} Morality can present all its principles collectively, also _in concreto_, together with their practical consequences, at least in possible experiences, and thereby avoid the misunderstanding of abstraction. The transcendental assertions, on the other hand, which presume to insights reaching out past the whole field of every possible experience, are situated neither such that their abstract synthesis might be exhibited in some intuition {{{ 293 }}} _a priori_, nor such that the misunderstanding could be brought to light through any experience whatever. Transcendental reason, then, permits no other touchstone than the attempt to unite its assertions with one another and hence, beforehand, the free and unhindered contest of them among themselves—and this contest we shall now set in motion.[^*]

[^*]: The antinomies follow one another in the order of the transcendental ideas adduced above.
