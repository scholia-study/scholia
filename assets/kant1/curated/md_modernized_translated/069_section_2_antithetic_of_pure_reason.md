## Section 2. Antithetic of pure reason

If thetic is any sum total of dogmatic doctrines, then by antithetic I understand not dogmatic assertions of the opposite, but the conflict of seemingly dogmatic cognitions (_thesin cum antithesi_), without attributing to one of them a preeminent claim to approval over the other. Antithetic therefore does not concern itself at all with one-sided assertions, but rather considers the general cognitions of reason only according to their conflict with one another and the causes thereof. Transcendental antithetic is an investigation into the antinomy of pure reason, its causes and its result. If we do not employ our reason merely for the use of the principles of understanding {{ 449 }} upon objects of experience, but venture to extend it beyond the boundary of the latter, there arise ***ratiocinating*** propositions, which can neither hope for confirmation nor fear refutation in experience, and each of which is not only in itself free from contradiction, but even finds the conditions of its necessity in the nature of reason, only that, unfortunately, the opposite has on its side equally valid and necessary grounds of assertion.

The questions which naturally present themselves in such a dialectic of pure reason are therefore: 1. In which propositions pure reason is inevitably subjected to an antinomy. 2. On what causes this antinomy is based. 3. Whether and in what way a path to certainty nevertheless remains open to reason under this contradiction.

A dialectical proposition of pure reason must therefore have about it this feature distinguishing it from all sophistical propositions, that it does not concern an arbitrary question which is raised only with some arbitrary {{{ 291 }}} intention, but one upon which every human reason in its progress must necessarily stumble; and secondly, that along with its opposite it carries with it not merely an artificial illusion which immediately disappears when it is seen through, but a natural and unavoidable illusion {{ 450 }} which, even when one is no longer deceived by it, still deludes, although it does not deceive, and thus can indeed be rendered harmless, but never eradicated.

Such a dialectical doctrine will relate not to the unity of understanding in concepts of experience, but to the unity of reason in mere ideas, whose conditions—since they must first agree with the understanding as a synthesis according to rules, and yet at the same time, as the absolute unity thereof, with reason—will, if they are adequate to the unity of reason, be too large for the understanding, and, if they are adapted to the understanding, too small for reason; from which there must arise a conflict that cannot be avoided, no matter how one may proceed.

These ratiocinating assertions therefore open up a dialectical arena, where every party keeps the upper hand that has permission to make the attack, and that party is certain to succumb which is compelled to act merely on the defensive. Hence, too, gallant knights, whether they champion the good or the bad cause, are sure to carry off the wreath of victory, if they only take care that they have the privilege of making the last attack, and are not bound to withstand a new assault by their opponent. One can easily imagine that this arena has from of old been entered often enough, that many {{ 451 }} victories have been won on both sides, but that for the last one, which decided the matter, care was always so taken that the champion of the good cause should keep the field alone, by forbidding his opponent to take up arms henceforth. As impartial umpires, we must set entirely aside whether it is the good or the bad cause for which the disputants fight, and let them first settle their dispute among themselves. Perhaps, after they have fatigued rather than injured one another, they will of themselves perceive the vanity of their dispute and part as good friends.

This method of watching a dispute of assertions, or {{{ 292 }}} rather of provoking it oneself, not in order to decide at last in favor of one or the other party, but to investigate whether its object is not perhaps a mere delusion after which everyone vainly grasps, and by which he could gain nothing even if he met with no resistance at all: this procedure, I say, can be called the ***sceptical method***. It is entirely distinct from scepticism, a principle of an artful and scientific ignorance which undermines the foundations of all cognition in order, if possible, to leave no reliability and certainty thereof remaining anywhere. For the sceptical method aims at certainty, in that, in such a dispute which on both sides is honestly intended and {{ 452 }} conducted with understanding, it seeks to discover the point of misunderstanding, in order, as wise legislators do, to draw instruction for themselves, from the embarrassment of judges in lawsuits, regarding what is defective and not precisely defined in their laws. The antinomy which reveals itself in the application of laws is, in our limited wisdom, the best testing trial of nomothetics, in order thereby to make reason, which does not easily become aware of its missteps in abstract speculation, attentive to the moments in the determination of its principles.

This sceptical method, however, is essentially peculiar to transcendental philosophy alone, and can at all events be dispensed with in any other field of investigation, but in this one it cannot. In mathematics its use would be absurd, because in it no false assertions can hide themselves and remain invisible, since the proofs must always proceed along the thread of pure intuition, and indeed through a synthesis that is at all times evident. In experimental philosophy, a doubt of postponement can indeed be useful, yet at least no misunderstanding is possible that could not easily be resolved, and in experience the ultimate means of deciding the dispute must after all finally lie, whether they be discovered early or late. Morality {{ 453 }} can also present its principles in their entirety _in concreto_, together with their practical consequences, at least in possible experiences, and thereby avoid the misunderstanding of abstraction. On the other hand, transcendental assertions, which arrogate to themselves insights extending even beyond the field of all possible experiences, are neither in a position where their abstract synthesis could be given in any intuition {{{ 293 }}} _a priori_, nor so constituted that the misunderstanding could be detected by means of any experience. Transcendental reason therefore admits of no other touchstone than the attempt to unify its assertions among themselves, and consequently, prior to this, the free and unhindered contest of the same with one another, and this we will now set on foot.[^*]

[^*]: The antinomies follow one another according to the order of the above-mentioned transcendental ideas.
