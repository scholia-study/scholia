## {{{ 33 }}} IV. On the difference between analytic and synthetic judgments

Whenever a judgment thinks the relation of a subject to its predicate (and I will weigh only the affirmative kind, the negative being an easy extension afterward), that relation can hold in one of two ways. In the one, predicate _B_ pertains to subject _A_ as a component lodged (though hidden) inside the concept _A_ itself; in the other, _B_ falls altogether beyond the concept _A_, notwithstanding that it stands linked to it. The former judgment I term ***analytic***, the latter ***synthetic***. Analytic judgments (the affirmative sort), then, are the ones whose linkage of predicate to subject rests on identity; where instead that linkage is conceived apart from {{ 11 }} identity, the judgment shall count as synthetic. One might equally name the first kind ***explicative*** and the second ***ampliative judgments***, since the predicate in the former contributes nothing fresh to the subject's concept, but only dissects it into the part-concepts already (if obscurely) lying within it: the latter, by contrast, fasten onto the subject's concept a predicate nowhere thought within it, one that no dissection {{{ 34 }}} of that concept could ever have brought to light. By way of example, should I say that all bodies are extended, the judgment is analytic. For to locate extension as tied to it, I have no need to reach past the concept I attach to the word body; I have only to unfold that concept—to grow aware, that is, of the manifold I forever think in it—and there the predicate presents itself; the judgment is accordingly analytic. Should I instead say that all bodies are heavy, the predicate proves to be something quite other than anything the mere concept of body as such holds for my thinking. Appending such a predicate thus produces a synthetic judgment.

***Judgments of experience as such are, without exception, synthetic.*** It would, after all, be incongruous to rest an analytic judgment upon experience, for in framing such a judgment I may not stray outside my concept in the least, and so stand in need of no experiential evidence whatever. That a body possesses extension is a proposition that _a priori_ {{ 12 }} stands established, and is no verdict of experience. For prior to any recourse to experience, every condition of my judgment already lies ready in the concept, from which the predicate need merely be lifted out under the principle of contradiction, whereby I at once also apprehend the judgment as necessary—something experience could not even begin to instruct me in. Now even though I nowhere embed the predicate of heaviness in the concept of body as such, that concept still points to an object of experience by way of one portion of it, and to that portion I am free to append yet other portions of the selfsame experience, portions counted as belonging with the first {{{ 35 }}}. In advance I can cognize the concept of body analytically by way of its marks—extension, impenetrability, figure, and the like—each of them thought within this concept. But now I enlarge my cognition: turning back toward the experience out of which I had drawn this concept of body, I discover heaviness likewise forever conjoined with the marks just named, and I therefore annex it, synthetically, to that concept as a predicate. Experience, then, is what underwrites the possibility of synthesizing the predicate of heaviness with the body-concept; for the two concepts, though neither is lodged inside the other, still cohere—only contingently, to be sure—as members of a single whole, that whole being experience, itself nothing but a synthetic joining of intuitions.

With synthetic judgments _a priori_, however, this resource fails us wholly {{ 13 }} and entirely. If I am required to pass beyond concept _A_ so as to cognize a further concept _B_ as bound to it: on what do I lean for support, and by what means is the synthesis effected, seeing that in this case I lack the convenience of scanning the domain of experience for it? Consider the statement: whatever comes to pass has its cause. Within the concept of a thing that comes to pass I certainly think an existence upon which a time has gone before, and so forth, and out of that, analytic judgments admit of being formed. Yet the concept of a cause resides wholly outside the former, pointing to something other than what comes to pass, and hence is nowhere included in this latter representation. How, then, do I come to say of whatever comes to pass in general something altogether different from it, and cognize the concept of cause—though it is not held within the former—as nevertheless attaching to it, and attaching necessarily at that? What, in this case, is the unknown = x upon which the understanding steadies itself when, over and above the concept of A, it supposes it has lit upon a predicate B alien to that concept, {{{ 36 }}} one it nonetheless reckons to be joined with it? Experience it cannot be; for the principle just adduced has attached this second representation to the first not only with a universality surpassing anything experience might supply, but also under the stamp of necessity, and thus altogether _a priori_ and from mere concepts. Now the entire final purpose of our speculative _a priori_ cognition reposes on principles of this synthetic, that is, ampliative sort; the analytic principles {{ 14 }}, for their part, are of course supremely important and indispensable, yet serve only to bring concepts to the clarity demanded for a sure and wide-ranging synthesis, understood as a truly new gain.
