## {{ 362 }} C. On the pure use of reason

Can reason be isolated, and is it then still a source in its own right of concepts and judgments that arise solely out of it, and by means of which it relates itself to objects, or is it merely a subordinate faculty for giving to given cognitions a certain form, which is called logical, and through which the cognitions of the understanding are only ranked beneath one another and lower rules beneath other, higher ones (whose condition encompasses the condition of the former within its sphere), so far as this can be brought about by comparing them? That is the question that shall occupy us here, for the present only in a preliminary way. In fact, a multiplicity of rules together with a unity among principles is something reason demands, in order to place the understanding in thoroughgoing coherence with itself, just as the understanding, for its part, subsumes what is manifold in intuition beneath concepts and by that means binds it together. But a principle of this kind prescribes no law to the objects and carries within it no ground that would make it possible to cognize such objects and determine them as such at all; it is rather merely a subjective law of husbandry with the stock of our understanding, one that, through a comparison of its concepts, aims to reduce the {{{ 242 }}} universal use of them to the least possible number, without our being thereby warranted in demanding of the objects themselves any such {{ 363 }} concordance as might further the ease and expansion of our understanding, or in according that maxim objective validity into the bargain. In a word, the question runs thus: does reason in itself, that is, pure reason _a priori_, harbor synthetic principles and rules, and in what do these principles consist?

Its formal and logical procedure in inferences of reason already furnishes us with adequate guidance as to the ground on which its transcendental principle is to be founded within synthetic cognition by means of pure reason.

***First***, the inference of reason is not directed upon intuitions, so as to place them beneath rules (as the understanding is with its categories), but upon concepts and judgments. Hence, although pure reason likewise bears upon objects, it entertains no immediate relation to them and their intuition, but only to the understanding and its judgments, which turn first of all toward the senses and the intuition belonging to them, so as to fix the object of these. The unity of reason, then, is not the unity of some possible experience; rather, it stands essentially apart from the latter, which is the unity of the understanding. That whatever happens should have a cause is in no way a principle that reason cognizes and prescribes. It is what renders the unity of experience possible, and it takes nothing on loan {{ 364 }} from reason, which, apart from this relation to possible experience, could never have decreed any such synthetic unity out of mere concepts.

***Second***, in its logical employment reason searches for the universal condition of its judgment (the conclusion), and the inference of reason is itself nothing but a judgment effected through the subsumption of its condition beneath a universal rule (the major premise). Since this rule, in its turn, is subjected to the very same experiment of reason, so that the condition of the condition has to be sought out (through a prosyllogism) for as long as this remains feasible, one can see well enough that reason's own peculiar principle taken quite generally (in its logical employment) runs as follows: to locate, for the conditioned cognition of the understanding, the unconditioned by which the unity of that cognition is brought to completion.

{{{ 243 }}} This logical maxim, however, can turn into a principle of ***pure reason*** in no other way than by our supposing the following: that once the conditioned is given, the entire series of conditions ranked one beneath another—a series that is therefore itself unconditioned—is given along with it (that is to say, comprised within the object and its combination).

Yet a principle of pure reason of this sort is manifestly ***synthetic***; for although the conditioned relates analytically to some condition or other, it does not relate to the unconditioned. From it, moreover, various {{ 365 }} synthetic propositions are bound to arise of which the pure understanding remains wholly ignorant, seeing that its concern extends only to objects belonging to a possible experience, whose cognition and synthesis stands under conditions at every point. The unconditioned, on the other hand, should it genuinely occur, admits of separate examination in accordance with all those determinations by which it is marked off from every conditioned thing, and must on that account yield material for a good many synthetic propositions _a priori_.

The principles that spring from this highest principle of pure reason will, however, be ***transcendent*** in respect of all appearances, that is, no empirical use answering to it can ever be made of it. It will accordingly stand wholly apart from every principle of the understanding (whose use is entirely ***immanent***, since they take nothing but the possibility of experience for their theme). Now whether that principle—namely that the chain of conditions (whether in the synthesis of appearances or else in the thinking of things as such) reaches all the way to the unconditioned—possesses objective correctness or not; what conclusions issue from it for the empirical employment of the understanding, or whether, on the contrary, there is nowhere any objectively valid proposition of reason of this kind at all, but only a merely logical precept bidding us, as we ascend toward ever higher conditions, to draw nearer to their completeness and so to carry into our cognition the highest unity of reason attainable by us; whether, I repeat, this requirement of reason has, owing to a {{ 366 }} misconstrual, been mistaken for a transcendental principle of pure reason—one that overhastily postulates such an unbounded completeness on the part of the chain of conditions in the objects themselves; and further, what misreadings and illusions may in that case creep into those inferences of reason whose major premise has been taken over from pure reason (and which is perhaps a begging of the question rather than a postulate), and which climb from experience upward to the conditions it rests on: all of that will form our task in the transcendental dialectic {{{ 244 }}}, which we now propose to unfold from its sources, sources lying deeply concealed within human reason. We shall divide it into two chapters, the ***first*** of which shall deal with the ***transcendent concepts*** belonging to pure reason, the ***second*** with its transcendent and ***dialectical inferences of reason***.
