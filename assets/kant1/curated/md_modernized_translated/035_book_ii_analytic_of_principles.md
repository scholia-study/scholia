## {{{ 130 }}} Book II. Analytic of Principles

General logic is built on a plan that coincides quite precisely with the division of the higher faculties of cognition. These are: **understanding**, **power of judgment**, and **reason**. That doctrine therefore treats in its Analytic of ***concepts***, ***judgments***, and ***inferences***, precisely in accordance with the functions and the order of those powers of mind which one comprehends under the broad designation of the understanding in general.

{{ 170 }} Since this merely formal logic abstracts from all content of cognition (whether it be pure or empirical) and busies itself solely with the form of thinking (of discursive cognition) in general, it can also include in its analytical part the canon for reason, whose form has its secure prescription, which, without taking into consideration the particular nature of the cognition employed therein, can be discerned _a priori_ through the mere dissection of the actions of reason into their moments.

Transcendental logic, since it is limited to a determinate content, namely solely to pure cognitions _a priori_, cannot imitate it in this division. For it is shown: that the ***transcendental use of reason*** is not at all objectively valid, consequently does not belong to the ***logic of truth***, i.e., the Analytic, but rather, as a ***logic of illusion***, requires a special part of the scholastic edifice under the name of transcendental ***Dialectic***.

Understanding and the power of judgment accordingly have their canon of objectively valid, and therefore true, use in transcendental logic, and thus belong in its analytical part. But ***reason***, in its attempts to establish something _a priori_ about objects and to extend cognition {{{ 131 }}} {{ 171 }} beyond the bounds of possible experience, is entirely ***dialectical***, and its illusory assertions do not fit at all into a canon, such as the Analytic is supposed to contain.

The ***Analytic of Principles*** will accordingly be solely a canon for the ***power of judgment***, which teaches it to apply to appearances the concepts of the understanding, which contain the condition for rules _a priori_. For this reason, in taking the proper ***principles of the understanding*** as my theme, I shall employ the designation of a ***doctrine of the power of judgment***, by which this business is more precisely designated.
