## {{ 462 }} The Antinomy of Pure Reason

## The Second Conflict among the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

In the world, each and every composite substance is made up of simple parts, and nothing whatever exists except what is simple or what has been put together out of the simple.

## Proof.

For grant that composite substances were not built up from simple parts: then, once every composition had been cancelled in thought, there would be left neither any composite part nor—there being no simple parts—any simple one, and hence nothing at all, so that no substance would ever have been given in the first place. So either it is impossible to cancel all composition in thought, or, once such cancellation has occurred, something subsisting apart from any composition whatever, that is, the simple, {{{ 302 }}} must remain over. In the former case, though, the composite would once more fail to be made of substances (for with substances composition is but a contingent relation among them, one without which they would still have to subsist as, in their own right, {{ 464 }} abiding beings). And since this case runs counter to what was presupposed, only the second option is left standing: that the substantial composite found in the world is built up from simple parts.

It follows from this at once that the world's things are, without exception, simple beings, that their composition is no more than an outward condition of them, and that, although we can never see the elementary substances entirely freed and isolated from this condition of combination, reason is nonetheless bound to think them as the primary subjects of all putting-together, and thus, ahead of it, as simple beings.

## {{ 463 }} Antithesis.

No composite thing anywhere in the world is built up from simple parts, and nothing simple exists in it at all.

## Proof.

Posit the following: a composite thing (taken as a substance) is built up out of simple parts. Since any external relation whatever—and therefore any composition of substances too—can obtain only in space, the space a composite occupies must be built up from exactly as many parts as the composite itself is. Now space is built up not from simple parts but from spaces. Every part of the composite, then, has to take up a space. Yet the utterly first parts of anything composite are simple. The simple, accordingly, takes up a space. But since anything real that takes up a space encloses within it a plurality of mutually external parts, and is on that account composite—and, being a real composite, is made up not of accidents (for accidents cannot stand external to one another apart from a substance) {{{ 303 }}} but of substances—the simple would turn out to be a substantial composite, which is self-contradictory.

The antithesis's second claim—that within the world nothing whatever simple {{ 465 }} exists—is intended here to carry just this import: that the existence of what is absolutely simple can be exhibited through no experience or perception, outer or inner alike, so that the absolutely simple amounts to a mere idea, one whose objective reality is never exhibitable in any possible experience, and which accordingly, in laying out the appearances, is left wholly without application or object. For suppose we could locate, for this transcendental idea, some object of experience: then the empirical intuition of one or another object would have to be recognized as containing utterly no manifold whose members lie apart from one another and are bound together into a unity. Now since one cannot legitimately move from the failure to be conscious of such a manifold to its outright impossibility in every intuition of an object, while that latter impossibility is precisely what absolute simplicity demands, it follows that no perception, of whatever sort, can yield a valid inference to such simplicity. Since, therefore, no object that is absolutely simple can ever be met with in any possible experience, and since the sensible world has to be taken as the entire compass of all possible experiences, it contains nowhere within it anything simple at all.

This second claim of the antithesis reaches much further than the first: whereas the first merely bars the simple from the intuition of the composite, this one clears it away out of the whole of nature; which is why it admitted of proof not from the concept of a given object of outer intuition (the composite), but rather from that object's relation to a possible experience as such.

## {{{ 304 }}} {{ 466 }} Remark on the Second Antinomy.

## I. On the Thesis.

Whenever I talk of a whole that of necessity is built up from simple parts, all I mean by it is a substantial whole in the strict sense of a _Compositum_, that is, the contingent unity of a manifold that, once ***severally*** (at least in thought) ***given***, is brought into a mutual connection and so makes up a single thing. Space, strictly speaking, ought to be termed not a _Compositum_ but a _Totum_, since its parts become possible only within the whole, rather than the whole becoming possible by way of its parts. If anything, it might be styled a _Compositum ideale_, though never a _reale_. Still, this is nothing but a nicety. Because space is not anything composed of substances (nor even of real accidents), it follows that, should I cancel all composition within it, nothing—not so much as the point—can be left behind; for the point has its possibility solely as the limit of a space, and so of a composite {{ 468 }}. Space and time, accordingly, are not built up from simple parts. Whatever pertains only to the state of a substance—even where it possesses a magnitude, as alteration does—is likewise not built up from the simple; that is to say, a given degree of alteration does not come about by the accumulation of many simple alterations. Our move from the composite to the simple carries weight only for things that subsist on their own. Accidents of a state, by contrast, have no subsistence on their own. One may thus, with little trouble, wreck the proof {{{ 306 }}} for the necessity of the simple as the components of every substantial composite—and with it wreck one's cause altogether—by stretching it too far and insisting on its validity for everything composite indiscriminately, as has more than once actually been done.

For the rest, what I address here is only the simple insofar as it is of necessity given within the composite, seeing that the composite admits of being resolved into it as into its own components. The strict sense of the term {{ 470 }} ***_Monas_*** (as Leibniz employed it) really ought to reach only as far as the simple that is ***directly*** given as a simple substance—in self-consciousness, for instance—and not the element of a composite, which would be more aptly termed the _Atomus_. And because it is only with a view to the composite that I mean to establish simple substances as its elements, I might well label the thesis of the second antinomy a transcendental atomistics. But since that term was long ago put to use in naming a particular way of accounting for corporeal appearances (_molecularum_), and hence takes empirical concepts for granted, the principle may just as well be called the dialectical principle of ***monadology***.

## {{{ 305 }}} {{ 467 }} II. On the Antithesis.

Against this proposition of the infinite divisibility of matter—whose supporting ground is purely mathematical—the ***monadists*** raise objections that render themselves suspect from the outset, in that they will not grant the plainest mathematical demonstrations the standing of insights into how space is constituted (space being, in truth, the formal condition of the possibility of all matter), but treat them instead as nothing more than conclusions spun from abstract yet arbitrary concepts that admit of no application to real things. As though it were so much as conceivable to invent some mode of intuiting other than what the original intuition of space affords us, and as though space's _a priori_ determinations did not thereby bear at once on all that is possible for the sole reason that it fills this very space. Were one to give them a hearing, then over and above the mathematical point—simple, yet no part, being merely the limit of a space—one would have to conceive of physical points as well, which, though likewise simple, enjoy the distinction of filling space, as its parts, through their bare aggregation. I shall not rehearse here the ordinary and lucid refutations of this incongruity, which are to be had in plenty—since it is in any event altogether pointless to try, with nothing but discursive concepts, to argue away the self-evidence of mathematics—and shall observe only this: that where philosophy {{ 469 }} makes trouble for mathematics on this point, it does so because it loses sight of the fact that the question here concerns nothing but ***appearances*** and the conditions of them. Here, however, it will not do merely to supply, alongside the pure ***concept of the understanding*** of the composite, the concept of the simple; what is required is that, for the ***intuition*** of the composite (that is, of matter), one supply the ***intuition*** of the simple—and this, given the laws of sensibility, and therefore in the case of objects of the senses as well, is flatly impossible. Thus one may always grant, of a whole made of substances that is conceived by pure understanding alone, that ahead of any composition of it we must already possess the simple; and yet this fails to hold of the _totum substantiale phaenomenon_, which, being an empirical intuition in space, brings with it the unavoidable feature that not one of its parts is simple, {{{ 307 }}} for the reason that no part of space is simple. The monadists, to be sure, have shown enough finesse in seeking to sidestep this difficulty: they take space not to be a condition under which the objects of outer intuition (bodies) are possible, but conversely posit those bodies, together with the dynamical relation of substances at large, as that which makes space itself possible. Yet our only concept of bodies is one of them as appearances, and precisely as appearances they cannot but require space beforehand, since space is what first makes any outer appearance possible at all; the loophole is thus of no avail, having in any case been adequately blocked earlier, in the Transcendental Aesthetic. If they were things in themselves, the monadists' proof would carry the day after all.

{{ 471 }} What is distinctive about the second dialectical assertion is that it faces a dogmatic assertion set against it—the one assertion, among all the sophistical ones, that ventures to demonstrate palpably, upon an object of experience, the actuality of something we ranked above as belonging only to the transcendental ideas, namely the absolute simplicity of substance: to wit, that inner sense's object, the thinking I, is a wholly simple substance. Without engaging with this at present (having examined it at greater length above), I will note only the following: that whenever something is thought simply as an object, with no synthetic determination of its intuition added on (which is just what occurs with the entirely naked representation: I), then naturally no manifold and no putting-together can be discerned in a representation of that sort. And since, besides, the predicates by which I think this object are merely intuitions belonging to inner sense, nothing can turn up in it that would attest to a manifold lying apart from one another, and thereby to a real composition. It is thus purely a consequence of self-consciousness that, since the subject doing the thinking is at once its own object, it is incapable of dividing itself (however divisible the determinations that inhere in it); for taken with regard to itself, every object is an absolute unity. All the same, were this subject to be regarded ***externally***, as an object of intuition, it would after all quite likely exhibit composition in its appearance. And that is precisely how it must always be regarded whenever one wishes to determine whether or not there is within it a manifold lying ***apart from one another***.
