## {{ 462 }} The Antinomy of Pure Reason

## Second Conflict of the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

Every composite substance in the world consists of simple parts, and there exists everywhere nothing but the simple, or what is composed of it.

## Proof.

For let us assume that composite substances do not consist of simple parts: then, if all composition were suspended in thought, no composite part, and (since there are no simple parts) also no simple part would remain, and consequently no substance at all would have been given. Either, therefore, it is impossible to suspend all composition in thought, or, after its suspension, there must remain something existing without any composition, i.e., the simple {{{ 302 }}}. In the former case, however, the composite would again not consist of substances (because with these, composition is only an accidental relation of substances, without which these must exist as self-subsisting {{ 464 }} beings). Since now this case contradicts the assumption, there remains only the second: namely, that the substantial composite in the world consists of simple parts.

From this it follows immediately that the things in the world are all simple beings, that composition is only an external state of them, and that, although we can never completely extract and isolate these elementary substances from this state of connection, yet reason must think them as the first subjects of all composition, and consequently, prior to it, as simple beings.

## {{ 463 }} Antithesis.

No composite thing in the world consists of simple parts, and there exists everywhere nothing simple in it.

## Proof.

Assume: a composite thing (as substance) consists of simple parts. Since all external relation, and consequently all composition from substances, is possible only in space: the space occupied by the composite must consist of as many parts as the composite itself consists of. Now space does not consist of simple parts, but of spaces. Therefore every part of the composite must occupy a space. But the absolutely first parts of every composite are simple. Therefore the simple occupies a space. Since now everything real that occupies a space contains within itself a manifold of elements existing outside one another, and is consequently composite, and indeed as a real composite consists not of accidents (for these cannot exist outside one another without substance), {{{ 303 }}} but of substances: the simple would be a substantial composite; which contradicts itself.

The second proposition of the antithesis—that there exists {{ 465 }} nothing simple at all in the world—is here intended to mean only this: that the existence of the absolutely simple cannot be demonstrated from any experience or perception, whether outer or inner, and that the absolutely simple is therefore a mere idea, whose objective reality can never be demonstrated in any possible experience, and consequently in the exposition of appearances it is without any application or object. For let us assume that an object of experience could be found for this transcendental idea: then the empirical intuition of some object would have to be recognized as one which contains absolutely no manifold outside one another and bound together into a unity. Since now no inference holds from the non-consciousness of such a manifold to the complete impossibility of the same in any intuition of an object, and since this latter is yet absolutely necessary for absolute simplicity: it follows that simplicity can never be inferred from any perception, whatsoever it may be. Since, therefore, something as an absolutely simple object can never be given in any possible experience, and since the sensible world must be regarded as the sum total of all possible experiences: nothing simple is given anywhere in it.

This second proposition of the antithesis goes much further than the first, which banishes the simple only from the intuition of the composite, whereas this one removes it from the whole of nature; hence it has also been capable of being proved not from the concept of a given object of outer intuition (the composite), but from its relation to a possible experience in general.

## {{{ 304 }}} {{ 466 }} Remark on the Second Antinomy.

## I. On the Thesis.

When I speak of a whole which necessarily consists of simple parts, I understand by it only a substantial whole, as the true _compositum_, i.e., the accidental unity of the manifold which, ***given separated*** (at least in thought), is placed in a reciprocal connection and thereby constitutes a single thing. Space should properly not be called a _compositum_, but a _totum_, because its parts are possible only in the whole, and not the whole through the parts. It could at most be called a _compositum ideale_, but not _reale_. Yet this is mere subtlety. Since space is not a composite of substances (not even of real accidents), if I suspend all composition in it, nothing at all, not even the point, must remain; for the latter is possible only as the boundary of a space (consequently of a composite) {{ 468 }}. Space and time, therefore, do not consist of simple parts. What belongs only to the state of a substance, although it may have a magnitude (e.g., alteration), also does not consist of the simple; i.e., a certain degree of alteration does not arise through an increase of many simple alterations. Our inference from the composite to the simple holds only for self-subsisting things. But accidents of state do not exist by themselves. One can therefore easily ruin the proof {{{ 306 }}} for the necessity of the simple, as the constituent parts of all substantial composites, and thereby ruin one's cause altogether, if one extends it too far and wishes to make it valid for every composite without distinction, as has indeed already happened several times.

I am, moreover, speaking here only of the simple in so far as it is necessarily given in the composite, since the latter can be resolved into it as into its constituent parts. The proper meaning of the word {{ 470 }} ***_Monas_*** (according to Leibniz's usage) should indeed refer only to the simple which is ***immediately*** given as a simple substance (e.g., in self-consciousness) and not as an element of the composite, which might better be called the _atomus_. And since I wish to prove the simple substances as elements of the composite only with respect to the composite itself, I might call the thesis of the second antinomy the transcendental atomistics. But because this word has long since been used to designate a particular way of explaining bodily appearances (_molecularum_) and therefore presupposes empirical concepts, it may rather be called the dialectical principle of ***monadology***.

## {{{ 305 }}} {{ 467 }} II. On the Antithesis.

Against this proposition of an infinite division of matter, the ground of proof of which is purely mathematical, objections are brought forward by the ***monadists*** which already make themselves suspicious by the fact that they refuse to accept the clearest mathematical proofs as insights into the constitution of space, in so far as it is in fact the formal condition of the possibility of all matter, but rather regard them only as inferences from abstract but arbitrary concepts which could not be related to actual things. Just as if it were even possible to devise any other kind of intuition than that which is given in the original intuition of space, and as if the determinations of it _a priori_ did not at the same time concern everything that is possible only in that it fills this space. If one gives ear to them, then besides the mathematical point, which is simple but no part, but rather merely the boundary of a space, one would have to think of physical points which, to be sure, are also simple, but have the advantage that, as parts of space, they can fill it through their mere aggregation. Without repeating here the common and clear refutations of this absurdity, which are met with in abundance, since it is indeed completely in vain to wish to ratiocinate away the evidence of mathematics through merely discursive concepts, I will only remark that, if philosophy {{ 469 }} here cavils with mathematics, this happens because it forgets that in this question we are dealing only with ***appearances*** and their condition. Here, however, it is not enough to find the concept of the simple for the pure ***concept of understanding*** of the composite, but rather to find the ***intuition*** of the simple for the ***intuition*** of the composite (matter); and this is entirely impossible according to the laws of sensibility, and consequently also with objects of the senses. It may therefore always hold of a whole of substances, which is thought merely through pure understanding, that we must have the simple prior to all composition of it; yet this does not hold of the _totum substantiale phaenomenon_, which, as an empirical intuition in space, carries with it the necessary property that no part of it is simple {{{ 307 }}}, because no part of space is simple. Nevertheless, the monadists have been clever enough to wish to escape this difficulty by not presupposing space as a condition of the possibility of objects of outer intuition (bodies), but rather presupposing these and the dynamical relation of substances in general as the condition of the possibility of space. Now we have a concept of bodies only as appearances, but as such they necessarily presuppose space as the condition of the possibility of all outer appearance; and the evasion is therefore in vain, as indeed it has already been sufficiently cut off above in the Transcendental Aesthetic. If they were things in themselves, then the proof of the monadists would indeed hold.

{{ 471 }} The second dialectical assertion has the peculiarity about it that it has a dogmatic assertion against it which, among all the ratiocinating ones, is the only one that undertakes to prove demonstratively, in an object of experience, the reality of what we above reckoned merely to transcendental ideas, namely the absolute simplicity of substance: namely, that the object of inner sense, the I that thinks, is an absolutely simple substance. Without entering upon this now (since it has been more fully considered above), I only remark: that if something is thought merely as an object, without adding any synthetic determination of its intuition (as indeed happens through the completely bare representation: I), then to be sure nothing manifold and no composition can be perceived in such a representation. Since, moreover, the predicates through which I think this object are merely intuitions of the inner sense, nothing can occur in them that would prove a manifold outside one another, and consequently real composition. Thus self-consciousness by itself brings it about that, because the subject that thinks is at the same time its own object, it cannot divide itself (although it can divide the determinations inhering in it); for with respect to itself every object is absolute unity. Nevertheless, if this subject is considered ***externally***, as an object of intuition, it would indeed exhibit composition in appearance in itself. But it must always be so considered if one wishes to know whether or not there is in it a manifold ***outside one another***.
