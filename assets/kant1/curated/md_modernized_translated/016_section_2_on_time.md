## {{{ 57 }}} {{ 46 }} Section 2. On Time

## § 4. Metaphysical Exposition of the Concept of Time.

Time, to begin with, is 1) not any empirical concept drawn in some manner out of an experience. For simultaneity, or a following of one upon another, would itself never enter into perception at all were the representation of time not laid down at its basis _a priori_. It is solely on the supposition of this representation that we can represent to ourselves: that some things are at one and the same time (simultaneously), or at different times (one after the other).

2) Time is a representation that is necessary and underlies every intuition whatever. One cannot, in respect of appearances generally, cancel time itself, although one may quite well withdraw the appearances out of time. Time, accordingly, is something given _a priori_. It is in {{{ 58 }}} time alone that any actuality of appearances becomes possible. The appearances may collectively drop away, yet time itself — being the universal condition of their possibility — admits of no cancellation.

3) {{ 47 }} This _a priori_ necessity is likewise the ground for the possibility of apodictic principles about time's relations, that is, of axioms of time as such. Time possesses but a single dimension: distinct times do not coexist but succeed each other (whereas distinct spaces, conversely, do not succeed each other but coexist). Such principles are not to be extracted from experience, for experience could furnish neither rigorous universality nor apodictic certainty. We could say no more than this: ordinary perception instructs us so; and never: thus it must necessarily be. These principles function as rules under which experiences are possible at all, and they teach us in advance of experience rather than by means of it.

4) Time is not a discursive concept — a general one, as it is termed — but rather a pure form belonging to sensible intuition. Distinct times amount to nothing but portions of that very same time. Now a representation that can be furnished only by way of one sole object is, however, an intuition. Furthermore, the proposition that distinct times cannot be simultaneous is one that no general concept would allow us to derive. This proposition is synthetic, and out of mere concepts it can never spring. It is, consequently, something directly comprised within the intuition and the representation of time.

5) The infinitude of time means nothing beyond this, that every determinate {{ 48 }} magnitude of time proves possible only by way of restrictions placed on a single underlying time. Hence the primordial representation ***time*** has to be given as something wholly unrestricted. But in any case where the parts themselves, together with every magnitude of an object, can be represented as determinate only through limitation, there the representation as a whole cannot be supplied by concepts (these, after all, carry only partial representations); rather, an immediate intuition has to underlie them.

## {{{ 59 }}} § 5. Transcendental Exposition of the Concept of Time.

I am therefore able to refer back to number 3, where, so as to be concise, I ranked whatever is genuinely transcendental among the items of the metaphysical exposition. To this I would append the following: the concept of alteration, and along with it the concept of motion (understood as alteration of place), can occur only through, and within, the representation of time; and were this representation not an inner intuition given _a priori_, then no concept of any kind whatsoever would be capable of rendering intelligible how an alteration is possible — an alteration being the uniting of contradictorily opposed predicates (for instance, the being at some place and the non-being of that very thing at the same place) in one and the selfsame object. Only within time is it possible for both {{ 49 }} contradictorily opposed determinations to be found together in a single thing — that is to say, one following upon ***another***. In this way our concept of time accounts for the possibility of just as much synthetic cognition _a priori_ as the general doctrine of motion — itself far from unfruitful — lays out.

## § 6. Inferences from these Concepts.

a) Time is nothing that could stand on its own account, nor anything clinging to things as an objective determination and so left behind once one has abstracted from every subjective condition under which they are intuited: for on the former alternative it would be a thing that, lacking any actual object, was nevertheless itself actual. With respect to the second alternative, on the other hand, if time were a determination or ordering inhering in the things themselves, it could not run ahead of those objects as the condition of them and be intuited and cognized _a priori_ by means of synthetic propositions. This last, by contrast, comes about perfectly well provided that time is merely the subjective condition whereby any intuiting can occur within us at all. For in such a case this form of inner intuition allows of being represented in advance of the objects, thus _a priori_.

b) Time is none other than the very form of inner sense — the form, that is, under which we intuit ourselves and our own inner condition. For {{{ 60 }}} {{ 50 }} time can constitute no determination of outer appearances: it has nothing to do with any shape or location and so forth; what it does fix, on the other hand, is the relation among representations within our inner condition. And precisely because this inner intuiting furnishes no shape, we endeavour to compensate for the deficiency by means of analogies, portraying the succession of time as a line prolonged without end, along which the manifold makes up a series of but one dimension, and we reason from that line's features to all the features of time — with a single exception, namely that the parts of the line exist alongside one another, whereas those of time invariably follow in sequence. This too makes plain that the representation of time is itself an intuition, seeing that the whole of its relations admits of being set forth in an outer intuition.

c) Time constitutes the formal condition, _a priori_, of every appearance whatever. Space, being the pure form of all outer intuition, stands as an _a priori_ condition confined solely to outer appearances. By contrast, since all representations — whether or not they take outer things for their object — do nevertheless, considered in themselves, belong as determinations of the mind to the inner condition, and since this inner condition falls in turn under the formal condition of inner intuition, and thus of time: time is accordingly an _a priori_ condition of every appearance whatsoever, namely the direct condition of inner appearances (of our souls) {{ 51 }} and, precisely thereby, the indirect condition of outer appearances also. If I am entitled to affirm _a priori_: all outer appearances are in space and stand determined _a priori_ by the relations of space, then, out of the principle of inner sense, I may affirm quite universally: all appearances whatsoever, i.e. every object of the senses, lie in time and necessarily enter into relations of time.

Should we abstract from our manner of inwardly intuiting ourselves, and — by the mediation of that intuition — of likewise gathering every outer intuition into the faculty of representation, and thus take objects just as they may be in their own right, then time turns out to be nothing at all. Its objective validity extends to appearances alone, for these are already things that we accept as ***objects of our senses***; but it forfeits its objectivity the moment one abstracts from the sensibility proper to our intuiting — that is, from the mode of representation peculiar to us — and speaks instead of ***things in general***. Time is thus no more than a subjective condition of our (human) intuition {{{ 61 }}} (an intuition that is invariably sensible, that is, so far as we find ourselves affected by objects), while in itself, taken apart from the subject, it is nothing whatever. For all that, with respect to every appearance — and hence to every thing that might present itself to us in experience — it is of necessity objective. We cannot say: all things are in time, since in the concept of {{ 52 }} things as such one leaves aside every mode of intuiting them, and yet it is exactly this mode that is the real condition under which time enters into the representation of objects. But once that condition is joined to the concept, so that the statement runs: all things as appearances (objects of sensible intuition) are in time, then the principle possesses its full objective correctness together with _a priori_ universality.

What we maintain, then, establishes the ***empirical reality*** of time — its objective validity, that is, in relation to whatever objects may at any time be presented to our senses. And because our intuiting is on every occasion sensible in kind, there can be presented to us in experience no object whatever that would fall outside time's condition. We contest, on the other hand, every claim of time to absolute reality — the claim, that is, that it would belong to things simply as such, as a condition or a property, even where no account is taken of the form of our sensible intuition. Properties of this sort, which pertain to things as they are in themselves, could in any case never reach us by way of the senses. It is in this that the ***transcendental ideality*** of time consists: on such a view, once one abstracts from the subjective conditions of sensible intuition, time amounts to nothing whatever and cannot be reckoned {{ 53 }} to the objects in themselves — apart, that is, from their relation to our intuition — either as something subsisting or as something inhering. Still, this ideality is no more to be set alongside the subreptions of sensation than is that of space, since in the latter case one does presuppose of the appearance itself — the appearance in which those predicates inhere — that it possesses an objective reality, whereas here such reality drops away completely, save insofar as it is merely empirical, that is, regards the object in question simply as appearance: on which point the foregoing remark of the earlier section may be consulted.

## § 7. Elucidation.

Against the present theory — which allows time an empirical reality but denies it any absolute and transcendental one — I have heard from perceptive {{{ 62 }}} men an objection voiced with such unanimity that I infer it must present itself, as a matter of course, to every reader for whom these reflections are unfamiliar. It goes as follows: alterations are actual (as is shown by the succession of changes among our own representations, even supposing one wished to deny all outer appearances along with their alterations). But since alterations can take place only in time, it follows that time is something actual. There is no difficulty in answering this. I concede the argument in its entirety. Time is indeed actual — it is, namely, the actual form belonging to inner intuition. It possesses, accordingly, a subjective reality as regards inner experience: that is, I genuinely do have a representation of {{ 54 }} time and of my determinations within it. It is thus to be viewed as actual not in the character of an object, but as the manner in which I represent myself as an object. Yet if I myself, or else some other being, were able to intuit me without this condition of sensibility, then those selfsame determinations that we now represent to ourselves as alterations would deliver a cognition wherein no representation of time — and consequently none of alteration either — made any appearance whatever. What remains, then, is its empirical reality, standing as the condition of all our experiences. It is only absolute reality that, in light of what was set out above, cannot be granted to it. It amounts to nothing beyond the form belonging to our inner intuition.[^*] Take away from it the particular condition constituting our sensibility, and the concept of time vanishes along with it; time clings not to the objects themselves but purely to the subject that does the intuiting.

The reason, however, why this objection is raised so unanimously — and that by the very people who, for their part, can find nothing cogent to urge against the doctrine of the ideality {{ 55 }} of space — is the following. They held out no hope of demonstrating the absolute reality of space with apodictic force, for they are met by idealism, on whose showing the actuality of outer objects is susceptible of no rigorous proof: the actuality, by contrast, of the object of our inner sense (of myself and of my state) stands immediately clear through consciousness. The former might be a bare semblance, whereas the latter, on their view, is beyond denial something actual. What they overlooked, though, is that the two of them — however little one may contest their actuality {{{ 63 }}} as representations — belong all the same solely to appearance, and appearance always presents two aspects: the one on which the object is regarded in itself (leaving aside the way in which it is intuited, whose constitution for just that reason stays perpetually problematic), the other on which one looks to the form of the intuition of this object, a form to be sought not in the object taken in itself but in the subject to whom that object appears, yet one that genuinely and necessarily attaches to this object's appearance.

Time and space, then, are two wellsprings of cognition out of which a variety of synthetic cognitions can be drawn _a priori_ — as pure mathematics, above all, supplies a shining example in the case of the cognitions concerning {{ 56 }} space and its relations. For the two of them, taken jointly, are the pure forms of all sensible intuition, and it is on that basis that they render synthetic propositions _a priori_ possible. These _a priori_ sources of cognition, however, mark out their own limits by that very circumstance (that they are nothing but conditions of sensibility), the limit being that they bear only upon objects insofar as these are taken as appearances and do not exhibit things as they are in themselves. That domain alone forms the field within which they hold; step beyond it, and no further objective use of them can be made. This reality of space and of time, for that matter, leaves the reliability of experiential cognition wholly intact: for we remain equally sure of it whether these forms attach of necessity to the things in themselves or merely to our intuiting of those things. By contrast, whoever affirms the absolute reality of space and time — taking them, that is, either as subsisting or merely as inhering — is bound to fall into disagreement with the very principles of experience. For should they come down on the side of the first alternative (usually the camp of the mathematical investigators of nature), they are then obliged to posit two eternal, infinite, self-standing non-things (space and time), which are there (although nothing actual answers to them) with no purpose but to hold within themselves everything that is actual. Take up the second party instead (to which certain metaphysical students of nature belong), on which space and time pass for relations of appearances (side by side or one after the other) abstracted from experience {{ 57 }} though, once set apart, confusedly represented: in that case they are forced to deny to the mathematical doctrines _a priori_ their validity — or at any rate their apodictic certainty — as concerns actual things (in space, for example), inasmuch as no such certainty is to be had _a posteriori_, and on this opinion the _a priori_ concepts of {{{ 64 }}} space and time are mere fabrications of the imagination, whose source has genuinely to be looked for in experience, out of whose abstracted relations imagination has fashioned something that does contain what is universal in them, yet cannot subsist without the restrictions that nature has tied to them. The first group gains this much, that they clear the field of appearances on behalf of their mathematical claims. Yet by these same conditions they land themselves in great confusion whenever the understanding seeks to pass beyond that field. The second group, for its part, does gain in respect of this last point, namely that the representations of space and time put no obstacle in their path when they mean to judge of objects not as appearances but purely in their relation to the understanding; but they can neither assign any ground for the possibility of mathematical cognitions _a priori_ (lacking, as they do, a genuine and objectively valid intuition _a priori_) nor bring the propositions of experience into the requisite accord {{ 58 }} with those assertions. On our own account of the genuine nature of these two primitive forms of sensibility, both difficulties are set to rest.

That, in the end, the transcendental aesthetic can hold no more than these two elements — space and time — becomes evident from the consideration that all the remaining concepts pertaining to sensibility, even the concept of motion, which brings the two of them together, take something empirical for granted. For motion presupposes that something movable be perceived. But within space, regarded on its own, nothing movable is to be met with: hence what is movable has to be a thing discoverable ***in space only through experience***, and so an empirical datum. In the same way the transcendental aesthetic is unable to number the concept of alteration among its data _a priori_: for it is not time itself that alters, but rather something situated in time. What this calls for, accordingly, is the perception of some existence together with the succession of its determinations — in short, experience.

[^*]: True, I can say: my representations follow upon one another; but this signifies no more than that we are aware of them as standing in a temporal sequence, that is, in accordance with the form of inner sense. Time is not, on that ground, anything in itself, nor is it a determination that attaches to things objectively.
