## Section 4. The discipline of pure reason in respect of its proofs

What sets the proofs of transcendental and synthetic propositions apart from all other proofs of a synthetic cognition _a priori_ is the following peculiarity: in them reason is not permitted to address the object outright through its concepts, but has first to demonstrate _a priori_ both that the concepts are objectively valid and that their synthesis is possible. This is no mere rule of prudence that one happens to need; it goes to the very essence of the proofs and to whether they are possible at all. Should I have to pass beyond the concept of an object _a priori_, that cannot be done without a particular clue that lies {{{ 510 }}} outside the concept in question. In mathematics this guiding clue is _a priori_ intuition, which steers my synthesis, so that every inference {{ 811 }} may be drawn there straight from pure intuition. Within transcendental cognition, as long as nothing but concepts of the understanding is at stake, the standard that serves this purpose is possible experience. The proof, that is, does not demonstrate that a given concept (say, of what happens) points straightaway to a further concept (that of a cause)—for a transition of that sort would be an unwarrantable leap—but demonstrates instead that experience, and with it the object of experience, could not exist at all were such a linkage absent. So the proof had, in one and the same move, to make evident that one can reach synthetically and _a priori_ a certain cognition of things which lay nowhere in the concept one had of them. Lacking such vigilance, proofs go careering off wildly across open country, like a flood that has broken its banks, driven to whatever point the slope of some concealed association chances to carry them. A show of conviction resting on association's subjective causes, and mistaken for insight into a natural affinity, can in no way balance out the reservations that must, in all fairness, arise in the face of such hazardous steps. This is why every effort to prove the principle of sufficient reason has, as the connoisseurs generally concede, come to nothing; and until transcendental critique arrived on the scene, thinkers—unable, after all, to give the principle up—chose rather to fall back defiantly on sound common sense (a recourse that is invariably a sign that reason's cause {{ 812 }} is lost) than to hazard fresh dogmatic proofs.

If, however, the proposition to be proved happens to be a claim of pure reason, and if I mean, through mere ideas no less, to venture past my empirical concepts, then that proof would need, still more urgently, to carry within it the vindication of such a synthetic step (assuming one were possible) as an indispensable condition of its power to prove anything. So, no matter how convincing the alleged proof of the simplicity of our thinking substance may look when drawn from the unity of apperception, one reservation blocks it irremovably: since absolute simplicity is, after all, not a concept that could be tied straight to a perception but has to be reached merely as an inferred idea, one cannot in the least see how the bare consciousness which is—or can at any rate {{{ 511 }}} be—contained ***in all thinking*** (granting that, to that degree, it is a simple representation) is supposed to deliver me over to the consciousness and cognizance of a thing ***in which*** thought alone can reside. For when I picture to myself my body's force in motion, then, so far as that goes, it is an absolute unity for me, and my representation of it is simple; and this lets me express it, too, by the movement of a single point, since here its volume contributes nothing and, with no loss of force, may be conceived as tiny as one pleases—hence as seated in a point as well. {{ 813 }} Even so, I shall not draw from this the conclusion that a body, when nothing but its moving force is given to me, may be conceived as a simple substance merely because the representation of it leaves aside every magnitude of spatial content and is on that account simple. And it is just here—in the fact that the simple met with in abstraction differs completely from the simple met with in the object, and that the I, which taken the first way holds no manifold at all ***within itself***, taken the second way, where it stands for the soul itself, may prove a highly composite concept, one that holds and marks out a great many things ***under itself***—that I catch sight of a paralogism. But to sense this in advance (for absent some such preliminary surmise nobody would ever grow suspicious of the proof), it is indispensable to keep constantly at hand a criterion for whether synthetic propositions of this kind—ones meant to prove more than experience is able to yield—are so much as possible: a criterion that amounts to this, that the proof proceed not straight to the predicate one is after, but solely by way of a principle for expanding our given concept _a priori_ up to ideas and giving those ideas reality. Where this caution is unfailingly observed, where, prior to any attempt at the proof, one first prudently deliberates with oneself about how, and on what grounds of hope, such an expansion by pure reason might be looked for, and from what source, in a case of this kind, one proposes to fetch insights that admit neither of being unfolded from concepts {{ 814 }} nor of being anticipated with reference to possible experience—there one may be spared a great deal of laborious yet barren toil, since one then asks of reason nothing that manifestly outruns its power, or rather brings reason, which in the grip of its speculative itch for expansion submits reluctantly to any limit, under the discipline of restraint.

The ***first*** rule, accordingly, runs thus: undertake no transcendental proofs until you have thought through, and given an account to yourself of, the source from which {{{ 512 }}} you propose to draw the principles you plan to raise them upon, and the right by which you may look to those principles for a sound outcome of your inferences. Where they are principles of the understanding (causality, say), it is pointless to reach ideas of pure reason by their means, for such principles are valid only of objects of a possible experience. Where they are meant to be principles drawn from pure reason, once again the whole effort is wasted. Reason does possess principles of that kind, true enough, but taken as objective principles they are dialectical throughout, and can hold good, if at all, only in the manner of regulative principles governing the systematically connected empirical use. Yet where supposed proofs of this description are already before you, meet the fraudulent conviction with the _non liquet_ of your seasoned power of judgment; and {{ 815 }} even if you cannot as yet see through the deception it works, you are still fully entitled to require a deduction of the principles it puts to use—a deduction that can never be supplied to you if those principles are alleged to issue from pure reason alone. And so you are not even obliged to trouble yourselves with unfolding and rebutting every baseless show one by one; you may instead throw out, in whole heaps and all at once, the entire dialectic, inexhaustible in its tricks, at the bar of a critical reason that insists on laws.

The ***second*** distinctive trait of transcendental proofs is this: for every transcendental proposition there is but a ***single*** proof to be had. If my inference is to proceed not from concepts but from the intuition answering to a concept—whether a pure intuition, as in mathematics, or an empirical one, as in natural science—then the intuition put at the basis furnishes me with a manifold of material for synthetic propositions that I can combine in more than one fashion and, being free to begin from more than one starting point, can reach the same proposition along several paths.

Now any transcendental proposition whatever sets out from one concept only, and pronounces the synthetic condition under which the object is possible according to that concept. There can accordingly be no more than one ground of proof, {{ 816 }} for apart from this concept nothing else is present through which the object might be determined, so that the proof can hold nothing beyond the determining of an object as such according to this concept—itself, again, only one. Thus in the transcendental analytic we derived the principle that whatever happens has a cause from {{{ 513 }}} the one and only condition of the objective possibility of a concept of what happens at all, namely: that an event's determination in time—and hence that event, as something belonging to experience—could not be, unless it were placed under a dynamical rule of this kind. And this, too, is the sole ground of proof there can be; for the represented event acquires objective validity—that is, truth—only in that, through the causal law, an object comes to be determined for the concept. Other proofs of the principle have of course been tried—one from contingency, for instance—but examine that one in broad daylight and no token of contingency turns up except ***happening*** itself, i.e., an existence ahead of which there runs a non-being of the object, so that one lands right back at the identical ground of proof. When the proposition to be established is that whatever thinks is simple, one does not linger over the manifold in thinking, but keeps solely to the concept of the I, itself a simple concept and the one back to which all thought is traced. The transcendental proof of God's existence stands on just the same footing: it turns solely on the reciprocability of the concepts of the most real {{ 817 }} and the necessary being, and admits of being looked for in no other quarter.

Through this cautionary observation, the critique of reason's assertions is narrowed down to very small compass. Wherever reason transacts its affairs by way of bare concepts, one proof and one alone is possible—if, that is, any proof is possible at all. So, once you observe the dogmatist stepping forward with ten proofs in hand, you may confidently conclude that he has not a single one. For, possessed of one that demonstrated apodictically—as, in matters of pure reason, it is bound to—what use would he have for the others? His purpose is nothing but that of the well-known barrister before parliament: this argument suits the one man, that argument the next—the object being to turn the frailty of his judges to account, judges who, rather than enter deeply into things and eager to have done with the affair, snatch at whatever first catches their eye and rule on that basis.

The ***third*** rule peculiar to pure reason, once it is placed under a discipline touching transcendental proofs, is that its proofs may at no time be ***apagogic*** but must always be ***ostensive***. In every sort of cognition the direct or ostensive proof is the one that unites conviction of the truth with an insight into where that truth springs from; the apagogic proof, by contrast, {{{ 514 }}} is able to yield certainty, yet not an understanding of the truth as regards how it hangs together with the grounds that make it possible {{ 818 }}. The apagogic sort are therefore more of a stopgap than a procedure that meets all of reason's purposes. Still, they hold one edge in self-evidence over the direct proofs: a contradiction always brings more clarity to the representation than the finest connection can, and thereby draws nearer to the intuitiveness of a demonstration.

The genuine reason for resorting to apagogic proofs across the various sciences is presumably the following. When the grounds from which some cognition is to be derived lie either too various or too deeply buried, one puts it to the test whether the cognition might not be gotten at by its consequences instead. Now the _modus ponens_—arriving at a cognition's truth from the truth of its consequences—would be legitimate only where every possible consequence of it is true; for then, and only then, is a single ground possible for those consequences, and that ground is the true one. This way of proceeding, however, is not feasible, since surveying every possible consequence of some assumed proposition surpasses our powers; and yet one does resort to this manner of concluding—albeit, to be sure, with a certain leniency—whenever the task is only to establish something as a hypothesis, conceding the inference by analogy that if as many consequences as one has so far tried square nicely with an assumed ground, all the rest that are possible will fall into line with it as well. That is precisely why no hypothesis can ever be turned, {{ 819 }} along this route, into demonstrated truth. The _modus tollens_ of rational inferences, which conclude from the consequences back to the grounds, proves not only with entire stringency but also with the greatest of ease. For should even one false consequence be derivable from a proposition, that proposition is thereby false. Rather than travel the entire chain of grounds within an ostensive proof—the route that can bring one to a cognition's truth by a thorough insight into how it is possible—one has merely to detect a single false consequence among those issuing from its contrary, whereupon that contrary is false as well, and the cognition one set out to prove is accordingly true.

The apagogic manner of proof, however, is admissible only in those sciences where it is out of the question, in the place of the objective—namely of the cognition of what belongs to the object— {{{ 515 }}} ***to foist*** the subjective side of our representations. But where that objective side has the upper hand, it must happen again and again that the contrary of a given proposition either clashes merely with the subjective conditions of thinking and not with the object, or else that the two propositions clash only under a subjective condition wrongly held to be objective, so that, the condition being false, both may be false together, with no warrant for concluding from the falsehood of the one to the truth of the other.

{{ 820 }} In mathematics such a subreption cannot occur, which is why apagogic proofs find their proper home precisely there. In natural science, where everything rests on empirical intuitions, that same act of surreptition can for the most part be forestalled by comparing many observations; but even there this kind of proof is, on the whole, of slight moment. The transcendental undertakings of pure reason, however, are without exception carried out inside the very element of dialectical semblance—that is, inside the subjective, which presents itself to reason among its premises as objective, or indeed presses itself on reason as such. Here, then, where synthetic propositions are concerned, one is by no means at liberty to make good one's assertions by disproving the contrary. For ***either*** this disproof amounts to nothing more than exhibiting the clash of the opposing opinion with the subjective conditions under which our reason can comprehend anything—which does nothing whatever to warrant discarding the matter itself on that score (much as, for example, the unconditioned necessity in a being's existence simply cannot be grasped by us, and hence ***subjectively*** sets itself, and rightly so, against any speculative proof of a necessary highest being, though it sets itself ***in itself*** and wrongly against the very possibility of such an original being); or else both sides, the affirming party and the denying alike, misled by the transcendental semblance, place beneath their case an impossible concept of the object, and here the rule obtains: _non entis nulla_ {{ 821 }} _sunt praedicata_—that is, whatever was asserted of the object, be it affirmed or denied, is in both cases wrong, and there is no arriving apagogically at cognition of the truth by refuting the contrary. Take, for instance, the supposition that the world of sense is given ***in itself*** in its totality: it is then false that this world must be either infinite in respect of space or else finite and bounded—false, that is, for the reason that each alternative is false. For appearances (being mere representations) that were nonetheless supposed to be given ***in themselves*** (as objects) are {{{ 516 }}} something impossible, and the infinitude of this fancied whole, though it would indeed be unconditioned, would nevertheless run counter (since in appearances everything is conditioned) to that unconditioned determination of magnitude which the concept for its part presupposes.

The apagogic manner of proof is, besides, the very piece of trickery by which the admirers of our dogmatic reasoners' rigor have always been strung along: it plays, so to speak, the part of the champion who sets out to establish the honor and the unquestionable right of the side he has espoused by undertaking to come to blows with anyone who should care to call it in question—though bluster of this sort settles nothing about the issue itself, only about the relative strength of the combatants, and even that only on the side of whoever is on the attack. The onlookers, {{ 822 }} watching how each man is by turns victorious and defeated in his round, frequently find in this a reason to grow skeptically doubtful of the very object under dispute. They have, however, no ground for it, and one need only cry to them: _non defensoribus istis tempus eget_. Each party has to plead its cause by a rightful proof conducted through a transcendental deduction of the grounds of proof, that is, directly, so that it may be seen what its claims on behalf of reason can urge in their own support. For if his adversary props himself on subjective grounds, he is admittedly refuted with ease, yet to no profit for the dogmatist, who as a rule leans just as heavily on the subjective causes of judgment and can be cornered by his adversary in exactly the same way. But let both parties proceed by direct means alone, and they will either come of their own accord to notice the difficulty—indeed the impossibility—of tracing out the title to their assertions, and be left in the end with nothing but an appeal to long possession, or else critique will readily lay bare the dogmatic semblance and force pure reason to abandon the pretensions it has pitched too high in speculative use and to fall back within the confines of its own proper ground, namely practical principles.
