## {{{ 314 }}} {{ 480 }} Of the Antinomy of Pure Reason

## Fourth Conflict of the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

There belongs to the world something which, either as its part or as its cause, is an absolutely necessary being.

## Proof.

The world of sense, as the sum total of all appearances, contains at the same time a series of alterations. For without these, even the representation of the series of time as a condition of the possibility of the world of sense would not be given to us.[^*] But every alteration stands under its condition, which precedes it in time, and under which it is necessary. Now every conditioned that is given presupposes, in respect of its existence, a complete series of conditions up to the absolutely unconditioned, which alone is absolutely necessary. Therefore something absolutely necessary must exist, if an alteration exists as its consequence. But this necessary being itself belongs to the world of sense. For assume that it exists outside of it, then the series of world alterations would {{ 482 }} derive its beginning from it, without this necessary cause itself belonging to the world of sense. Now this is impossible. For since the beginning of a time-series can be determined only by that which precedes in time, the supreme condition of the beginning of a series of alterations must exist in time when this series did not yet exist (for the beginning is an existence prior to which a time precedes in which the thing that begins was not yet). Therefore, the causality of the necessary cause of alterations, and consequently the cause itself, belongs to time, and therewith to appearance (in which alone time is possible as its form); consequently, it cannot be thought as separate from the {{{ 316 }}} world of sense as the sum total of all appearances. Therefore, in the world itself something absolutely necessary is contained (whether this be the whole world-series itself, or a part of it).

## {{{ 315 }}} {{ 481 }} Antithesis.

There exists nowhere an absolutely necessary being, either in the world or outside the world as its cause.

## Proof.

Assume that the world itself is a necessary being, or that there is such a being in it, then in the series of its alterations there would either be a beginning that is unconditioned-necessary, and therefore without a cause, which contradicts the dynamical law of the determination of all appearances in time; or the series itself would be without any beginning, and, although contingent and conditioned in all its parts, yet as a whole absolutely necessary and unconditioned; which contradicts itself, because the existence of a multitude cannot be necessary if no single part of it possesses an in-itself necessary existence.

Assume, on the contrary, that there is an absolutely necessary world-cause {{ 483 }} outside the world, then this cause, as the supreme member in the ***series of the causes*** of world alterations, would first begin the existence of the latter and their series.[^**] But in that case it would also have to begin to act, and its causality would belong to time, and therefore to the sum total of appearances, i.e., to the world, consequently the cause {{{ 317 }}} itself would not be outside the world, which contradicts the presupposition. Therefore, there is no absolutely necessary being anywhere, either in the world or outside of it (yet in causal connection with it).

## {{ 484 }} Observation on the Fourth Antinomy.

## I. On the Thesis.

In order to prove the existence of a necessary being, I am here obligated to use no other than the ***cosmological*** argument, which ascends namely from the conditioned in appearance to the unconditioned in concept, by regarding the latter as the necessary condition of the absolute totality of the series. To attempt the proof from the mere idea of a supreme being in general belongs to another principle of reason, and such a proof will therefore have to be presented separately.

The pure cosmological proof can establish the existence of a necessary being only by leaving it at the same time undecided whether this being is the world itself or a thing distinct from it. For in order to determine the latter, principles are required which are no longer cosmological and do not proceed in the series of appearances, but rather concepts of contingent beings in general (in so far as they are considered merely as objects of the understanding) and a principle of connecting these with a necessary being through mere concepts, all of which belongs to a ***transcendent*** philosophy, for which this is not yet the place.

But if one once begins the proof cosmologically, by laying as a foundation the series of appearances and the regressus in it according to empirical laws of causality, then one cannot afterwards leap away from this and pass over to something that does not belong in {{ 486 }} the series at all as a member. For {{{ 318 }}} something must be regarded as a condition in the very same meaning in which the relation of the conditioned to its condition in the series was taken, which relation was to lead to this highest condition in continuous progress. If this relation is sensible and belongs to the possible empirical use of the understanding, then the supreme condition or cause can close the regressus only according to laws of sensibility, and therefore only as belonging to the time-series, and the necessary being must be regarded as the supreme member of the world-series.

Nevertheless, people have taken the liberty of making such a leap (μετάβασις εἰς ἄλλο γένος). Namely, from the alterations in the world, they inferred their empirical contingency, i.e., their dependence on empirically determining causes, and obtained an ascending series of empirical conditions, which was indeed quite correct. But since they could find in this series no first beginning and no supreme member, they suddenly departed from the empirical concept of contingency and took the pure category, which then gave rise to a merely intelligible series, the completeness of which rested on the existence of an absolutely necessary cause, which now, since it was bound by no sensible conditions, was also freed from the time-condition of beginning its causality itself. This procedure is, however, entirely illegitimate, as may be inferred from what follows.

Contingent in the pure sense of the category is that whose contradictory opposite is possible. Now, from empirical contingency one cannot infer that intelligible contingency at all. What is altered, {{ 488 }} the opposite of its state is real at another time, and therefore also possible; consequently, this is not the contradictory opposite of the previous state, for which it would be required that at the very same time when the previous state existed, its opposite could have been in its place, which can in no way be inferred from alteration. A body which was in motion = _A_, comes to rest = _non A_. Now, from the fact that a state opposite to state A {{{ 320 }}} follows upon it, it cannot at all be inferred that the contradictory opposite of A is possible, and consequently that A is contingent; for to show that, it would be required that at the very same time when the motion existed, rest could have existed in its place. Now we know nothing further than that rest was real in the succeeding time, and therefore also possible. But motion at one time and rest at another time are not contradictorily opposed to each other. Therefore, the succession of opposite determinations, i.e., alteration, in no way proves contingency according to concepts of pure understanding, and consequently cannot lead to the existence of a necessary being according to pure concepts of the understanding. Alteration proves only empirical contingency, i.e., that the new state could not of itself have taken place at all, without a cause belonging to the preceding time, in accordance with the law of causality. This cause, even if it is assumed to be absolutely necessary, must in this way nevertheless be found in time, and must belong to the series of appearances.

## {{ 485 }} II. On the Antithesis.

If, in ascending the series of appearances, one thinks one meets with difficulties against the existence of an absolutely necessary supreme cause, these difficulties must not be based upon mere concepts of the necessary existence of a thing in general, and must therefore not be ontological; rather, they must arise out of the causal connection with a series of appearances, in order to assume for it a condition which is itself unconditioned, and must consequently be cosmological and inferred according to empirical laws. Specifically, it must be shown that the ascent in the series of causes (in the world of sense) can never end at an empirically unconditioned condition, and that the cosmological argument from the contingency of world-states according to their alterations turns out to be against the assumption of a first cause that absolutely begins the series.

{{ 487 }} But a strange contrast shows itself in this antinomy: namely, that from the very same ground of proof from which the existence {{{ 319 }}} of a primary being was inferred in the thesis, its non-existence is inferred in the antithesis, and indeed with the same sharpness. First it was said: ***there is a necessary being***, because the whole of past time contains in itself the series of all conditions, and therewith therefore also the unconditioned (necessary). Now it is said: ***there is no necessary being***, precisely because the whole of elapsed time contains in itself the series of all conditions (which consequently are one and all in turn conditioned). The cause of this is as follows. The first argument looks only to the ***absolute totality*** of the series of conditions, of which one determines the other in time, and thereby obtains an unconditioned and necessary being. The second argument, on the contrary, takes into consideration the ***contingency*** of everything that is determined in the ***time-series*** (because prior to each a time precedes, in which the condition itself must again be determined as conditioned), {{ 489 }} whereby all unconditionedness and all absolute necessity entirely disappear. Meanwhile, the mode of inference in both is quite in accordance with common human reason, which frequently falls into the position of disagreeing with itself after considering its object from two different standpoints. M. de Mairan regarded the controversy between two famous astronomers, which arose from a similar difficulty concerning the choice of standpoint, as a sufficiently remarkable {{{ 321 }}} phenomenon to write a special treatise upon it. For the one inferred thus: ***the moon rotates on its axis***, because it constantly turns the same side to the earth; the other: ***the moon does not rotate on its axis***, precisely because it constantly turns the same side to the earth. Both inferences were correct, according to the standpoint from which one chose to observe the motion of the moon.

[^*]: Time indeed, as a formal condition of the possibility of alterations, objectively precedes them; yet subjectively and in the reality of consciousness, this representation is nevertheless given, like any other, only through the occasion of perceptions.

[^**]: The word: beginning, is taken in a twofold meaning. The first is ***active***, when the cause begins a series of states as its effect (infit); the second is ***passive***, when the causality in the cause itself commences (fit). I here infer from the former to the latter.
