## {{{ 314 }}} {{ 480 }} The Antinomy of Pure Reason

## Fourth Conflict of the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

To the world there belongs something that, either as its part or as its cause, is an absolutely necessary being.

## Proof.

The world of sense, being the whole of all appearances, likewise harbors within itself a series of alterations. For, lacking these, we should not so much as be given the representation of the time-series that serves as a condition for the possibility of the world of sense.[^*] Every alteration, however, stands beneath a condition that goes before it in time and renders it necessary. Now, in respect of its existence, whatever is given as conditioned presupposes a complete series of conditions reaching up to the absolutely unconditioned, and this alone is absolutely necessary. Hence, should an alteration exist as its consequence, something absolutely necessary must exist as well. This necessary thing, though, belongs to the world of sense itself. For assume it to lie outside that world: the series of the world's alterations {{ 482 }} would then take its beginning from it, and yet this necessary cause would not itself belong to the world of sense. Yet this is impossible. For a beginning within a time-series can be fixed only by that which precedes it temporally, and so the highest condition for the beginning of a series of alterations must be present at the time in which that series did not yet exist (since a beginning is an existence preceded by a time throughout which the thing that begins was not yet there). Accordingly, the causality belonging to the necessary cause of these alterations — and thus the cause itself as well — pertains to time, and thereby to appearance (wherein alone time is possible, and that as its form); it therefore cannot be conceived in separation from the {{{ 316 }}} world of sense, that is, the sum total of all appearances. There is thus contained in the world itself something absolutely necessary (be it the entire world-series as such, or merely a portion of it).

## {{{ 315 }}} {{ 481 }} Antithesis.

Nowhere does there exist an absolutely necessary being — neither within the world, nor beyond it as its cause.

## Proof.

Assume that the world itself, or something within it, is a necessary being: then, in the series of its alterations, there would be present either a beginning that, being unconditionally necessary, would lack any cause — which runs counter to the dynamical law by which all appearances are determined in time — or else a series that, itself beginningless and, notwithstanding the contingency and conditionedness of each of its parts, is taken as a whole to be absolutely necessary and unconditioned; and this last is self-contradictory, since no multitude can have a necessary existence when not one of its parts possesses an existence necessary in its own right.

Assume, conversely, that there is an absolutely necessary world-cause {{ 483 }} outside the world: this, as the highest member in the ***series of causes*** of the world's alterations, would be what first inaugurates the existence of the latter along with their series.[^**] But then it too would have to commence acting, so that its causality would fall within time and, for that very reason, within the sum total of appearances — that is, within the world — with the result that it itself, {{{ 317 }}} the cause, would not lie outside the world after all, in contradiction to the presupposition. Accordingly, no absolutely necessary being exists anywhere — neither within the world nor beyond it (albeit bound to it in causal connection).

## {{ 484 }} Remark on the Fourth Antinomy.

## I. On the Thesis.

For the purpose of proving that a necessary being exists, my task here is to employ no argument other than the ***cosmological*** one, which ascends from the conditioned within appearance to the unconditioned within the concept, treating the latter as the condition necessarily required for the absolute totality of the series. Attempting the proof from the bare idea of a highest being among all beings whatsoever pertains to a different principle of reason, and one of that kind will accordingly need separate treatment.

Now the pure cosmological proof can demonstrate the existence of a necessary being in no other way than by leaving it simultaneously unsettled whether that being is the world as such or something distinct from it. For to make out the latter, one needs principles that no longer count as cosmological and that fail to advance along the series of appearances, but rather concepts of contingent beings as such (so far as these are weighed merely as objects of the understanding), together with a principle for linking such beings to a necessary being by means of concepts alone — all of which is the business of a ***transcendent*** philosophy, for which there is as yet no room here.

But once the proof has been launched cosmologically — by placing beneath it, as its ground, the series of appearances together with the regress internal to that series, and this in keeping with empirical causal laws — one cannot thereafter leap off from this and cross over to something that belongs in no way in {{ 486 }} the series as one of its members. For in precisely that same sense must {{{ 318 }}} a thing be regarded as a condition, wherein the relation of the conditioned to its own condition was taken up within the series — that series which was meant to lead onward, in continuous advance, to this highest condition. Now if this relation is a sensible one and falls within the possible empirical employment of the understanding, then the highest condition or cause can bring the regress to a close only by laws of sensibility — hence only as something belonging to the temporal series — and the necessary being has to be viewed as the highest member of the world-series.

All the same, the liberty has been taken of executing just such a leap (μετάβασις εἰς ἄλλο γένος). For from the alterations in the world one concluded to empirical contingency — that is, to their dependence upon empirically determining causes — and thereby arrived at a series of empirical conditions that mounts upward, which was, moreover, quite correct. But since no first beginning and no highest member could be found here, one abruptly quit the empirical concept of contingency and reached for the pure category, which then set going a merely intelligible series whose completeness rested upon there being an absolutely necessary cause — a cause that, now bound to no sensible conditions, was likewise released from the temporal condition of having itself to begin its causality. This procedure, however, is wholly illegitimate, as the following considerations make clear.

In the pure sense of the category, contingent is whatever admits of its contradictory opposite as possible. But from empirical contingency there is simply no inferring that intelligible one. In the case of what undergoes alteration, {{ 488 }} the opposite (of its state) is actual at some other time and therefore possible as well; and so this is not the contradictory opposite of the earlier state, which would demand that, in the very same time at which the earlier state obtained, its opposite could have stood in its place — something that can by no means be inferred from the alteration. A body that was moving = _A_ passes into rest = _non A_. Now from the circumstance that upon this {{{ 320 }}} there ensues a state opposed to state A, nothing at all can be concluded to the effect that A's contradictory opposite is possible, and thus that A is something contingent; for that would demand that, at the very same time the motion occurred, rest could instead have taken its place. All we know, at this point, is that rest was actual in the ensuing time and therefore possible as well. Motion at one time, however, and rest at another are by no means contradictorily opposed to each other. The succession of opposed determinations, then — that is, alteration — in no way proves contingency by concepts of the pure understanding, and it is therefore likewise incapable of leading to a necessary being's existence by pure concepts of the understanding. Alteration establishes only empirical contingency — that is, the fact that, by the law of causality, the new state could not possibly have occurred of itself, absent a cause belonging to the preceding time. Such a cause — even granting that it be taken as absolutely necessary — must in this manner still be met with in time and reckoned to the series of appearances.

## {{ 485 }} II. On the Antithesis.

Should one fancy that, in mounting upward through the series of appearances, one runs into difficulties telling against the existence of an absolutely necessary supreme cause, then these too must not be grounded upon bare concepts concerning the necessary existence of some thing in general — and hence must not be ontological — but must rather emerge out of the causal linkage to a series of appearances (so as to posit for that series a condition that is itself unconditioned), and must accordingly be inferred cosmologically and by empirical laws. It has, namely, to be shown that the ascent in the series of causes (within the world of sense) can never terminate in an empirically unconditioned condition, and that the cosmological argument — drawn from the contingency of the world's states as manifested in their alterations — comes out against admitting a first cause that absolutely inaugurates the series.

{{ 487 }} Yet a curious contrast comes to light in this antinomy: namely, that from precisely the same ground of proof by which the existence {{{ 319 }}} of an original being was concluded in the thesis, the non-being of that being is concluded in the antithesis, and with no less stringency. At the outset it ran: ***a necessary being exists***, on the ground that the whole of past time embraces the series of all conditions and therewith the unconditioned (the necessary) as well. Now it runs: ***no necessary being exists***, and for that very reason — because the whole of elapsed time embraces the series of all conditions (which one and all are consequently, in their turn, conditioned). The ground of this is as follows. The first argument attends solely to the ***absolute totality*** of the series of the conditions, of which each determines another in time, and thereby comes to possess something unconditioned and necessary. The second, by contrast, brings under review the ***contingency*** of all that stands determined within the ***time-series*** (since before each there lies a time throughout which the condition must, in its turn, be determined as itself conditioned), {{ 489 }} whereby every unconditioned and all absolute necessity drops away completely. Meanwhile, the manner of inference in each is thoroughly suited even to ordinary human reason, which not infrequently comes to be at odds with itself once it weighs its object from two distinct standpoints. Herr von Mairan considered the quarrel between two celebrated astronomers, which sprang from a similar difficulty over the choice of standpoint, a sufficiently curious {{{ 321 }}} phenomenon to make it the subject of a treatise of its own. The one reasoned thus: ***the moon revolves about its axis***, for the reason that it perpetually presents the same face to the earth; the other: ***the moon does not revolve about its axis***, and for that very reason, that it perpetually presents the same face to the earth. Both conclusions were sound, according to the standpoint one adopted from which one wished to observe the moon's motion.

[^*]: Time, indeed, as the formal condition for the possibility of alterations, goes objectively before them; yet subjectively, and within the actuality of consciousness, this representation is, no differently than any other, supplied only on the prompting of perceptions.

[^**]: The word "to begin" is employed in a twofold signification. The one is ***active***, where the cause begins a series of states as its effect (infit); the other ***passive***, where the causality within the cause itself sets in (fit). From the former I here draw my inference to the latter.
