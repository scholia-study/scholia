## Section 1. On the ultimate end of the pure use of our reason

A propensity in its very nature drives reason to press beyond its use in experience, to hazard—in a pure use, and by way of mere ideas—an advance to the utmost limits of all cognition, and to come to rest only once its circuit is complete, in a systematic whole subsisting on its own. Now, is this striving founded merely upon reason's speculative interest, or instead wholly and only upon its practical one?

I shall leave aside for now the fortune that pure reason enjoys in its speculative undertaking, and inquire solely into those problems whose resolution constitutes its ultimate end—reach it or not—and beside which every other problem carries only the worth of a means. By the very nature of reason, these {{ 826 }} supreme ends will once more have to form a unity, so that the interest of humanity subordinate to no higher interest may be furthered as one.

The concluding aim at which reason's speculation, in its transcendental use, finally arrives bears on three objects: the will's freedom, the soul's immortality, and God's existence. As to all three, reason's purely speculative interest is but slight, and it is hardly for its sake that anyone would take up the {{{ 519 }}} exhausting toil of transcendental research, forever contending with obstacles, seeing that not one of the discoveries conceivably to be made here admits of a use that would demonstrate its worth _in concreto_, that is, within the study of nature. Grant that the will is free—even so, this can bear only on the intelligible cause of our willing. For where the phenomena of its manifestations are concerned, namely the actions, an inviolable ground-maxim—lacking which no reason could be exercised in an empirical use—obliges us to explain them exactly as we explain every other appearance of nature, that is, by nature's unchangeable laws. Suppose, secondly, that the soul's spiritual nature (and with it its immortality) admitted of insight—still, nothing can be built on this, neither as a ground of explanation touching the appearances of the present life, nor touching the special makeup {{ 827 }} of the state to come, for our concept of an incorporeal nature is nothing but negative, in no degree extending our cognition, and furnishing no serviceable material for inferences save such as could pass only as inventions, which philosophy disallows. Suppose, thirdly, that the existence of a supreme intelligence had been demonstrated: we would then indeed render comprehensible to ourselves the purposiveness in the world's contrivance and order taken generally, yet by no means be warranted to draw from it any specific institution and order, or, where such is not observed, to conclude to it audaciously; for a necessary rule of reason's speculative use forbids us to pass over natural causes and to relinquish what experience can teach us, so as to derive something we do know from what utterly exceeds all our acquaintance. In short, these three propositions stay, for speculative reason, transcendent throughout, and possess no immanent use whatever—none, that is, admissible for objects of experience and thereby in any manner useful to us—being rather, taken by themselves, wholly futile and, on top of that, extremely laborious strainings of our reason.

If, accordingly, these three cardinal propositions are in no way necessary to us for ***knowledge***, and yet are pressed upon us insistently by our reason: {{ 828 }} then their significance can properly bear only on the ***practical***.

{{{ 520 }}} Practical is whatever is possible through freedom. But when the conditions under which our free choice is exercised are empirical, reason can there have no use save a regulative one, serving merely to produce the unity of empirical laws; thus, for instance, in the doctrine of prudence reason's entire task lies in bringing together all the ends prescribed to us by our inclinations into the one end, ***happiness***, and in making the means to it accord—so that reason can thereupon deliver no laws of free conduct except ***pragmatic*** ones, directed at attaining the ends the senses commend to us, and thus no pure laws determined wholly _a priori_. Pure practical laws, on the other hand—laws whose end reason supplies entirely _a priori_, and which do not command under empirical conditions but unconditionally—would be products of pure reason. Of just this sort are the ***moral*** laws; and so it is these alone that pertain to the practical use of pure reason and allow of a canon.

Thus the whole equipment of reason, in the enterprise one may name pure philosophy, is directed in truth only upon the three problems already considered. Yet these, for their part, look beyond themselves to a still more distant intent, namely ***what is to be done***, supposing the will free, supposing there be a {{ 829 }} God and a world to come. Since what is here at issue is our behavior with a view to the highest end, the final purpose of the nature that so wisely provides for us, in fashioning our reason, is properly set upon the moral alone.

Caution, however, is called for: since we are turning our regard to an object foreign[^*] to transcendental philosophy, we must neither digress into episodes and so damage the unity of the system, nor, on the other side, by saying too little of this fresh material, let it come up short in distinctness or in conviction. I hope to achieve both by holding myself as closely as I can to the {{{ 521 }}} transcendental, and by putting wholly to one side whatever here might be psychological, that is, empirical.

And here the first thing to remark is that, for the present, I shall use the concept of freedom only in its practical sense, while the concept in its transcendental import—which cannot, as a ground of explanation of the appearances, {{ 830 }} be posited empirically, but is itself a problem for reason—I here set aside as already disposed of above. A power of choice, namely, is merely ***animal*** (_arbitrium brutum_) when nothing but sensible impulses can determine it, that is, ***pathologically***. The power of choice, by contrast, that can be determined apart from sensible impulses, and thus by moving causes which reason alone represents, is termed ***free choice*** (_arbitrium liberum_); and whatever hangs together with it, be it as ground or as consequence, is called ***practical***. Practical freedom can be proved through experience. For the human power of choice is determined not solely by what allures, that is, what affects the senses directly; rather, we possess a capacity to master the impressions on our sensible faculty of desire through representations of what, even in a more remote fashion, is beneficial or harmful; and these considerations of what is worth desiring with a view to our whole condition, that is, what is good and beneficial, rest upon reason. Reason accordingly issues laws too—imperatives, that is, objective ***laws of freedom***—which pronounce ***what ought to happen***, though perhaps it never does, and which are thereby marked off from ***laws of nature***, whose sole concern is ***what does happen***; and for this reason they are likewise named practical laws.

{{ 831 }} But whether reason itself, in these acts by which it prescribes laws, is not once more determined by influences of another kind, and whether what, with respect to sensible impulses, bears the name of freedom may not, with respect to higher and more remotely operating causes, once again be nature—that does not concern us in the practical domain, where we interrogate reason at first only for the ***precept*** of conduct; it is instead a purely speculative question, one we may lay aside so long as our concern is directed to acting or forbearing. Practical freedom, then, we come to know through experience as one among the natural causes, namely a causality that reason exercises in determining the will, while {{{ 522 }}} transcendental freedom asks for an independence of this reason itself (as regards its causality in beginning a series of appearances) from every determining cause of the sensible world, and to that extent appears to contradict the law of nature, and so all possible experience, thus remaining a problem. For reason in its practical use, however, this problem has no place; and so, within a canon of pure reason, we are occupied with just two questions bearing on the practical interest of pure reason, questions for which a canon of its use must be feasible, namely: is there a God? is there a life to come? The question regarding transcendental freedom touches merely speculative knowledge, and this we may set to one side as entirely a matter of indifference when {{ 832 }} the practical is at stake—a question, moreover, on which the antinomy of pure reason already affords discussion enough.

[^*]: Every practical concept is directed to objects of liking or disliking, that is, of pleasure and displeasure, and therefore at the least indirectly to objects of our feeling. But since this feeling is no power for representing things, lying rather outside the whole faculty of cognition, the elements of our judgments, in so far as they relate to pleasure or displeasure, and hence to the practical, have no place within the sum of transcendental philosophy, which deals exclusively with pure cognitions _a priori_.
