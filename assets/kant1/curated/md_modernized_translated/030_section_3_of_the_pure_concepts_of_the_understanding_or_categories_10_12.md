## {{{ 90 }}} {{ 102 }} §10. 3. Section. Of the pure concepts of the understanding, or categories.

General logic, as has been said several times already, abstracts from all content of cognition and expects that representations be given to it from elsewhere, from wherever {{{ 91 }}} it may be, in order first to transform these into concepts, which proceeds analytically. On the contrary, transcendental logic has a manifold of sensibility _a priori_ lying before it, which transcendental aesthetic offers to it, in order to provide a matter for the pure concepts of the understanding, without which they would be without any content, and thus completely empty. Space and time, however, contain a manifold of pure intuition _a priori_, yet belong nevertheless to the conditions of the receptivity of our mind, under which alone it can receive representations of objects, and which must therefore also always affect the concept of these. But the spontaneity of our thinking requires that this manifold first be gone through, taken up, and combined in a certain way, in order to make a cognition out of it. This action I call synthesis.

{{ 103 }} By ***synthesis***, however, in the most general sense, I understand the action of adding different representations to each other, and of comprehending their manifoldness in one cognition. Such a synthesis is ***pure*** if the manifold is given not empirically but _a priori_ (as is that in space and time). Prior to all analysis of our representations, these must first be given, and no concepts can arise analytically as to ***their content***. But the synthesis of a manifold (whether it be given empirically or _a priori_) first produces a cognition, which to be sure may at first still be crude and confused, and thus in need of analysis; yet synthesis is that which properly collects the elements for cognitions and unites them into a certain content; it is therefore the first thing to which we must attend if we wish to judge of the first origin of our cognition.

Synthesis in general is, as we shall see hereafter, the mere effect of the imagination, a blind but indispensable function of the soul, without which we would have no cognition at all anywhere, but of which we are seldom even conscious. To bring this synthesis to concepts, however, is a function that belongs to the understanding, and by means of which it first provides us with cognition in the proper sense.

{{ 104 }} ***Pure synthesis, represented generally***, now gives the pure concept of the understanding. But by this synthesis I understand that which rests on a ground of synthetic unity _a priori_: {{{ 92 }}} thus our counting (as is more noticeable in larger numbers) is a ***synthesis according to concepts***, because it takes place according to a common ground of unity (e.g., the decadic system). Under this concept, therefore, unity in the synthesis of the manifold becomes necessary.

Analytically, different representations are brought ***under*** a concept (a business with which general logic is concerned). But transcendental logic teaches us how to bring to concepts not the representations, but the ***pure synthesis*** of representations. The first that must be given to us _a priori_ for the sake of the cognition of all objects is the ***manifold*** of pure intuition; the ***synthesis*** of this manifold by means of the imagination is the second, but it does not yet yield any cognition. The concepts which give ***unity*** to this pure synthesis, and which consist solely in the representation of this necessary synthetic unity, contribute the third thing to the cognition of an object that comes before us, and they rest on the understanding.

The same function which gives unity to the different representations in a {{ 105 }} judgment also gives unity to the mere synthesis of different representations in an intuition, which, expressed generally, is called the pure concept of the understanding. The very same understanding, therefore, and indeed by means of the very same actions through which it brought about the logical form of a judgment in concepts by means of analytical unity, also brings a transcendental content into its representations by means of the synthetic unity of the manifold in intuition in general, on which account they are called pure concepts of the understanding, which relate _a priori_ to objects, a feat which general logic cannot perform.

In such a way there arise exactly as many pure concepts of the understanding, which relate _a priori_ to objects of intuition in general, as there were logical functions in all possible judgments in the preceding table: for the understanding is completely exhausted by these functions, and its capacity is entirely measured by them. We will call these concepts ***categories***, after Aristotle, since our intent is originally the same as his, even though in the execution it diverges very far from it.

This now is the registry of all originally pure concepts of synthesis that the understanding contains _a priori_ within itself, and by virtue of which alone it is a pure understanding; for by them alone can it understand something in the manifold of intuition, i.e., think an object of the same. This division is systematically generated from a common principle, namely the faculty of judging (which is the same as the faculty of thinking), and has not arisen rhapsodically from a haphazard search for pure concepts, {{ 107 }} of whose completeness one could never be certain, since it is only inferred through induction, without mentioning that in the latter way one never sees why precisely these and not other concepts dwell in the pure understanding. It was an enterprise worthy of an acute man like Aristotle to seek out these fundamental concepts. But since he had no principle, he rounded them up as they ran up against him, and first turned up ten of them, which he called ***categories*** {{{ 94 }}} (predicaments). Subsequently, he believed he had found five more of them, which he added under the name of post-predicaments. But his table still remained deficient. Besides, there are also some _modi_ of pure sensibility to be found among them (_quando, ubi, situs_, likewise _prius, simul_), and even an empirical one (_motus_), which do not belong in this ancestral register of the understanding at all, or else the derivative concepts are counted among the primary concepts (_actio, passio_), and some of the latter are missing entirely.

For the sake of the latter, it is therefore still to be noted: that the categories, as the true ***ancestral concepts*** of the pure understanding, also have their equally pure ***derivative concepts***, which could by no means be omitted in a complete system of transcendental philosophy, but with the mere mention of which I can be satisfied in a merely critical essay.

{{ 108 }} May I be permitted to call these pure but derivative concepts of the understanding the ***predicables*** of pure understanding (in contrast to the predicaments). If one has the original and primitive concepts, the derivative and subaltern ones can easily be added, and the family tree of the pure understanding completely drawn up. Since I am concerned here not with the completeness of the system, but only with the principles for a system, I reserve this supplement for another occupation. But one can satisfy this purpose to a considerable extent if one takes the ontological textbooks in hand and subordinates, for example, to the category of causality the predicables of force, action, and passion; to the category of community those of presence and resistance; and to the predicaments of modality those of coming to be, passing away, change, etc. The categories, combined with the _modi_ of pure sensibility or else with one another, yield a great number of derivative concepts _a priori_, to note which and, if possible, to catalogue to completeness would be a useful and not unpleasant, but here dispensable effort.

I intentionally dispense with the definitions of these categories in this treatise, although I may be in possession of them. In the sequel I will analyze these concepts to the degree that {{ 109 }} is sufficient in relation to the Doctrine of Method which I am treating. In a system of pure reason, one could rightly demand them of me; but here they would only draw attention away from the main point of the investigation {{{ 95 }}} by raising doubts and objections which, without detracting at all from the essential aim, can well be referred to another occupation. Meanwhile, from the little that I have adduced here, it is already clear that a complete dictionary with all necessary explanations is not only possible but easy to produce. The compartments are now there; it is only necessary to fill them, and a systematic topic like the present one does not easily let us miss the place to which each concept peculiarly belongs, and at the same time lets us easily notice those that are still empty.

## §11.

Some nice observations can be made concerning this table of categories, which could perhaps have significant consequences in respect of the scientific form of all cognitions of reason. For that this table is uncommonly useful, indeed indispensable, in the theoretical part of philosophy, for completely drafting ***the plan for the whole of a science***, so far as it rests on concepts _a priori_, and for dividing it systematically according to ***definite principles***, is already clear of itself from the fact that the said table contains all the elementary concepts of the understanding completely, indeed even the form of a system of them in the human understanding, {{ 110 }} and consequently gives instructions on all ***moments*** of an intended speculative science, indeed even their ***order***, as I have indeed given a sample of elsewhere[^*]. Here are now some of these remarks.

***The first is***: that this table, which contains four classes of concepts of the understanding, can first be split into two divisions, the first of which is directed to objects of intuition (both pure and empirical), the second to the existence of these objects (either in relation to each other or to the understanding).

The first class I would call that of the ***mathematical*** categories, the second that of the ***dynamical*** ones. The first class has, as one sees, no correlates, which are met with solely in the second class. {{{ 96 }}} This difference must surely have a ground in the nature of the understanding.

***Second Remark***. That there is everywhere an equal number of categories in each class, namely three, is likewise food for thought, since otherwise all division _a priori_ by means of concepts must be dichotomy. But in addition to this, the third category everywhere arises from the connection of the second with the first of its class.

{{ 111 }} Thus ***totality*** is nothing other than plurality considered as unity; ***limitation*** nothing other than reality combined with negation; ***community*** is the ***causality*** of a substance in reciprocal determination of others; finally, ***necessity*** is nothing other than existence given through possibility itself. But one must by no means think that on this account the third category is a merely derivative and not a primary concept of the pure understanding. For the combination of the first and the second, in order to produce the third concept, requires a special act of the understanding, which is not identical with that which is exercised in the case of the first and the second. Thus the concept of a ***number*** (which belongs to the category of totality) is not always possible where the concepts of multitude and of unity are present (e.g., in the representation of the infinite); or from the fact that I combine the concept of a ***cause*** and that of a ***substance***, the ***influence***, i.e., how one substance can be the cause of something in another substance, is not yet immediately understood. From this it is clear that a special act of the understanding is required for this, and similarly for the others.

***Third Remark***. Of a single category, namely that of ***community***, which is found under the third title, the agreement with the {{ 112 }} form of a disjunctive judgment corresponding to it in the table of logical functions is not so striking as in the case of the others.

In order to assure oneself of this agreement, one must note: that in all disjunctive judgments the sphere (the multitude of everything that is contained under them) is represented as a whole divided into parts (the subordinate concepts), and, because one cannot be contained under another, they are thought of as ***coordinated*** with, not ***subordinated*** to, one another, so that they determine each other not ***one-sidedly***, as in a ***series***, but ***reciprocally***, as in an ***aggregate*** (when one {{{ 97 }}} member of the division is posited, all others are excluded, and vice versa).

Now, a similar connection is thought of in ***a whole of things***, where one is not ***subordinated*** as effect to another as cause of its existence, but is at the same time and reciprocally ***coordinated*** as cause in respect of the determination of the others (e.g., in a body, the parts of which reciprocally attract and also resist one another); which is an entirely different kind of connection from that which is found in the mere relation of cause to effect (of ground to consequence), in which the consequence does not reciprocally determine the ground in turn, and therefore does not constitute a whole with the latter (as the creator of the world does not with the world). The same procedure of the understanding, when it represents to itself {{ 113 }} the sphere of a divided concept, it also observes when it thinks of a thing as divisible; and just as the members of the division in the former exclude one another and yet are combined in one sphere, so it represents the parts of the latter as such that existence (as substances) belongs to each of them exclusively of the others, and yet they are represented as combined in a whole.

## §12.

There is, however, still to be found in the transcendental philosophy of the ancients another chapter which contains pure concepts of the understanding that, although they are not counted among the categories, were nevertheless supposed to hold, like them, as concepts of objects _a priori_; in which case, however, they would increase the number of the categories, which cannot be. These are contained in the proposition so famous among the scholastics: _quodlibet ens est unum, verum, bonum_. Now, although the use of this principle in respect of its consequences (which yielded nothing but tautological propositions) turned out to be very meager, so that in more recent times it has been customary to grant it a place in metaphysics almost only out of courtesy, yet a thought which has maintained itself for so long a time, empty as it may seem to be, always deserves an investigation into its origin, and justifies the conjecture that it has its ground in some rule of the understanding which, as is often the case, has only been falsely interpreted. These supposedly transcendental {{ 114 }} predicates of ***things*** are nothing other than logical requirements {{{ 98 }}} and criteria of all ***cognition of things*** in general, and lay at their foundation the categories of quantity, namely ***unity***, ***plurality***, and ***totality***; except that they used these, which properly ought to have been taken in a material sense as belonging to the possibility of things themselves, in fact only in a formal sense as belonging to the logical demand in respect of any cognition, and yet they incautiously made these criteria of thinking into properties of things in themselves. For in every cognition of an object there is ***unity*** of the concept, which one can call ***qualitative unity***, insofar as under it only the unity of the comprehension of the manifold of cognitions is thought, such as the unity of the theme in a play, a speech, or a fable. Second, ***truth*** in respect of the consequences. The more true consequences follow from a given concept, the more criteria there are of its objective reality. This one could call the ***qualitative plurality*** of marks that belong to a concept as to a common ground (not being thought in it as a magnitude). Finally, third, ***perfection***, which consists in this, that, conversely, this plurality together leads back to the unity of the concept, and completely agrees with this and no other, which one can call the ***qualitative completeness*** {{ 115 }} (totality). From which it is clear that these logical criteria of the possibility of cognition in general here only transform the three categories of magnitude, in which the unity in the generation of the quantum must be assumed to be thoroughly homogeneous, in respect of the combination of even ***heterogeneous*** pieces of cognition in one consciousness, through the quality of a cognition as a principle. Thus the criterion of the possibility of a concept (not of its object) is the definition, in which the ***unity*** of the concept, the ***truth*** of all that may initially be derived from it, and finally the ***completeness*** of what has been drawn from it, constitute all that is necessary for the production of the entire concept; or similarly, the ***criterion of a hypothesis*** is the intelligibility of the assumed ***explanatory ground*** or its ***unity*** (without auxiliary hypotheses), the ***truth*** (agreement with itself and with experience) of the consequences to be derived from it, and finally the ***completeness*** of the explanatory ground in relation to them, which point back to nothing more and nothing less than what was assumed in the hypothesis, and which deliver back _a posteriori_ analytically {{{ 99 }}} what had been thought _a priori_ synthetically, and agree with it. — Thus, by the concepts of unity, truth, and perfection, the transcendental table of the categories is not supplemented at all, as if it were perhaps deficient, {{ 116 }} but rather, by entirely setting aside the relation of these concepts to objects, the procedure with them is brought under general logical rules of the agreement of cognition with itself.
