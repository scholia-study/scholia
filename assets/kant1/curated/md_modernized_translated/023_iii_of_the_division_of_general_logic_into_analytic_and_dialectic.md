## {{{ 79 }}} III. Of the Division of General Logic into **Analytic** and **Dialectic**

The old and famous question, with which it was thought to push logicians into a corner, and to bring them to the point where they must either allow themselves to be caught in a miserable circle or else confess their ignorance and consequently the vanity of their whole art, is this: ***What is truth?*** The nominal definition of truth, namely that it is the agreement of cognition with its object, is here granted and presupposed; but one demands to know what is the general and certain criterion of the truth of any cognition.

It is already a great and necessary proof of cleverness or insight to know what one should reasonably ask. For if the question is absurd in itself and demands unnecessary answers, then besides the embarrassment of the one who proposes it, it sometimes has the further disadvantage of misleading the incautious listener into absurd answers and presenting the ridiculous spectacle of one {{ 83 }} person (as the ancients said) milking the he-goat while the other holds a sieve underneath.

If truth consists in the agreement of a cognition with its object, then this object must thereby be distinguished from others; for a cognition is false if it does not agree with the object to which it is related, even if it contains something that might well hold of other objects. Now a general criterion of truth would be that which was valid of all cognitions without distinction of their objects. But it is clear that, since in such a criterion one abstracts from all content of cognition (relation to its object), while truth concerns precisely this content, it is quite impossible and absurd to ask for a mark of the truth of this content of cognition, and that therefore a sufficient and yet at the same time general sign of truth cannot possibly be given. Since we have above already called the content of a cognition its matter, one must say: no general sign of the truth of cognition in respect of its matter can be demanded, because it is self-contradictory.

{{{ 80 }}} But as regards cognition in respect of its mere form (setting aside all content), it is equally clear: that a logic, insofar as it expounds the {{ 84 }} general and necessary rules of the understanding, must present criteria of truth in these very rules. For what contradicts these is false, because the understanding thereby conflicts with its own general rules of thinking, and thus with itself. But these criteria concern only the form of truth, i.e., of thinking in general, and are to that extent quite correct, but not sufficient. For although a cognition might be in complete accordance with logical form, i.e., might not contradict itself, it can still nevertheless contradict its object. Thus the merely logical criterion of truth, namely the agreement of a cognition with the general and formal laws of the understanding and of reason, is indeed the _conditio sine qua non_, and thus the negative condition of all truth; but further than this logic cannot go, and error which affects not the form but the content cannot be detected by logic by means of any touchstone.

General logic now resolves the entire formal business of the understanding and of reason into its elements, and presents them as principles of all logical assessment of our cognition. This part of logic can therefore be called analytic, and is for that very reason the at least negative touchstone of truth, since one must first examine and estimate all cognition as to its form by these rules before one {{ 85 }} investigates it as to its content, in order to determine whether it contains positive truth in respect of the object. But because the mere form of cognition, however much it may agree with logical laws, is far from sufficient to determine the material (objective) truth of the cognition, no one can venture with logic alone to judge about objects and to assert anything, without having beforehand obtained reliable information about them from sources outside of logic, in order then merely to attempt their utilization and connection in a coherent whole according to logical laws, or rather, solely to test them thereby. Nevertheless, there is something so seductive in the possession of such a plausible art of giving the form of the understanding to all our cognitions, however empty and poor one may still be in respect of their content, that this general logic, which is merely a ***canon*** for assessment, has been used as it were as an ***organon*** for the actual production of, or at least {{{ 81 }}} the illusion of, objective assertions, and has consequently in fact been abused for that purpose. General logic, then, as a presumed organon, is called ***dialectic***.

However different the meaning was in which the ancients employed this designation of a science or art, one can yet safely infer from their actual use of it that for them {{ 86 }} it was nothing other than the ***logic of illusion***. A sophistical art of giving to one's ignorance, and indeed even to one's intentional illusions, the coloring of truth, by imitating the method of thoroughness which logic in general prescribes, and using its topics for the embellishment of every empty assertion. Now one can note it as a secure and useful warning: that general logic, ***considered as an organon***, is at all times a logic of illusion, i.e., dialectical. For since it teaches us nothing at all about the content of cognition, but only the mere formal conditions of agreement with the understanding, which are moreover entirely indifferent in respect of the objects, the demand to use it as a tool (organon) in order, at least in pretense, to expand and extend one's knowledge, must run to nothing but empty talk, asserting with some plausibility whatever one wants, or also attacking it at will.

Such instruction is in no way worthy of the dignity of philosophy. For this reason, this designation of dialectic has been added to logic rather as a ***critique of dialectical illusion***, and as such we also want it to be understood here.
