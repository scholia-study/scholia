## Sixth Section. Transcendental idealism as the key to the solution of the cosmological dialectic.

In the transcendental aesthetic we established well enough: that whatever gets intuited in space or in time — and thus every object of an experience that is possible for us — amounts to nothing beyond appearances, {{ 519 }} that is, to mere representations which, taken just as they are represented, whether as extended beings or as series of alterations, possess outside our thoughts no existence grounded in itself. This tenet I term ***transcendental idealism***.[^*] The realist in the transcendental {{{ 339 }}} sense converts these modifications of our sensibility into self-subsistent things, and thereby turns ***mere representations*** into things in themselves.

It would be to wrong us were one to saddle us with that empirical idealism, so long and so widely decried, which, in granting the proper actuality of space, denies — or at the very least finds questionable — the existence of the extended beings lodged within it, and allows no adequately demonstrable difference between dream and truth on this head. Where the appearances of inner sense in time are concerned: with these, taken as actual things, it meets no difficulty; indeed, it goes so far as to claim that this inner experience, of itself and by itself alone, furnishes adequate proof of the actual existence of its object (in itself, along with the whole of this temporal determination).

{{ 520 }} Our transcendental idealism, conversely, grants this: that objects given to outer intuition are actual to just the degree that they are intuited in space, and that, within time, every alteration is as actual as inner sense presents it. For, space being already a form of the very intuition we term outer, and there being no empirical representation whatsoever apart from objects situated in it: we are both able and obliged to take extended beings within it to be actual; and matters stand no differently with time. And yet that space itself, this time along with it, and, together with the two of them, the whole of appearances, are of themselves ***no things***, but purely and simply representations, and are wholly incapable of existing beyond our mind; indeed, even the inner, sensible intuition that our mind has of itself (as an object of consciousness), whose determination is presented by way of the succession of diverse states across time, is not the self proper, such as it exists in itself, nor is it the transcendental subject, but merely an appearance handed over to the sensibility of this being that remains unknown to us. That this inner appearance exists as a thing thus subsisting in itself is something that cannot be admitted, for its condition is time, and time can never serve as a determination of any thing considered in itself. Within space and within time, however, the empirical truth of appearances stands sufficiently secured, and is set well apart from any affinity with dream, {{ 521 }} so long as the two hang together, correctly and throughout, within one experience in accordance with empirical laws.

The objects of experience, then, are ***never given in them*** {{{ 340 }}} ***selves***, but come to be given solely within experience, and apart from it they have no existence whatever. That there could be dwellers on the moon, though no human being has ever laid eyes on any, must of course be admitted, yet this signifies no more than this: that, in the possible onward march of experience, we might light upon them; for whatever hangs together, within a single context, with a perception according to the laws of the empirical advance is actual. They are, then, actual at the point where they enter into an empirical connection with my actual consciousness, though this by no means makes them actual in themselves, that is, outside this onward advance of experience.

Truly, nothing is handed to us save perception together with the empirical advance leading from it to further possible perceptions. For, taken in themselves, appearances — being mere representations — have their actuality nowhere but in perception, which is, in point of fact, nothing else than the actuality of an empirical representation, that is, of an appearance. To name an appearance a real thing in advance of perception signifies either that, in the course of experience, we are bound to light upon such a perception, or else it carries no significance at all. For one might well assert that it exists of itself, with no bearing on our senses and on possible experience, {{ 522 }} were the subject under discussion a thing in itself. But the subject under discussion is merely an appearance in space and in time, both of which amount to no determinations of things in themselves, but solely to determinations of our sensibility; and so whatever resides within them (appearances) is not, of itself, anything, but consists of mere representations that, unless they are given within us (in perception), are to be encountered simply nowhere.

The capacity for sensible intuition is, properly speaking, no more than a receptivity — a susceptibility to being affected, in a certain manner, by representations whose mutual relation constitutes a pure intuition of space and of time (these being one and all forms of our sensibility), and which, to the extent that within this relation (space and time) they admit of connection and determination according to the laws by which experience is made one, receive the name ***objects***. What non-sensibly causes these representations stays altogether hidden from us, so that we are unable to intuit it as an object; for an object of that kind would have to be presented neither in space nor in time (which are but conditions of sensible representation), and without those conditions no intuition can so much as be thought by us. We may nevertheless assign to the {{{ 341 }}} merely intelligible cause of appearances at large the title of transcendental object, purely so as to have on hand something answering to sensibility taken as a receptivity. To this transcendental object we are free to assign the entire scope and interconnection of the perceptions possible for us {{ 523 }} and to declare: that, of itself, it stands given antecedently to all experience. The appearances, though, are, in conformity with it, given not of themselves but solely within this experience, since they are mere representations that stand for an actual object only in the guise of perceptions, namely when a given perception coheres with all the rest according to the rules by which experience is unified. One may accordingly put it thus: the actual things belonging to past time have their givenness in the transcendental object of experience; yet they count, for me, as objects — and as actual in bygone time — only so far as I picture to myself that a regressive series of possible perceptions (be it upon the guiding thread of history, or upon the traces of causes and their effects) leads, by empirical laws — the course of the world, in short — back to an elapsed stretch of time serving as the condition of the present time, a stretch that even then is pictured as actual only within the nexus of a possible experience and not of itself, so that every event that has run its course from unfathomable antiquity, before ever I existed, signifies nothing beyond the possibility of lengthening the chain of experience upward, from the perception now at hand, toward the conditions that fix it as regards time.

Should I, then, picture to myself the totality of existing objects of the senses across all time and all spaces at once: I am not thereby inserting them into the two ahead of experience; rather, such a picture is nothing else {{ 524 }} than the thought of a single possible experience taken in its absolute entirety. Within it, and nowhere besides, are those objects (which are nothing beyond mere representations) actually given. But to say that they exist ahead of all my experience amounts merely to saying that they are to be encountered in that portion of experience ***to which*** I, taking my start from perception, have first of all to press forward. The cause of the empirical conditions of this advance — hence upon which members, and likewise how far, I might light upon such in the regress — is transcendental, and for that reason bound to remain unknown to me. Yet it is not this cause that is at issue, but merely the rule governing the advance of experience, wherein the objects, that is to say appearances, come to be given to me. In its upshot, moreover, it comes to precisely the {{{ 342 }}} same thing: whether I say that, in the empirical progression through space, I might chance upon stars a hundredfold more remote than the outermost ones within my sight; or whether I say that such are perhaps to be met with somewhere in cosmic space, even though no human being has ever perceived them or ever shall; for even were they given as things in themselves, bearing no relation at all to possible experience as such, they remain, for me, nothing whatever — and hence no objects — save to the degree that they fall within the series of the empirical regress. It is only in a bearing of a wholly different sort — where {{ 525 }} these very appearances are to be pressed into service for the cosmological idea of an absolute whole, and where the question at stake accordingly overreaches the boundaries of possible experience — that it becomes of any moment to distinguish the manner in which one takes the actuality of such thought objects of the senses, this being needed to head off a treacherous delusion that cannot but arise out of a misreading of our own concepts of experience.

[^*]: I have, besides, from time to time called it ***formal*** idealism, in order to mark it off from ***material*** idealism, that is, from the ordinary sort, which throws the very existence of outer things into doubt, or denies it outright. In certain cases it seems the wiser course to reach for these terms rather than the ones cited above, so as to head off every misreading.
