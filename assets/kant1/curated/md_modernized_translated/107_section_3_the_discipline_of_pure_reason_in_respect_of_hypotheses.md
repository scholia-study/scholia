## {{ 797 }} Section 3. The discipline of pure reason in respect of hypotheses

Since, through the critique of our reason, we finally come to know at least this much—that in its pure and speculative use we are in fact capable of knowing nothing at all: ought it not, for that very reason, to throw open a wider field to ***hypotheses***, seeing that we are at any rate allowed to invent and to opine, if not indeed to assert?

{{ 798 }} Where imagination is not to ***rave*** but is instead to ***invent*** beneath the strict oversight of reason, there must always be something settled in advance as wholly certain, neither fabricated nor a bare opinion, and that something is the ***possibility*** of the object itself. It is then quite legitimate, as regards the object's actuality, to fall back upon opinion—which, however, if it is not to hang in the air, must be linked, as a ground of explanation, to what is genuinely given and therefore certain, and only then earns the name of a ***hypothesis***.

Now since we are unable to form even the faintest concept _a priori_ of the possibility of dynamical connection, and since the category of the pure understanding is of no use for inventing such a thing, but only for understanding it wherever it turns up within experience: we cannot, in conformity with these categories, originally excogitate so much as a single object endowed with some novel character not specifiable on empirical grounds, and lay it down as the basis of a permissible hypothesis; for that would amount to slipping empty chimeras of the brain under reason in place of concepts of real things. Thus one is not at liberty to think up any new original forces whatever—e.g. an understanding equipped to intuit its object without the senses, or a force of expansion dispensing with all contact, or a novel species of substances, e.g. one that would be present in space without impenetrability, and consequently no community {{{ 503 }}} {{ 799 }} of substances distinct from every kind that experience affords us, no mode of presence other than in space, no persistence other than in time alone. In a word: our reason is capable only of employing the conditions under which experience is possible as conditions under which things themselves are possible, and in no way of fashioning conditions of its own, as it were, in complete independence of these, since concepts of that kind, free of contradiction though they be, would all the same remain devoid of any object.

The concepts of reason, as already stated, are nothing more than ideas and possess, to be sure, no object in any experience—yet they do not on that account denote objects that are invented and at the same time assumed to be possible. They are entertained in a merely problematic manner, so that upon them (treated as heuristic fictions) regulative principles may be founded for the systematic use of the understanding across the field of experience. Should one abandon this, they become mere entities of thought, whose possibility admits of no proof and which cannot, therefore, be laid down as the basis for explaining actual appearances by way of a hypothesis. To ***think*** the soul as something simple is entirely allowable, the aim being to posit, in keeping with this ***idea***, a complete and necessary unity of all the mind's powers—even where it cannot be discerned _in concreto_—as the principle by which we appraise its inner appearances. But ***assuming*** the soul to be a simple substance (a transcendent concept) would yield a proposition that is not merely indemonstrable (as a number of {{ 800 }} physical hypotheses are) but reckless as well, arbitrary and blind, since the simple can turn up in no experience at all, and, if substance is here taken to mean the abiding object of sensible intuition, the very possibility of a ***simple appearance*** eludes all comprehension. Beings that are purely intelligible, or purely intelligible properties belonging to the things of the sensible world, cannot, with any legitimately grounded warrant of reason, be adopted as opinion—though, seeing that we possess no concepts of their possibility or impossibility, they can equally not be dogmatically disavowed by appeal to some presumed superior insight.

In explaining given appearances one is entitled to cite no things and no grounds of explanation beyond those already tied to the given ones in accordance with laws of appearance that are already familiar. A ***transcendental hypothesis***, one that would press a bare idea of reason into service for explaining the things of nature, would on that account {{{ 504 }}} amount to no explanation whatever, since what one fails to understand adequately from familiar empirical principles would then be accounted for by something one understands not at all. Besides, the principle of such a hypothesis would in truth serve merely to appease reason, contributing nothing to the advancement of the understanding's employment in respect of its objects. Order and purposiveness within nature must, once again, receive their explanation from grounds drawn from nature herself and {{ 801 }} in conformity with natural laws, and in this domain even the most extravagant hypotheses, so long as they remain physical, prove more bearable than a hyperphysical one—that is, an appeal to a divine author posited to serve this end. For that would be a principle of indolent reason (_ignava ratio_)—to bypass in one stroke every cause whose objective reality, at least as to its possibility, we might yet come to learn through ongoing experience, merely to settle down in a bare idea that suits reason's convenience so well. But as concerns the absolute totality of the ground of explanation within the series thereof, this can raise no obstacle with respect to the objects of the world, since, these being nothing but appearances, nothing consummated in the synthesis of the series of conditions may ever be expected of them.

Transcendental hypotheses belonging to the speculative use of reason, together with a license to resort, at a pinch, to hyperphysical grounds of explanation in order to compensate for the absence of physical ones, cannot be conceded at all—partly because reason is not thereby carried any further but on the contrary severs the whole onward course of its use, and partly because such a license would in the end have to rob it of every fruit that the cultivation of its own proper soil, namely experience, might yield. For should the explanation of nature grow difficult for us at this point or that, a transcendent ground of explanation stands perpetually ready to hand, one that spares us that {{ 802 }} labor of inquiry, and our search terminates not in insight but in the utter unintelligibility of a principle already so contrived beforehand that it could not but contain the concept of the absolutely first.

The second thing demanded, if a hypothesis is to deserve acceptance, is that it suffice for determining _a priori_ the consequences that are given. Whenever, to that end, one finds oneself driven to conjure up auxiliary hypotheses, they carry with them the suspicion {{{ 505 }}} of sheer fiction, since each of them severally calls for precisely the same warrant that the underlying thought itself stood in need of, and can therefore supply no trustworthy witness. Granting the presupposition of a cause unlimited in its perfection, there is admittedly no shortage of grounds for explaining every purposiveness, order, and magnitude to be met with in the world; that cause, nonetheless, in view of the deviations and evils that present themselves—at least by our concepts—stands in need of yet further hypotheses if it is to be salvaged against these as objections. Should the simple self-subsistence of the human soul, laid down as the ground of its appearances, come under attack from the difficulties raised by phenomena akin to the alterations of matter (its growth and diminution), then fresh hypotheses have to be summoned to its aid—hypotheses not, indeed, without a semblance of truth, yet destitute of all credentials save the one lent them by the opinion adopted as the chief ground {{ 803 }}, an opinion on whose behalf they are all the same meant to plead.

If the rational assertions cited here by way of illustration (the incorporeal unity of the soul and the existence of a supreme being) are meant to hold not as hypotheses but as dogmas demonstrated _a priori_, then they are simply not under discussion. In such an event, however, one had better make sure that the proof carries the apodictic certainty of a demonstration. For wishing to make the actuality of such ideas merely ***probable*** is a preposterous design, on a par with proposing to establish a theorem of geometry by nothing more than probability. Reason cut off from all experience is able to cognize everything solely _a priori_ and as necessary, or else not at all; its judgment, accordingly, is never an opinion, but consists either in withholding every judgment or in apodictic certainty. Opinions, and judgments of probability regarding what pertains to things, can arise only as grounds explaining what is actually given, or as consequences—following empirical laws—of that which lies at the basis as actual, and thus only within the series of the objects of experience. Beyond this field, to ***opine*** is as much as to toy with thoughts—unless it were that one merely held the opinion that, along some insecure route of judgment, one might perhaps come upon the truth.

{{ 804 }} Yet even though, in questions merely speculative belonging to pure reason, hypotheses find no place as a foundation on which to erect propositions, they remain wholly permissible for the mere purpose of defending such propositions should the need arise—that is {{{ 506 }}}, not in a dogmatic but rather in a polemical use. By defense, though, I understand not the piling up of further grounds of proof for one's assertion, but simply the thwarting of the adversary's specious insights, which are meant to do damage to the proposition we have put forward. Now every synthetic proposition arising from pure reason carries with it this distinctive feature: that whoever affirms the reality of certain ideas can never command enough knowledge to render his proposition certain, while the adversary on the opposing side is equally unable to muster the knowledge required to affirm its contrary. Such parity in the fortune allotted to human reason confers no advantage on either party where speculative cognition is concerned, and precisely here lies the true arena of quarrels that admit of no settlement. It will, however, be shown in what follows that, as regards the ***practical employment***, reason does possess a right to assume something which, within the province of bare speculation, it could by no means be authorized to presuppose absent adequate grounds of proof, since presuppositions of that sort all diminish the completeness of speculation—a completeness about which the practical interest troubles itself not in the slightest. There, accordingly, it holds a possession whose legitimacy it is not required to prove, and for which, in fact, it could furnish no proof either. The opponent, {{ 805 }} then, is the one obliged to furnish proof. Yet since this adversary, aiming to establish the non-being of the disputed object, knows just as little of it as the first party who affirms its actuality: an advantage here declares itself on the side of whoever advances something as a presupposition necessary on practical grounds (_melior est conditio possidentis_). For he is free, in a kind of self-defense, to press into service on behalf of his good cause the very means his adversary directs against it, namely hypotheses, serving not in the least to reinforce the proof of his position, but only to make plain that the adversary grasps far too little of what the quarrel is about to preen himself on any superiority of speculative insight where we are concerned.

Within the domain of pure reason, then, hypotheses are licensed solely as instruments of warfare—not to establish a right by their means, but merely to defend one. The adversary, however, is one we must in this case invariably look for inside ourselves. For speculative reason, in its transcendental use, is dialectical ***in itself***. Those objections we might have cause to dread reside within us. We are obliged to hunt them out, as one hunts down old claims that yet never fall under any statute of limitations, so as to build a lasting peace upon their destruction {{{ 507 }}}. Any outward calm is merely a semblance. The seed of these contestations, rooted in the very nature of human reason, has to be eradicated {{ 806 }}; yet how are we to eradicate it unless we allow it liberty, and indeed even feed it, so that it may put forth its foliage, reveal itself by that means, and then be uprooted altogether? So think up for yourselves objections that no adversary has yet lit upon, and go so far as to hand him weapons, or concede to him the most advantageous ground he could ever desire! There is in all this nothing whatever to be dreaded, but much rather to be hoped—namely, that you will procure for yourselves a possession never again to be contested throughout all time to come.

To your full array of arms there now also belong the hypotheses of pure reason, which, though no better than leaden weapons (steeled by no law of experience), can nonetheless always accomplish as much as any that an adversary might wield against you. So if, opposing the soul's immaterial nature—assumed on some other, non-speculative ground and exempt from all bodily alteration—the difficulty arises that experience nevertheless appears to demonstrate the heightening no less than the disordering of our powers of mind to be nothing more than diverse modifications of our organs: you may blunt the force of that demonstration by supposing our body to be merely the foundational appearance upon which, as its condition, the whole capacity of sensibility and with it all thought rests in the present state (during life). Departure from the body would mark the termination of this sensible use to which your cognitive power is put, and the inauguration of its intellectual use. The body, on this view, would {{ 807 }} not be what causes thought, but only a condition that restricts it, so that, while it must count as promoting sensible and animal life, it must all the more count as a hindrance to pure and spiritual life; and the reliance of the one upon the body's constitution would establish nothing about the reliance of life in its entirety upon the condition of our organs. You are able, however, to press yet further and even to discover fresh doubts, whether not yet raised at all or not pushed far enough.

The contingency of procreations—which in human beings, as in creatures devoid of reason, hangs upon opportunity, and moreover often upon sustenance, upon governance with its moods and caprices, indeed often even upon vice—poses a formidable difficulty against the opinion {{{ 508 }}} of a persistence, reaching out into eternities, of a creature whose life first got under way amid circumstances so inconsequential and so utterly abandoned to our freedom. So far as the persistence of the whole kind (here upon earth) goes, the difficulty in respect of it weighs but little, since the chance operative in the single case is none the less subject to a rule in the aggregate; but to look, in respect of every individual, for so potent an effect from causes so slight does certainly seem precarious. In opposition to this, however, you are able to marshal a transcendental hypothesis: that all life, properly speaking, is only intelligible, in no way subordinate to the {{ 808 }} alterations of time, having neither commenced with birth nor destined to close with death; that this life amounts to no more than an appearance—i.e. a sensible representation of the pure spiritual life—and that the entire sensible world is but an image hovering before our present mode of cognition, one that, dreamlike, possesses in itself no objective reality; that, were we to intuit both things and our own selves ***as they are***, we should find ourselves set within a realm of spiritual natures, our one genuine community with which neither took its start through birth nor will terminate through the death of the body (these being mere appearances), etc.

Now although, of everything we here advance hypothetically to fend off the assault, we neither know the slightest part nor seriously maintain it—the whole being not so much as an idea of reason, but merely a concept ***devised*** for the sake of counter-defense—we proceed in all this quite rationally, inasmuch as, to the adversary who fancies he has exhausted every possibility, passing off the want of its empirical conditions falsely as a proof that what we believe is utterly impossible, we do no more than show him: that he is as little able, by mere laws of experience, to span the entire range of things possible in themselves, as we are able, beyond experience, to gain anything for our reason in a well-grounded fashion. Whoever puts such hypothetical counter-remedies in place against the presumptions of the brazenly {{ 809 }} denying adversary must not be supposed to want to appropriate them as his own genuine opinions. He lets them go the moment he has disposed of the adversary's dogmatic self-conceit. For however modest and restrained it may appear when someone conducts himself, in respect of others' assertions, in a merely refusing and negating way, the pretension is nonetheless, the moment he sets out to press these objections of his as proofs of the contrary, no less {{{ 509 }}} haughty and self-important than had he sided with the affirming party and taken up its assertion.

From all this it becomes evident that, in reason's speculative employment, hypotheses possess no validity as opinions taken in their own right, but hold good only in relation to the opposing transcendent pretensions set against them. For stretching the principles that govern possible experience out over the possibility of things as such is no less transcendent than claiming objective reality for concepts of a kind whose objects can be met with nowhere save beyond the limit of all possible experience. Whatever pure reason pronounces assertorically must—as with everything reason cognizes—be necessary, or it is nothing whatsoever. Consequently it harbors, in truth, no opinions at all. The hypotheses under discussion, though, are nothing but problematic judgments which at any rate cannot be refuted, even if, to be sure, nothing can prove them, {{ 810 }} and are therefore strictly private opinions, of which one nonetheless cannot properly divest oneself (even for the sake of inner calm) in the face of scruples that stir within. In that character, however, they must be kept, and one must scrupulously prevent them from coming forward as self-authenticated and possessed of any absolute validity, thereby drowning reason beneath fabrications and delusions.
