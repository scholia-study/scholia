## {{{ 36 }}} V. In all theoretical sciences of reason synthetic _a priori_ judgments are contained as principles

1. ***Mathematical judgments are one and all synthetic.*** This proposition appears so far to have escaped the observations of those who dissect human reason, and indeed to stand in flat opposition to all their surmises, even though it is beyond contradiction certain and, for what follows, of great importance. For since it was observed that the reasonings of mathematicians all proceed by way of the principle of contradiction (as the very nature of any apodictic certainty requires), people convinced themselves that the principles too were cognized through that same principle, and here they went astray; for although a synthetic proposition can indeed be apprehended by way of the principle of contradiction, this holds only when a second synthetic proposition is laid down in advance, one out of which the first may be derived, and never on its own account.

First of all it must be remarked: that proper mathematical propositions are at all times judgments _a priori_ and not empirical, since they carry with them a necessity {{{ 37 }}} that cannot be gleaned from experience {{ 15 }}. But should one refuse to grant this, very well, then I restrict my proposition to ***pure mathematics***, whose very concept already implies that it contains not empirical but merely pure cognition _a priori_.

At the outset one would of course be inclined to think: that the proposition 7+5 = 12 is a purely analytic one, which flows by the principle of contradiction from the concept of seven and five taken as a sum. But upon closer scrutiny it becomes apparent that the concept of the sum of 7 and 5 embraces nothing beyond the union of both numbers into one, through which it is left entirely unthought which single number it is that combines the two. By merely thinking to myself that union of seven and five I have in no way already thought the concept of twelve, and however long I go on dissecting my concept of such a possible sum, the twelve will still not be met with inside it. One is obliged to go beyond these concepts by summoning the aid of an intuition answering to one of the pair, say the five fingers of one's hand or (as Segner in his arithmetic does) five dots, and thus, one by one, to join the units of the five that intuition furnishes to the concept of the seven. For I begin by taking the number 7, and, calling in the fingers of my hand as the intuition belonging to the concept of the 5, I now transfer the units that I had previously {{ 16 }} put together to make up the number 5, one after another onto that image of mine, adding them to the number 7, and in this way watch the number 12 emerge. Now that 5 ***ought*** to be joined to 7 was something I did think within the concept of a sum = 7+5, though not that this sum should be identical with the number 12. The arithmetical proposition, accordingly, is invariably synthetic — a point one appreciates the more clearly on choosing numbers of some size, since it then shines out plainly that, however we might rotate and reshuffle our concepts, we could, apart from the help of intuition, and by the bare dissection of our concepts alone, never arrive at the sum.

{{{ 38 }}} No more is any principle of pure geometry an analytic one. The claim that between two points the straight line is the shortest is a synthetic proposition. For in my concept of the ***straight*** there lies nothing pertaining to magnitude, but a quality only. The concept of the shortest is therefore something wholly superadded, and no amount of dissection will extract it from the concept of a straight line. Here, then, intuition has to be summoned to our aid, for through it alone is the synthesis rendered possible.

A small number of principles presupposed by geometers are, it is true, really analytic and depend upon the principle of contradiction; but these, like identical propositions, function merely as links binding the method together and {{ 17 }} not in the capacity of principles, for example a = a, the whole equals itself, or (a + b) > a, i.e. the whole exceeds its part. And even these, notwithstanding that they are valid on the basis of mere concepts, gain admission into mathematics solely on the ground that they permit of presentation in intuition. What ordinarily persuades us in this matter that the predicate of judgments of this apodictic kind is already lodged in our concept, so that the judgment counts as analytic, is nothing but the ambiguity residing in the expression. We ***are supposed***, that is, to think a certain predicate together with a given concept, and this necessity already adheres to the concepts themselves. But what is at issue is not what we ***ought to think*** in addition to the given concept, but rather what we ***actually***, obscurely though it be, ***think*** within it, and it thereby comes to light that, while the predicate does attach to those concepts necessarily, it does so not as thought within the concept itself, but by way of an intuition that has to be joined to the concept.

2. ***Natural science (physics) harbors within itself synthetic judgments _a priori_ as principles.*** Let me put forward just a pair of propositions by way of illustration: the one holding that throughout all changes in the bodily world the quantity of matter stays constant, or else the one that in every transmission of motion action and counteraction must forever balance each other. In each of the two it stands clear not merely that there is necessity, {{ 18 }} and consequently an _a priori_ origin, but likewise that they are synthetic propositions. For it is not {{{ 39 }}} permanence that I think to myself in the concept of matter, but merely matter's presence in space through its filling thereof. Thus I do in fact pass beyond matter's concept, so as to think onto it something _a priori_ that I had not thought within it. The proposition, then, is not analytic but synthetic, and is nonetheless thought _a priori_ — and so it stands with the other propositions belonging to the pure portion of natural science.

3. ***In metaphysics***, granting that one takes it merely for a science attempted up to now yet, by the very nature of human reason, indispensable, ***synthetic cognitions _a priori_ are supposed to be contained***, and its concern is by no means simply to take apart concepts that we frame for ourselves _a priori_ of things and so to elucidate them analytically, but rather our aim is to broaden our cognition _a priori_, and to that end we have to enlist principles of the sort that lay upon the given concept something it did not already hold, going, through synthetic judgments _a priori_, so far afield indeed that experience itself is unable to keep pace with us, as in the proposition: the world has to possess a first beginning, and more of the same; and so metaphysics, at least ***as measured by its aim***, is composed of nothing save synthetic propositions _a priori_.
