## Section 1. The discipline of pure reason in its dogmatic use

Mathematics affords the most dazzling instance of a pure reason that, with no help from experience, successfully broadens its own scope. Instances of this sort prove infectious, especially for a faculty that, by its nature, cannot help expecting the same good fortune elsewhere that it once met with in a single case. And so pure {{ 741 }} reason, when put to transcendental use, cherishes the hope of expanding itself with equal success and rigor {{{ 469 }}} as it did in mathematics—chiefly by carrying over the very method that yielded such palpable benefit in the one domain. A great deal therefore hangs, for us, on knowing this: whether the method of attaining apodictic certainty that the latter science calls mathematical is one and the same as that by which such certainty is pursued in philosophy, where it would have to bear the name dogmatic.

***Philosophical*** cognition amounts to ***rational cognition from concepts***, whereas the ***mathematical*** sort issues from the ***construction*** of concepts. To construct a concept, however, means this: to display _a priori_ the intuition that answers to it. Constructing a concept thus calls for a non-empirical intuition, one that—being an intuition—is a single object, and which yet, precisely as the construction of a concept (of a general representation), has to convey in the representation a validity holding for every possible intuition ranged under that same concept. So it is that I construct a triangle when I display the object answering to this concept, whether through sheer imagination in pure intuition or, thereafter, on paper in empirical intuition too, on both occasions wholly _a priori_, having borrowed the model for it from no experience whatever. The single figure I have drawn is empirical, and serves all the same to convey the concept unimpaired {{ 742 }} in its generality, for in this empirical intuition one looks solely to the act of constructing the concept—an act to which numerous determinations, such as those of size, of the sides and of the angles, are wholly indifferent—so that one leaves out of account these variations, which do nothing to alter the concept of the triangle.

Philosophical cognition, then, regards the particular only within the universal, while mathematical cognition regards the universal within the particular, indeed within the single instance, and does so nonetheless _a priori_ and by the agency of reason, so that, exactly as this single instance stands determined under certain general conditions of construction, the object of the concept—answered to by this instance merely as its schema—must likewise be conceived as determined in general.

The essential contrast between these two species of rational cognition lies, then, in this form, and by no means in any difference of their matter or their objects. Those who fancied that philosophy could be set apart from mathematics by declaring that the one {{{ 470 }}} takes for its object merely ***quality***, the other merely ***quantity***, have mistaken an effect for a cause. It is the form of mathematical cognition that causes it to reach only as far as _quanta_. For construction is possible for the concept of magnitudes alone—that is, _a priori_ {{ 743 }} laying-out in intuition—whereas qualities allow of presentation in no intuition save an empirical one. A rational cognition of qualities is on that account possible through concepts only. Thus an intuition answering to the concept of reality can be drawn by no one from any source other than experience, and can never be come by _a priori_, from oneself, ahead of the empirical consciousness of it. The conical figure one will be able to render intuitive with no empirical help, simply by following the concept, but the color of this cone must first be furnished in some experience or other. As for the concept of a cause as such, I have no means of presenting it in intuition except by an example that experience puts into my hand, and so forth. Philosophy, besides, treats of magnitudes no less than mathematics does—of totality, say, of infinity, and so on. Mathematics likewise busies itself with the distinction between lines and surfaces as spaces of differing quality, and with the continuity of extension as one such quality of it. Yet though in cases like these the two share a common object, the way of handling it by reason turns out wholly different under philosophical as against mathematical treatment. The one confines itself to general concepts; the other can get nowhere with the mere concept and rushes forthwith to intuition, where it inspects the concept _in concreto_—not empirically, though, {{ 744 }} but only in an intuition it has displayed _a priori_, that is, constructed, and in which whatever issues from the general conditions of construction must hold likewise, and generally, of the object of the constructed concept.

Hand a philosopher the concept of a triangle and leave him to work out, after his own fashion, how the sum of its angles might come to stand toward a right angle. All he now possesses is the concept of a figure shut in by three straight lines, and along with it the concept of an equal number of angles. He may brood over this concept as long as he pleases, he will draw nothing new from it. He can take apart the concept of the straight line, or of an angle, or of the number three, and make it distinct, without ever reaching further properties {{{ 471 }}} that simply do not reside in these concepts. Let the geometer, by contrast, take the question up. He starts straightaway by constructing a triangle. Knowing that two right angles come, taken together, to exactly the sum of all the adjacent angles that may be drawn from a single point on a straight line, he prolongs one side of his triangle and gets two adjacent angles equal together to two right ones. He then divides the outer of these angles by running a line parallel to the triangle's opposite side, and observes that an outer adjacent angle here comes about which equals an inner one, and so forth. By such means, along a chain of inferences forever steered by intuition, he reaches a fully luminous {{ 745 }} and at once general resolution of the question.

Mathematics, though, constructs not magnitudes alone (_quanta_), as geometry does, but bare magnitude as well (_quantitatem_), as in algebra, where it leaves wholly aside the makeup of the object meant to be thought under such a concept of magnitude. It then settles on a particular notation for all constructions of magnitudes as such (numbers)—for addition, subtraction, and so on, and the extraction of roots; and once it has, in addition, marked out the general concept of magnitudes according to their several relations, it sets forth in intuition, under certain general rules, every operation by which magnitude is produced and modified; where one magnitude is to be divided by another, it brings their two characters together according to the notation for division, and so forth, and thereby attains, through a symbolic construction—just as geometry does through an ostensive or geometric one (of the objects themselves)—the very point beyond the reach of discursive cognition working with mere concepts.

What can be the cause of so divergent a predicament in which two artificers of reason find themselves, the one taking his route by concepts, the other by intuitions that he displays _a priori_ in keeping with concepts? Given the transcendental groundwork laid out above, {{ 746 }} the cause is plain. The matter here does not turn on analytic propositions, producible by mere dissection of concepts (on this score the philosopher would surely outdo his competitor), but on synthetic ones—and, at that, such as are to be cognized _a priori_. For I am not to attend to what I {{{ 472 }}} really think in my concept of the triangle (that is nothing but the bare definition); I am, rather, to press past it toward properties that lie outside this concept and yet pertain to it. Now, this cannot happen otherwise than by my fixing my object according to the conditions either of empirical or of pure intuition. The first would supply only an empirical proposition (got by measuring its angles), one carrying neither generality nor, still less, necessity—and there is no talk of anything of that kind. The second procedure, however, is the mathematical, and in this case the geometric, construction, whereby, in a pure intuition as much as in an empirical one, I annex the manifold belonging to the schema of a triangle as such, and hence to its concept—by which means general synthetic propositions must indeed be constructed.

I would therefore be philosophizing about the triangle to no purpose—reflecting, that is, discursively {{ 747 }} upon it—without getting the least bit past that bare definition from which, in fairness, I should have to set out. There does exist a transcendental synthesis out of concepts alone, which once again is the philosopher's alone to accomplish, yet which bears never on more than a thing in general, and on the conditions under which a perception of it might belong to possible experience. But in mathematical tasks that is not in question, nor is existence at all; what is in question is the properties of the objects in themselves, and this only so far as those properties are bound up with the concept of them.

In the instance cited we have aimed merely at making plain how vast a difference lies between the discursive employment of reason by concepts and the intuitive employment through the construction of concepts. The question naturally follows what the cause may be that renders such a twofold employment of reason necessary, and on what conditions one is able to tell whether the first alone, or the second as well, has a place.

In the end, every cognition of ours bears on possible intuitions; for it is by these alone that an object gets given. Now an _a priori_ concept (a non-empirical concept) either already carries a pure intuition within itself, and is then open to construction; {{{ 473 }}} or it carries nothing beyond the synthesis of possible intuitions not given _a priori_, and then one may indeed judge by its means synthetically and {{ 748 }} _a priori_, though only discursively, by concepts, and never intuitively, through construction of the concept.

Now, of all intuition, the sole thing given _a priori_ is the naked form of appearances—space and time; and a concept of these, taken as _quanta_, admits of _a priori_ display in intuition, that is, of construction, whether alongside their quality (their shape) or else as their mere quantity (the bare synthesis of the homogeneous manifold) by way of number. The matter of appearances, though—that through which ***things*** come to be given us in space and time—is representable in perception only, and hence _a posteriori_. The one concept that presents _a priori_ this empirical content of appearances is the concept of the ***thing*** as such; and synthetic cognition of it _a priori_ is competent to supply no more than the bare rule for synthesizing whatever perception may hand over _a posteriori_—never the _a priori_ intuition of the real object, since that intuition has of necessity to be empirical.

Synthetic propositions directed upon ***things*** as such, whose intuition cannot be furnished _a priori_ at all, are transcendental. Transcendental propositions, accordingly, admit of being furnished never by construction of concepts, but only by concepts _a priori_. They hold no more than the rule under which a certain synthetic unity of what cannot be _a priori_ presented in intuition (of the perceptions) is to be pursued {{ 749 }} empirically. But not a single one of their concepts can they display _a priori_ in any case whatever; this they accomplish only _a posteriori_, by way of the experience that first grows possible in keeping with those synthetic principles.

If one is to pass a synthetic judgment about a concept, one has to step out of that concept and, indeed, toward the intuition in which it stands given. For should one keep to what the concept holds, the judgment would be merely analytic, an unpacking of the thought in line with what actually lies inside it. But from the concept I am free to move to the pure or empirical intuition that answers to it, so as to weigh it there _in concreto_ and, {{{ 474 }}} whether _a priori_ or _a posteriori_, to learn what belongs to its object. The first is the rational and mathematical cognition by construction of the concept; the second is the merely empirical (mechanical) cognition, one incapable of ever delivering necessary and apodictic propositions. So I might well dissect my empirical concept of ***gold*** and thereby win nothing beyond the power to count off all that I really think at this word—by which my cognition undergoes, to be sure, a logical betterment, but takes on no increase or addition. What I do instead is take the matter that turns up under this name and run perceptions upon it, and these will hand me a variety of {{ 750 }} synthetic, yet empirical, propositions. The mathematical concept of a triangle I would construct—give it, that is, _a priori_ in intuition—and along this road arrive at a synthetic, yet rational, cognition. But once the transcendental concept of a reality, a substance, a force, and the like is handed me, it marks out neither an empirical nor a pure intuition, but solely the synthesis of empirical intuitions (which for that reason cannot be furnished _a priori_); and so, since the synthesis is unable to pass _a priori_ out to the intuition answering to it, there can spring from that concept no determining synthetic proposition, but only a principle for the synthesis[^*] of possible empirical intuitions. A transcendental proposition is thus a synthetic rational cognition by mere concepts, and therefore discursive, since through it all synthetic unity of empirical cognition first grows possible, while by it no intuition whatever gets given _a priori_.

{{ 751 }} There is, then, a twofold employment of reason which—for all the generality of cognition and its _a priori_ generation that the two hold in common—runs nonetheless very differently in its course, and this for the reason that within appearance, as that whereby every object comes to be given {{{ 475 }}} us, two ingredients are present: the form of intuition (space and time), open to being cognized and settled entirely _a priori_, and the matter (the physical), or the content, standing for a something encountered in space and time, one that consequently harbors an existence and answers to sensation. Touching the latter—which admits of determinate givenness by no route but the empirical—there is nothing we can hold _a priori_ save indeterminate concepts for the synthesis of possible sensations, so far as these come under the unity of apperception (within a possible experience). Touching the former, we are able to settle our concepts in intuition _a priori_ by fashioning for ourselves, in space and time, the very objects, through a uniform synthesis, in that we take them for nothing but _quanta_. The first is termed the employment of reason by concepts, wherein all we can do is bring appearances, as to their real content, under concepts—concepts thereupon determinable in no way but the empirical, that is, _a posteriori_ (though in keeping with those concepts as rules of an empirical synthesis); the second is the employment of reason through construction of concepts, wherein {{ 752 }} these concepts, bearing already on an _a priori_ intuition, can just for that reason be given as settled _a priori_, and without any empirical _data_, in pure intuition. To ponder everything that is there (a thing in space or in time)—whether, and how far, it is a quantum or not; that an existence in it, or else a lack, has to be represented; how far this something (the filler of space or time) is a primary substratum or a mere determination; whether its existence bears a relation to something else as cause or as effect; and, finally, whether, in point of existence, it stands isolated or in mutual dependence with others; to ponder the possibility of this existence, its actuality and its necessity, or the reverse of these: the whole of this pertains to ***rational cognition from concepts***, and it is this we call ***philosophical***. But to settle an intuition _a priori_ in space (shape), to partition time (duration), or just to cognize what is common to the synthesis of one selfsame thing throughout time and space, together with the magnitude of an intuition in general that springs therefrom (number)—that is a ***business of reason*** by construction of concepts, and bears the name ***mathematical***.

The signal good fortune that reason meets with by means of mathematics quite naturally breeds the surmise that, {{{ 476 }}} if not reason itself, at any rate its method will succeed outside the province of magnitudes as well, {{ 753 }} inasmuch as it reduces all its concepts to intuitions it can furnish _a priori_, thereby making itself, so to speak, lord over ***nature***: pure philosophy, on the other hand, fumbles about within nature with discursive _a priori_ concepts, unable to render the reality of them _a priori_ intuitive and thus to warrant it. Nor, indeed, do the masters of this art seem to want for such confidence in themselves, nor the public to want for high hopes of their skill, were they ever to set their hand to the business. For, having hardly ever philosophized about their mathematics (a hard task, that!), the specific difference of the one employment of reason from the other never so much as crosses their mind. Current rules, empirically applied and borrowed from common reason, then serve them in the stead of axioms. Whence the concepts of space and time, with which they deal (as the sole original _quanta_), may arise concerns them not at all; and just so it strikes them as idle to inquire into the source of pure concepts of the understanding, and thereby into the compass of their validity, and preferable simply to put them to use. In all of this they act entirely rightly, provided only that they do not step beyond their allotted bound, namely that of ***nature***. But so doing, they slip unawares off the field of sensibility onto the treacherous soil of pure and even transcendental concepts, where the ground (_instabilis tellus, innabilis unda_) suffers them neither to stand {{ 754 }} nor to swim, and lets only hurried steps be taken, of which time retains not the faintest trace—whereas their march in mathematics lays down a highroad that even the remotest posterity may tread in confidence.

Since we have laid it on ourselves as a duty to fix the bounds of pure reason in its transcendental use exactly and with certainty, while this species of striving carries the peculiarity that, for all the most forceful and lucid warnings, it still lets itself be kept in play by hope before it wholly renounces the design of pressing out beyond the bounds of experience into the enchanting tracts of the intellectual: it becomes necessary to strip away, as it were, this last anchor of a fanciful hope, and to show that adherence to the mathematical method can, in this species of cognition, procure not the slightest gain—unless the gain be that of laying open the {{{ 477 }}} bare spots of that method the more distinctly: that geometry and philosophy are two wholly separate things, however much, in natural science, they reach a hand to one another, so that the procedure of the one can never be aped by the other.

The soundness of mathematics rests upon definitions, axioms, and demonstrations. I shall be content to show this: that not one of these components can, in the sense the mathematician gives them, be furnished by philosophy, nor aped by it; that the geometer, {{ 755 }} working by his method within philosophy, brings off nothing but houses of cards, while the philosopher, working by his within the domain of mathematics, can raise nothing but idle prattle—although just this, the knowing of one's bounds, is what philosophy consists in—and that even the mathematician, unless his talent be already hemmed in by nature and penned within his specialty, can neither spurn philosophy's warnings nor override them.

1. On **definitions**. ***To define*** ought, as the very word conveys, to signify strictly no more than to lay out originally, within its bounds, the comprehensive concept of a thing.[^**] Set against such a requirement, an ***empirical*** concept cannot be defined in the least, but only ***explicated***. For, since in it we hold but a handful of marks belonging to some class of sense-objects, one can never be sure whether, under the word that stands for the same object, one thinks now more, now fewer of its marks {{ 756 }}. So the one may think, in the concept of ***gold***, over and above weight, color, and ductility, the further property of not rusting, while the other perhaps knows nothing of it. One relies on certain marks only so long as they suffice for telling things apart; fresh observations, though, strike some out and put others in, so that the concept never rests between secure bounds. And what, besides, would be the use of defining such a concept, seeing that when, say, water and its properties come up for discussion, one will not dwell {{{ 478 }}} on what one thinks at the word water, but will move on to trials, and the word, with the scant marks clinging to it, is meant to make up merely a ***designation*** and not a concept of the thing—so that the supposed definition is nothing other than a settling of the word. Secondly, no _a priori_ given concept, either, can strictly speaking be defined—substance, say, cause, right, equity, and the rest. For I can never rest assured that the distinct representation of a concept given (as yet confusedly) has been comprehensively developed, unless I know it to be adequate to the object. But since the concept of it, as it comes given, may hold many an obscure representation that we skip over in the dissecting, though we draw on them always in application: the comprehensiveness of my concept's dissection {{ 757 }} stays forever in doubt, and can be rendered only ***presumably***, never ***apodictically***, certain by way of manifold matching examples. In place of the term definition I would sooner press into service that of ***exposition***, which stays cautious yet, and by which the critic may let the thing pass to a certain degree and still keep his misgivings as to its comprehensiveness. Since, then, concepts given neither empirically nor _a priori_ lend themselves to definition, there are left over none but concepts thought up at will, on which one may try this trick. A concept of my own I can, in such a case, always define; for I must, after all, know what I meant to think, having myself devised it on purpose, with its coming to me neither by the nature of the understanding nor by experience—yet I cannot claim to have thereby defined a genuine object. For if the concept rests on empirical conditions—a ship's clock, say—then this arbitrary concept does not yet yield the object and its possibility; from it I do not so much as know whether it has any object at all, and my account may better be styled a declaration (of my project) than the definition of an object. Hence there would be left over no concepts fit for defining but those holding an arbitrary synthesis constructible _a priori_—so that mathematics, and it alone, has definitions. For the object it thinks, it also sets forth _a priori_ in intuition, and this object {{ 758 }} can surely contain no more and no less than the concept, since through the account the concept of the object was given originally—that is, without the account's being drawn from anything else. The German {{{ 479 }}} tongue has, for the terms ***exposition***, ***explication***, ***declaration***, and ***definition***, nothing beyond the single word ***Erklärung***; and so we must already give a little ground on the stringency of the requirement—having, that is, refused philosophical accounts the honorific of definition—and will hold this whole remark to the point that philosophical definitions come about only as ***expositions*** of given concepts, mathematical ones as ***constructions*** of concepts originally made, the former merely analytically, by dissection (whose completeness is not apodictically sure), the latter synthetically, and thus ***make*** the concept itself, whereas the former only ***explain*** it. From this there follows:

a) that one must not, in philosophy, ape mathematics by putting the definitions out in front, except perhaps by way of a mere trial. For, being dissections of given concepts, these concepts go before—confusedly still, but they go before—and the incomplete exposition precedes the complete, so that from a few marks drawn out of a dissection not yet finished we can settle a good deal in advance, before we have come to the complete exposition, that is, to the definition; in short, in philosophy {{ 759 }} the definition, as apportioned distinctness, ought to close the work sooner than open it.[^***] In mathematics, on the contrary, we have no concept whatever ahead of the definition, through which the concept is first given; it must, then, and it can too, always take its start from there.

b) Mathematical definitions can never go wrong. For, because {{{ 480 }}} the concept is first given through the definition, it holds exactly, and only, what the definition means to have thought by its means. But although nothing false, as to content, can arise in it, still, now and then—though rarely—a slip in the form (the dress) may occur, namely in point of precision. Thus the ordinary account of the circle, that it is a ***curved*** line all of whose points stand at equal distance from a single {{ 760 }} one (the center), carries the fault that the qualifier curved has crept in without need. For there has to be a particular theorem, drawn from the definition and readily proved: that any line all of whose points stand at equal distance from a single one is curved (no stretch of it straight). Analytic definitions, by contrast, can go wrong in manifold ways—either by importing marks that in truth did not lie in the concept, or by falling short of the comprehensiveness that makes up the essence of a definition, since one cannot be quite so sure of the completeness of one's dissection. For this reason mathematics' way of defining is not to be aped in philosophy.

2. On **axioms**. These are synthetic principles _a priori_, in so far as they are immediately certain. Now one concept does not admit of being joined to another synthetically and yet immediately, since, for us to get past a concept, a third, mediating cognition is wanted. And since philosophy is nothing but rational cognition by concepts, no principle will be met with in it that might earn the name of an axiom. Mathematics, on the other hand, has room for ***axioms***, since, by constructing its concepts in the intuition of the object, it is able to tie that object's predicates to it _a priori_ and immediately, {{ 761 }} as, for instance, that three points always lie in a single plane. A synthetic principle from bare concepts, by contrast, can never be immediately certain—take the proposition: whatever happens has its cause; for I have to cast about for a third thing, namely the condition of time-determination within an experience, and could not recognize such a principle straight off, immediately from the concepts alone. Discursive principles, then, are something altogether other than intuitive ones, that is, axioms. The former call, every time, for a deduction besides, which the latter can do wholly without; and since these last are self-evident on the very ground {{{ 481 }}} that philosophical principles, for all their certainty, can never lay claim to, it wants a very great deal for any synthetic proposition of pure and transcendental reason to be as manifest (as the defiant phrase runs) as the proposition ***that twice two make four***. True, in the Analytic, at the table of the principles of the pure understanding, I did also bring in certain axioms of intuition; but the principle cited there was itself no axiom, and served merely to state the _Principium_ of the possibility of axioms in general—and is itself only a principle from concepts. For the very possibility of mathematics has to be shown within transcendental philosophy. Philosophy, therefore, has no axioms, and may never lay down its _a priori_ principles thus flatly, but has to make do with vindicating its warrant for them by {{ 762 }} thorough deduction.

3. On **demonstrations**. Only an apodictic proof, so far as it is intuitive, can be styled a ***demonstration***. Experience does teach us what is there, but not that it could be in no way otherwise. On that account empirical grounds of proof can supply no apodictic proof. Out of _a priori_ concepts, however (in discursive cognition), intuitive certainty—that is, self-evidence—can never spring, however apodictically certain the judgment may otherwise stand. It is mathematics alone, then, that holds demonstrations, since it draws its cognition not from concepts but from the construction of them—that is, from the intuition furnishable _a priori_ to match the concepts. Even the way of algebra, with its equations, out of which it turns up the truth by reduction along with the proof, is admittedly no geometric but still a characteristic construction, in which one lays out at the signs the concepts—above all that of the ratio of magnitudes—in intuition, and, with never a glance at the heuristic side, secures every inference against error by setting each one before the eye. Philosophical cognition, on the contrary, must forgo this advantage, having always to view the general _in abstracto_ (through concepts), whereas mathematics can weigh the general _in concreto_ (in the single intuition) and yet by a pure _a priori_ representation, {{ 763 }} whereby each false step shows itself. The former, accordingly, I would rather name ***acroamatic*** (discursive) proofs, since they can be conducted through nothing but words (the object in thought), as opposed to {{{ 482 }}} ***demonstrations***, which, as the term itself already signals, go forward in the intuition of the object.

From all of this it now follows that it in no way suits the nature of philosophy, above all in the field of pure reason, to swagger with a dogmatic bearing and to trick itself out in the titles and sashes of mathematics, into whose order it does not, after all, belong—though it has every ground to hope for a sisterly bond with it. Those are hollow presumptions that can never come off, and must indeed thwart their own aim, which is to expose the illusions of a reason that misjudges its bounds and, by a sufficient clearing-up of our concepts, to bring the self-conceit of speculation back down to a modest but sound self-knowledge. Reason, then, in its transcendental ventures, will not be able to gaze ahead so assured, as though the road it has covered ran quite straight to the goal, nor to reckon so boldly on the premises it has laid at bottom that there were no need to look back time and again and mind whether, as the inferences run on, {{ 764 }} there might not turn up errors overlooked in the principles—errors making it needful either to fix them more closely or to recast them altogether.

I sort all apodictic propositions (be they now provable or immediately certain) into ***dogmata*** and ***mathemata***. A directly synthetic proposition from concepts is a ***dogma***; a like proposition through construction of concepts, on the other hand, is a ***mathema***. Analytic judgments, strictly, teach us nothing further about the object than what the concept we hold of it already carries within it, since they do not widen the cognition past the concept of the subject but merely light it up. They cannot, therefore, aptly be styled dogmas (a word one might perhaps render by ***tenets***). But of the two sorts of synthetic proposition _a priori_ just considered, ordinary usage lets only those belonging to philosophical cognition carry this name, and one would scarcely call the propositions of arithmetic or of geometry dogmata. This usage thus bears out the account we gave, that only judgments from concepts, and not those from construction of concepts, may be styled dogmatic.

Now the whole of pure reason, in its merely speculative {{{ 483 }}} use, holds not one directly synthetic judgment from concepts. For by way of ideas it is, as we have shown, capable of no synthetic judgments at all that would carry objective validity; by way of concepts of the understanding, {{ 765 }} on the other hand, it does raise secure principles, yet in no way directly from concepts—always only indirectly, by referring these concepts to something wholly contingent, namely ***possible experience***; for then, once this (something as an object of possible experiences) is presupposed, they are apodictically certain right enough, though in themselves (directly) _a priori_ they cannot so much as be cognized. Thus no one can soundly see into the proposition: whatever happens has its cause, out of these given concepts alone. Hence it is no ***dogma***, even though from another vantage—namely the sole field of its possible use, that is, experience—it admits of being proved perfectly well and apodictically. It is styled a ***principle*** and not a ***theorem***, even though it must be proved, and this because it has the singular property of itself first making possible its own ground of proof, namely experience, and of having always to be presupposed in the latter.

If, then, in the speculative use of pure reason there are, as to content, no dogmata whatever, every ***dogmatic*** method—be it borrowed from the mathematician or meant to grow into a manner of its own—is unfitting in itself. For it does no more than veil the faults and blunders and beguile philosophy, whose true aim is to let all reason's steps be seen in their brightest light. The method may, for all that, still be ***systematic***. For our reason (subjectively) is itself a system, though in its pure use, {{ 766 }} by way of bare concepts, only a system of inquiry after principles of unity, for which ***experience*** alone is in a position to yield the stuff. Of the peculiar method of a transcendental philosophy, though, nothing can be said in this place, since we are engaged only in a critique of how our resources stand—whether we are to build at all, and how high we may run up our building from the stuff we have (the pure _a priori_ concepts).

[^*]: By way of the concept of cause I do genuinely quit the empirical concept of an occurrence (where something happens), yet not toward the intuition that displays the concept of cause _in concreto_, but toward time-conditions in general, such as might be found in experience to answer to the concept of cause. I proceed, then, by concepts alone, and cannot proceed by construction of concepts, since the concept is a rule for the synthesis of perceptions—which, not being pure intuitions, cannot be furnished _a priori_.

[^**]: ***Comprehensiveness*** signifies the clarity and adequacy of the marks; ***bounds***, the precision, that there are no more of them than pertain to the comprehensive concept; ***originally***, however, that this fixing of bounds is derived from nowhere and hence would still need a proof, which would leave the supposed account unfit to stand at the head of all judgments about an object.

[^***]: Philosophy swarms with faulty definitions, above all with such as truly do contain elements toward a definition, though not yet in full. Now, were one able to make no start at all with a concept until one had defined it, then all philosophizing would fare very badly indeed. But since, as far as the elements (of the dissection) reach, a good and secure use of them is always to be had, even defective definitions—propositions, that is, that are not strictly definitions yet, but are otherwise true and hence approaches to them—can be turned to great use. In mathematics the definition belongs _ad esse_, in philosophy _ad melius esse_. To arrive there is a fine thing, but often a very hard one. The jurists are seeking still a definition for their concept of right.
