## {{{ 51 }}} {{ 37 }} Section 1. On Space

## § 2. Metaphysical exposition of this concept.

Through outer sense — which is a property of our mind — we set before ourselves objects as external to us, and, without exception, as located in {{{ 52 }}} space. It is there that their figure, their size, and their mutual relation stand determined, or admit of determination. As for inner sense, through which the mind has intuition of itself, or of its own inner condition, it affords, admittedly, no intuition of the soul as such, taken as an object; and yet a determinate form does exist, one under which alone the intuition of the mind's inner condition becomes possible, with the result that whatever pertains to the inner determinations comes to be represented in temporal relations. Time admits of no outward intuition, just as little as space admits of being intuited as something internal to us. So what, in truth, are space and time? Are they actual beings? Are they nothing more than determinations — or else relations — of things, though determinations that would attach to those things in themselves even were the things never intuited; or do they instead cling solely to the form of intuition, and thereby to the subjective make-up {{ 38 }} of our mind, apart from which such predicates could be attributed to no thing at all? In order to gain instruction on this matter, we shall begin by expounding the concept of space. Now by ***exposition*** (_expositio_) I mean the clear — though not necessarily complete — representation of that which belongs to a concept; and an exposition counts as ***metaphysical*** when what it contains is that which presents the concept as ***given*** _a priori_.

1) The concept of space is no empirical one, got by abstraction from outer experiences. For if certain sensations are to be related to something external to me (namely, to something occupying a place in space distinct from the place where I myself am), and if, moreover, I am to represent them as external to and beside each other — thus not simply as differing, but as situated at different points — then the representation of space must already be presupposed as their ground. It follows that the representation of space can in no way be culled from the relations of outer appearance by means of experience; on the contrary, such outer experience first becomes possible only by way of this very representation.

2) Space is a representation that is necessary and _a priori_, forming the ground of every outer intuition. One can never form a representation to the effect that there is no space, even though it is entirely possible {{ 39 }} to conceive that no objects at all are met with in it. Space {{{ 53 }}} is therefore taken as the condition that makes appearances possible, not as a determination hanging upon them, and it constitutes an _a priori_ representation that of necessity grounds outer appearances.

3) Space is no discursive concept — no general concept, as the phrase goes, of the relations of things at large — but is rather a pure intuition. For, to begin with, only one single space lets itself be represented, and talk of several spaces refers merely to parts of one and the very same single space. Nor can these parts stand prior to the one all-embracing space, functioning as it were as the components from which it might be assembled; they admit of being thought only ***within it***. In its essence it is single, and the manifold it contains — together with the general concept of spaces at large — depends entirely upon limitations. What follows is that, where space is concerned, an _a priori_ (hence non-empirical) intuition underlies every concept we form of it. For this reason every geometrical principle as well — say, that any two sides of a triangle taken together exceed the remaining one — is drawn never from universal concepts of line and of triangle, but out of intuition, and that _a priori_, carrying apodictic certainty.

4) Space is represented as an infinite ***given*** magnitude. {{ 40 }} Admittedly, any concept whatever has to be thought as a representation that an infinite plurality of differing possible representations includes beneath it (serving as their shared mark), so that the concept holds these ***under itself***; yet no concept, qua concept, admits of being thought as if it enclosed an infinite plurality of representations ***within itself***. Space, however, is thought precisely thus (since the parts of space, without limit, all coexist). The original representation of space, then, is an _a priori_ ***intuition***, and by no means a ***concept***.

## {{{ 54 }}} §3. Transcendental exposition of the concept of space.

By the term ***transcendental exposition*** I mean the accounting for a concept as a principle by whose means the possibility of further synthetic _a priori_ cognitions may be grasped. To this end two things are demanded: 1) that cognitions of that kind do in fact spring from the given concept, 2) that such cognitions are possible solely on the supposition of one particular way of explaining the concept.

Geometry, now, is that science which fixes the properties of space in a synthetic and yet _a priori_ manner. What character, then, must belong to the representation of space, if a cognition of this kind about it is to be possible? In its origin it has to be {{ 41 }} intuition; for out of a bare concept there is no drawing of propositions that reach past the concept, and yet just this occurs in geometry (Introduction V). This intuition, however, has to be met with within us _a priori_ — that is, in advance of any perception of an object — and must accordingly be a pure and not an empirical intuition. For the propositions of geometry are without exception apodictic — that is, joined to an awareness of their necessity — as when space is said to possess but three dimensions; and propositions of that kind can be neither empirical nor judgments of experience, nor yet derived from any such (Intro. II).

How, in that case, is it possible for an outer intuition to reside in the mind ahead of the objects themselves, an intuition wherein the concept of those objects admits of _a priori_ determination? Plainly in no way other than this: that it is lodged solely in the subject, as that subject's formal capacity to be affected by objects and so to obtain of them an ***immediate representation***, which is to say an ***intuition*** — and therefore merely as the form of outer ***sense*** taken generally.

It is thus our explanation, and it alone, that makes intelligible the ***possibility*** of geometry as a synthetic _a priori_ cognition. Whatever mode of explanation fails to yield as much — however much, to appearances, it might resemble ours — is by these very tokens most reliably set apart from it.

## {{{ 55 }}} {{ 42 }} Conclusions from the above concepts.

a) Space presents no property at all belonging to things in themselves, nor does it present them in their mutual relation — that is, it presents no determination of them clinging to the objects as such, a determination that would persist even were every subjective condition of intuition set aside. For determinations, be they absolute or relative, cannot be intuited in advance of the existence of the things they qualify, and thus cannot be intuited _a priori_.

b) Space amounts to nothing beyond the form belonging to all appearances of the outer senses, namely the subjective condition of sensibility on which alone outer intuition is, for us, made possible. Given that the subject's receptivity — its being liable to be affected by objects — must of necessity come before any intuition of those objects, it becomes intelligible how the form belonging to all appearances may be present in the mind ahead of every actual perception, that is, _a priori_, and how this form, being a pure intuition within which all objects have to be determined, may hold within it the principles governing their relations before any experience at all.

Only from the vantage of a human being, then, are we in a position to speak of space, of extended beings, and the like. Should we set aside that subjective condition apart from which no outer intuition can come to us — the condition, namely, of our being susceptible to affection by objects — then {{ 43 }} the representation of space means nothing whatsoever. This predicate gets attributed to things only to the extent that they make their appearance to us — only, that is, as objects of sensibility. The abiding form of this receptivity — sensibility, as we term it — is a condition necessary to every relation wherein objects are intuited as outside us, and, once these objects are set aside, it is a pure intuition that we designate as space. Because the special conditions of sensibility can be turned by us into conditions not for the possibility of the things but only for that of their appearances, we are certainly entitled to say that space embraces everything liable to appear to us outwardly, yet not the things as they are in themselves, irrespective of whether they be intuited or not, and irrespective of the subject doing the intuiting. For concerning the intuitions of other thinking beings we are wholly unable to judge {{{ 56 }}} whether they lie under the very conditions that bound our own intuition and that possess universal validity for us. When the restriction placed on a judgment is folded into the concept of the subject, the judgment thereupon holds without condition. Take the proposition: all things stand side by side in space; it is valid subject to the restriction that these things be regarded as objects of our sensible intuition. But if at this point I incorporate the condition into the concept and state: all things, considered as outer appearances, stand side by side in space, then the rule is valid universally {{ 44 }} and free of any restriction. What our expositions thus impart is the ***reality*** of space (its objective validity, that is) as regards all that can present itself to us outwardly as an object, and at one and the same time the ***ideality*** of space as regards things whenever reason weighs them in themselves — that is, leaving the make-up of our sensibility out of account. Hence we maintain the ***empirical reality*** of space (as regards every outer experience that is possible), while maintaining at once its ***transcendental ideality*** — the point being that space is nothing once we drop the condition making all experience possible and treat it as something underlying the things in themselves.

Apart from space, however, there is likewise no other representation, subjective and directed upon something external, that could bear the title of objective _a priori_. For not one of them permits the deriving of synthetic propositions _a priori_, in the way that intuition in space does (§3). For this reason no ideality whatever attaches to them, properly speaking, though they do concur with the representation of space in one respect: that they pertain solely to the subjective make-up of the manner of sense — of sight, say, of hearing, of feeling, by way of the sensations of color, of sound, and of warmth — which, being nothing but sensations rather than intuitions, yield the cognition of no object in itself, and _a priori_ least of all.

{{ 45 }} The purpose of this remark extends no further than this: to forestall anyone's hitting upon the idea of illustrating the alleged ideality of space through examples that fall far short, seeing that colors, taste, and the like are, quite rightly, viewed not as qualities of things but only as {{{ 57 }}} modifications of our subject, modifications that may even vary from one human being to the next. For here what is in its own origin nothing more than appearance — a rose, for instance — passes, in the empirical understanding, for a thing in itself, and this all the same can show itself to each eye differently in point of color. The transcendental concept of appearances in space, on the other hand, stands as a critical reminder that nothing at all which is intuited within space is a thing in itself, and that space is no form belonging to things such as might be their own apart from us — the reminder being, rather, that the objects taken in themselves remain wholly unknown to us, and that the things we name outer objects are but representations of our sensibility and nothing besides, having space for their form, while their genuine correlate — the thing in itself — is by this means neither cognized nor cognizable, and is furthermore something after which, in experience, no inquiry is ever made.
