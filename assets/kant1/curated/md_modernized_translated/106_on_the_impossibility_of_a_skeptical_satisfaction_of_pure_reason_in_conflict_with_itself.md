## {{ 786 }} On the impossibility of a skeptical satisfaction of pure reason in conflict with itself

The awareness of my own ignorance—unless that ignorance is at the same time recognized as necessary—is not something that should terminate my investigations, but is rather the genuine cause for arousing them. Ignorance of any sort pertains either to things or to the determination and limits of my cognition. Now if this ignorance happens to be contingent, then it must drive me on—in the first case, to pursue the things (objects) ***dogmatically***; in the second, to pursue the limits of my possible cognition ***critically***. That my ignorance, however, is unconditionally necessary, and hence releases me from any further inquiry, is not something that can be established empirically, out of ***observation***, but only critically, by ***fathoming*** the earliest wellsprings of our cognition. The boundary-determination of our reason, accordingly, can be accomplished only on grounds that hold _a priori_; but its restriction—which amounts to a cognition, albeit an indeterminate one, of an ignorance that can never be entirely removed—admits also of being cognized _a posteriori_, by way of whatever, amid all our knowing, still remains left for us to know. That cognition of one's own ignorance which is possible solely by means of a critique of reason itself thus counts as ***science***, whereas the latter is merely ***perception***, and of it there is no saying how far the conclusion {{ 787 }} drawn from it may reach. Were I to picture the earth's surface to myself as a plate (in keeping with the sensible {{{ 496 }}} appearance), I would have no way of knowing how far it reaches. Yet experience does teach me the following: that no matter where I arrive, I invariably behold a space around me into which I might proceed still further; consequently I come to know the bounds of my geography as it actually stands on each occasion, but not the limits of every possible mapping of the earth. But once I have in fact progressed far enough to know that the earth is a globe and its surface the surface of a globe, then even from a small portion of it—say, the extent of a single degree—I am able to determine the diameter, and by means of it the complete delimitation of the earth, that is, its outer surface, and to cognize this according to principles that are _a priori_; and though I remain ignorant regarding the objects that this surface may hold, I am not ignorant regarding the compass it embraces, its magnitude and its bounds.

The totality of all possible objects for our cognition strikes us as a level surface furnished with its apparent horizon—that is, with what encompasses the whole extent of it—and this we have named the rational concept of unconditioned totality. To reach this totality empirically is out of the question, and every attempt to fix it _a priori_ by a certain principle has proven futile. In the meantime, {{ 788 }} every question that our pure reason raises is nonetheless directed at whatever might lie beyond this horizon, or at most along its bounding line.

David Hume, that celebrated thinker, was one such geographer of human reason, and he supposed that he had adequately dealt with all those questions at a stroke by banishing them beyond reason's horizon—a horizon that he was nevertheless incapable of determining. He lingered above all over the principle of causality, and observed of it—quite correctly—that its truth (indeed, not even so much as the objective validity that belongs to the concept of an operative cause in general) rests upon no insight whatsoever, that is, upon no _a priori_ cognition; and that, accordingly, what makes up its whole standing is not in the slightest the necessity of this law, but a merely general usefulness of it in the course of experience, together with a subjective necessity springing from that usefulness, which he terms habit. From the inability of our reason, then, to put this principle to any use that would carry beyond all experience, he drew the conclusion that all of reason's pretensions to reach past the empirical are null and void.

{{{ 497 }}} A procedure of this sort—one that submits the _Facta_ of reason to scrutiny and, as circumstances warrant, to rebuke—may be termed the ***censure*** of reason. There is no doubting that such censure leads unfailingly to ***doubt*** directed against every transcendent employment of the principles. {{ 789 }} This, however, is merely the second step, one that by no means yet brings the labor to completion. The first step in the affairs of pure reason, the one distinctive of its childhood, is ***dogmatic***. The second step just named is ***skeptical***, and it bespeaks the wariness of a power of judgment grown shrewd through experience. But a third step is now required as well—one that falls to none but a matured and virile power of judgment, resting upon maxims that are firm and vindicated in their universality: namely, to submit to appraisal not reason's _Facta_ but reason itself in the whole of its capacity and its aptitude for pure cognitions _a priori_; and this is not the censure but the ***critique*** of reason, through which not merely ***bounds*** but the determinate ***limits*** of reason are established—not merely ignorance in this or that part, but with respect to all possible questions of a certain sort, and that too not merely by conjecture but by proof from principles. Skepticism, then, is a place of rest for human reason, where it may take stock of its dogmatic wanderings and sketch out the terrain in which it stands, so as thereafter to choose its route with greater assurance—but it is no place of habitation for settled residence; for such a residence can be met with only in a complete certainty, whether of the objects themselves as cognized, or of the limits within which our every cognition of objects {{ 790 }} lies enclosed.

Our reason is by no means some plane spread out to an indeterminable breadth, whose bounds we discern only in a rough, general way; rather, it has to be likened to a sphere, the radius of which can be ascertained from the curvature of the arc across its surface (the nature of synthetic propositions _a priori_), and from which, moreover, its content and its delimitation can be stated with assurance. Beyond this sphere (the field of experience) nothing is an object for it; indeed, even questions concerning supposed objects of that kind touch only on subjective principles governing a thoroughgoing {{{ 498 }}} determination of the relations that may arise among the concepts of the understanding within this sphere.

We do in fact possess synthetic cognition _a priori_, as the principles of the understanding, which run ahead of experience, make plain. Now should someone find himself utterly unable to render the possibility of these cognitions comprehensible, he may well at the outset doubt whether they really do dwell in us _a priori_; but he is not yet in a position to declare them impossible by the mere powers of the understanding, nor to write off as null every step that reason takes along their guiding thread. He can say only this: could we but discern the origin and the genuineness of these cognitions, we should then be able to settle the compass and the limits of our reason; yet before {{ 791 }} that has come to pass, every assertion of the latter is ventured blindly. And in just this manner a pervasive doubt regarding all dogmatic philosophy—which pursues its way without any critique of reason itself—would be perfectly well grounded; yet this affords no reason to deny reason such an advance altogether, supposing that advance were made ready and made secure by a sounder laying of foundations. For, to begin with, every concept—indeed, every question that pure reason sets before us—lies not in experience at all, but once more within reason itself, and must therefore be capable of resolution and of being grasped with respect to its validity or its correctness. Neither are we entitled to turn these problems away under cover of our own incapacity—as though their resolution truly lay in the nature of things—and to refuse them any further inquiry, given that reason has, within its own womb alone, brought forth these very ideas, and is on that account answerable for their validity or their dialectical semblance.

All skeptical polemic is in truth aimed only at the dogmatist—who, without lodging any mistrust in his original objective principles, that is, without critique, proceeds gravely on his way—and its sole purpose is to throw him off his reckoning and bring him around to self-knowledge. Taken by itself, it settles nothing whatever concerning what we know and, conversely, what we cannot know. All the miscarried {{ 792 }} dogmatic ventures of reason are _Facta_, and to subject these to censure is always of use. This, however, can settle nothing about the expectations of reason—about its hoping for, and staking claims to, a happier outcome of its future exertions; and so bare censure can {{{ 499 }}} never bring to a close the quarrel over the rightful entitlements of human reason.

Since Hume is perhaps the most brilliant among all the skeptics, and beyond contradiction the foremost as regards the influence that the skeptical procedure is capable of exerting upon the awakening of a searching examination of reason, it is well worth the trouble to lay out—so far as befits my aim—the course of his reasonings and the wanderings of a man so discerning and so worthy of esteem, wanderings that had, after all, set out upon the track of truth.

Hume may perhaps have had it in his thoughts—though he never brought it to full development—that in a certain class of judgments we reach out beyond our concept of the object. Judgments of that kind I have called ***synthetic***. As for how I might, by means of experience, pass beyond the concept I have hitherto possessed, there is nothing here to give one pause. Experience is itself a synthesis of this sort among perceptions, one that enlarges the concept I possess through a given perception by means of further perceptions that come to be added to it. But we take ourselves to be capable, _a priori_ as well, of passing beyond our concept and enlarging our cognition {{ 793 }}. This we undertake either by means of the pure understanding, with regard to what can at the least be an ***object of experience***, or else even by means of pure reason, with regard to such properties of things—or indeed the existence of such objects—as can never make their appearance in experience. Our skeptic failed to draw this distinction between the two kinds of judgment, as he really ought to have, and flatly took this enlargement of concepts out of their own resources—the self-parturition, so to speak, of our understanding (reason included), unfertilized by any experience—to be impossible, and thus took all the ostensible principles of it that are _a priori_ to be figments, and concluded that they amount to nothing more than a habit arising out of experience and its laws, hence to merely empirical rules, contingent in themselves, to which we impute a spurious necessity and universality. In support of this odd proposition, however, he leaned upon the universally acknowledged principle governing the relation of cause to effect. For, since no faculty of the understanding can carry us from the concept of one thing to the existence of another that would thereby be given as universal and necessary, he supposed himself able to conclude from this that, apart from experience, we possess nothing that could enlarge our concept {{{ 500 }}} and warrant us in any such judgment that expands itself _a priori_. That sunlight, in illuminating the wax, {{ 794 }} should at once melt it while it hardens clay—this no understanding could guess from the concepts we previously held of these things, far less infer by way of a law, and only experience is able to instruct us in such a law. In the transcendental logic, by contrast, we saw that, although we can indeed never pass ***immediately*** beyond the content of the concept given to us, we are nevertheless able—wholly _a priori_, yet in reference to a third thing, namely ***possible*** experience, and so _a priori_ after all—to cognize the law of its connection with other things. Thus, when wax that was formerly solid melts, I can cognize _a priori_ that something must have gone before (the sun's warmth, say) upon which, by a constant law, this melting ensued—even though, without experience, I could cognize neither the cause from the effect nor the effect from the cause in any ***determinate*** fashion _a priori_ and without experience's instruction. He thus wrongly inferred, from the contingency of our determining ***according to the law***, the contingency ***of the law*** itself; and the stepping-out from the concept of a thing toward possible experience—which takes place _a priori_ and constitutes that concept's objective reality—he mistook for the synthesis of objects belonging to actual experience, a synthesis that is of course invariably empirical; by this means, however, he converted a principle of affinity, which is seated in the understanding and pronounces a necessary connection, into a rule of association, such as is encountered solely within the {{ 795 }} reproductive imagination and can exhibit only contingent connections, never objective ones.

The skeptical wanderings of this otherwise supremely acute man, however, arose above all from a shortcoming he nonetheless had in common with all dogmatists—namely, that he did not systematically take in, at one view, all the modes of the understanding's synthesis _a priori_. For in that case—to say nothing of the rest here—he would have discovered, for instance, the ***principle of persistence*** to be one of the same kind, anticipating experience every bit as much as the principle of causality does. By that route he could likewise have marked out determinate limits for the understanding as it expands itself _a priori_, and for pure reason. But since he merely ***restricts*** our understanding without ***bounding*** it—bringing about a general mistrust, yet no determinate acquaintance with the ignorance that is for us unavoidable; since he hauls certain principles of the understanding {{{ 501 }}} before the censor without ever setting this understanding, in the whole of its capacity, upon the assaying-scales of critique; and since, having denied it what it genuinely cannot achieve, he presses on and contests its every capacity for self-expansion _a priori_, notwithstanding that he never brought this whole capacity to appraisal—there befalls him just what invariably fells skepticism: namely, that he is himself thrown into doubt, inasmuch as his objections rest merely upon _Factis_, which are contingent, and not upon principles capable of effecting a necessary renunciation of {{ 796 }} the entitlement to dogmatic assertions.

Because he likewise draws no distinction between the well-grounded claims of the understanding and reason's dialectical presumptions—against which, after all, his attacks are principally aimed—reason, whose wholly characteristic impetus is here not disturbed in the slightest but merely impeded, feels that the room for its own expansion has not been sealed off, and can never be turned aside altogether from its endeavors, however much it gets pinched here and there. For against attacks one girds oneself for a counterdefense, and plants one's head all the more rigidly upon the matter, so as to force one's demands through. A thorough reckoning, however, of the whole of its capacity, and the conviction that thereby arises of the certainty of a modest holding amid the vanity of loftier claims, removes all strife and prompts one to settle, peaceably, for a restricted but indisputable possession.

Against the uncritical dogmatist—who has taken no measure of the sphere of his understanding, and has thus never determined the limits of his possible cognition by principles, and who accordingly does not know beforehand how much lies within his power, but proposes to discover it through mere trial—these skeptical assaults are not merely dangerous but downright ruinous to him. For should he be caught out on so much as a single assertion that he cannot vindicate, {{ 797 }} whose semblance, moreover, he is unable to develop from principles, then the suspicion spreads to all of them, however persuasive they may in other respects always seem.

And so the skeptic is the disciplinarian who schools the dogmatic reasoner toward a sound critique both of the understanding and of reason itself. Once he has reached that point, he need dread no further assault; for he then marks off his own possession from whatever lies wholly beyond it, upon which he raises no claim and over which, accordingly, he cannot become entangled in disputes. The skeptical procedure, therefore, {{{ 502 }}} while in itself unsatisfying for the questions of reason, is nonetheless a form of preparatory practice, apt to rouse reason's wariness and to point it toward thorough means by which it can be secured in its rightful possessions.
