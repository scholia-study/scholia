## {{{ 65 }}} {{ 59 }} § 8. General Remarks on the Transcendental Aesthetic

I. First of all it will be requisite to make plain, with all possible distinctness, what our position is in regard to the basic character of sensible cognition as such, so that no misconstruction of it may arise.

Our intention, then, has been the following: that the whole of our intuition amounts to nothing beyond the representation of appearance; that the things submitted to our intuition are not, as they stand in themselves, what we take them for in intuiting them, and that their relations, too, are not so ordered in themselves as they present themselves to us; and further, that were we to cancel our subject — or even merely the subjective make-up of the senses at large — every characteristic and every relation obtaining among objects throughout space and time, space and time themselves not excepted, would fall away, incapable, qua appearances, of subsisting in their own right and capable of existing only within us. What the bearing of the objects in themselves may be, once abstracted from this entire receptivity of our sensibility, remains for us altogether unknown. We are familiar with nothing beyond the manner in which we ourselves take them up — a manner distinctive of us, which, though it pertains to every human being, need not for all that pertain to every being as such. And this manner alone is what we {{ 60 }} have to deal with. Its pure forms are space and time, while sensation as such supplies its matter. The first of these we are able to cognize solely _a priori_ — in advance, that is, of any actual perception — for which reason it bears the name of pure intuition; the second, by contrast, is that ingredient of our cognition on whose account the cognition is termed _a posteriori_, or empirical intuition. The former cling to our sensibility with unqualified necessity, no matter what character our sensations happen to bear; the latter admit of great variety. Even could we heighten this intuition of ours to the utmost pitch of distinctness, no such heightening would bring us one step nearer to how objects are constituted in themselves. For in every case what we would cognize fully is still only our own manner of intuiting — our sensibility, that is — and this invariably under those {{{ 66 }}} conditions of space and time that cleave to the subject from the outset; as to what the objects might be in their own right, not even the most fully clarified cognition of their appearance, the only cognition granted us, would ever bring it to our knowledge.

The claim, accordingly, that our sensibility in its entirety is nothing but a confused way of representing things — one that captures solely what pertains to them in themselves, yet does so only beneath a heaped-together mass of marks and partial representations that we never draw apart in consciousness — corrupts the very concept of sensibility and of appearance, and thereby drains the whole doctrine concerning them of use and content. The difference {{ 61 }} that sets an indistinct representation apart from a distinct one is purely logical and does not touch the content. There is no doubt that the concept of ***right*** which sound understanding puts to work holds precisely what the finest speculation is able to draw out of it; the only difference is that, in ordinary and practical dealings, one remains unaware of these manifold representations lodged within the thought. Hence it will not do to call the common concept sensible, or to say it harbors a mere appearance; for right admits of no appearing whatever — its concept resides in the understanding and sets forth a property of actions (the moral property) that pertains to them in themselves. The representation of a ***body*** in intuition, on the other hand, holds nothing at all that might pertain to an object taken in itself, but only the appearance of a something, together with the way in which we come to be affected through it; and this receptiveness on the part of our cognitive capacity is what we call sensibility, which stays — however thoroughly one might care to penetrate that appearance (the appearance) to its very base — divided by an immeasurable gulf from cognition of the object in itself.

The philosophy of Leibniz and Wolff has, in consequence, directed every inquiry into the nature and origin of our cognitions along a wholly wrong line of sight, treating the difference between the sensible and the intellectual as a merely logical one, when in truth it is transcendental {{ 62 }} through and through: it concerns not the form of distinctness or its lack, but rather the very source and content of these cognitions, so that by way of sensibility we come to cognize the character of things in themselves not obscurely but not whatsoever, and, the moment our subjective character is withdrawn, the represented object — bearing the properties that sensible intuition had lent it — is to be found nowhere at all, nor {{{ 67 }}} could it be found, since it is just this subjective character that fixes the form of that object as appearance.

In other contexts, to be sure, we mark off, within appearances, that which clings essentially to the intuition of them and obtains for any and every human sense, from that which accrues to them only by accident, being valid not for the relation to sensibility taken generally but merely for some special placement or organization of this or that particular sense. The former kind of cognition is then said to be one that represents the object as it stands in itself, the latter merely the appearance of that object. Yet this distinction is empirical, nothing more. Should one come to rest at this point (as usually happens) and fail to view that empirical intuition, in its turn, as itself mere appearance (as one ought to), so that within it nothing is met with that could bear on any thing in itself, then our transcendental distinction has slipped away, and we go on believing, after all, that we cognize things in themselves, notwithstanding that {{ 63 }} throughout (in the world of sense), right down to the profoundest inquiry into its objects, we are dealing with nothing except appearances. The rainbow, for instance, we shall readily call a mere appearance attending a rain lit by sun, and this rain we shall call the thing in itself — which is indeed right, so long as we take the latter concept in a merely physical sense, namely as that which, throughout the range of ordinary experience and under every diversity of position relative to the senses, stands determined in intuition thus and never otherwise. But should we take this empirical thing quite generally and inquire — with no regard to its concurrence with each human sense — whether it, too, represents an object in itself (not the raindrops, since these, being appearances, already rank as empirical objects), then the inquiry into how the representation stands related to the object turns transcendental, and now not merely those drops are mere appearances, but their rounded figure as well, indeed the very space through which they descend, are nothing in themselves, being no more than modifications, or foundations, of our sensible intuition — while the transcendental object stays unknown to us.

A second matter of weight for our transcendental aesthetic is that it should not win a measure of favor merely as a specious hypothesis, but should carry as much certainty and freedom from doubt as could ever be asked of any theory meant to function as an organon. To set this certainty in a fully convincing light, let us take up some case or other in which {{{ 68 }}} {{ 64 }} its validity may come plainly into view, one apt as well to lend further clarity to what was brought forward in §3.

Grant, then, that space and time are objective in their own right, conditions for the possibility of things as they are in themselves: the first thing to emerge is that from both there issue, _a priori_, apodictic and synthetic propositions in great abundance — pre-eminently in the case of space, which for that very reason we shall single out here for examination by way of illustration. Seeing that geometrical propositions are cognized synthetically _a priori_ and carry apodictic certainty, my question runs: whence do you take propositions of this sort, and upon what does our understanding lean in reaching truths so utterly necessary and universally valid? No path lies open save that of concepts or that of intuitions — both, however, taken as given whether _a priori_ or _a posteriori_. As for the second, namely concepts drawn from experience, and likewise the empirical intuition on which such concepts rest, these can furnish no synthetic proposition beyond one that is itself merely empirical — a proposition of experience — and hence one incapable ever of carrying necessity and absolute universality, the very features that stamp every proposition of geometry. As for what would be the first and sole means — the attaining of cognitions of this kind through bare concepts or through intuitions _a priori_ — it is plain that bare concepts yield no synthetic cognition at all, but analytic {{ 65 }} cognition only. Take just the proposition that two straight lines shut in no space whatever, so that no figure can arise from them, and attempt its derivation from the concept of straight lines together with the number two; or again the proposition that three straight lines make a figure possible, and attempt the like from these concepts on their own. Every exertion of yours comes to nothing, and you find yourself driven to fall back on intuition, exactly as geometry has always done. So you provide yourself with an object in intuition; but of what kind is this intuition — a pure intuition _a priori_, or an empirical one? Were it empirical, no proposition of universal validity, and still less an apodictic one, could ever spring from it; for experience is powerless to supply anything of the sort. You are obliged, then, to furnish your object _a priori_ within intuition and to rest your synthetic proposition upon it. Now suppose there were not resident in you a power of intuiting _a priori_; suppose this subjective {{{ 69 }}} condition, as to its form, were not at once the universal _a priori_ condition beneath which alone the object of such (outer) intuition can itself be; suppose the object (the triangle) were a something in itself, standing in no relation to your subject — how then could you claim that whatever is bound up, in your subjective conditions, with constructing a triangle must belong of necessity to the triangle in itself as well? For to your concepts (of three lines) you could add nothing further (the figure) {{ 66 }} such as would then have to be met with of necessity in the object, given that this object stands there ahead of your cognition and not by means of it. And so, were space (and with it time) not a bare form of your intuition, holding _a priori_ those conditions under which alone things can figure for you as outer objects — things that, apart from these subjective conditions, are in themselves nothing — you would be able to settle nothing synthetic _a priori_ about outer objects at all. It stands therefore beyond all doubt — not as something merely possible, nor even as probable — that space and time, being the conditions required for all experience, outer and inner alike, are no more than subjective conditions attaching to every intuition of ours; and it is with reference to these that all objects rank as sheer appearances rather than as things given, after this fashion, on their own account — objects of which, just on this score, much may be affirmed _a priori_ so far as their form goes, yet never the slightest thing about the thing in itself that may lie at the ground of these appearances.

II. In support of this doctrine of the ideality of both outer and inner sense — hence of all objects of the senses as nothing but appearances — one observation is of particular service: that whatever, in our cognition, belongs to intuition (setting aside, that is, the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, and the will, none of which are cognitions) comprises nothing but mere relations — of positions within an intuition (extension), of the shifting {{ 67 }} of positions (motion), and of the laws by which such shifting is governed (the forces of motion). As for what is present at the position itself, or what, over and above the change of position, is at work within the things themselves, of that nothing is thereby given. But no thing in itself gets cognized through mere relations; so it is reasonable to conclude that, since outer sense delivers to us nothing but representations of relation, {{{ 70 }}} this sense likewise can hold within its representation only how an object stands related to the subject, and not the inner being that belongs to the object in itself. With inner intuition matters stand in just the same way. For not only do the representations of ***outer senses*** furnish the genuine stuff with which we stock our mind; the time, too, in which we set these representations — a time preceding our very awareness of them in experience and lying at their ground as the formal condition of the way in which we lodge them in the mind — carries within it, already, relations of succession, of coexistence, and of what endures alongside succession (the abiding). Now whatever, as representation, can go before every act of thinking anything at all is intuition, and, where it holds nothing but relations, it is the form of intuition — which, representing nothing except so far as something comes to be set within the mind, can amount to nothing else than the way in which the mind, through an activity of its own — namely {{ 68 }} this setting of its representation — and so through itself, comes to be affected, that is, an inner sense as regards its form. Anything that a sense represents is, to that extent, always appearance; and so an inner sense would have either to be denied altogether, or else the subject that forms its object could be represented by it only as appearance, not as that subject would judge of itself if its intuition were pure self-activity, that is, intellectual. The whole of the difficulty here turns solely on how a subject can inwardly intuit its own self; but a difficulty of this kind besets every theory alike. Self-consciousness (apperception) is the simple representation of the I, and were all that is manifold in the subject given, through it alone, in ***self-active*** fashion, then inner intuition would be intellectual. In the human being, such consciousness calls for an inner perception of the manifold already furnished within the subject, and the way in which this manifold reaches the mind devoid of spontaneity has, on account of just this difference, to be named sensibility. If the power of becoming conscious is to search out (to apprehend) what lies within the mind, it must affect that mind, and only thus can it bring forth an intuition of its own self — an intuition, however, whose form, resting antecedently at the ground of the mind, fixes within the representation of time the manner in which the manifold {{ 69 }} lies gathered in the mind; whereupon it intuits itself, not in the way it would, directly and self-actively, {{{ 71 }}} represent itself, but according to the manner in which it undergoes affection from within, and thus as it appears to itself, not as it is in fact.

III. If I say that, in space and time, intuition sets before us both the outer objects and the mind's intuition of itself, each just as it affects our senses — that is, as it ***appears*** — I do not mean by this that such objects would be a mere ***semblance***. For within the appearance the objects — indeed the very characters we attach to them — are invariably taken as something actually given; only that, in so far as such a character depends purely on the subject's mode of intuition in the relation the given object bears to it, this object, as ***appearance***, comes to be distinguished from that same thing as an object ***in itself***. I therefore do not assert that bodies merely ***seem*** to exist outside me, or that my soul merely ***seems*** to be given in my self-consciousness, when I hold that the qualitative character of space and time, conformably to which — as a condition of their existence — I place them both, resides in my mode of intuition and not in these objects taken in themselves. It would be a failing all my own, were I to make a bare semblance out of what I ought to reckon {{ 70 }} as appearance.[^*] This, however, does not follow upon our principle of the ideality of all our sensible intuitions; on the contrary, once one lends ***objective reality*** to those forms of representation, it becomes impossible to keep everything from being turned thereby into sheer ***semblance***. For if one takes space and time to be characters that, in point of their possibility, would have to be encountered in things themselves, and then weighs the absurdities into which one is thereupon drawn — seeing that two infinite things, which are no substances, and equally {{{ 72 }}} nothing that genuinely inheres in substances, yet must for all that be existent, {{ 71 }} nay the necessary condition for the existence of everything, and must moreover persist even when all existing things have been cancelled — then one can hardly reproach the worthy Berkeley for lowering bodies to bare semblance; indeed our own existence too, made in this manner to hang upon the self-standing reality of such a non-thing as time, would have to be converted, together with it, into nothing but semblance — an absurdity that up to now no one has let himself fall into.

IV. In natural theology, where the object one thinks is not merely, for us, no object of intuition whatever, but is such that it can be no object of sensible intuition even to itself, great care is taken to strip away from the whole of its intuition (for its cognition must throughout be intuition of that sort and not ***thought***, which invariably discloses limits) the conditions of time and of space. Yet with what warrant can this be done, once one has already turned the two of them into forms of things in themselves — and forms, at that, which persist _a priori_ as conditions of the existence of things even where one had cancelled the things in their own right: for, as conditions of all existence as such, they would have to be conditions of God's existence no less. There remains no course, if one is unwilling to make them objective {{ 72 }} forms of all things, but to make them subjective forms of our mode of intuition, outer as well as inner — a mode termed sensible for the reason that it is ***not original***, that is, not one by which the very existence of the object of intuition is itself given (a kind that, so far as we can discern, can pertain to the primordial being alone), but rather one that hangs upon the existence of the object, and is therefore possible solely in that the subject's capacity for representation is affected by that object.

Nor need we restrict this manner of intuiting, throughout space and time, to human sensibility; it might well hold that every finite thinking being is bound to accord with the human being on this point (a thing we cannot settle), yet for all this universal validity it does not thereby cease to be sensibility, precisely for the reason that it is derivative (_intuitus derivativus_) and not original (_intuitus originarius_), and so is not intellectual intuition — the latter, on the ground just given, appearing to belong to the primordial being alone, and never to a being that is dependent both in its existence and in its intuition (an intuition that determines that being's {{{ 73 }}} existence in relation to given objects); though this last observation is to be reckoned, within our aesthetic theory, merely as clarification and not as a ground of proof.

[^*]: The predicates belonging to an appearance may be assigned to the object itself in its relation to our sense — say, to the rose its red hue or its scent; semblance, however, can never be assigned to the object as a predicate, just because it credits the object ***for itself*** with what pertains to it only in relation to the senses, or to the subject at large — say, the pair of handles once ascribed to Saturn. Whatever is in no way to be found in the object as it is in itself, but is met with always in that object's relation to the subject and cannot be parted from the representation the subject has of it, is appearance; and on this footing the predicates of space and time are with full right assigned to the objects of the senses as such, and here there is no semblance. If, on the other hand, I credit the rose ***in itself*** with redness, Saturn with the handles, or every outer object with extension ***in itself***, taking no heed of any determinate way in which these objects stand related to the subject and failing to hold my judgment within that limit, then, and only then, does semblance make its appearance.
