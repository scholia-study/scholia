## {{{ 65 }}} {{ 59 }} General Remarks on the Transcendental Aesthetic

I. First, it will be necessary to explain as clearly as possible what our opinion is regarding the fundamental constitution of sensible cognition in general, in order to prevent all misinterpretation of it.

We wanted to say, then, that all our intuition is nothing but the representation of appearance; that the things we intuit are not in themselves what we intuit them to be, nor are their relations so constituted in themselves as they appear to us; and that if we lift away our subject or even only the subjective constitution of the senses in general, all the constitution, all relations of objects in space and time, indeed space and time themselves, would vanish, and as appearances they cannot exist in themselves, but only in us. What may be the case with objects in themselves and abstracted from all this receptivity of our sensibility remains entirely unknown to us. We cognize nothing but our way of perceiving them, which is peculiar to us, and which therefore does not necessarily belong to every being, though it does to every human being. With this {{ 60 }} alone we have to do. Space and time are the pure forms of this perception, sensation in general is its matter. The former alone we can cognize _a priori_, i.e., prior to all actual perception, and it is therefore called pure intuition; the latter, however, is that in our cognition which causes it to be called _a posteriori_ cognition, i.e., empirical intuition. The former adhere to our sensibility absolutely and necessarily, of whatever kind our sensations may be; the latter can be very different. Even if we could bring this intuition of ours to the highest degree of distinctness, we would not thereby come any closer to the constitution of objects in themselves. For in any case we would still completely cognize only our way of intuiting, i.e., our sensibility, and even this always only under the {{{ 66 }}} conditions of space and time originally adhering to the subject; what the objects in themselves may be would never become known to us even through the most enlightened cognition of their appearance, which alone is given to us.

That, therefore, our entire sensibility is nothing but the confused representation of things, which contains solely that which belongs to them in themselves, but only under a heap of marks and partial representations that we do not consciously disentangle, is a falsification of the concept of sensibility and of appearance, which makes the entire doctrine of them useless and empty. The difference {{ 61 }} between an indistinct and a distinct representation is merely logical and does not concern the content. Without doubt, the concept of ***right*** used by common understanding contains the very same thing that the most subtle speculation can develop out of it, only that in common and practical use one is not conscious of these manifold representations in this thought. Therefore, one cannot say that the common concept is sensible and contains a mere appearance, for right can never appear, but rather its concept lies in the understanding and represents a constitution (the moral one) of actions that belongs to them in themselves. By contrast, the representation of a ***body*** in intuition contains nothing at all that could belong to an object in itself, but rather merely the appearance of something and the way in which we are affected by it; and this receptivity of our cognitive capacity is called sensibility and remains, even if one should see through that appearance right to its bottom, nevertheless sky-high distinct from the cognition of the object in itself.

The Leibniz-Wolffian philosophy has therefore assigned an entirely incorrect point of view to all investigations into the nature and origin of our cognitions by considering the distinction between the sensible and the intellectual as merely logical, whereas it is obviously transcendental {{ 62 }} and concerns not merely the form of distinctness or indistinctness, but their origin and content, so that through the former we do not merely cognize the constitution of things in themselves indistinctly, but rather not at all, and, as soon as we take away our subjective constitution, the represented object with the properties that sensible intuition ascribed to it is nowhere to be encountered, nor {{{ 67 }}} can it be encountered, since precisely this subjective constitution determines its form as appearance.

We are indeed otherwise accustomed to distinguish among appearances that which essentially adheres to the intuition of them and holds for every human sense in general, from that which belongs to them only accidentally, in that it is valid not in relation to sensibility in general, but only to a particular position or organization of this or that sense. And then one calls the former cognition one that represents the object in itself, but the latter only the appearance of it. This distinction, however, is merely empirical. If one stops here (as commonly happens) and does not in turn view that empirical intuition (as should happen) as mere appearance, in which nothing at all that concerns any thing in itself is to be encountered, then our transcendental distinction is lost, and we then believe we cognize things in themselves, although we {{ 63 }} have to do with nothing but appearances everywhere (in the world of sense), even in the deepest research into its objects. Thus we would indeed call the rainbow a mere appearance in a sun-shower, but this rain the thing in itself, which is also correct, insofar as we understand the latter concept merely physically, as that which in general experience under all different positions to the senses is yet determined thus and not otherwise in intuition. But if we take this empirical element in general and ask, without turning to its agreement with every human sense, whether this also represents an object in itself (not the raindrops, for these are then already, as appearances, empirical objects), then the question of the relation of the representation to the object is transcendental, and not only are these drops mere appearances, but even their round shape, indeed even the space in which they fall, are nothing in themselves, but rather mere modifications or groundings of our sensible intuition, while the transcendental object remains unknown to us.

The second important concern of our transcendental aesthetic is that it not merely acquire some favor as a plausible hypothesis, but be as certain and indubitable as can ever be demanded of a theory that is to serve as an organon. In order to make this certainty fully clear, we will choose some case wherein {{{ 68 }}} {{ 64 }} its validity can become obvious and serve to clarify further what has been adduced in §3.

Assume, then, that space and time are in themselves objective and conditions of the possibility of things in themselves, then it is first shown: that a large number of apodictic and synthetic propositions occur of both _a priori_, especially of space, which we will therefore investigate here as our primary example. Since the propositions of geometry are cognized synthetically _a priori_ and with apodictic certainty, I ask: whence do you take such propositions, and upon what does our understanding support itself in order to arrive at such absolutely necessary and universally valid truths? There is no other way than through concepts or through intuitions, both of which, however, are given either _a priori_ or _a posteriori_. The latter, namely, empirical concepts, as also that upon which they are grounded, empirical intuition, can yield no synthetic proposition except one that is also merely empirical, i.e., a proposition of experience, and thus can never contain necessity and absolute universality, such as is nevertheless characteristic of all propositions of geometry. But as regards the first and only means, namely, of arriving at such cognitions through mere concepts or through intuitions _a priori_, it is clear that from mere concepts no synthetic cognition, but rather merely analytic cognition {{ 65 }} can be obtained. Take only the proposition that through two straight lines no space at all can be enclosed, and thus no figure is possible, and try to derive it from the concept of straight lines and the number two; or also, that from three straight lines a figure is possible, and try it likewise merely from these concepts. All your effort is in vain, and you find yourself forced to take refuge in intuition, as geometry indeed always does. You therefore give yourself an object in intuition; but of what kind is this, is it a pure intuition _a priori_ or an empirical one? Were it the latter, no universally valid, much less an apodictic proposition could ever arise from it: for experience can never deliver such. You must therefore give your object _a priori_ in intuition, and ground your synthetic proposition upon this. Now if there did not lie in you a capacity to intuit _a priori_; if this subjective {{{ 69 }}} condition as to form were not at the same time the universal _a priori_ condition under which alone the object of this (outer) intuition itself is possible; if the object (the triangle) were something in itself without relation to your subject: how could you say that what lies necessarily in your subjective conditions for constructing a triangle must also belong necessarily to the triangle in itself? For you could not add anything new (the figure) to your concepts (of three lines) {{ 66 }} which must therefore necessarily be encountered in the object, since the latter is given prior to your cognition and not through it. If, therefore, space (and similarly time) were not a mere form of your intuition, which contains conditions _a priori_ under which alone things can be outer objects for you, which without these subjective conditions are in themselves nothing: then you could not determine anything at all synthetically about outer objects _a priori_. It is therefore indubitably certain, and not merely possible or even probable, that space and time, as the necessary conditions of all (outer and inner) experience, are merely subjective conditions of all our intuition, in relation to which therefore all objects are mere appearances and not things given in this way for themselves; of which objects, also, for this reason, much can be said _a priori_ as regards their form, but never the least about the thing in itself that may lie at the ground of these appearances.

II. To confirm this theory of the ideality of outer as well as inner sense, and thus of all objects of the senses as mere appearances, the observation can especially serve: that everything in our cognition that belongs to intuition (thus excepting the feeling of pleasure and displeasure and the will, which are not cognitions at all) contains nothing but mere relations, of places in an intuition (extension), change {{ 67 }} of places (motion), and laws in accordance with which this change is determined (moving forces). But what is present in the place, or what it effects in the things themselves besides the change of place, is not given thereby. Now by mere relations a thing in itself is yet not cognized: hence it is indeed to be judged that since through outer sense nothing but mere relational representations {{{ 70 }}} are given to us, this sense can also contain only the relation of an object to the subject in its representation, and not the inner element that belongs to the object in itself. It is the same with inner intuition. Not only does the representation of ***outer senses*** make up the proper material with which we occupy our mind, but the time in which we place these representations, which itself precedes the consciousness of them in experience and lies at the ground as the formal condition of the way in which we place them in the mind, already contains relations of succession, of coexistence, and of that which is simultaneous with succession (the permanent). Now that which can precede any action of thinking something as representation is intuition, and, if it contains nothing but relations, it is the form of intuition, which, since it represents nothing except insofar as something is placed in the mind, can be nothing other than the way in which the mind is affected by its own activity, namely, {{ 68 }} this placing of its representation, and thus by itself, i.e., an inner sense according to its form. Everything that is represented through a sense is to that extent always appearance, and an inner sense must therefore either not be admitted at all, or the subject, which is the object of it, can be represented through it only as appearance, not as it would judge of itself if its intuition were mere self-activity, i.e., intellectual. Here all difficulty rests only on how a subject can intuit itself internally; but this difficulty is common to every theory. Consciousness of oneself (apperception) is the simple representation of the I, and if through this alone all the manifold in the subject were given ***actively (by self-activity)***, then inner intuition would be intellectual. In humans, this consciousness requires inner perception of the manifold that is previously given in the subject, and the way in which this is given in the mind without spontaneity must be called sensibility for the sake of this distinction. If the capacity to become conscious is to seek out (apprehend) that which lies in the mind, it must affect the mind, and can only in such a way produce an intuition of itself, whose form, however, which previously lies at the ground in the mind, determines the way in which the manifold {{ 69 }} is together in the mind in the representation of time; so that it then intuits itself not as it would represent itself immediately and actively {{{ 71 }}}, but according to the way in which it is affected from within, consequently as it appears to itself, not as it is.

III. When I say: in space and time intuition represents both outer objects and the self-intuition of the mind as they affect our senses, i.e., as they ***appear***, this is not to say that these objects are a mere ***illusion***. For in the appearance the objects, indeed even the properties we ascribe to them, are always regarded as something actually given, only that, insofar as this property depends only on the subject's mode of intuition in relation to the given object, this object as ***appearance*** is distinguished from itself as object ***in itself***. Thus I do not say that bodies ***seem*** merely to be outside me, or that my soul ***seems*** only to be given in my self-consciousness, when I assert that the quality of space and time, in accordance with which, as the condition of their existence, I place both, lies in my mode of intuition and not in these objects in themselves. It would be my own fault if I made a mere illusion out of that which I should count {{ 70 }} as appearance.[^*] This, however, does not happen according to our principle of the ideality of all our sensible intuitions; on the contrary, if one ascribes ***objective reality*** to those forms of representation, then one cannot avoid everything being thereby transformed into mere ***illusion***. For if one regards space and time as properties which, as regards their possibility, must be encountered in things in themselves, and reflects on the absurdities in which one then becomes entangled, in that two infinite things, which are not substances, nor {{{ 72 }}} any thing actually inherent in substances, but yet must be existing, {{ 71 }} indeed the necessary condition of the existence of all things, must remain even if all existing things are lifted away: then one can indeed not blame the good Berkeley if he degraded bodies to mere illusion; indeed, even our own existence, which would in this way be made dependent on the self-subsisting reality of a non-entity such as time, would be transformed along with it into sheer illusion, an absurdity of which no one has yet allowed himself to be guilty.

IV. In natural theology, where one thinks of an object that is not only not an object of intuition for us, but cannot even be an object of sensible intuition to Himself, one is carefully intent on removing the conditions of time and space from all His intuition (for such must all His cognition be, and not ***thinking***, which always demonstrates limitations). But with what right can one do this if one has previously made both into forms of things in themselves, and indeed such as remain _a priori_ as conditions of the existence of things even if one had lifted away the things themselves: for as conditions of all existence in general, they would also have to be conditions of the existence of God. Nothing remains, if one does not wish to make them into objective {{ 72 }} forms of all things, but to make them into subjective forms of our outer as well as inner mode of intuition, which is called sensible for the very reason that it is ***not original***, i.e., such that through it the very existence of the object of intuition is given (which, as far as we can see, can belong only to the primordial Being), but is dependent on the existence of the object, and is thus possible only in that the representative capacity of the subject is affected by it.

It is also not necessary that we limit the mode of intuition in space and time to the sensibility of human beings; it may be that all finite thinking beings must necessarily agree with humans in this (although we cannot decide this), yet even for the sake of this universal validity it does not cease to be sensibility, precisely because it is derivative (_intuitus derivativus_), not original (_intuitus originarius_), mithin not intellectual intuition, which latter, for the reason just adduced, seems to belong solely to the primordial Being, but never to a being that is dependent both as regards its existence and its intuition (which {{{ 73 }}} determines its existence in relation to given objects); although the latter remark must be counted only as an illustration for our aesthetic theory, not as a ground of proof.

[^*]: The predicates of appearance can be ascribed to the object itself in relation to our sense, e.g., the red color or the fragrance to the rose; but illusion can never be ascribed to the object as a predicate, for the very reason that it ascribes to the object ***for itself*** what belongs to it only in relation to the senses or to the subject in general, e.g., the two handles that were initially ascribed to Saturn. That which is not to be encountered at all in the object in itself, but is yet always to be found in the relation of the same to the subject and is inseparable from the representation of the latter, is appearance, and thus the predicates of space and of time are rightly ascribed to the objects of the senses as such, and herein is no illusion. By contrast, if I ascribe redness to the rose ***in itself***, the handles to Saturn, or extension ***in itself*** to all outer objects without looking to a determinate relation of these objects to the subject and limiting my judgment thereto, then and only then does illusion arise.
