## 4. The postulates of empirical thought in general

1. Anything that accords with experience's formal conditions (with respect to intuition and to concepts alike) is ***possible***.

2. {{ 266 }} Anything bound up with the material conditions of experience (with sensation) is ***actual***.

3. {{{ 186 }}} Anything whose connection to the actual stands determined by experience's general conditions is (exists) ***necessary***.

## Elucidation.

A distinctive feature attaches to the categories of modality: as predicates appended to a concept, they add nothing whatever to it as a determination of the object, and merely voice its bearing on the faculty of cognition. Even where the concept of a thing is already fully complete, I may yet inquire, regarding that object, whether it is merely possible, or actual as well, or—should it be the latter—whether it is moreover necessary? No additional determinations get thought into the object itself by this means; what is asked is solely how the object (with all its determinations included) is situated toward the understanding and its empirical employment, toward the empirical power of judgment, and toward reason (as applied to experience).

It is for just this reason, too, that the principles of modality amount to nothing beyond clarifications of the concepts of possibility, actuality, and necessity as employed empirically, and with that, simultaneously, to restrictions confining every category to a merely empirical employment, disallowing and refusing the transcendental {{ 267 }} one. For should these concepts carry more than a bare logical import and do more than express analytically the form of ***thinking***—should they instead pertain to ***things*** together with their possibility, actuality, or necessity—then their reach must extend to possible experience and to its synthetic unity, wherein alone objects of cognition come to be given.

The postulate concerning the possibility of things demands, accordingly, that the concept of them harmonize with the formal conditions of an experience as such. These conditions, however—that is, the objective form of experience taken generally—embrace every synthesis demanded for cognizing objects. Should a concept enclose a synthesis within it, that concept counts as empty and answers to no object whenever the synthesis in question lies outside experience—either drawn from experience, whereupon we speak of an ***empirical concept***, or serving as the _a priori_ condition upon which experience generally (its very form) rests, whereupon we have a ***pure concept*** belonging to experience even so, since its object admits of being met with nowhere but there. For whence should one fetch the sign {{{ 187 }}} of an object's possibility, where that object has been thought by way of an _a priori_ synthetic concept, if not from the very synthesis constituting the form under which objects are cognized empirically {{ 268 }}? Freedom from contradiction within such a concept is, to be sure, a logical condition that cannot be dispensed with; yet for the concept's objective reality—that is, for the possibility of the sort of object thought through it—this falls far short of sufficing. So it is that the concept of a figure bounded by two straight lines harbors no contradiction, since the concepts of two straight lines and of their intersection do not by themselves negate a figure; the impossibility here rests not with the concept as such but with constructing it in space—that is, with the conditions of space and its determining; and these conditions, in turn, possess objective reality of their own, which is to say they bear upon possible things, for they hold within themselves, _a priori_, the form of experience in general.

Let us now bring before our eyes just how widely useful and influential this postulate of possibility proves to be. Suppose I represent to myself an abiding thing, such that whatever undergoes change belongs only to its state: from a concept of that kind, taken by itself, I can never make out that a thing so conceived is possible. Or suppose I represent something meant to be so constituted that, once it is posited, invariably and without fail something further ensues from it—such a thing may well be conceivable free of contradiction; but whether any property of this sort (namely causality) is encountered in some possible thing is not thereby settled. Lastly, I may represent to myself several things {{ 269 }} (substances) constituted so that the state of one entails a consequent state in another, and this reciprocally; yet whether a relation of that kind can belong to any things is in no way to be gathered from these concepts, which hold nothing but an arbitrary synthesis. Their objective reality—that is to say, their transcendental truth—is recognized, then, on this ground alone: that these concepts articulate _a priori_ the relations that perceptions bear within every experience; recognized, indeed, in a manner free of dependence on experience, and yet not free of all reference to the form of an experience as such and to that synthetic unity wherein alone objects lend themselves to empirical cognition.

But were someone to wish to fashion wholly novel concepts of substances, of forces, {{{ 188 }}} of reciprocal actions from the material that perception lays before us, without taking over from experience itself any instance of their combination, then he would lapse into nothing but fantasies of the brain, whose possibility is attended by no sign at all, seeing that with them experience is neither enlisted as instructress nor made the source from which the concepts are drawn. Fabricated concepts of this kind cannot come by the sign of their possibility after the manner of the categories—_a priori_, as conditions on which all experience hangs—but only _a posteriori_, as concepts furnished through experience itself, and {{ 270 }} their possibility is either known _a posteriori_ and empirically or not known whatsoever. Take a substance that would be abidingly present in space and yet leave it unfilled (that intermediate entity between matter and thinking beings which certain people have wanted to bring in), or some peculiar basic power of our mind for intuiting the future beforehand (rather than merely inferring it), or, lastly, a capacity of the mind for sharing in a community of thoughts with other human beings (however far off they might be): these are concepts whose possibility rests on nothing at all, since it admits of no grounding in experience and its familiar laws, and apart from these it comes to no more than an arbitrary linkage of thoughts—one that, contradiction-free though it is, can lay no claim to objective reality, and thus none to the possibility of any such object as one means here to think. Where reality is concerned, it more or less rules itself out to think a reality _in concreto_ without summoning experience to assist: for reality can bear on sensation alone, as experience's matter, and does not touch the form of the relation—that form with which, if anything, one might toy in inventions.

I set aside, however, everything whose possibility is only to be gleaned from actuality within experience, and I dwell here solely on the possibility of things by way of _a priori_ concepts, of which I go on to {{ 271 }} assert that it can obtain never from such concepts standing alone, but on every occasion only as the formal and objective conditions of an experience as such.

There is, admittedly, a semblance that a triangle's possibility might be recognized from its concept taken on its own (independent of experience it surely is); for we can in truth confer on it an object entirely _a priori_—that is, construct it. Yet since this {{{ 189 }}} yields only the form of an object, the triangle would forever stay a mere product of imagining, its object's possibility still in doubt, inasmuch as something further is called for—namely, that such a figure be thought under nothing but those conditions upon which all objects of experience repose. That space is a formal _a priori_ condition of outer experiences; that the selfsame figurative synthesis by which we construct a triangle in imagination is one and the same as that which we carry out in apprehending an appearance so as to frame an empirical concept of it—this, and this alone, is what ties to the concept the representation of such a thing's possibility. And in this way the possibility of continuous magnitudes—indeed of magnitudes at large, their concepts being one and all synthetic—first grows clear never out of the {{ 272 }} concepts on their own, but out of them functioning as formal conditions for determining objects in experience generally; and where, after all, would one so much as look for objects answering to the concepts, if not within experience, by which alone objects reach us? This despite the fact that, without actually dispatching experience itself in advance, we can—purely by reference to the formal conditions under which anything at all is determined within experience as an object, and hence altogether _a priori_, though still only by reference to those conditions and inside their limits—recognize and characterize the possibility of things.

The postulate for cognizing the ***actuality*** of things calls for ***perception***, and so for sensation of which one is aware; not, indeed, in immediate touch with the object itself whose existence is to be cognized, but at least the object's connection with some actual perception, following the analogies of experience, which lay out every real linkage within an experience taken generally.

Within the ***mere concept*** of a thing not a single sign of its existence can be come upon. For however complete that concept may be—so that nothing in the least is wanting for thinking a thing along with all its inner determinations—existence has, for all that, no bearing on any of this, but bears solely on the question whether such a thing is given to us, so that its perception might, if it comes to that, run ahead of the concept {{ 273 }}. For the concept's running ahead of the perception betokens the thing's mere possibility, whereas the perception, which supplies the matter for the concept, is the one and only sign of {{{ 190 }}} actuality. One may, however, cognize a thing's existence even ahead of perceiving it—thus comparatively _a priori_—provided only that it hangs together with certain perceptions by the principles governing their empirical linkage (the analogies). For then the thing's existence does, after all, cohere with our perceptions within a possible experience, and, guided by the thread of those analogies, we are able to pass from our actual perception across to the thing along the series of possible perceptions. It is thus that we cognize the existence of a magnetic matter pervading every body from our perception of iron filings drawn toward it, notwithstanding that a direct perception of this stuff lies beyond us, given how our organs are constituted. For, generally speaking, following the laws of sensibility and the connectedness of our perceptions within an experience, we should light upon the direct empirical intuition of that matter as well, were our senses more refined—their coarseness being wholly irrelevant to the form of possible experience as such. Wherever, then, perception together with its train extends by empirical laws, there likewise extends our cognition of the {{ 274 }} existence of things. Should we not set out from experience, or fail to advance by the laws of the empirical interconnection of appearances, we vainly make a show of trying to guess at or track down the existence of any thing whatever. One forceful objection, though, against these rules for proving existence in a mediated way is pressed by ***idealism***, and here is the fitting place to refute it.

***

## Refutation of idealism.

Idealism—the ***material*** sort is what I have in mind—is that doctrine which pronounces the existence of objects in space beyond us to be either merely questionable and ***indemonstrable*** or else outright false and ***impossible***; the ***former*** is the ***problematic*** idealism of Descartes, who holds but a single empirical claim (assertio)—to wit, ***I am***—beyond doubt; the ***second*** is the ***dogmatic*** idealism of Berkeley, who pronounces space {{{ 191 }}}, along with all the things it clings to as their inseparable condition, to be something impossible in itself, and on that account pronounces the things in space, too, mere figments. Dogmatic idealism becomes inescapable once space is viewed as a property meant to belong to things in themselves; for then space, with everything it underlies as condition, turns into an absurdity. The basis of that idealism, however, we have already removed in the transcendental aesthetic. Problematic idealism, by contrast—advancing no thesis on the matter and merely professing our inability to demonstrate, through immediate experience, an existence apart from our own—is reasonable and befits a searching philosophical cast of mind: namely, to sanction no conclusive judgment until a sufficient proof has been secured. {{ 275 }} The proof thus called for has to establish that of outer things we possess ***experience*** and not bare ***imagination***; and this can scarcely be accomplished otherwise than by showing that even our ***inner*** experience—undoubted by Descartes—the very ***experience*** in question, is possible only on the supposition of an outer experience.

## Theorem.

***The bare consciousness of my own existence—empirically determined though it is—establishes the existence of objects in space beyond me.***

## Proof.

Of my own existence I am conscious as determined within time. Every time-determination presupposes something ***abiding*** in perception. This abiding element, however, cannot be anything lodged in me, precisely because my own existence in time is what first stands to be determined by means of it. The perception of this abiding element is therefore possible solely through a thing external to me, and not through the bare ***representation*** of such a thing. It follows that my existence in time can be determined only {{ 276 }} by way of the existence of actual things that I perceive outside myself. Now consciousness in time is necessarily bound up with consciousness of the possibility of this time-determination; hence it is likewise necessarily bound up with the existence of things outside me as the condition of that time-determination—which is to say, consciousness of my own existence {{{ 192 }}} is at one and the same time an immediate consciousness of the existence of other things beyond me.

***Remark 1.*** In the foregoing proof one notices that the very game idealism was playing is turned back upon it, and with the better right. Idealism took it that the sole immediate experience is the inner one, from which outer things are then only ***inferred***—yet unreliably, as happens whenever one reasons from given effects to ***determinate*** causes, since the cause of the representations we may wrongly pin on outer things could just as well reside within ourselves. Here, however, it is demonstrated that outer experience {{ 277 }} is in fact the immediate one,[^*] and that only through its mediation does there become possible—not the consciousness of our own existence, to be sure, but the determining of that existence in time, which is to say inner experience. Granted, the representation ***I am***, giving voice to the consciousness capable of accompanying all thinking, is what immediately carries within it the existence of a subject, yet without any ***cognition*** of that subject as yet, and so no empirical cognition either, i.e., experience; for such cognition demands, over and above the thought of something existent, intuition besides—here inner intuition—with respect to which, namely time, the subject has to be determined, a task for which outer objects are indispensable throughout, so that inner experience itself turns out to be possible only mediately, and only by way of outer experience.

***Remark 2.*** With this every empirical employment of our cognitive faculty in determining time now falls perfectly into line. Not merely can we perceive all time-determination solely through the alteration of outer relations (motion) as referred to what abides in {{ 278 }} space (say, the sun's motion relative to the objects on earth); beyond that, we possess nothing abiding at all to place {{{ 193 }}} beneath the concept of a substance by way of intuition, save ***matter*** alone, and even this abidingness is not skimmed from outer experience but presupposed _a priori_ as the necessary condition of all time-determination, and thus likewise as a determination of inner sense—regarding our own existence—by the existence of outer things. My consciousness of my own self in the representation ***I*** is no intuition whatever, but a purely ***intellectual*** representation of the self-activity belonging to a thinking subject. This I, accordingly, carries not the slightest predicate of intuition that might, as something ***abiding***, serve time-determination in inner sense for a correlate—the way ***impenetrability***, for instance, attaches to matter as ***empirical*** intuition.

***Remark 3.*** That the existence of outer objects is requisite for the possibility of a determinate consciousness of our own selves does not entail that every intuitive representation of outer things carries their existence along with it, for such a representation may perfectly well be the sheer work of imagination (in dreams no less than in madness); it is so, however, only through reproducing former outer perceptions, which, as has been shown, are themselves possible only through the actuality of outer objects. All that was to be proved at this point is that inner experience in general is possible only by means of outer {{ 279 }} experience in general. Whether some supposed experience or other might be nothing but imagination is a thing to be settled by that experience's particular determinations and by measuring it against the criteria of all actual experience.

***

Turning at last to the third postulate: it concerns the material necessity belonging to existence, not the merely formal and logical necessity found in the linkage of concepts. Now, since no existence of objects of the senses admits of being cognized wholly _a priori_—though it may be so comparatively _a priori_, relative to some other, already given existence—and since even then one can only ever reach the existence that has to be lodged somewhere within the experiential nexus of which the given perception forms a part: the necessity of an existence is never to be cognized out of concepts, but on every occasion only out of its linkage with {{{ 194 }}} whatever is being perceived, in keeping with the universal laws of experience. Now there exists nothing that could be cognized as necessary under the condition of other given appearances save the existence of effects issuing from given causes according to laws of causality. It is therefore not the existence of things (substances), but that of their state, whose necessity is all that we can cognize, and {{ 280 }} that from other states given in perception, following empirical laws of causality. Whence it follows that the touchstone of necessity resides entirely in the law of possible experience: that whatever comes to pass stands determined _a priori_ through its cause within appearance. Hence it is only the necessity of ***effects*** within nature, whose causes are given us, that we cognize; and the token of necessity in existence stretches no farther than the field of possible experience, and even inside that field it does not apply to the existence of things as substances, since these can never be looked upon as empirical effects, nor as anything that comes to pass and arises. Necessity, then, has to do only with the relations among appearances by the dynamical law of causality, and with the possibility, resting thereon, of inferring _a priori_ from some given existence (a cause) to another existence (its effect). Whatever comes to pass is hypothetically necessary; such is the principle that places the world's alteration under a law—a rule of necessary existence, absent which no nature could so much as occur. Hence the maxim that nothing comes about by blind hazard (_in mundo non datur casus_) is an _a priori_ law of nature; and equally, that no necessity in nature is a blind one, but rather conditioned and thereby comprehensible {{ 281 }} necessity (_non datur fatum_). Both are laws through which the interplay of alterations is made subject to a ***nature of things*** (as appearances)—or, what amounts to the same, to the unity of the understanding, within which alone those alterations can belong to a single experience as the synthetic unity of appearances. These two principles fall among the dynamical ones. The first is, strictly, a corollary of the principle of causality (as it stands among the analogies of experience). The second falls among the principles of modality, which annex to the causal determination the further concept of necessity—a necessity that, however, stands beneath a rule of the understanding. The principle of continuity had barred, within the series of appearances (alterations), every abrupt leap {{{ 195 }}} (_in mundo non datur saltus_), and barred as well, within the whole assemblage of empirical intuitions in space, every gap or chasm between two appearances (_non datur hiatus_); for the proposition admits of being put thus: nothing can find its way into experience that would evidence a vacuum, or even let one pass as a part of the empirical synthesis. As for the void one might think outside the field of possible experience (outside the world), that does not come before the tribunal of the mere understanding, which passes verdict only on questions bearing on the use of given appearances toward empirical cognition; it is instead a matter for idealizing reason, which reaches out past the {{ 282 }} sphere of a possible experience and would pronounce on what encloses and delimits that experience itself, and so must be pondered within the transcendental dialectic. These four propositions, namely (_in mundo non datur hiatus, non datur saltus, non datur casus, non datur fatum_), we could readily set forth, as with every principle of transcendental provenance, in an order matching the order of the categories, and prove to each its allotted place; but the reader already practiced will manage this unaided, or will easily hit upon the thread leading to it. They all converge, however, on this single aim: to let nothing into the empirical synthesis that might do damage or detriment to the understanding and to the continuous interconnection of all appearances—that is, to the unity of its concepts. For the understanding is the sole place in which the unity of experience, where all perceptions must find their station, comes to be possible.

Whether the field of the possible outruns the field embracing all that is actual, and whether the latter in turn outruns the sum of what is necessary—these are pretty questions, calling moreover for a synthetic resolution, and they too fall to nothing but the tribunal of reason; for they amount to asking, roughly, whether all things as appearances belong collectively to the single assemblage and context of one experience—of which each given perception is a part {{ 283 }}, one incapable therefore of being joined with any further appearances—or whether my perceptions may belong to more than a single possible experience (within its general interconnection). To experience at large the understanding furnishes _a priori_ only the rule, conforming to the subjective and formal conditions of sensibility no less than of apperception, which alone render experience possible. Other forms of intuition (than space and time), and other forms of understanding as well (than the discursive {{{ 196 }}} form of thinking, or of cognition through concepts), we are utterly unable to devise or render intelligible to ourselves, possible though they might be; and even could we do so, they still would not belong to experience—the one cognition wherein objects are given us. Whether perceptions other than those belonging generally to our whole possible experience, and thus a wholly other field of matter, might yet occur is something the understanding cannot decide; its concern runs only to the synthesis of what is given. Otherwise, how paltry our customary inferences are—by which we conjure up a vast kingdom of the possible, whereof everything actual (every object of experience) is but a slight portion—stands glaringly plain. Everything actual is possible; from which, by the logical rules of conversion, there naturally issues the merely particular proposition that some possible things {{ 284 }} are actual, which then appears to signify as much as: many things are possible that are not actual. There is, granted, a semblance that one might even, outright, set the count of the possible above that of the actual, on the ground that something must still accrue to the former in order to constitute the latter. This accrual to the possible, however, is unknown to me. For whatever were to be superadded over and above it would be impossible. To my understanding there can accrue only something beyond the concord with the formal conditions of experience—namely, linkage with some perception or other; and whatever is linked to that by empirical laws is actual, even where it goes unperceived directly. But that, in thoroughgoing linkage with what perception gives me, a further series of appearances—and hence more than one all-encompassing experience—should be possible is not to be inferred from what is given, and, where nothing at all is given, still less so, since without matter nothing whatever lends itself to thought. Whatever is possible only under conditions that are themselves merely possible is not possible in ***every respect***. It is in this latter sense, though, that the question gets taken up when one wants to know whether the possibility of things ranges further than experience is able to reach.

I have touched on these questions only so as to leave no gap in {{ 285 }} what, on the common view, is reckoned to fall among the concepts of the understanding. In fact, however, absolute possibility (the possibility that holds good in every respect) constitutes no mere concept of the understanding and can be of no empirical service whatever; it belongs to {{{ 197 }}} reason alone, which reaches out beyond every possible empirical employment of the understanding. For that reason we have had to rest content here with a purely critical remark, leaving the matter otherwise in obscurity pending some further, future handling.

As I mean now to bring to a close this fourth number, and with it the entire system of the principles of the pure understanding, I owe still an account of why I have styled the principles of modality postulates in particular. I decline to take the term here in the sense certain more recent philosophical writers have lent it—against the usage of the mathematicians, to whom it truly belongs—namely, that to postulate should amount to putting forward a proposition as immediately certain, without any warrant or proof; for were we to concede this in the case of synthetic propositions, however self-evident they might appear—conceding that, deduction aside, they may be pinned upon unqualified assent on the strength of their own mere pronouncement—then the whole critique of the understanding is forfeit, and since bold pretensions are never in short supply, ones from which even common belief {{ 286 }} (which is no credential, though) does not hold back, our understanding will lie exposed to every fancy, powerless to refuse its assent to pronouncements which, illegitimate though they be, nonetheless demand admission as genuine axioms in that very same tone of self-assurance. Whenever, then, a determination gets added _a priori_ and synthetically to the concept of a thing, such a proposition must, without exception, have appended to it—if not a proof, then at the least a deduction of its assertion's legitimacy.

The principles of modality, however, are not objectively synthetic, since the predicates of possibility, actuality, and necessity in no degree enlarge the concept of which they are predicated, as though they set something more alongside the representation of the object. Yet being, all the same, forever synthetic, they are so only subjectively—that is, to the concept of a thing (of something real), about which they otherwise pronounce nothing, they annex the cognitive power in which that concept springs up and has its seat: so that, standing merely in the understanding in linkage with the formal conditions of experience, its object is termed possible; standing in connection with perception (sensation as the matter of the senses) and determined thereby, through the understanding's mediation, the object is actual; determined through the connection of perceptions {{ 287 }} in keeping with concepts, the object is termed necessary. {{{ 198 }}} What the principles of modality state of a concept, then, is nothing beyond the act of the cognitive faculty by which that concept is brought forth. Now in mathematics a postulate designates the practical proposition holding nothing but the synthesis by which we first supply ourselves an object and bring forth its concept—say, to describe a circle on a plane, from a given point, with a given line; and a proposition of that kind is for that very reason unprovable, since the procedure it prescribes is precisely the one by which we first bring forth the concept of such a figure. On just the same warrant, accordingly, we are entitled to postulate the principles of modality, for they contribute no increase to their concept of things as such,[^**] but merely register the manner in which such a concept comes to be bound up with the cognitive power at all.

***

[^*]: The ***immediate*** consciousness of the existence of outer things is, in the theorem set out above, not presupposed but proved—whether or not we manage to see into the possibility of such consciousness. The question touching that possibility would run: do we have only an inner sense and no outer one, nothing but outer imagining? Yet plainly, in order to imagine anything whatever as outer—that is, to set it before sense in intuition—we must already be in possession of an outer sense, and by its means distinguish straightaway the mere receptivity of an outer intuition from the spontaneity that marks every act of imagining. For merely to imagine an outer sense as well would be to abolish the very faculty of intuition that is to be determined by the power of imagination.

[^**]: ***By the actuality*** of a thing I do, admittedly, posit something more than its possibility, yet not ***in the thing*** itself; for actuality can never hold more than what its complete possibility already held. Rather, possibility having been no more than a positing of the thing in relation to the understanding (to its empirical employment), actuality is at the same time the thing's linkage with perception.
