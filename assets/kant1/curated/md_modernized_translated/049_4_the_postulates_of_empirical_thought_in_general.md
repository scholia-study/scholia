## 4. The postulates of empirical thought in general

1. What agrees with the formal conditions of experience (in accordance with intuition and concepts) is ***possible***.

2. {{ 266 }} What is connected with the material conditions of experience (sensation) is ***actual***.

3. {{{ 186 }}} That whose connection with the actual is determined according to general conditions of experience is (exists) ***necessary***.

## Elucidation.

The categories of modality have this peculiarity: that as determinations of the object they do not in the least augment the concept to which they are attached as predicates, but only express its relation to the faculty of cognition. If the concept of a thing is already quite complete, I can still ask of this object whether it is merely possible or also actual, or, if it is the latter, whether it is indeed also necessary? Through this, no further determinations are thought in the object itself, but rather the only question is how it (together with all its determinations) relates to the understanding and its empirical use, to the empirical power of judgment, and to reason (in its application to experience).

For this very reason, the principles of modality are nothing further than explanations of the concepts of possibility, actuality, and necessity in their empirical use, and therewith at the same time restrictions of all categories to merely empirical use, without admitting or permitting the transcendental {{ 267 }} [use]. For if these are not to have a merely logical significance and to express the form of ***thinking*** analytically, but are to concern ***things*** and their possibility, actuality, or necessity, then they must extend to possible experience and its synthetic unity, in which alone objects of cognition are given.

The postulate of the possibility of things therefore requires that their concept agree with the formal conditions of an experience in general. This, namely the objective form of experience in general, contains all synthesis that is required for the cognition of objects. A concept that contains a synthesis within itself is to be held as empty and relates to no object if this synthesis does not belong to experience, either as borrowed from it, and then it is called an ***empirical concept***, or as one upon which, as an _a priori_ condition, experience in general (its form) rests, and then it is a ***pure concept***, which nevertheless belongs to experience, because its object can be encountered only in the latter. For whence does one want to take the character {{{ 187 }}} of the possibility of an object that has been thought through a synthetic concept _a priori_, if it does not occur from the synthesis that constitutes the form of the empirical cognition of objects {{ 268 }}? That in such a concept no contradiction must be contained is indeed a necessary logical condition; but it is by far not enough for the objective reality of the concept, i.e., the possibility of such an object as is thought through the concept. Thus in the concept of a figure enclosed by two straight lines there is no contradiction, for the concepts of two straight lines and their intersection contain no negation of a figure; but the impossibility rests not on the concept in itself, but on its construction in space, i.e., on the conditions of space and its determination; these, however, have in turn their objective reality, i.e., they apply to possible things, because they contain in themselves the form of experience in general _a priori_.

And now we want to present to view the extensive utility and influence of this postulate of possibility. If I represent to myself a thing that is persistent, so that everything that changes belongs merely to its state, I can never cognize from such a concept alone that such a thing is possible. Or if I represent to myself something that is supposed to be so constituted that, if it is posited, something else always and inevitably follows from it, this may indeed be thought in this way without contradiction; but whether such a property (as causality) is to be encountered in any possible thing cannot be judged thereby. Finally, I can represent to myself different things {{ 269 }} (substances) that are so constituted that the state of one draws after it a consequence in the state of the other, and so reciprocally; but whether such a relation can belong to any things at all cannot be gathered in the least from these concepts, which contain a merely arbitrary synthesis. Only from the fact, therefore, that these concepts express _a priori_ the relations of perceptions in every experience, does one cognize their objective reality, i.e., their transcendental truth, and indeed to be sure independently of experience, yet not independently of all relation to the form of an experience in general and the synthetic unity in which alone objects can be empirically cognized.

But if one wanted to construct entirely new concepts of substances, of forces, {{{ 188 }}} of reciprocal actions from the matter that perception offers us, without borrowing the example of their connection from experience itself: then one would fall into mere chimeras, whose possibility has absolutely no sign to support it, because in their case one does not take experience as one's instructor, nor borrow these concepts from it. Such fabricated concepts cannot receive the character of their possibility _a priori_ as conditions upon which all experience depends, in the manner of the categories, but only _a posteriori_ as those that are given through experience itself, and {{ 270 }} their possibility must either be cognized _a posteriori_ and empirically, or it cannot be cognized at all. A substance that would be persistently present in space, yet without filling it (like that intermediate thing between matter and thinking beings which some have wished to introduce), or a special fundamental power of our mind to intuit the future beforehand (and not, say, merely to infer it), or finally a faculty of the mind to stand in community of thoughts with other people (however distant they might be): these are concepts whose possibility is entirely groundless, because it cannot be based on experience and its known laws, and without them it is an arbitrary combination of thoughts which, although it contains no contradiction, can yet make no claim to objective reality, hence to the possibility of such an object as one here wishes to think. As far as reality is concerned, it is obviously forbidden of itself to think of such a thing _in concreto_ without calling in the aid of experience: because it can only apply to sensation, as the matter of experience, and does not concern the form of relation, with which one might at any rate play in fabrications.

But I pass over everything whose possibility can only be gathered from actuality in experience, and consider here only the possibility of things through concepts _a priori_, concerning which I continue to {{ 271 }} assert that they can never occur from such concepts for themselves alone, but always only as formal and objective conditions of an experience in general.

It indeed has the appearance as if the possibility of a triangle could be cognized from its concept in itself (it is certainly independent of experience); for in fact we can give it an object entirely _a priori_, i.e., construct it. Because this {{{ 189 }}}, however, is only the form of an object, it would still always remain a mere product of the imagination, concerning whose object the possibility would still remain doubtful, as that for which something more is required, namely, that such a figure be thought under the very conditions on which all objects of experience rest. That space is a formal _a priori_ condition of outer experiences, that this very same formative synthesis through which we construct a triangle in the imagination is entirely identical with that which we exercise in the apprehension of an appearance in order to make an empirical concept of it: this alone is what connects the representation of the possibility of such a thing with this concept. And so the possibility of continuous magnitudes, indeed even of magnitudes in general, because the concepts of them are altogether synthetic, is never clear from the concepts {{ 272 }} themselves, but first of all from them as formal conditions of the determination of objects in experience in general; and where else would one want to seek objects that correspond to the concepts, if it were not in experience, through which alone objects are given to us? Although we, without even presupposing experience itself, but merely in relation to the formal conditions under which something is determined as an object in it at all, and thus completely _a priori_, yet only in relation to it and within its limits, can cognize and characterize the possibility of things.

The postulate for cognizing the ***actuality*** of things requires ***perception***, hence sensation of which one is conscious; not indeed immediately of the object itself whose existence is to be cognized, but yet its connection with some actual perception in accordance with the analogies of experience, which exhibit all real connection in an experience in general.

In the ***mere concept*** of a thing, no character of its existence can be found at all. For although it may be ever so complete, so that not the least is lacking to think a thing with all its inner determinations, yet existence has nothing at all to do with all this, but only with the question: whether such a thing is given to us in such a way that the perception of it could at least precede the concept {{ 273 }}. For that the concept precedes perception signifies its mere possibility; but perception, which provides the matter for the concept, is the sole character of {{{ 190 }}} actuality. But one can also cognize the existence of a thing prior to its perception, and thus comparatively _a priori_, if it is only connected with some perceptions in accordance with the principles of their empirical connection (the analogies). For in that case the existence of the thing is still connected with our perceptions in a possible experience, and we can, under the guidance of those analogies, proceed from our actual perception to the thing in the series of possible perceptions. Thus we cognize the existence of a magnetic matter penetrating all bodies from the perception of attracted iron filings, although an immediate perception of this matter is impossible for us given the constitution of our organs. For in general, in accordance with the laws of sensibility and the context of our perceptions in an experience, we would also come upon the immediate empirical intuition of it if our senses were finer, the coarseness of which does not affect the form of possible experience in general. Wherever perception and its appendage reach in accordance with empirical laws, there also reaches our cognition of the {{ 274 }} existence of things. If we do not begin with experience, or do not proceed in accordance with the laws of the empirical connection of appearances, we make a vain display of wanting to guess or investigate the existence of any thing. But a powerful objection to these rules for proving existence mediately is made by ***idealism***, the refutation of which is here in its proper place.

***

## Refutation of Idealism.

Idealism (I understand by it the ***material*** one) is the theory that declares the existence of objects in space outside us to be either merely doubtful and ***indemonstrable***, or false and ***unimpossible***; the ***former*** is the ***problematic*** idealism of Cartesius, who declares only one empirical assertion (assertio), namely: ***I am***, to be indubitable; the ***second*** is the ***dogmatic*** idealism of Berkeley, who declares space {{{ 191 }}}, with all the things to which it adheres as an inseparable condition, to be something that is in itself impossible, and therefore also declares the things in space to be mere imaginations. Dogmatic idealism is unavoidable if one regards space as a property that is to belong to things in themselves; for then it, along with everything to which it serves as a condition, is a non-entity. The ground for this idealism, however, has been removed by us in the transcendental Aesthetic. Problematic idealism, which asserts nothing about this, but rather only alleges our incapacity to prove an existence outside of our own through immediate experience, is rational and conforming to a thorough philosophical way of thinking: namely, not to permit any decisive judgment before a sufficient proof has been found. {{ 275 }} The required proof must therefore demonstrate that we have also ***experience*** and not merely ***imagination*** of outer things; which will probably not be capable of being done unless one can prove that even our ***inner*** experience, which is indubitable to Cartesius, is possible only under the presupposition of outer experience.

## Theorem.

***The mere, but empirically determined consciousness of my own existence proves the existence of objects in space outside me.***

## Proof.

I am conscious of my existence as determined in time. All time-determination presupposes something ***persistent*** in perception. This persistent thing, however, cannot be something in me, because my very existence in time can first be determined only through this persistent thing. Therefore, the perception of this persistent thing is possible only through a thing outside me and not through the mere ***representation*** of a thing outside me. Consequently, the determination of my existence in time is possible only {{ 276 }} through the existence of actual things that I perceive outside me. Now consciousness in time is necessarily connected with the consciousness of the possibility of this time-determination: therefore it is also necessarily connected with the existence of things outside me, as a condition of time-determination; i.e., the consciousness of my own existence {{{ 192 }}} is at the same time an immediate consciousness of the existence of other things outside me.

***Note 1.*** One perceives in the preceding proof that the game played by idealism has been turned against it with greater justice. Idealism assumed that the only immediate experience is inner experience, and that from it we only ***infer*** outer things, but, as always when one infers ***determinate*** causes from given effects, only unreliably, since the cause of the representations can also lie in ourselves, which we perhaps falsely attribute to outer things. But here it is proved that outer experience {{ 277 }} is actually immediate,[^*] and that only by means of it is not indeed the consciousness of our own existence, but yet the determination of it in time, i.e., inner experience, possible. To be sure, the representation: ***I am***, which expresses the consciousness that can accompany all thinking, is that which immediately includes the existence of a subject within itself, but is not yet a ***cognition*** of it, hence also not empirical cognition, i.e., experience; for to that, besides the thought of something existing, intuition is also required, and here inner intuition, in regard to which, i.e., time, the subject must be determined, for which outer objects are absolutely required, so that consequently inner experience itself is possible only mediately and only through outer experience.

***Note 2.*** With this, all empirical use of our cognitive faculty in the determination of time perfectly agrees. Not only is it the case that we can perceive all time-determination only through the change in outer relations (motion) in relation to the persistent in {{ 278 }} space (e.g., the motion of the sun in regard to the objects of the earth), but we even have nothing persistent {{{ 193 }}} that we could underlie the concept of a substance as intuition except merely ***matter***, and even this persistence is not drawn from outer experience, but is presupposed _a priori_ as a necessary condition of all time-determination, and hence also as the determination of inner sense in regard to our own existence through the existence of outer things. The consciousness of myself in the representation ***I*** is no intuition at all, but a merely ***intellectual*** representation of the self-activity of a thinking subject. Therefore this I has not the least predicate of intuition which, as ***persistent***, could serve as a correlate for time-determination in inner sense, in the way that, say, ***impenetrability*** is in matter as an ***empirical*** intuition.

***Note 3.*** From the fact that the existence of outer objects is required for the possibility of a determinate consciousness of ourselves, it does not follow that every intuitive representation of outer things at the same time includes their existence, for that representation may very well be the mere effect of the power of imagination (in dreams as well as in madness); but it is so merely through the reproduction of former outer perceptions, which, as has been shown, are possible only through the actuality of outer objects. It has here only been intended to prove that inner experience in general is possible only through outer {{ 279 }} experience in general. Whether this or that assumed experience is not mere imagination must be ascertained according to its particular determinations and through comparison with the criteria of all actual experience.

***

Finally, as regards the third postulate, it concerns material necessity in existence, and not merely formal and logical necessity in the connection of concepts. Since no existence of the objects of the senses can be cognized completely _a priori_, but yet comparatively _a priori_ and relatively to some other already given existence, while even then we can still only arrive at that existence which must be contained somewhere in the context of the experience of which the given perception is a part: therefore the necessity of existence can never be cognized from concepts, but always only from the connection with {{{ 194 }}} that which is perceived, in accordance with the general laws of experience. Now there is no existence that could be cognized as necessary under the condition of other given appearances, except the existence of effects from given causes in accordance with the laws of causality. Therefore, it is not the existence of things (substances) but only of their state of which we can cognize necessity, and {{ 280 }} indeed from other states that are given in perception, in accordance with the empirical laws of causality. From this it follows that the criterion of necessity lies solely in the law of possible experience: that everything that happens is determined _a priori_ by its cause in appearance. Hence we cognize only the necessity of ***effects*** in nature, the causes of which are given to us; and the character of necessity in existence does not reach further than the field of possible experience, and even in this it does not apply to the existence of things as substances, because these can never be regarded as empirical effects, or as something that happens and arises. Necessity therefore concerns only the relations of appearances in accordance with the dynamical law of causality, and the possibility grounded thereon of inferring _a priori_ from a given existence (a cause) to another existence (the effect). Everything that happens is hypothetically necessary; this is a principle that subjects change in the world to a law, i.e., to a rule of necessary existence, without which even nature itself would not take place. Hence the proposition: nothing happens by blind chance (_in mundo non datur casus_), is an _a priori_ law of nature; likewise: no necessity in nature is blind, but is conditioned, and consequently intelligible {{ 281 }} necessity (_non datur fatum_). Both are such laws through which the play of changes is subjected to a ***nature of things*** (as appearances) or, what is the same thing, to the unity of the understanding, in which alone they can belong to an experience as the synthetic unity of appearances. These two principles belong to the dynamical ones. The former is properly a consequence of the principle of causality (among the analogies of experience). The second belongs to the principles of modality, which add to the determination of causality the concept of necessity, which, however, stands under a rule of the understanding. The principle of continuity forbade any leap in the series of appearances (changes) ({{{ 195 }}} _in mundo non datur saltus_), but also any gap or cleft between two appearances in the sum total of all empirical intuitions in space (_non datur hiatus_); for thus one can express the proposition: that nothing can enter into experience that proves a vacuum, or even admits it as a part of the empirical synthesis. For as regards the empty space that one may think outside the field of possible experience (the world), this does not fall under the jurisdiction of the mere understanding, which decides only on those questions that concern the utilization of given appearances for empirical cognition, and is a task for idealizing reason, which goes beyond the {{ 282 }} sphere of a possible experience and wishes to judge of that which surrounds and limits this experience, and must therefore be considered in the transcendental Dialectic. These four propositions (_in mundo non datur hiatus, non datur saltus, non datur casus, non datur fatum_) we could easily, like all principles of transcendental origin, represent according to their order in conformity with the order of the categories and prove to each its place; but the already practiced reader will do this by himself, or easily discover the guide to it. But they all unite solely in admitting nothing into the empirical synthesis that could do damage or prejudice to the understanding and the continuous connection of all appearances, i.e., to the unity of its concepts. For it is in the understanding alone that the unity of experience, in which all perceptions must have their place, becomes possible.

Whether the field of possibility is greater than the field that contains everything actual, and whether this latter is in turn greater than the multitude of that which is necessary, are interesting questions, and indeed admit of synthetic solution, but fall only under the jurisdiction of reason; for they mean approximately to ask whether all things as appearances belong collectively to the sum total and context of a single experience of which every given perception is a part, which therefore {{ 283 }} cannot be connected with any other appearances, or whether my perceptions can belong to more than one possible experience (in its general connection). The understanding gives _a priori_ to experience in general only the rule in accordance with the subjective and formal conditions both of sensibility and of apperception, which alone make it possible. Other forms of intuition (than space and time), likewise other forms of the understanding (than the discursive {{{ 196 }}} form of thinking or of cognition through concepts), even if they were possible, we cannot in any way think up and make comprehensible to ourselves; but even if we could, they would still not belong to experience as the sole cognition in which objects are given to us. Whether other perceptions than those that belong to our entire possible experience in general, and therefore an entirely different field of matter can still occur, the understanding cannot decide; it has to do only with the synthesis of that which is given. Otherwise, the poverty of our usual inferences, by which we bring forth a great empire of possibility of which everything actual (every object of experience) is only a small part, is very conspicuous. Everything actual is possible; from this there follows naturally, in accordance with the logical rules of conversion, the merely particular proposition: some possible {{ 284 }} is actual, which then seems to mean as much as: many things are possible that are not actual. It indeed has the appearance as if one could directly place the number of the possible above that of the actual, because something more must be added to the former to constitute the latter. But this addition to the possible I do not know. For what was to be added beyond that would be impossible. There can only be added to my understanding something beyond agreement with the formal conditions of experience, namely, the connection with some perception; but what is connected with this in accordance with empirical laws is actual, even though it is not immediately perceived. But that, in thoroughgoing connection with what is given to me in perception, another series of appearances, and hence more than a single all-encompassing experience should be possible, cannot be inferred from what is given, and even less so if nothing at all is given, because without matter nothing at all can be thought. What is possible only under conditions that are themselves merely possible is not possible in ***every respect***. But the question is taken in this latter sense when one wants to know whether the possibility of things extends further than experience can reach.

I have made mention of these questions only in order to leave no gap in {{ 285 }} that which, according to common opinion, belongs to the concepts of the understanding. In fact, however, absolute possibility (which is valid in every respect) is no mere concept of the understanding, and can in no way be of empirical use, but belongs solely to {{{ 197 }}} reason, which goes beyond all possible empirical use of the understanding. Hence we have had to content ourselves here with a merely critical note, but for the rest have left the matter in obscurity until further proceedings in the future.

Since I wish to close this fourth section, and with it at the same time the system of all principles of the pure understanding, I must still provide the reason why I have called the principles of modality postulates. I do not wish to take this expression here in the significance that some recent philosophical writers have given it, contrary to the sense of the mathematicians to whom it properly belongs, namely: that postulating should mean as much as passing off a proposition as immediately certain without justification or proof; for if we were to concede this in the case of synthetic propositions, however evident they might be, that one might attach them to unconditional assent without a deduction and on the authority of their own utterance, then all critique of the understanding would be lost, and since there is no lack of bold pretensions, which even common belief {{ 286 }} (which is yet no credential) does not refuse: our understanding would thus lie open to every delusion, without being able to deny its assent to those utterances which, although illegitimate, yet demand to be admitted in the very same tone of confidence as actual axioms. If, therefore, a determination is synthetically added _a priori_ to the concept of a thing, then there must unconditionally be added to such a proposition, if not a proof, at least a deduction of the legitimacy of its assertion.

But the principles of modality are not objectively synthetic, because the predicates of possibility, actuality, and necessity do not in the least augment the concept of which they are asserted, by adding anything to the representation of the object. Since they are nevertheless always synthetic, they are so only subjectively, i.e., they add to the concept of a thing (the real), about which they otherwise say nothing, the cognitive power wherein it arises and has its seat: so that if it is merely connected in the understanding with the formal conditions of experience, its object is called possible; if it is in connection with perception (sensation as the matter of the senses) and determined through the latter by means of the understanding, then the object is actual; if it is determined through the connection of perceptions {{ 287 }} in accordance with concepts, then the object is called necessary. {{{ 198 }}} The principles of modality therefore say nothing else of a concept than the action of the cognitive faculty whereby it is produced. Now in mathematics a postulate means the practical proposition that contains nothing but the synthesis through which we first give ourselves an object and produce its concept—e.g., to describe a circle with a given line from a given point on a plane; and such a proposition cannot be proved, because the procedure that it requires is precisely that through which we first produce the concept of such a figure. We can therefore with the very same right postulate the principles of modality, because they do not at all augment their concept of things in general,[^**] but only indicate the way in which it is connected with the cognitive power in general.

***

[^*]: The ***immediate*** consciousness of the existence of outer things is not presupposed in the preceding theorem, but proved, whether we may comprehend the possibility of this consciousness or not. The question regarding the latter would be: whether we have only an inner sense but no outer sense, but merely outer imagination. But it is clear that, in order for us even to imagine something as outer, i.e., to present it to sense in intuition, we must already have an outer sense, and thereby immediately distinguish the mere receptivity of an outer intuition from the spontaneity that characterizes every imagination. For merely to imagine an outer sense would itself destroy the faculty of intuition, which is supposed to be determined by the power of imagination.

[^**]: ***Through the actuality*** of a thing I indeed see more than possibility, but not ***in the thing***; for it can never contain more in actuality than what was contained in its complete possibility. But because possibility was merely a positing of the thing in relation to the understanding (its empirical use), actuality is at the same time a connection of the thing with perception.
