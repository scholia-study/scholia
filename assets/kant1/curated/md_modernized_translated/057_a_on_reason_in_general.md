## A. On reason in general

Every one of our cognitions begins with the senses, moves on from there to the understanding, and terminates in reason, above which we find nothing higher within us for working up the raw material of intuition and gathering it under the supreme unity of thinking. Now that I am expected to give an account of this highest cognitive power, I find myself in something of a quandary. There is, as with the understanding, a use of it that is purely formal—that is, logical—in which reason leaves aside every content belonging to cognition, but there is likewise a real use, inasmuch as reason itself is the source of certain concepts and principles that it derives from neither the senses nor the understanding. The former capacity has of course long since been accounted for by the logicians as the power of inferring mediately (as distinct from immediate inferences, _consequentiis immediatis_); the latter, however, which itself brings forth concepts, is thereby still {{{ 238 }}} not comprehended. Since what emerges at this point is a partition of reason into a {{ 356 }} logical and a transcendental capacity, we must look for some higher concept of this cognitive source—one broad enough to embrace both of the aforementioned—while the analogy with the concepts of the understanding gives us grounds to expect that this logical concept will simultaneously unlock the transcendental one, and that the table listing the functions of the former will at the same time deliver the ancestral line of reason's own concepts.

Earlier, in the opening part of our transcendental logic, we defined the understanding by way of the faculty of rules; here we set reason apart from it by proposing to call it the ***faculty of principles***.

The word 'principle' carries a double sense, and in ordinary usage it designates nothing more than a cognition capable of being put to use as a principle, notwithstanding that, taken on its own and by its own origin, it is not a principle at all. Every universal proposition whatsoever—even one lifted from experience (by way of induction)—is fit to function as the major premise of a rational inference; but this does not thereby make it a principle in its own right. The axioms of mathematics (for example, that between two points there can lie but one straight line) are indeed universal cognitions _a priori_, and hence they are, with good reason, termed principles in a relative sense—relative to whatever instances fall under them. Even so, that does not license me to say that I cognize this feature {{ 357 }} of straight lines universally and in itself out of principles; rather, I cognize it solely in pure intuition.

Accordingly, the label 'cognition from principles' I would reserve for the case in which, by means of concepts, I cognize the particular as contained in the universal. On this understanding, every rational inference amounts to a way of deriving one cognition out of a principle. For the major premise invariably supplies a concept of such a sort that whatever is brought under its condition comes to be cognized through it in conformity with a principle. And since any universal cognition is capable of standing as the major premise within a rational inference, and since the understanding puts forward universal propositions of this sort _a priori_, they too, in view of the use to which they might be put, may bear the name of principles.

Yet once we examine these principles of the pure understanding as they are in themselves, in respect of their origin, they turn out to be anything but cognitions drawn from concepts. For they would not so much as be possible _a priori_ were we not to draw upon pure intuition (in mathematics) or upon the conditions of a possible experience taken quite generally. The proposition that everything {{{ 239 }}} that comes to pass has its cause is in no way derivable from the concept of that which comes to pass in general; on the contrary, it is the principle that first shows us how a determinate empirical concept of what comes to pass may be arrived at.

Synthetic cognitions out of concepts, therefore, lie altogether beyond {{ 358 }} what the understanding can provide, and it is these, strictly speaking, to which I give the name of principles outright: while every universal proposition whatever may pass merely for a comparative principle.

There is a long-standing wish—one that, who can tell how far off, may possibly at some point come true: that in place of the boundless multiplicity of civil statutes people might for once go in search of their principles; for it is here alone that the secret of what is called simplifying legislation can reside. But even in this case the laws are nothing more than curbs placed on our freedom, confining it to conditions under which it remains throughout in harmony with itself; they therefore pertain to something that is wholly a product of our own doing, and of which, through those very concepts, we can ourselves be the cause. But the question how objects in themselves, how the nature of things, could be subject to principles and admit of determination by nothing but concepts, is a demand that is, if not downright impossible, then at any rate thoroughly preposterous. However this matter may finally turn out (for the inquiry into it still lies ahead of us), this much at least becomes clear from what has been said: that cognition from principles (regarded in itself) is something wholly distinct from mere cognition of the understanding, which, though it may indeed stand ahead of other cognitions under the guise of a principle, does not of itself (so far as it is synthetic) rest upon thinking alone, nor does it hold within itself any universal proceeding by concepts.

{{ 359 }} Granting that the understanding is a faculty for bringing appearances to unity by means of rules, reason is then the faculty for bringing the rules of the understanding themselves to unity under principles. Hence it is never directed, in the first instance, upon experience or upon any object at all, but rather upon the understanding, so as to confer _a priori_, through concepts, a unity on the latter's manifold cognitions—a unity that one may name the unity of reason, and which is of a wholly different sort than anything the understanding is able to accomplish.

Such is the general concept of the faculty of reason, to the extent that it admitted of being rendered intelligible in the total absence of examples (which are not to be furnished until later on).
