## {{ 399 }} Chapter 1. On the Paralogisms of Pure Reason

A logical paralogism has its falsity in the mere form of a rational syllogism, let its content be whatever it may. A transcendental paralogism, by contrast, rests on a transcendental ground that makes one draw a formally invalid conclusion. A fallacy of this sort, accordingly, is rooted in the very nature of human reason and brings with it an illusion that, while unavoidable, is not beyond resolution.

We arrive now at a concept that our earlier general roster of transcendental concepts did not list, though it nonetheless has to be reckoned among them—and this without in any way modifying that table or pronouncing it incomplete. It is the concept—or, should one {{{ 263 }}} rather have it so, the judgment—***I think***. Yet it is easy to see that this is the vehicle carrying all concepts whatever, the transcendental ones included, so that it is invariably co-thought along with them and is thus itself no less transcendental, {{ 400 }} though it merits no title of its own, since its whole office is to exhibit every act of thinking as something belonging to consciousness. Nevertheless, however free of the empirical (the imprint of the senses) it may be, it does help us mark off two sorts of object grounded in the very nature of our representational power. ***I***, taken as thinking, count as an object of inner sense, and the name for this is soul. Whatever, on the other hand, stands as an object of the outer senses bears the name body. The phrase 'I, as a thinking being', therefore, already picks out the subject matter of that psychology which one may term the rational doctrine of the soul, on the assumption that I ask to know nothing of the soul beyond what may be concluded—apart from all experience (which fixes me more determinately and _in concreto_)—from the concept ***I*** so far as this turns up throughout all thinking.

Now the ***rational*** doctrine of the soul really is a venture of just this sort; for were even the slightest empirical element of my thinking—some specific perception of my own inner condition—admitted among this science's sources of cognition, it would thereby cease to be rational and turn into an ***empirical*** doctrine of the soul. So we already have before us a would-be science resting on the one proposition ***I think***, and its soundness or unsoundness is something we may here examine quite appropriately, as befits the character of a transcendental philosophy. It should give no one pause that in this proposition, which voices the perception of oneself, I do in fact possess an inner experience, {{ 401 }} so that the rational doctrine of the soul built upon it can never be pure but rests partly on an empirical principle. For this inner perception amounts to nothing beyond the bare apperception ***I think***—the very thing that first makes possible all the transcendental concepts, wherein one says: ***I think*** substance, cause, and so on. For inner experience as such together with its possibility—or, equally, perception as such together with its bearing on other perception, where no particular difference or determination among perceptions is empirically supplied—may not pass as empirical cognition; it has instead to be treated as cognition of the empirical taken generally, and it falls under the inquiry into how any experience whatever is possible, an inquiry that is of course transcendental. The tiniest object of perception (say, mere {{{ 264 }}} pleasure or displeasure) annexed to the general representation of self-consciousness would straightaway convert rational psychology into an empirical psychology.

***I think***, then, is the one and only text of rational psychology, and out of it the whole of that science's wisdom is meant to be unfolded. It is readily apparent that this thought, once it is made to bear on an object (my own self), admits of nothing but transcendental predicates of that object: for the faintest empirical predicate would ruin the science's rational purity and its freedom from all experience.

{{ 402 }} Here, though, our sole task will be to keep to the guiding thread of the categories; only, because in this instance a thing—namely I, as a thinking being—is what is first given, we shall leave the earlier ordering of the categories among themselves (as their table lays it out) untouched, yet start at this point from the category of substance, by which a thing in itself is represented, and then work our way back down their sequence. Hence the topic of the rational doctrine of the soul, the source from which all its remaining content has to be drawn, runs as follows:[^*]

{{{ 265 }}} {{ 403 }} Out of these elements every concept belonging to the pure doctrine of the soul springs solely by way of combination, without so much as acknowledging any further principle. Considered simply as an object of inner sense, this substance furnishes the concept of ***immateriality***; as a simple substance, that of ***incorruptibility***; through its identity, as an intellectual substance, it furnishes ***personality***; and the three together furnish ***spirituality***; its relation to objects in space furnishes ***commercium*** with bodies; the thinking substance is thereby represented as the principle of life within matter, that is, as soul (_anima_) and as the basis of ***animality***, and this last, once curbed by ***spirituality***, gives ***immortality***.

It is upon this that there rest four paralogisms of a transcendental doctrine of the soul, a doctrine mistakenly regarded as a science of pure reason treating of what our thinking being is by nature. Yet at their {{ 404 }} basis we are able to place nothing beyond the plain representation I—wholly devoid of content in its own right, and such that one cannot even call it a concept, but only a bare consciousness attending every concept. By means of this I, or He, or It (the thing) that carries out the thinking, all that gets represented is a transcendental subject of the thoughts, = X; it is known only through those thoughts, which serve as its predicates, and taken by itself we can form of it not the slightest concept, so that we keep circling it perpetually, obliged as we are to draw on its representation before we can pass any judgment about it at all—an awkwardness inseparable from the case, since consciousness in itself is less a representation singling out some particular object than a form belonging to representation generally, so far as representation deserves the name of cognition; and it is of the latter alone that I can say I thereby think anything whatever.

Still, it must appear odd right from the start that the condition on which I think at all—which is accordingly no more than a feature of my own subject—should hold good at the same time for whatever thinks, and that on the basis of a seemingly empirical proposition we should venture to rest an apodictic and {{{ 266 }}} universal judgment, to wit: that everything which thinks is so constituted as the deliverance of self-consciousness pronounces it to be in {{ 405 }} my own case. The reason for this, however, is that to things we are bound of necessity to attribute _a priori_ every property making up the conditions apart from which we cannot think them at all. Now no outer experience, but self-consciousness alone, can give me even the barest representation of a thinking being. Objects of that kind, therefore, come to no more than the carrying-over of this consciousness of mine onto other things, which are pictured as thinking beings by that transfer alone. The proposition 'I think' is, however, entertained here in merely problematic fashion—not as it might carry a perception of some existence (the Cartesian _cogito, ergo sum_), but purely in point of its possibility, so as to discover what properties might issue, from a proposition thus utterly simple, for the subject of it (whether or not any such subject actually exists).

Were anything more than the _cogito_ to underlie our pure rational cognition of thinking beings as such; were we to enlist as well the observations on how our thoughts interplay and the natural laws of the thinking self derivable from them: what would emerge is an empirical psychology, a sort of ***physiology*** of inner sense, able perhaps to account for that sense's appearances, but never fit to bring to light properties lying wholly beyond {{ 406 }} possible experience (such as that of the simple), nor to teach anything ***apodictically*** about the nature of thinking beings in general; it would thus fall short of being a ***rational*** psychology.

Since the proposition ***I think*** (entertained problematically) carries within it the form of every judgment of the understanding whatever and rides along with all the categories as their vehicle: it becomes plain that whatever is inferred from it can involve only a transcendental employment of the understanding, one that refuses any admixture of experience, and about whose upshot—given what we established earlier—we can already form no favorable notion beforehand. Let us, then, track it with a critical eye across all the predicaments of the pure doctrine of the soul, yet, {{{ 267 }}} to be brief, allow the scrutiny of them to run on as a single unbroken sequence.

To begin with, one general observation may sharpen our vigilance toward this style of inference. I cognize no object simply in virtue of thinking; a given intuition must first be determined by me relative to that unity of consciousness in which all thinking has its being—only then can I cognize any object at all. Accordingly, my being aware of myself as thinking does not amount to my cognizing myself; that happens only when I am aware of the intuition of myself as determined in respect of the function of thought. For this reason all the _modi_ of {{ 407 }} self-awareness in thinking are as yet no concepts of the understanding regarding objects (categories); they are mere logical functions, handing thought no object whatever to be cognized, and consequently handing me my own self no more than the rest as an object. What counts as the ***object*** is not the awareness of the ***determining*** self but solely that of the ***determinable*** self—that is, of my inner intuition (to the degree that its manifold admits of being conjoined according to the general condition governing the unity of apperception in thought).

1) Throughout every judgment I am, invariably, the determining ***subject*** of the relation that makes the judgment up. But that I who think must always, in thinking, hold as ***subject***, as something not to be viewed as merely clinging to thought after the manner of a predicate, is a proposition apodictic and indeed ***identical***; it does not, however, imply that I, qua ***object***, am a ***being that subsists on its own*** or a ***substance***. This latter claim reaches very far and hence calls for data nowhere to be found in thinking—perhaps (so far as I regard the thinking merely as such) more than I shall ever come across anywhere within it.

2) That the I of apperception is, in consequence, in each act of thinking a ***singular*** incapable of being resolved into a multitude of subjects, and hence marks out a logically simple subject—this already resides in the concept {{ 408 }} of thinking and is thus an analytic proposition; but it does not carry the meaning that the thinking I is a simple ***substance***, which would make it a synthetic proposition. The concept of substance is invariably tied to intuitions, and these, in my case, can be nothing but sensible, lying therefore altogether beyond the field of the understanding and its thinking—though thinking is precisely what is at issue here when it is claimed that the {{{ 268 }}} I is simple in thought. It would, moreover, be astonishing if what ordinarily demands so much apparatus—in order to make out, within what intuition sets before us, which element in it is substance, and, harder still, whether that substance can also be simple (as with the parts of matter)—were here handed to me outright, as though by revelation, in the barest representation there is.

3) The proposition asserting my own identity amid all the manifold of which I am conscious is one that likewise lies within the very concepts, and so is analytic; but this identity of the subject, of which I can grow conscious across all its representations, has no bearing on the intuition of that subject whereby it is given as object, and it cannot therefore stand for the identity of the person—by which is meant the awareness that one's own substance, as a thinking being, stays identical through every alteration of its states—an identity whose proof the mere analysis of 'I think' could never furnish, calling instead for {{ 409 }} sundry synthetic judgments that rest upon the intuition given.

4) 'I set my own existence, as that of a thinking being, apart from other things beyond me (my body among them)'—this too is analytic; for by ***other*** things I mean just those I conceive as ***set apart*** from myself. But whether this awareness of myself could so much as occur in the absence of things beyond me by which representations reach me—whether, that is, I might exist as a merely thinking being (without being a human)—of this the proposition tells me nothing at all.

So it turns out that dissecting the awareness of myself in thinking as such yields not the least gain as regards cognizing myself qua object. The logical elucidation of thought in general gets mistaken for a metaphysical determination of the object.

One great stumbling block—indeed the single one—confronting our whole critique would appear were it feasible to establish _a priori_ that every thinking being is in itself a simple substance, and, as such (a corollary of the very same proof-ground), bears personality inseparably about it and is aware of an existence divorced from all matter. For in that event we should after all have set foot past the sensible world, entering the domain {{ 410 }} of ***noumena***, whereupon let none dispute our title to range further within it, to till it, and—according as each man's {{{ 269 }}} fortunate star smiles on him—to stake out holdings there. For the proposition 'Any thinking being as such is a simple substance' is synthetic _a priori_, because, first, it oversteps the concept placed at its base and appends the ***mode of existence*** to thinking as such, and, second, it fastens onto that concept a predicate (simplicity) incapable of being given in any experience. Synthetic propositions _a priori_, then, would prove workable and permissible not merely, as we maintained, with respect to objects of possible experience and specifically as principles for the very possibility of such experience, but might reach out to things generally and in themselves as well—an inference that would spell the close of this entire critique and enjoin us to leave matters as they stood. Yet the peril here proves less grave once one draws nearer to the matter.

Governing the procedure of rational psychology there is a paralogism, exhibited in the following rational syllogism.

***Whatever admits of being thought only as subject likewise exists only as subject and is on that account substance.***

{{ 411 }} ***Now a thinking being, viewed purely as such, admits of being thought only as subject.***

***Hence it too exists only as such—that is, as substance.***

In the major premise the being spoken of is one thinkable at large, in every regard, and so also as it might be furnished in intuition. In the minor premise, by contrast, the being figures only so far as it regards itself as subject in bare relation to thinking and the unity of consciousness, and not concurrently in relation to the intuition by which it would be handed to thought as object. Thus it is _per sophisma figurae dictionis_—by a deceptive inference, that is—that the conclusion gets drawn.[^**]

{{{ 270 }}} {{ 412 }} That this diagnosis of the celebrated argument as a paralogism hits the mark exactly comes out clearly if one cares to look back here at the general remark on the systematic exhibition of the principles, and at the section dealing with the noumena, where it was shown: that the concept of a thing capable of existing on its own as subject but not as mere predicate still brings with it no objective reality whatever—that one cannot tell, in other words, whether any object could ever answer to it, since the possibility of such a manner of existing is not grasped, so that the concept delivers no cognition at all. If, then, it is under the title of a substance to point to an object that can be given—if it is to become a cognition—then some abiding intuition, being the condition one cannot dispense with for a concept's objective reality (namely the very means by which the object is given), has to be set beneath it. But in {{ 413 }} inner intuition we possess nothing abiding at all, since the I is merely my consciousness that I think; and so, if we halt at thinking alone, we are short of the condition required for bringing the concept of substance—of a subject that persists on its own, that is—to bear on oneself as a thinking being; whereupon the simplicity of substance tied to that concept collapses together with the concept's objective reality, converting into a bare logical and qualitative oneness of self-consciousness in thought as such, the subject being composite or not, no matter which.

## Refutation of Mendelssohn's Proof of the Soul's Abidingness.

This penetrating philosopher was quick to detect, in the customary argument meant to show that the soul (granting it to be a simple being) cannot pass out of existence through ***partition***, a shortfall in meeting the aim of guaranteeing it the requisite endurance, seeing that one might yet suppose its existence to lapse {{{ 271 }}} by ***fading away***. In his _Phädon_ he set out to keep this perishing—which would be genuine annihilation—away from the soul, presuming to demonstrate: that a simple being can in no way cease to exist, for, being incapable of diminution and hence of forfeiting bit by bit some portion of its existence and so of ***gradually*** passing into {{ 414 }} nothingness (having no parts, and thus no plurality, within it), it would leave no interval of time whatever between an instant in which it is and a further instant in which it is no more—which is impossible. — But he failed to reflect that, even were we to concede the soul this simple nature—that it holds, namely, no manifold ***lying side by side***, and so no extensive magnitude—one can no more refuse it than any existing thing an intensive magnitude, a degree of reality, that is, in respect of all its powers, indeed of everything at all that constitutes existence, a degree that may fall away through endlessly many ever-slighter degrees, so that the supposed substance (the thing whose abidingness is not already settled on other grounds) could be turned to nothing, not indeed by partition, yet by a stepwise slackening (_remissio_) of its forces (through languescence, then, if I may help myself to the term). For consciousness itself possesses at all times a degree open to still further {{ 415 }} reduction[^***], and hence so does the power of being conscious of oneself, and so do all the other powers. — The abidingness of the soul, viewed as a bare object of inner sense, thus stays undemonstrated and even indemonstrable, though its abidingness throughout life—since the thinking being (as a human) is at once an object of the outer senses to itself—is evident on its own; but with this the rational psychologist is in no way content, {{{ 272 }}} bent as he is on proving, out of mere concepts, the soul's absolute abidingness reaching even past life[^****].

{{ 416 }} Suppose now we take the propositions above—as, taken they must be, holding for every thinking being within rational psychology considered as a system—in ***synthetic*** interconnection, and set out from the {{{ 273 }}} {{ 417 }} category of relation with the proposition that all thinking beings are, as such, substances, running back down their series until the circle comes round: we light in the end on the existence of these beings, an existence of which, within this system and in independence of external things, they are not only conscious {{ 418 }} but which they can, moreover (as regards the abidingness that necessarily belongs to the character of substance), settle out of their own resources. From this, however, it emerges that ***idealism***—the problematic kind, at any rate—cannot be escaped within that very rationalist system, and that, if the existence of outer things is in no way needed for settling one's own existence in time, those outer things are assumed to no purpose whatever, past all power of ever proving them.

If instead we pursue the ***analytic*** route—where the 'I think', as a proposition already enclosing an existence within itself, is taken as given, so that modality forms the base—and take it apart to learn its content, namely whether and how this I settles its existence in space or time by that alone, then the propositions of the rational doctrine of the soul would set out not from the concept of a thinking being at large but from an actuality, and from the way in which this actuality is {{ 419 }} conceived—once all that is empirical about it has been detached—there would be inferred what belongs to a thinking being in general, as the table below displays.

{{{ 274 }}} Since the second proposition leaves it open whether ***I*** can exist and be conceived solely as subject and not likewise as another's predicate, the concept of a subject is being employed here in a merely logical way, and it stays undecided whether substance is to be meant by it or not. In the third proposition, by contrast, the absolute unity of apperception—the simple I within that representation to which every combining or separating that constitutes thought is referred—acquires weight in its own right, even while I have thus far fixed nothing touching the subject's makeup or subsistence. Apperception is a real something, and its simplicity is already contained in its very possibility. In space, meanwhile, nothing real is simple; for points (space's sole simple items) are nothing but boundaries, not themselves anything {{ 420 }} apt to constitute space as a part of it. What follows from this is the impossibility of accounting for my makeup, as a merely thinking subject, on the grounds of ***materialism***. But since my existence is treated as given in the first proposition—which does not run 'every thinking being exists' (a claim that would assert absolute necessity and hence say far too much of them), but only '***I exist*** thinking'—the proposition is empirical and holds the determinability of my existence solely as concerns my representations in time. Since here again I stand in need, first of all, of something abiding, of which nothing whatever is given me in inner intuition so long as I merely think myself: the mode in which I exist—as substance or as accident—simply cannot be settled by this bare self-consciousness. And so, if ***materialism*** will not serve to explain my existence, ***spiritualism*** proves every bit as inadequate to it; the upshot being that by no means at all, whatever it might be, can we know a thing about our soul's makeup where the possibility of its detached existence is concerned.

And how, in any case, should it be possible—by way of the unity of consciousness, which we ourselves are acquainted with only because we cannot do without it for the possibility of experience—to pass beyond experience (our existence in life) and even to broaden our cognition to the nature {{ 421 }} of thinking beings generally through the proposition 'I think', empirical yet left undetermined as regards every kind of intuition?

There exists, then, no rational psychology as a doctrine capable of yielding us any supplement to our self-knowledge, but only one as a {{{ 275 }}} ***discipline***—setting for speculative reason in this field boundaries not to be crossed, on one side that we may not cast ourselves into the embrace of a soulless materialism, on the other that we may not stray off, adrift, into a spiritualism baseless for us amid life; it prompts us rather to look on this refusal of our reason to answer satisfyingly the inquisitive questions that overreach the present life as a signal from reason itself, bidding us redirect our self-knowledge from barren, exorbitant speculation to a fruitful practical use—a use which, even while forever aimed only at objects of experience, nonetheless fetches its principles from higher and shapes our conduct as though our vocation stretched infinitely past experience, and thus past this present life.

From all of this it becomes visible that rational psychology owes its origin to nothing but a misunderstanding. The unity of consciousness underlying the categories is here mistaken for an intuition of the {{ 422 }} subject as object, and to it the category of substance is then applied. Yet it is no more than the unity within ***thinking***, by which on its own no object is given, so that the category of substance—presupposing, as it forever does, a given ***intuition***—finds no application to it, leaving this subject wholly uncognized. By thinking the categories, therefore, the subject of the categories cannot arrive at a concept of itself as an object falling under them; for to think them it has to place beneath them its own pure self-consciousness—the very thing that was to have been explained. By the same token, that subject wherein the representation of time takes its original rise is unable, by that means, to fix its own existence in time; and where the fixing of existence in time is barred, so too is that other fixing—of the subject itself (as a thinking being in general) by means of categories.[^*****]

***

{{ 423 }} And so a cognition ventured past the confines of possible experience, one belonging nevertheless to humanity's supreme interest, evaporates—so far as it is to be credited to speculative philosophy—into {{{ 276 }}} {{ 424 }} thwarted hope; wherein all the same the severity of the critique, by demonstrating at once that it is impossible to fix anything dogmatically about an object of experience past experience's boundary, renders reason, amid this interest of its own, a service far from trifling: it makes reason equally secure against every conceivable claim to the contrary; and this can come about in no other way than by one's either proving one's proposition apodictically or, failing that, hunting down the roots of the failure, which, should they lie within the necessary confines of our reason, must then bind every antagonist under that identical law of forgoing all pretension to dogmatic assertion.

Even so, none of this costs us in the slightest the warrant—indeed the necessity—of positing a life to come, on the principles of that practical use of reason which is joined to the speculative; for on ordinary human reason the merely speculative proof could in any case never have exerted the least influence. It is poised on so fine a needle-point that even the Schools can hold it there {{{ 277 }}} only so long as they keep it forever spinning round that point like a top, so that in their own view it affords no lasting groundwork on which anything might be raised. The proofs {{ 425 }} of use to the world all keep here their full, unlessened worth, and gain rather in lucidity and artless conviction once those dogmatic presumptions are cleared away, for they lodge reason within its proper province—the order of ends, which is withal an order of nature too—a province that then, as a practical faculty in itself unfettered by the conditions of the latter, is warranted to enlarge the former, and with it our own existence, past the limits of experience and of life. Reasoning by the ***analogy with the nature*** of the living beings in this world—in whom reason has necessarily to adopt as a principle that not one organ, faculty, or drive is to be met with that is dispensable, or out of proportion to its use, and thus purposeless, but that each is exactly fitted to its vocation in life—the human being, who alone can after all harbor within himself the final end of it all, would have to be the sole creature left out of the rule. For his natural endowments—not merely with respect to the talents and drives for putting them to work, but chiefly the moral law within him—run out so far past every use and gain he could wring from them in this life that this law schools him to value above everything else the mere consciousness of an upright {{ 426 }} disposition, even where every advantage fails, even down to the ghostly show of fame after death, and he feels an inward calling to render himself, through his bearing in this world and by forgoing many advantages, fit for citizenship in a better one that he holds in idea. This forceful proof-ground, never to be overthrown, attended by an ever-swelling cognition of the purposiveness in all that lies before our eyes, by a vista onto the boundlessness of creation, and hence too by the awareness of a certain limitlessness in the possible growth of our knowledge together with a drive matched to it, remains standing yet, even should we have to renounce discerning the necessary endurance of our existence from the merely theoretical cognition of ourselves.

## {{{ 278 }}} Conclusion of the Resolution of the Psychological Paralogism.

What produces the dialectical semblance in rational psychology is the mix-up of an idea belonging to reason (that of a pure intelligence) with the utterly indeterminate concept, in every respect, of a thinking being as such. I think my own self on behalf of a possible experience, while abstracting still from all actual experience, and I draw from this the conclusion that I could grow conscious of my existence even outside experience and its {{ 427 }} empirical conditions. Thus I mistake the feasible ***abstracting*** from my empirically determined existence for a supposed awareness of an existence my thinking self might have ***in separation***, and I take myself to cognize the substantial in me as the transcendental subject, when all I hold in thought is the unity of consciousness that underlies every act of determining, this being the mere form of cognition.

The problem of explaining the soul's community with the body does not, strictly, fall to the psychology under discussion here, since its purpose is to prove the soul's personality even apart from that community (after death), making it, in the strict sense, ***transcendent***, notwithstanding that it busies itself with an object of experience—but only so far as that object stops being one of experience. That said, our doctrine allows an adequate reply to be given to this question as well. The difficulty prompting the problem consists, as everyone knows, in the assumed unlikeness between the object of inner sense (the soul) and the objects of the outer senses, since to the former only time attaches, to the latter space as well, as the formal condition of their intuition. But bear in mind that the two kinds of object are marked off from one another in this respect not inwardly, but {{ 428 }} only so far as the one ***shows itself*** outwardly to the other, so that whatever grounds the appearance of matter as a thing in itself might well be not so unlike after all—and with that the difficulty melts away, leaving none but this: how a community of substances is at all possible, a question whose solution lies quite beyond the field of psychology and, as the reader will easily gather from what the Analytic said of basic forces and faculties, past all doubt beyond the field of every human cognition too.

[^*]: Should the reader, faced with these expressions in their transcendental remoteness, not so readily guess their psychological import—or why the soul's final attribute falls under the category of ***existence***—he will find them amply clarified and vindicated in the sequel. For the rest, as concerns the Latin terms that, against the taste of sound style, have found their way in where equivalent German ones would serve, both in this section and throughout the whole work, I offer in my defense this: I would sooner surrender a little of the language's grace than saddle the terminology of the schools with even the slightest obscurity.

[^**]: In the two premises thinking is understood in senses wholly unlike: in the major, as bearing on an object at large (and so as it might be given in intuition); in the minor, only as it subsists in relation to self-consciousness, where accordingly no object at all is thought and merely the relation to oneself as subject (as the form of thinking) is represented. In the former the talk is of things thinkable in no other way than as subjects; in the latter, though, not of ***things*** but of ***thinking*** (all objects being abstracted from), within which the I serves throughout as the subject of consciousness; whence the conclusion cannot run 'I can exist in no other way than as subject', but only 'in thinking my existence I can put myself to use solely as the subject of the judgment'—an identical proposition that lays bare absolutely nothing about the manner of my existence.

[^***]: Clarity is not, whatever the logicians say, the consciousness of a representation; for some degree of consciousness—one insufficient, however, for recollection—must be present even in various obscure representations, since, lacking all consciousness, we should draw no distinction within a combination of obscure representations, whereas we are in fact able to do so with the marks of certain concepts (those of right and equity, say, or of the musician who, extemporizing, sounds many notes together). A representation is clear, rather, when the consciousness in it reaches as far as **consciousness** ***of its difference*** from others. Where that reaches far enough for telling apart, but not for consciousness of the difference, the representation would have to be called obscure still. Consciousness, then, has degrees without number, right down to the point of disappearing.

[^****]: Those who fancy they have done enough to launch a new possibility once they defy anyone to point out a contradiction in their assumptions (as, by and large, do all who suppose they can grasp the possibility of thinking—of which their only instance comes from the empirical intuitions of human life—as persisting even once that life has ceased), may be thrown into deep embarrassment by other possibilities not a whit more daring. Of such a kind is the possibility that a ***simple substance*** be divided into several substances and, the reverse way, that several run together (coalesce) into one simple substance. For though divisibility does presuppose a composite, it does not thereby demand a composite of substances, but merely one of degrees (of the sundry powers) belonging to one and the same substance. Just as one may picture every power and faculty of the soul, that of consciousness included, as having dwindled by half, yet so that substance would still be left over: so one may likewise picture, and without contradiction, this extinguished half as kept in being—not within the soul but outside it—and picture how, given that here everything ever real in the soul, and so possessed of a degree, hence the soul's whole existence, so that nothing goes missing, has been halved, a distinct substance would thereupon arise outside it. For the plurality that got divided was already on hand beforehand—yet not as a plurality of substances, but of each reality as a quantum of existence within it; and the substance's unity was but one manner of existing, which this division alone has changed into a plurality of subsistence. But by the same route several simple substances could once more run together into one, with nothing lost save the plurality of subsistence, since the single one would hold within it the reality-degree of all the earlier ones combined; and it may be that the simple substances yielding us the appearance of a matter (not, admittedly, through any mechanical or chemical influence on one another, but through one unknown to us, of which the former sort would be only the appearance) bring forth, by such a ***dynamic*** division of the parent souls regarded as ***intensive magnitudes***, child souls, while the parents in turn replenish their loss through coalition with fresh material of the like kind. I am the last to allow such brain-spun fancies the least worth or validity, and the earlier principles of the Analytic have besides driven home firmly enough that the categories (substance among them) are to be put to no use save an experiential one. But if the rationalist is bold enough to fashion, out of the sheer capacity for thinking and without any abiding intuition through which an object would be given, a being subsisting on its own—merely because the unity of apperception in thought permits him no explanation drawn from the composite, when he would do better to own that he cannot explain how a thinking nature is possible—then why should the ***materialist***, though he can just as little cite experience on behalf of his own possibilities, not be entitled to a like boldness, and use his principle, keeping the former's formal unity intact, toward the opposite end?

[^*****]: The: **I think**, as was already noted, **is** an empirical proposition, and it encloses within itself the proposition 'I exist'. I cannot, though, assert that all that thinks exists; for at that the attribute of thinking would render every being possessing it a necessary being. My existence, accordingly, is not to be viewed as inferred from the proposition 'I think', the way Descartes supposed (else the major premise 'all that thinks exists' would have to come first), but is one and the same with it. It voices an indeterminate empirical intuition—perception, that is (and hence it does prove that sensation, belonging therefore to sensibility, already underlies this existential proposition)—yet it comes ahead of the experience meant to determine perception's object through the category with respect to time; and existence here is not yet a category, since a category bears not on an object indeterminately given, but only on one of which a concept is had and about which one wishes to learn whether it is posited outside that concept as well, or not. An indeterminate perception here signifies merely something real that has been given, and given solely for thinking at large—thus not as appearance, nor as a thing in itself (noumenon), but as something that does in fact exist and is designated as such in the proposition 'I think'. For it should be noticed that, in calling the proposition 'I think' empirical, I do not thereby mean that the I in it is an empirical representation; on the contrary, that I is purely intellectual, since it pertains to thinking as such. Yet without some empirical representation to supply the matter for thinking, the act 'I think' would not occur, and the empirical is nothing but the condition under which the pure intellectual capacity is applied or used.
