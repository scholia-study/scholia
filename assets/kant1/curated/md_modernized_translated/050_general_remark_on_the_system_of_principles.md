## {{ 288 }} General remark on the system of principles

It is a very remarkable circumstance that the possibility of no thing whatever is open to our insight from the mere category; instead, an intuition must always lie ready at hand, one upon which the pure concept of understanding can have its objective reality set forth. Consider, say, the categories of relation. That 1) something might exist solely as ***subject*** and not as a mere determination belonging to other things—might, that is, be ***substance***; or that 2) since something exists, something further must exist, so that anything at all might function as a cause; or 3) that, where a plurality of things is present, the presence of one of them should entail something for the others, and this mutually, so that on this footing a community of substances might obtain—none of this admits of being seen from mere concepts. The same goes for the other categories too—e.g., how one thing taken along with many might be one and the same, i.e., a magnitude, and so on.||| So {{{ 199 }}} long, then, as intuition is wanting, there is no telling whether an object is being thought at all by means of the categories, nor whether any object could ever answer to them anywhere; and this bears out that, on their own, they amount to no ***cognitions*** whatever but are mere ***forms of thought***, serving to fashion cognitions from intuitions once these are {{ 289 }} given. For just this reason, too, no synthetic proposition can ever be built up out of categories alone. Take: in all existence there is substance, i.e., something able to exist only as a subject and never as a mere predicate; or again, each and every thing is a quantum, and so forth—cases in which nothing at all is available to carry us past a given concept so as to attach a further one to it. This is also why no one has ever managed to demonstrate a synthetic proposition from pure concepts of understanding taken by themselves—say, the proposition: whatever exists contingently has a cause. The furthest one has ever reached is the proof that, lacking this relation, the existence of what is contingent would be ***wholly beyond our comprehension***—i.e., that through the understanding we could not cognize _a priori_ the existence of any such thing; yet this yields no conclusion that the very same relation is at the same time what conditions the possibility of the matters themselves. Should one therefore turn back to look at the way we proved the principle of causality, one will become aware that our demonstration held good only of objects of a possible experience: whatever happens (every occurrence, that is) presupposes a cause—and this, moreover, in such a fashion that the proof went through only as a principle for the possibility of experience, hence for the ***cognition*** of an object presented in ***empirical intuition***, and by no means from concepts alone. Granted, one cannot deny that this proposition—that everything {{ 290 }} contingent has to have a cause—shines forth clearly to anyone from concepts alone; but by then the concept of the contingent has already been framed so as to carry within it not the modal category (something whose non-being ***admits of being thought***) but rather the category of relation (something capable of existing only as the upshot of another), and on that understanding it is, to be sure, an identical proposition: whatever can exist solely as a consequence carries its cause with it. Indeed, whenever we are called upon to give examples of contingent existence, it is always to ***alterations*** that we appeal, and not merely to the bare possibility of the ***thought of the opposite***.[^*] {{{ 200 }}} {{ 291 }} An alteration, though, is an occurrence, and as such is made possible only by a cause, so that its own non-being is, taken by itself, possible; contingency is accordingly recognized in the circumstance that a thing can exist only as a cause's effect, and so, once a thing is posited as contingent, to assert that it has a cause is an analytic proposition.

Still more remarkable, however, is the following: that to grasp how things are possible according to the categories, and so to display the ***objective reality*** of these latter, what we need is not intuitions merely but, throughout, even ***outer intuitions***. Suppose we take the pure concepts of ***relation***: we then find that 1) to furnish, as a correlate to the concept of ***substance***, something ***persistent*** within intuition—and by that means to secure this concept's objective reality—we have need of an intuition in ***space*** (namely, of matter), since space alone stands persistently determined, whereas time, and with it everything belonging to inner sense, is in perpetual flux. 2) To exhibit ***alteration***—the intuition that answers to the concept of ***causality***—we have to take as our example motion, i.e., alteration in space; and it is indeed by this alone that alterations become intuitable to us whose possibility lies past the comprehension of any pure understanding. Alteration consists in the union, within the existence of one and the very same thing, of determinations contradictorily opposed to one another. By what means it is possible that a given {{ 292 }} state should be succeeded, in the same thing, by one opposed to it—of this no reason can render itself an account without an example, nor can it so much as be made intelligible short of intuition; and the intuition in question is that of a point's motion through space, whose existence at differing locations (a succession, that is, of opposed determinations) is what alone first sets alteration before us; for, to make even our own inner alterations thinkable afterward, we are obliged to picture time—the form of inner sense—by figure as a line, and inner alteration by the tracing out of that line (motion), so that the successive {{{ 201 }}} existing of ourselves across differing states is brought home to us through outer intuition; and the real ground of this lies in the fact that every alteration takes for granted something persistent in intuition if it is so much as to be perceived as an alteration at all, while within inner sense no persistent intuition is ever encountered. – Finally, so far as its possibility is concerned, the category of ***community*** simply cannot be grasped through bare reason, and thus there is no gaining insight into this concept's objective reality apart from intuition—outer intuition in space, that is. For how is the possibility to be thought that, given the existence of several substances, something (as effect) should follow reciprocally in the existence of the others from that of a single one—so that, since something is present in {{ 293 }} the first, on this ground something must be present in the rest as well, a thing not to be understood from the existence of these latter taken alone? For precisely this is what community demands, and yet, among things each of which isolates itself utterly through its own subsistence, it remains wholly unintelligible. Thus Leibniz, when he assigned the substances of the world a community such as the understanding alone thinks them to have, found a deity necessary as intermediary; for taken by their existence alone, that community struck him—rightly enough—as inconceivable. We, on the other hand, are quite able to make the possibility of such community (of substances taken as appearances) intelligible to ourselves, provided we represent them in space, that is, in outer intuition. For space already harbors within itself, _a priori_, formal outer relations that serve as conditions for the possibility of the real ones (in action and counteraction, hence in community). — By the same token it is readily shown that things can be possible as ***magnitudes***—and the category of magnitude thereby have its objective reality made out—only within outer intuition, and that by the latter's mediation alone can this afterward be carried over to inner sense too. To keep from being prolix, however, I shall have to consign the examples of all this to the reader's own reflection.

This entire observation carries great weight—not solely for corroborating the refutation of idealism given above, but far more for marking out for us, once the topic becomes ***self-knowledge*** drawn from mere inner consciousness {{ 294 }} and from the determining of our nature with no assistance from outer empirical intuitions, just where the bounds of the possibility of any such cognition lie.

The concluding upshot of this whole section runs, then, as follows: the principles of the pure understanding, one and all, are nothing further than _a priori_ principles {{{ 202 }}} for the possibility of experience, to which alone, likewise, every synthetic proposition _a priori_ has its reference—indeed, the very possibility of such propositions rests wholly upon this reference.

[^*]: The non-being of matter is easy enough to think, and yet the ancients drew from this no inference of its contingency. But not even that alternating of being and non-being in some given state of a thing—wherein all alteration consists—proves in the least the contingency of that state, as though from the actuality of its opposite; the rest of a body, for instance, which sets in after motion, does not yet prove that the body's motion was contingent on the ground that the former is opposite to the latter. For here that opposite is merely logical, not ***realiter*** ***opposed*** to the other. To establish the contingency of its motion, what one would need to prove is that ***instead*** of moving at the prior moment of time the body might ***at that time*** have been at rest—not that it comes to rest ***afterward***; for in the latter case the two opposites are perfectly capable of coexisting.
