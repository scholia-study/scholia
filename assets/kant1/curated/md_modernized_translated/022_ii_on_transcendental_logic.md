## II. On Transcendental Logic

As was indicated earlier, general logic sets aside the whole content of a cognition—every bearing it may have upon the object—and attends solely to logical form, namely to how cognitions stand toward one another, which is to say the form of thinking as such. Now because intuitions come in two kinds, pure as well as empirical (as the transcendental aesthetic makes plain), a corresponding difference might well be found between pure and empirical {{ 80 }} thinking of objects. Were that so, we should have a logic that did not leave every content of cognition out of account; for a logic confined to the rules by which an object is thought purely would shut out any cognition whose content happened to be empirical. Such a logic would moreover reach back to the source from which our cognitions of objects arise, at least so far as that source cannot be charged to the objects, whereas general logic takes no interest whatever in where cognition has its beginning but examines representations—be they lodged in us from the first _a priori_, {{{ 78 }}} or furnished only through experience—purely under the rules by which the understanding sets them to work against one another in the act of thinking, and so busies itself only with that form of the understanding which can be conferred on representations, from whatever quarter they may otherwise have sprung.

At this point I insert an observation whose bearing carries over into every discussion still to come, one that ought to be held firmly before us, namely this: that the term transcendental is owed not to every cognition _a priori_, but only to the cognition through which we come to know that, and in what fashion, particular representations (whether intuitions or concepts) are put to use or are rendered possible purely _a priori_ (transcendental, in other words, marking the possibility of a cognition, or its employment, {{ 81 }} _a priori_). For this reason space itself, and every geometrical specification of it _a priori_ alike, is no transcendental representation; what alone may carry that name is the cognition that these representations arise from no empirical source whatever, together with the possibility of how they are nonetheless able to apply _a priori_ to objects of experience. The employment of space in regard to objects generally would, in the same way, prove transcendental too: restrict it, though, to objects of the senses alone, and it thereby comes to be styled empirical. The line drawn between the transcendental and the empirical thus has its home solely within a critique of cognitions and says nothing about how those cognitions are related to their object.

Anticipating, then, that there might after all exist concepts bearing _a priori_ upon objects—not in the guise of pure or sensible intuitions, but as sheer operations of pure thought, hence concepts, though ones owing their origin to neither experience nor an aesthetic ground—we sketch out for ourselves beforehand the idea of a science of pure understanding and of the cognition yielded by reason, whereby objects come to be thought entirely _a priori_. Any such science—one that fixed the source, the reach, and the objective validity belonging to cognitions of this kind—would necessarily bear the name ***transcendental logic***, for its business lies purely with the laws governing understanding and reason, yet only to the degree that {{ 82 }} these laws are brought to bear on objects _a priori_, and not, in the manner of general logic, on rational cognitions empirical and pure without any distinction being drawn.
