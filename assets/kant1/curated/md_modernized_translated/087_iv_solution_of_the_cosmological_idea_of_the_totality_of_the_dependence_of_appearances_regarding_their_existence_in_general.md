## {{{ 378 }}} {{ 587 }} IV. Solution of the cosmological idea of the totality of appearances' dependence, taken with regard to their existence in general.

In the previous number we considered the alterations of the sensible world as they stand in their dynamical series, where each is subordinate to another that serves as its cause. Here that series of states functions for us purely as a thread by which to reach an existence capable of being the highest condition of all that is alterable — namely, the ***necessary being***. The concern now is not with unconditioned causality, but with the unconditioned existence of the substance itself. The series before us, then, is strictly a series of concepts rather than of intuitions, to the extent that one concept furnishes the condition for another.

Yet it is easy to see: that because everything comprised in the totality of appearances is alterable, and thus conditioned as regards its existence, nowhere in the series of dependent existence could there be an unconditioned member whose existence would be necessary without qualification; and that, accordingly, if appearances were things in themselves — so that for just this reason their condition would invariably fall, along with the conditioned, into a single, self-same series of intuitions — {{ 588 }} then a necessary being could never figure as the condition under which the appearances of the sensible world exist.

The dynamical regress, however, carries within it something proper to it that sets it apart from the mathematical: namely that, whereas the mathematical has to do only with putting parts together into a whole, or with breaking a whole down into its parts — so that the conditions of such a series must invariably count as parts of it, and hence as things of the same kind, and therefore as appearances — the dynamical regress, by contrast, is concerned not with how an unconditioned whole might arise from given parts, or an unconditioned part be found for a given whole, but with tracing a state back to its cause, or the contingent existence of the substance itself back to a necessary one; and here the condition is under no obligation to form, together with the conditioned, one empirical series.

{{{ 379 }}} So, with the seeming antinomy that confronts us, one avenue still lies open to us: that the two mutually opposed propositions may each hold good simultaneously, though under different relations, in such a way that all things belonging to the sensible world are through and through contingent, and therefore possess an existence that is never anything but empirically conditioned, while for all that the series taken whole admits of a non-empirical condition as well — namely, an unconditionally necessary being. For such a being, being an intelligible condition, would not itself count among the members of the series (not even as its highest member), and would leave {{ 589 }} no member of the series empirically unconditioned; instead it would let the entire sensible world remain in the empirically conditioned existence that runs through each of its members. Herein, then, this manner of grounding appearances upon an unconditioned existence would part ways with the empirically unconditioned causality (freedom) of the foregoing article: for with freedom the thing itself, functioning as cause (_substantia phaenomenon_), still belonged within the series of conditions, and it was solely ***its causality*** that was thought as intelligible; whereas in the present case the necessary being must be thought wholly apart from the series of the sensible world (as an _ens extramundanum_) and as merely intelligible — which is the one and only means of keeping it from falling itself under the law governing the contingency and dependence of all appearances.

The ***regulative principle*** of reason, in view of this problem of ours, therefore runs as follows: that within the sensible world every existence is empirically conditioned, and that no property whatever, anywhere within it, carries an unconditioned necessity; that among the conditions in the series there is not one for which the empirical condition should not always be looked for in some possible experience, and pursued as far as one is able; and that nothing licenses us to trace any existence back to a condition lying beyond the empirical series, or to treat it, even within that series, as utterly self-standing and independent — though this in no way debars us from allowing that the series in its entirety might rest upon some {{ 590 }} intelligible being (one which, for that very reason, stands clear of every empirical condition, and holds instead that on which the possibility of all these appearances rests).

Nor is the aim here at all to demonstrate the unconditionally necessary existence of a being, or even merely to rest upon it the possibility of a merely intelligible condition for the existence of the appearances of the sensible world; the aim is rather only this: that, in the same way as we hold reason in check {{{ 380 }}} so that it will not let go of the thread of empirical conditions and lose itself among ***transcendent*** grounds of explanation that permit of no exhibition _in concreto_, we should on the other side likewise confine the law of the merely empirical employment of the understanding, so that it does not settle the possibility of things as such, and so that it does not, on this ground, ***pronounce the intelligible impossible***, unusable though the intelligible be to us for explaining appearances. All that is thereby established, then, is that the sweeping contingency of every natural thing, together with every one of its (empirical) conditions, sits perfectly well alongside the arbitrary assumption of a necessary — albeit merely intelligible — condition; so that no real contradiction turns up between these two claims, and they may accordingly ***both alike be true***. Grant that a being of the understanding of this utterly necessary kind may in itself be impossible {{ 591 }}: even so, no such conclusion can be drawn from the general contingency and dependence of all that pertains to the sensible world, nor from the principle bidding us never to come to rest at any one member of that series, in so far as it is contingent, and to appeal to a cause beyond the world. Reason pursues one path in its empirical employment and another, distinctive path in its transcendental employment.

The sensible world holds nothing beyond appearances, and these are nothing but representations, themselves in every instance sensibly conditioned; and since the objects we deal with here are never things in themselves, there is nothing surprising in the fact that we are at no point authorized to spring, from whatever member of the empirical series it might be, clean out of the web of sensibility, as if these members were things in themselves subsisting apart from their transcendental ground, things one might quit in order to hunt for the cause of their existence somewhere beyond them; a move that would, with contingent ***things***, ultimately have to be made, but never with bare ***representations*** of things — representations whose very contingency is nothing more than phenomenon, and can issue in no regress other than the one fixing the phenomena, which is to say the empirical regress. But to conceive an intelligible ground for the appearances — that is, for the sensible world — and to conceive it as set free from the latter's contingency, runs counter neither to the unbounded empirical regress within the series of appearances {{ 592 }} nor to the through-and-through contingency of that same series. And that is likewise the sole thing we had to deliver in order to lift the seeming antinomy — the sole thing, indeed, achievable by this route alone. For if the {{{ 381 }}} condition belonging on each occasion to every conditioned (as regards existence) is sensible, and just on that account part of the series, then it is in its turn conditioned once more (as the antithesis of the fourth antinomy brings out). Thus either a standing conflict with reason — which insists on the unconditioned — had to persist, or else the unconditioned had to be lodged beyond the series, within the intelligible, whose necessity neither calls for nor tolerates any empirical condition, and which is, in relation to appearances, unconditionally necessary.

Reason's empirical employment — with regard to the conditions under which anything exists in the sensible world — takes no injury from our conceding a being that is merely intelligible; it advances instead, following the principle of pervasive contingency, from empirical conditions upward to higher ones that are themselves every bit as empirical. And just as little, on the other hand, does this regulative principle rule out our positing an intelligible cause standing outside the series, once the concern is with reason's pure employment (in respect of ends). For there that cause designates nothing more than the ground — for us merely transcendental and beyond our knowledge — of the possibility of the sensible series as a whole; and this ground's existence, which hangs on none of that series' conditions and, measured against them, is necessary without qualification, {{ 593 }} stands in no opposition whatever to the boundless contingency of the first, and therefore none to the regress in the series of empirical conditions that nowhere reaches its close.
