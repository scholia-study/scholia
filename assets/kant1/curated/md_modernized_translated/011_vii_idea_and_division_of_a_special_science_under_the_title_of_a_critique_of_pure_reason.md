## VII. Idea and division of a special science, under the title of a Critique of Pure Reason

From all this there now emerges the idea of a special science, which can be called the ***Critique of Pure Reason***. For reason {{{ 43 }}} is the faculty that provides the principles of cognition _a priori_. Therefore pure reason is that which contains the principles for cognizing something absolutely _a priori_. An ***organon*** of pure reason would be a sum total of those principles according to which all {{ 25 }} pure cognitions _a priori_ can be acquired and actually brought about. The exhaustive application of such an organon would provide a system of pure reason. But since this requires very much, and it is still an open question whether here an extension of our cognition is possible at all and in what cases it is possible: we can regard a science of the mere estimation of pure reason, of its sources and limits, as the ***propaedeutic*** to the system of pure reason. Such a science would have to be called not a ***doctrine***, but only a ***critique*** of pure reason, and its utility in regard to speculation would really be only negative, serving not for the extension but only for the purification of our reason, and to keep it free of errors, by which a very great deal is already gained. I call all cognition transcendental that is occupied not so much with objects but rather with our mode of cognition of objects in general, insofar as this is to be possible _a priori_. A ***system*** of such concepts would be called ***transcendental philosophy***. But this is again too much for the beginning. For because such a science would have to contain completely both analytic cognition and synthetic _a priori_ cognition, it is, as far as our aim is concerned, of too wide an extent, since we need to carry the analysis only as far as is indispensably necessary in order to gain complete insight into the principles of synthesis {{ 26 }} _a priori_, which is our sole concern. This investigation, which we can properly call not doctrine but only transcendental critique, because it does not have the extension of the cognitions themselves but only their correction as its aim {{{ 44 }}}, and is to supply the touchstone of the worth or unworth of all cognitions _a priori_, is that with which we are now occupied. Such a critique is accordingly a preparation, if possible, for an organon, and if this should not succeed, at least for a canon of the same, according to which the complete system of the philosophy of pure reason, whether it consist in the extension or mere limitation of its cognition, could in any case someday be exhibited both analytically and synthetically. For that this is possible, indeed that such a system can be of no very great extent, so that one can hope to complete it entirely, can be judged in advance from the fact that it is not the nature of things, which is inexhaustible, but the understanding, which judges about the nature of things, and even this in turn only in regard to its cognition _a priori_, that constitutes the object, whose supply, since we cannot seek it externally, cannot remain hidden from us, and in all likelihood is small enough to be completely recorded, judged according to its worth or unworth, and brought under {{ 27 }} correct estimation. Still less may one expect here a critique of the books and systems of pure reason, but rather that of the faculty of pure reason itself. Only if this lies at the foundation does one have a secure touchstone for estimating the philosophical content of old and new works in this field; otherwise the unauthorized historian and judge evaluates the groundless assertions of others through his own, which are equally groundless.

Transcendental philosophy is the idea of a science, for which the critique of pure reason is to sketch the entire plan architectonically, i.e., from principles, with a full guarantee for the completeness and security of all the parts that constitute this edifice. It is the system of all principles of pure reason. That this critique is not itself already called transcendental philosophy rests merely on the fact that, in order to be a complete system, it would also have to contain an exhaustive analysis of all human cognition _a priori_. Now it is true that our critique must certainly also lay before our eyes a complete enumeration of all the root concepts that constitute the aforementioned pure cognition {{{ 45 }}}. But from the exhaustive analysis of these concepts themselves, as well as from the complete review of those derived from them, it properly abstains, partly because this analysis would not be purposeful {{ 28 }}, since it does not have the hesitation that is encountered in synthesis, for the sake of which the entire critique actually exists, partly because it would be contrary to the unity of the plan to burden oneself with the responsibility for the completeness of such an analysis and derivation, from which one could after all be relieved in respect to one's aim. This completeness of the analysis, however, as well as the derivation from the _a priori_ concepts that are to be provided in the future, is easy to supply, if only they are first present as exhaustive principles of synthesis, and nothing is lacking in respect of this essential aim.

To the critique of pure reason there accordingly belongs everything that constitutes transcendental philosophy, and it is the complete idea of transcendental philosophy, but not yet this science itself, because it goes only so far in the analysis as is requisite for the complete estimation of synthetic cognition _a priori_.

The foremost consideration in the division of such a science is: that no concepts whatsoever may enter into it that contain anything empirical within themselves, or that the _a priori_ cognition be entirely pure. Hence, although the highest principles of morality and the fundamental concepts of it are _a priori_ cognitions, they nevertheless do not belong in {{ 29 }} transcendental philosophy, because, although they do not themselves lay the concepts of pleasure and displeasure, of desires and inclinations, etc., which are all of empirical origin, at the foundation of their precepts, they must necessarily draw them into the composition of the system of pure morality in the concept of duty, as an obstacle that must be overcome, or as an enticement that must not be made into a determining ground. Hence transcendental philosophy is a philosophy of pure, merely speculative reason. For everything practical, insofar as it contains motives, relates to feelings, which belong to empirical sources of cognition.

{{{ 46 }}} If one now wishes to set up the division of this science from the general viewpoint of a system in general, then the one we now present must contain, first, a ***Doctrine of Elements***, and second, a ***Doctrine of Method*** of pure reason. Each of these main parts would have its subdivision, the grounds for which nevertheless cannot yet be presented here. Only so much seems necessary for an introduction or preliminary remark, that there are two stems of human cognition, which perhaps spring from a common, but to us unknown, root, namely sensibility and understanding, through the first of which objects are given to us, but through the second of which they are thought. Now if sensibility should contain representations _a priori_ {{ 30 }}, which constitute the condition under which objects are given to us, it would belong to transcendental philosophy. The transcendental doctrine of sense would have to belong to the first part of the science of elements, because the conditions under which alone the objects of human cognition are given precede those under which those same objects are thought.
