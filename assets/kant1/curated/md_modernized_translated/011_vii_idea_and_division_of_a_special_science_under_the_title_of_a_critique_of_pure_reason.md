## VII. Idea and division of a special science, under the title of a Critique of Pure Reason

All of this yields the idea of a distinct science, one that may bear the title ***Critique of Pure Reason***. For reason {{{ 43 }}} is the power that supplies us with the principles of _a priori_ cognition. Pure reason, then, is the reason that holds within it the principles for cognizing something in a wholly _a priori_ manner. An ***organon*** of pure reason would amount to the totality of those principles whereby every {{ 25 }} pure _a priori_ cognition can be gained and genuinely established. Applied in full, such an organon would yield a system of pure reason. Yet because that asks for a great deal, and because it is as yet an open question whether—and, if so, in which cases—our cognition admits of any enlargement here at all: we can treat a science that merely appraises pure reason, together with its sources and its bounds, as the ***propaedeutic*** to a system of pure reason. A science of that kind would deserve the name not of a ***doctrine*** but only of a ***critique*** of pure reason, and, where speculation is concerned, its value would truly be negative alone—not enlarging our reason but merely cleansing it, holding it clear of errors, and that in itself is no small achievement. I give the name transcendental to every cognition that concerns itself less with objects than with the way in which we cognize objects, so far as that way is to be possible _a priori_. A ***system*** built from such concepts would bear the name ***transcendental philosophy***. This, however, is in turn more than a beginning can bear. For inasmuch as a science of that sort would have to hold, in full, both analytic cognition and synthetic cognition _a priori_, it reaches, as far as our purpose goes, too far—since we are permitted to carry the analysis only to the point where it becomes strictly indispensable for surveying, across their whole range, the principles of synthesis {{ 26 }} _a priori_, and it is those alone that we are after. This inquiry—which, strictly speaking, we may term not a doctrine but a transcendental critique, since its purpose {{{ 44 }}} is not to enlarge the cognitions themselves but only to set them right, and since it is meant to furnish the touchstone for the worth or worthlessness of every _a priori_ cognition—is what we are engaged upon at present. A critique of this kind is thus a groundwork—for an organon, if that can be managed, and, failing that, at least for a canon belonging to it—on the strength of which the full system of the philosophy of pure reason (be it a matter of enlarging or merely of bounding its cognition) could one day be laid out, along analytic as well as synthetic lines. That it is indeed feasible—and, more than that, that such a system need not be of any very large extent, so that one may reasonably hope to finish it in full—can be judged beforehand from the following: that what forms the subject matter here is not the nature of things, which has no end, but the understanding, which delivers verdicts upon the nature of things, and even this only as regards its _a priori_ cognition; and the stock of that cognition, since we have after all no license to look for it outside ourselves, cannot stay concealed from us, and to all appearances is modest enough to be taken in completely, weighed for its worth or worthlessness, and placed under {{ 27 }} a just estimate. Still less should one look here for a critique of the books and systems belonging to pure reason; what is meant is a critique of the very faculty of pure reason itself. Only where this serves as the foundation does one possess a reliable touchstone for gauging the philosophical substance of works old and new in this domain; otherwise the unqualified chronicler and judge measures the baseless claims of others against his own, which are no less baseless.

Transcendental philosophy is the idea of a science whose entire plan the critique of pure reason is charged with drafting architectonically—that is, out of principles—while guaranteeing in full the completeness and soundness of every component that goes into this structure. It is the system comprising all the principles of pure reason. The reason this critique does not already itself bear the name of transcendental philosophy lies purely in this: that, to constitute a complete system, it would also need to embrace an exhaustive analysis of the whole of human _a priori_ cognition. Admittedly our critique must indeed set out before us a full census of all the root concepts out of which the pure cognition in question {{{ 45 }}} is composed. But it rightly holds back from any exhaustive analysis of these concepts as such, and likewise from a full review of those derived from them—partly because such a breaking-down would serve no purpose {{ 28 }}, given that it lacks the misgiving one encounters in synthesis, which is precisely what the whole critique exists for, and partly because it would offend against the plan's unity to shoulder the task of vouching for the completeness of an analysis and derivation of that sort, from which, in light of one's purpose, one might in any case be spared. This completeness—of the breaking-down no less than of the derivation from the _a priori_ concepts still to be delivered—is in any event readily supplied, provided only that these are first on hand as fully worked-out principles of synthesis, and that nothing is lacking with regard to this essential purpose.

Everything that constitutes transcendental philosophy therefore falls within the critique of pure reason, and the critique is the complete idea of transcendental philosophy—yet not, as yet, that science itself, since it pushes the analysis only as far as the full appraisal of synthetic _a priori_ cognition demands.

The chief thing to attend to in dividing up such a science is this: that no concept may find its way in that carries anything empirical within it, or, put otherwise, that the _a priori_ cognition be entirely pure. Thus, even though the highest principles of morality, along with its basic concepts, are _a priori_ cognitions, they still have no place within {{ 29 }} transcendental philosophy, for although they do not themselves ground their precepts upon the concepts of pleasure and displeasure, of appetites and inclinations, and so forth—concepts that one and all arise from an empirical source—they must nonetheless, in framing the concept of duty, unavoidably bring such concepts in when the system of pure morality is drawn up, whether as an obstacle to be surmounted or as a lure that is not to be converted into a motive. Transcendental philosophy is, for this reason, the philosophy of a reason that is pure and merely speculative. For all that is practical, in so far as it carries incentives with it, has to do with feelings, and these count among the empirical sources of cognition.

{{{ 46 }}} Should one now set about dividing this science from the general vantage of a system as such, then the division we are here putting forward must comprise, in the first place, a ***doctrine of elements***, and in the second, a ***doctrine of method*** of pure reason. Each of these main divisions would carry subdivisions of its own, the grounds for which, however, do not yet admit of being laid out at this point. By way of introduction, or advance remark, only this much appears to be needed: that human cognition has two stems, which spring perhaps from one root that is shared but hidden from us—namely, sensibility and understanding—the first of them giving objects to us, whereas by the second they come to be thought. Now to the extent that sensibility should prove to contain _a priori_ representations {{ 30 }} that constitute the condition under which objects are given to us, it would fall within transcendental philosophy. The transcendental doctrine of the senses would have to be placed in the opening part of the elementary science, for the conditions that alone allow the objects of human cognition to be given rank ahead of those under which such objects come to be thought.
