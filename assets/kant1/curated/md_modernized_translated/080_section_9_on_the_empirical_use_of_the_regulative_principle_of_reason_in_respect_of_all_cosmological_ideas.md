## {{{ 353 }}} Ninth Section. On the empirical use of the regulative principle of reason, in respect of all cosmological ideas.

Since, as we have shown on several occasions, there is no transcendental use, neither of the understanding's pure concepts nor of reason's concepts; since the absolute totality of the series of the conditions in the sensible world rests solely upon a transcendental use of reason, which requires this unconditioned completeness of what it {{ 544 }} presupposes as a ***thing in itself***, yet the sensible world contains no such thing: the discussion can accordingly no longer concern the absolute magnitude of the series within it, whether these be limited or ***in themselves*** unlimited, but only how far, in the empirical regress, we are to trace experience back to its conditions, so that we come to rest, as reason's rule prescribes, at no answer to its questions save the one befitting the object.

Thus what remains to us alone is the ***validity of reason's principle***, taken as a rule governing the ***continuation*** and extent of any possible experience, once its invalidity as a constitutive principle of the appearances, taken in themselves, has been sufficiently demonstrated. Moreover, if we can set that validity beyond doubt before our eyes, the conflict of reason with itself is brought entirely to an end, since not only is the illusion that had set it at variance with itself removed through critical resolution, but in its place the sense in which reason accords with itself — and whose misinterpretation alone occasioned the conflict — is opened up, and a principle that was otherwise ***dialectical*** is transformed into a ***doctrinal*** one. Indeed, if this principle can be vindicated in its subjective significance — namely, to determine the understanding's greatest possible use in experience in a way conformable to that experience's objects — then it amounts to just as much as if, in the manner of an axiom (which, from {{ 545 }} pure reason, is impossible), it determined the objects in themselves _a priori_; for even the latter could have no greater influence, with respect to the objects of experience, upon the extension and correction of our {{{ 354 }}} cognition than by proving itself active in the most extensive empirical use of our understanding.
