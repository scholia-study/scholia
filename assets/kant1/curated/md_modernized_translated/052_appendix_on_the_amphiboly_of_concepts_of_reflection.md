## {{ 316 }} Appendix. 

## On the amphiboly of concepts of reflection, brought about by mistaking the understanding's empirical employment for its transcendental one.

Reflection (_reflexio_) does not deal with objects themselves in order to draw concepts straightaway from them; it is instead that condition {{{ 215 }}} of the mind in which we initially set about tracing the subjective conditions that would let us reach concepts at all. It consists in an awareness of how given representations stand toward our several sources of cognition, and this awareness is the only thing by which their mutual relation can be fixed aright. Before we handle our representation any further, the initial question runs thus: within which faculty of cognition do they have their common place? Is it before the understanding, or before the senses, that they get linked together or set side by side? Any number of judgments are adopted from habit or fastened together by inclination; yet since no reflection comes before them—or at any rate follows critically after—they count as judgments that took their rise in the understanding. Not every judgment calls for an ***investigation***—that is, a heeding of the grounds of truth—since, when a judgment is {{ 317 }} certain without mediation (for instance, that only one straight line can lie between two points), there is no closer indication of its truth to be pointed out than what it already states of itself. Every judgment, though—indeed every comparison—does call for a ***reflection***, namely a discrimination of which cognitive power the given concepts fall under. That operation whereby I set the comparison of representations as such alongside the very cognitive power within which the comparing takes place, and by which I make out whether the representations are being weighed against each other as items of the pure understanding or of sensible intuition—this I name ***transcendental reflection***. Now the relation under which the concepts are able to stand toward each other within a mental state is one of ***identity*** and ***difference***, of ***agreement*** and ***conflict***, of the ***inner*** and the ***outer***, and lastly of the ***determinable*** together with its ***determination*** (matter and form). Whether this relation is settled rightly turns on the cognitive power within which the concepts ***subjectively*** go together—that is, on whether it be sensibility or the understanding. For a difference in the latter yields a large difference in the manner in which the former are to be conceived.

Ahead of any objective judgment we set the concepts against one another so as to arrive at ***identity*** (of a plurality of representations gathered under a single concept), which serves ***universal*** judgments, or at their **difference**, which yields {{ 318 }} ***particular*** ones, or at **agreement**, out of which ***affirmative*** judgments may spring, and {{{ 216 }}} at ***conflict***, out of which negative ones may come, and so on. On this ground, it would seem, the concepts cited above deserve the name concepts of comparison (_conceptus comparationis_). But once the point at stake is no longer the logical form but the content of the concepts—that is, whether the things on their own account are alike or unlike, concordant or at odds, and so forth—those things may stand in a double relation to our cognitive power, to sensibility on the one hand and to the understanding on the other; and because everything hinges on the site, ***where*** they have their home, and on the fashion ***in which*** they are to belong together: it is transcendental reflection—the bearing of given representations upon one or the other manner of cognition—that alone can settle how they stand to each other; and whether the things are alike or unlike, in accord or in conflict, and the like, is not to be made out immediately from the concepts as such by bare comparison (_comparatio_), but first through telling apart the manner of cognition under which they fall, with the help of a transcendental reflection (_reflexio_). One might grant, to be sure, that ***logical reflection*** amounts to nothing more than a comparison, since in its course the cognitive power under which the given representations fall is left wholly out of view, so that, as regards where they are lodged in the mind, they may be handled as {{ 319 }} of one kind; ***transcendental reflection***, by contrast (which reaches to the objects themselves), holds within it the basis on which any objective comparing of representations against each other becomes possible at all, and for that reason stands far apart from logical reflection, seeing that the cognitive power the two fall under is not one and the same. Such transcendental reflection is an obligation that no one may throw off who means to pass any _a priori_ judgment upon things. We shall now put it to work, and it will shed on the task of fixing the understanding's genuine office a light that is far from slight.

1. ***Identity*** and ***difference***. Suppose an object comes before us on several occasions, yet each time carrying the very same inner determinations (_qualitas et quantitas_): then, taken as an object of the pure understanding, it stays throughout one and the same, not a multitude but a single thing (_numerica identitas_); if, on the other hand, it is appearance, the comparison of concepts no longer matters, for however thoroughly alike everything about them may be, the diversity of the sites this appearance occupies at one and the same time already suffices to ground the ***numerical difference*** of the object (of the senses) {{{ 217 }}} itself. So with a pair of water-drops one may set aside every inner difference (of quality and of quantity) entirely, and their being intuited at once in separate places already warrants counting them as numerically {{ 320 }} distinct. Leibniz treated the appearances as things in themselves, and so as _intelligibilia_, that is, objects of the pure understanding (though, because their representations were confused, he tagged them with the name of phenomena); on that footing his principle of ***indiscernibles*** (_principium identitatis indiscernibilium_) truly could not be gainsaid—but inasmuch as they are objects of sensibility, and the understanding's use upon them is not pure but only empirical, multiplicity and numerical difference are supplied already by space in its own right, as that which conditions outer appearances. For a given portion of space, no matter how fully it resembles and matches another, lies all the same beyond it and by that very fact ranks as a portion different from the first, one adjoined to it so as to yield a larger space; and the same must accordingly apply to whatever occupies at one time the sundry positions of space, be it otherwise ever so alike and equal.

2. ***Agreement*** and ***conflict***. Where reality is pictured through the pure understanding alone (_realitas noumenon_), no conflict admits of being thought among realities—no relation, that is, in which, brought together within one subject, they cancel each other's consequences, giving 3 − 3 = 0. What is real in appearance (_realitas phaenomenon_), on the other hand, {{ 321 }} can quite well stand in conflict among themselves, and, joined in one and the same subject, one part may destroy, in whole or in part, the ***consequence of the other***—as with two motive forces along one straight line, in that they pull or press a point toward opposite sides, or again as with a gratification that offsets a pain.

3. The ***inner*** and the ***outer***. Within an object of the pure understanding, that alone counts as inner which bears no relation at all (so far as existence goes) to anything set apart from it. The inner determinations of a _substantia phaenomenon_ in space, by contrast, are relations and nothing besides, and the substance itself is, from top to bottom, a sum made up of relations alone. Substance in space is known to us solely by way of forces at work within it—forces that either urge other things toward a spot (attraction) or bar them from forcing their way in (repulsion and impenetrability); of further properties composing the concept {{{ 218 }}} of the substance that shows itself in space, the substance we name matter, we have no acquaintance. Regarded as an object of the pure understanding, on the contrary, each substance is bound to possess inner determinations and forces directed upon inner reality. Yet what inner accidents am I able to conceive apart from the ones my inner sense holds out to me—that is, apart from whatever is itself a ***thinking*** or bears an analogy to thinking? This is why Leibniz turned {{ 322 }} every substance—since he pictured them to himself as _Noumena_—and indeed the very components of matter, once he had stripped away from them in thought all that could stand for an outer relation, and with it ***composition*** too, into simple subjects furnished with powers of representation, in short **monads**.

4. ***Matter*** and ***form***. Here are two concepts placed beneath every other act of reflection as its ground—so tightly are they woven into each employment of the understanding. The first stands for what is determinable at large, the second for the determining of it (each taken in a transcendental sense, once one leaves aside all difference in what gets given and in the way it comes to be determined). Logicians of an earlier day gave the name matter to the universal, but reserved form for the specific difference. Within any judgment the concepts supplied may be styled its logical matter, while the way they are related (through the copula) may be styled the judgment's form. In any essence, the pieces that constitute it (_essentialia_) make up the matter, and the mode of their linkage within a thing makes up the essential form. So too, as concerns things at large, boundless reality was looked upon as the matter belonging to all possibility, while the curbing of that reality (negation) was looked upon as the form whereby, on transcendental concepts, one thing gets marked off from another. The understanding, after all, insists to begin with that something be furnished {{ 323 }} (in the concept at the very least) if it is to be capable of determining that something in one manner or another. So within the concept of the pure understanding matter comes before form, and on that account Leibniz posited things (monads) first, and inwardly a representational power lodged in them, meaning afterward to build upon this their outer relation and the commerce of their states (the states, that is, of their representations). On this view space and time became possible—space merely by dint of the relation among substances, time by dint of tying their determinations together, one to another, as grounds and as consequences. And thus, in fact, it would have to stand, could the pure understanding be brought to bear on objects without mediation, and were space and {{{ 219 }}} time determinations belonging to things in themselves. But if these are nothing more than sensible intuitions, within which we fix every object solely as appearance, then intuition's form (being a subjective make-up of sensibility) runs ahead of all matter (the sensations), so that space and time go before every appearance and before all the _datis_ of experience, and are what first render experience possible in the first place. The philosopher of the intellect could not abide that form should stand ahead of the things themselves and settle what they can be—a wholly justified reproach, granted his premise that we intuit things just as they are (albeit under a confused representation). Since sensible intuition, {{ 324 }} however, is an altogether peculiar subjective condition underlying every perception _a priori_, and since its form is original, that form stands given on its own account; and rather than matter (or the appearing things themselves) lying at the base—as one would be forced to conclude by mere concepts—the possibility of that matter instead takes a formal intuition (time and space) as something already given.

## Remark on the amphiboly of concepts of reflection

Grant me leave to name the position we allot to a concept—whether within sensibility or within the pure understanding—its ***transcendental location***. On such an approach, the appraisal of this position—one that falls to each concept in keeping with how its use varies—along with the directions, laid down by rules, for assigning that location to all concepts, would amount to the ***transcendental topic***: a teaching that would keep us soundly safe from the pure understanding's surreptitious overreaches and the illusions bred by them, since at every turn it would sort out which cognitive power the concepts genuinely answer to. Any concept whatever, any rubric beneath which a host of cognitions gather, may be termed a ***logical location***. Upon this rests the ***logical topic*** of ***Aristotle***, which schoolmasters and orators could enlist in order to hunt, beneath certain rubrics of thought, {{ 325 }} for what best fitted a matter at hand, and thereupon to argue with a show of thoroughness or to prate copiously.

{{{ 220 }}} The transcendental topic, on the other hand, holds nothing beyond the four rubrics of all comparing and distinguishing already named, which part ways with the categories in this: through them it is not the object—taken by what makes up its concept (magnitude, reality)—but merely the comparison of representations, a comparison prior to the concept of things, that gets set out in the whole of its variety. Such comparison, though, demands before anything else a reflection—a settling, that is, of the location where the representations of the things under comparison belong, whether the pure understanding thinks them or sensibility tenders them in appearance.

One may compare concepts on the logical plane without any concern for where their objects have their seat—whether, as noumena, before the understanding, or, as phenomena, before sensibility. Yet should we mean to carry these concepts over to the objects, transcendental reflection becomes indispensable at the outset, telling us for which cognitive power the objects are meant to serve—the pure understanding or sensibility. Lacking this reflection, my handling of the concepts grows quite unsure, {{ 326 }} and there spring up would-be synthetic principles that critical reason is unable to endorse, principles resting on nothing but a transcendental amphiboly—a mix-up, that is, of the understanding's pure object with appearance.

In the absence of any such transcendental topic, and so taken in by the amphiboly of concepts of reflection, the renowned Leibniz raised up an ***intellectual system of the world***—or rather fancied that he discerned the inner make-up of things—by weighing every object against the understanding alone and against the detached formal concepts of his thought. Our table of the concepts of reflection hands us the unlooked-for gain of setting out plainly what sets his doctrine apart in each of its parts, and along with it the steering ground of this odd cast of mind, a ground that lay in nothing more than a misunderstanding. He set all things side by side by way of concepts only, and came upon, as one would expect, no differences save the ones by which the understanding tells its pure concepts apart. The conditions of sensible intuition, which bring differences of their own along with them, he declined to count as original; for sensibility struck him as merely a muddled sort of representation, not a source of representations in its own right; appearance was to his mind the representation ***of the thing*** {{{ 221 }}} ***in itself***, set apart from cognition through the understanding only in respect of logical {{ 327 }} form, since the former, in its habitual want of analysis, pulls a certain blend of side-representations into the concept of the thing, a blend the understanding is able to strain off. To put it briefly: **Leibniz** ***intellectualized*** the appearances, much as Locke, on his system of ***noogony*** (if these coinages may be allowed me), had, one and all, ***sensualized*** the concepts of the understanding—that is, palmed them off as nothing beyond empirical or detached concepts of reflection. Rather than looking to the understanding and to sensibility as two thoroughly distinct wells of representations that, ***only in combination***, could yet judge of things with objective validity, each of these great men clung to just one of the pair—the one he took to bear directly on things in themselves—while leaving the other to do nothing except muddle or arrange what the first supplied.

***Leibniz***, then, set the objects of the senses—things as such—against one another within the understanding alone. ***First***, so far as it is to pronounce them alike or unlike. Because he kept in view only their concepts, and not the place they hold in intuition, where alone objects can be furnished, and because he passed over entirely the transcendental location of these concepts (whether the object should fall among appearances or among things in themselves), {{ 328 }} it could hardly turn out but that he stretched his principle of indiscernibles—valid only of concepts of things at large—across to the objects of the senses (_mundus phaenomenon_) into the bargain, and took himself to have thereby won for natural cognition no slight enlargement. Granted, should I know some drop of water, as a thing in itself, by the whole of its inner determinations, I can allow none of them to pass for different from another once the full concept of the one matches the other. If, however, it is appearance within space, its place lies not just in the understanding (beneath concepts) but in sensible outer intuition (in space); and there the physical positions stay wholly indifferent toward the inner determinations of things, so that a position = b can take in a thing fully like and equal to another at the position = a exactly as readily as though it differed from that other ever so much within. The diversity of positions, all on its own and with no added conditions, renders the multiplicity and separation of objects {{{ 222 }}} as appearances not merely possible but necessary as well. Hence that seeming law is no law of nature at all. It is nothing but an analytic rule for setting things side by side through concepts alone.

***Second***, the tenet that realities (being sheer affirmations) never come into logical conflict with one another is a perfectly true proposition touching the relation {{ 329 }} of concepts, yet says nothing at all about nature, and nothing whatever about any thing in itself (of which no concept is ours). For real conflict occurs wherever A − B = 0, that is, wherever one reality, paired with another in a single subject, cancels the effect of that other—as every obstacle and counter-effect in nature keeps unceasingly displaying to us, and as, resting on forces, must all the same bear the name _realitates phaenomena_. General mechanics is even able to lay down the empirical condition of this conflict in an _a priori_ rule, by attending to the opposition of directions—a condition of which the transcendental concept of reality has not the least inkling. Though Herr von Leibniz did not quite herald this proposition with the fanfare of a fresh principle, he nonetheless turned it to fresh claims, and his successors wrote it in so many words into their Leibnizian-Wolffian structure of doctrine. By this principle, to take one case, all evils amount to nothing beyond consequences of the bounds set to created things—negations, in short—since these are the sole thing at odds with reality (in the bare concept of a thing at large it truly does hold so, though not in things regarded as appearances). In the same spirit his followers judge it not merely possible but even {{ 330 }} natural to gather all reality into one being without any troublesome conflict, for they recognize no conflict apart from that of contradiction (whereby the concept of a thing cancels itself), and none of mutual curtailment, wherein one real ground cancels the effect of another—and the conditions for picturing such a case to ourselves we light upon in sensibility alone.

***Third***, the Leibnizian monadology rests on no ground save this: that our philosopher framed the difference of inner and outer purely by reference to the understanding. Substances {{{ 223 }}} at large are bound to hold something ***inner***, which stands accordingly clear of every outer relation, and so clear of composition too. Thus the simple forms the groundwork of what is inner in things in themselves. Yet the inner side of their state can rest neither on place, nor figure, nor contact, nor motion (all of them determinations that are outer relations), and so we are able to credit substances with no inner state other than the very one by which we inwardly determine our own sense—to wit, the ***state of representations***. And so the monads came finished, cast as the raw stuff of the whole universe, though their active force lies in representations only, whereby they are strictly at work nowhere but inside themselves.

It was for precisely this reason, too, that his principle of the possible ***community*** {{ 331 }} ***of substances*** among themselves had to take the shape of a ***preestablished harmony*** and could be no physical influence. For as everything busies itself only within, that is, with its own representations, the representational state of the one substance could hold no effective bond of any sort with that of another; instead, some third cause, flowing alike into them all, had to bring their states into mutual accord—not, indeed, by occasional aid fitted specially to each single case (_Systema assistentiae_), but through the oneness of the idea of a cause holding good for all, wherein every one of them must draw its existence and permanence, and with these its reciprocal correspondence under universal laws.

***Fourth***, his famed ***doctrine*** of ***time*** and ***space***, wherein he intellectualized these forms of sensibility, sprang from none but this same deception of transcendental reflection. Should I want to represent to myself outer relations of things by the bare understanding, it can come about only through a concept of their mutual action, and should I have to join one state of a given thing to another state, it can come about only within the sequence of grounds and consequences. In this manner, then, Leibniz conceived space as a particular ordering within the community of substances, and time as the dynamical run of their states. {{ 332 }} The trait proper to both, though, and independent of things—the trait they appear to own of themselves—he charged to the ***confusedness*** of these concepts, a confusedness that led what is a bare form of dynamical relations {{{ 224 }}} to be mistaken for an intuition subsisting by itself, standing on its own and going before the things themselves. So space and time turned into the intelligible form of the linkage of things (substances and their states) taken in themselves. The things, meanwhile, were intelligible substances (_substantiae noumena_). Even so he meant to press these concepts into service for appearances, since he conceded to sensibility no manner of intuition of its own, but hunted for every representation of objects, the empirical included, inside the understanding, and left the senses no office beyond the paltry one of blurring and marring the representations of the former.

But suppose we could pronounce something synthetically ***about things in themselves*** by way of the pure understanding (a thing that is in any case impossible)—even then it could in no wise be carried over to appearances, seeing that these do not present things in themselves. So in this second case I shall, within transcendental reflection, be obliged at all times to weigh my concepts only beneath the conditions of sensibility, whence space and time come out as determinations not of things in themselves but of appearances: what things may be in themselves I neither {{ 333 }} know nor have any need to know, since a thing can never reach me save in appearance.

I go to work likewise with the concepts of reflection that remain. Matter is a _substantia phaenomenon_. Whatever pertains to it within I look for across all the parts of space it fills and in all the effects it works, effects that of course can never be anything but appearances of outer sense. Thus I am left with nothing inner in the strict sense, but only with what is inner by comparison, which in its turn is itself built out of outer relations. But the strictly inner of matter, judged by the pure understanding, is likewise a mere whim; for matter is in no place an object for the pure understanding; the transcendental object, on the other hand, that might underlie this appearance we name matter is a bare something whose nature we should fail to grasp even were somebody in a position to tell it to us. For nothing lies open to our understanding but what brings along with it, within intuition, a counterpart to our words. If the laments—***We have no insight whatever into the inner of things***—are meant to say no more than: by the pure understanding we do not make out what the things appearing to us may be in themselves, then they are altogether unjust and unreasonable; for they ask that one be able to cognize things, and so to intuit them, with no {{{ 225 }}} senses at all, and hence that we possess a faculty of cognition parting from the human not in degree merely but even {{ 334 }} in the very intuition and sort, so that we should be, not human beings, but beings of whom we cannot so much as declare whether they are once possible, far less how they might be constituted. Into nature's interior push observation and the dissection of appearances, and there is no telling how far this may reach as time goes on. Those transcendental questions, though, which pass beyond nature, we should for all that never manage to answer, even were the whole of nature bared to us, for it is not so much as granted us to watch our own mind by any intuition other than the one belonging to our inner sense. In that inner sense, after all, the secret of our sensibility's origin lies hidden. How it bears on an object, and what the transcendental ground of that unity might be, lie past doubt buried too far down for us—who are acquainted even with ourselves through inner sense alone, and thus as appearance—to put so ill-fitted a tool of inquiry to any use except turning up, again and again, mere appearances, whose nonsensible cause we would nonetheless dearly like to track down.

What lends this critique of the inferences drawn from the sheer acts of reflection its great usefulness is this: it shows up in plain terms the emptiness of every inference about objects that are matched against one another within the understanding alone, {{ 335 }} while at once bearing out the point we have chiefly pressed home—that appearances, though not taken up as things in themselves among the objects of the pure understanding, are for all that the sole ones in which our cognition can carry objective reality, precisely where intuition answers to the concepts.

In reflecting on the merely logical plane, we match our concepts against each other within the understanding by itself: whether the two hold the very same content, whether they clash or not, whether something sits inside the concept or comes to it from without, and which of them is the one given while the other is to rank only as a mode of thinking the given one. Yet let me bring these concepts to bear on an object as such (in the transcendental sense), leaving it open whether the object belongs to sensible or to intellectual intuition, and straightaway restrictions come to light {{{ 226 }}} (bidding us not to step outside this concept) that upset every empirical use of the concepts, and by that very fact establish that the representation of an object as a thing as such is not simply ***inadequate*** but, absent any sensible determination of it and set loose from every empirical condition, ***contradictory*** in its own right—so that one has either to leave every object aside (in logic) or, in adopting an object, to think it beneath the conditions of sensible intuition; whence the intelligible would call for a quite peculiar {{ 336 }} intuition, which we lack, and, wanting that, would be, ***for us***, nothing, while, on the other side, the appearances likewise cannot be objects in themselves. For if I do no more than think things as such, then admittedly the difference of outer relations cannot make for a difference in the things themselves but rather takes such a difference for granted; and where the concept of the one is inwardly in no way marked off from that of the other, I am only setting one and the same thing into differing relations. Furthermore, by joining one bare affirmation (reality) to a second, the positive is of course increased and nothing taken from it or cancelled; and so what is real in things as such cannot come into conflict with one another, and so on.

***

The concepts of reflection, as has been made plain, wield—by dint of a certain misreading—so strong a sway over how the understanding is used that they proved able to lure even one of the keenest-sighted among all philosophers into an alleged system of intellectual cognition, a system that sets out to fix its objects with no help coming from the senses. For just that reason, unfolding the deceptive source of these concepts' amphiboly—the source that gives rise to false principles—proves of great service in pinning down and safeguarding, dependably, the bounds of the understanding.

{{ 337 }} True enough, one has to say that whatever belongs to a concept universally, or runs counter to it, belongs likewise, or runs counter, to all the particular items falling under that concept (_dictum de Omni et Nullo_); but it would be preposterous to recast this logical principle so as to read: what a universal concept fails to hold is held, too, by none of the particular concepts ranged beneath it; for {{{ 227 }}} these rank as particular concepts for the very reason that they carry more within them than gets thought in the universal. Now the whole intellectual system of Leibniz is in fact reared upon this second principle; with it, then, that system collapses, and with it too all the double-meaning it breeds in the understanding's use.

The principle of indiscernibles truly rested on the assumption that, where a given distinction is not found in the concept of a thing as such, it is not to be found in the things themselves either; and hence that all things are through and through identical (_numero eadem_) that do not already differ from one another in their concept (in point of quality or quantity). But since, in the bare concept of any thing whatever, one has left aside sundry necessary conditions of an intuition, by an odd rush to judgment that which is left aside gets treated as something met with nowhere {{ 338 }} at all, and the thing is granted nothing beyond what its concept holds.

The concept of a cubic foot of space, think it where and as often as I please, stays in itself wholly the same. Two cubic feet, all the same, are set apart in space by their places alone (_numero diversa_); and these places are conditions of the intuition wherein the object of this concept is furnished, conditions that fall not to the concept but rather to sensibility taken whole. Just so, there is no conflict at all inside the concept of a thing when nothing negative has been coupled with something affirmative, and concepts that are merely affirmative can, once joined, bring about no cancellation of any kind. In sensible intuition, though, where reality (say, motion) is furnished, there turn up conditions (opposing directions) that had been left aside in the concept of motion as such, and these make a conflict possible—one that is admittedly not logical—namely, out of the sheerly positive, a zero = 0; nor could one say that all reality is on that account in accord with one another, merely because no conflict crops up among the concepts of it.[^*] {{{ 228 }}} {{ 339 }} Judged by bare concepts, the inner serves as the substratum for all relational, or outer, determinations. Should I therefore lay aside every condition of intuition and keep solely to the concept of a thing as such, I may leave aside all outer relation, and yet there has to be left over a concept of what marks no relation whatever but only inner determinations. Here it looks as though the upshot were this: that in each thing (substance) there dwells something strictly inner, going before all outer determinations by first making them possible; that this substratum is accordingly a thing harboring no further outer relations within, and hence ***simple*** (bodies being, when all is said, forever nothing but relations, at least of parts lying outside one another); and, since we are acquainted with no strictly inner determinations save those by way of our inner sense, that this substratum is not simple only but, on the analogy of our inner sense, {{ 340 }} determined as well through ***representations***—which is to say, that all things would in truth be ***monads***, or simple beings dowered with representations. The whole of this would even hold good, did there not enter, over and above the concept of a thing as such, something further among the conditions on which alone objects of outer intuition can reach us—conditions the pure concept leaves out of account. For at this point it comes to light that an abiding appearance in space (impenetrable extension) may hold relations and nothing besides, nothing strictly inner whatever, and still may serve as the first substratum of all outer perception. ***Through bare concepts***, that is, concepts of things in themselves, I can of course think nothing outer without something inner, exactly because relational concepts take for granted things given without qualification and are impossible apart from them. But since intuition holds within it something that has no place at all in the bare concept of a thing as such, and since this yields the substratum—which bare concepts would never cognize—namely a space that, with all it embraces, is built of nothing but formal, or else real, relations, I cannot say that, because no thing can be represented through bare concepts without something strictly inner, there is likewise, in the things themselves ranged under these concepts and in ***their*** {{{ 229 }}} ***intuition***, no outer that fails to rest on something strictly inner. For once we have set aside every condition of intuition, {{ 341 }} nothing, to be sure, is left us within the bare concept beyond the inner at large and its relation to itself, whereby alone the outer becomes possible. This necessity, though, resting on abstraction and on nothing else, has no hold over things once they are furnished in intuition bearing determinations that voice mere relations and rest on nothing inner—and this for the reason that they are not things in themselves but appearances and no more. All that we know even of matter is relations and relations only (what we style its inner determinations is inner only by comparison); yet among these are some self-standing and abiding, and by their means a determinate object comes to be furnished us. My having nothing further to think, once these relations are set aside, cancels the concept neither of a thing qua appearance nor of an object _in abstracto_; what it does cancel is every possibility of an object of the kind determinable by bare concepts, that is, of a noumenon. Granted, it gives one pause to be told that a thing should be made up, wholly and utterly, of relations; but a thing of that sort is likewise appearance only, and cannot be thought through pure categories in the least; it consists, itself, in the bare relation borne by something as such toward the senses. In like fashion, setting out from bare concepts, one can scarcely think the relations of things _in abstracto_ in any way {{ 342 }} but that the one is cause of determinations lodged in the other; for that just is the understanding's concept of relations as we hold it. Yet as we are in that case leaving aside all intuition, a whole mode by which the manifold could assign one another their place—the form of sensibility, namely (space)—falls away, though it goes before every empirical causality.

Take merely intelligible objects to be those things thought by pure categories with no schema of sensibility of any sort, and things of that description turn out impossible. For what conditions the objective use of every one of our understanding's concepts is simply the manner of our sensible intuition, whereby objects come to us; strip that manner away, and those concepts bear no relation to any object at all. Indeed, were one to posit some kind of intuition apart from this sensible one of ours, our functions for thinking would {{{ 230 }}} even so carry no weight at all in regard to it. Should we mean by them nothing but objects of a nonsensible intuition—objects our categories admittedly do not govern, and of which, therefore, no cognition can ever be ours (neither intuition nor concept)—then _Noumena_ in this purely negative sense must certainly be let in: for they then declare no more than that our style of intuition does not reach out to all things {{ 343 }} but only to the objects of our senses, that its objective validity is bounded in consequence, and that room accordingly stays open for some other style of intuition, and so for things as its objects too. In that event, however, the concept of a _Noumenon_ is problematic—the representation, that is, of a thing about which we are able to say neither that it can be nor that it cannot be, since no manner of intuition is known to us but our sensible one, and no manner of concepts but the categories, while neither of the two fits a supersensible object. We are not, then, entitled on that ground to widen positively the domain of the objects of our thought past the conditions of our sensibility, and to grant, alongside the appearances, objects of pure thought as well—noumena, that is—for the latter own no positive meaning that could be stated. For of the categories one has to own that by themselves they still fall short of cognizing things in themselves, and that, lacking the _data_ of sensibility, they would be no more than subjective forms of the understanding's unity, an object nowhere among them. Thinking, true, is of itself no product of the senses and to that degree suffers no limit from them, yet it does not thereby become at once of a use its own and pure with no accession of sensibility, for then it wants an object. Neither may the noumenon be styled such an ***object***; for that word points to exactly the problematic {{ 344 }} concept of an object meant for an intuition and an understanding altogether unlike ours, and hence itself a problem. So the concept of the noumenon is not the concept of an object but a task, one tied past escape to the boundedness of our sensibility: whether there might not be objects set entirely loose from that intuition of ours—a question open to none but an indeterminate answer, to wit, that because sensible intuition does not reach all things alike, room stays over for further and other objects, which therefore cannot be denied outright, yet, for want of a determinate concept (no category being {{{ 231 }}} equal to the task), cannot be affirmed as objects for our understanding either.

The understanding, then, draws a bound about sensibility without thereby widening its own province, and, in cautioning sensibility to lay no claim upon things in themselves but only upon appearances, it frames for itself an object in itself—though merely as a transcendental object, cause of appearance (and so no appearance itself), one thinkable neither as magnitude, nor as reality, nor as substance, and the like (these concepts demanding always sensible forms wherein to determine an object); an object, accordingly, of which it stays wholly unknown whether it is to be found in us or outside us as well, whether {{ 345 }} it would be cancelled along with sensibility or would linger on once we took sensibility off. Should we choose to name this object a _Noumenon_ on the ground that our representation of it is not sensible, we are at liberty to. But as not one of our understanding's concepts can be brought to bear on it, this representation stays for all that empty in our hands and does no work beyond marking off the limits of our sensible cognition and leaving vacant a space we are able to fill neither by possible experience nor by the pure understanding.

The critique of this pure understanding, then, gives no leave to fashion for oneself a fresh domain of objects over and above those that may present themselves to it as appearances, nor to wander off into intelligible worlds, not so much as into the concept of them. The blunder that most plausibly tempts one this way—pardonable, though never to be vindicated—consists in this: the understanding's use is, against its calling, rendered transcendental, and the objects, that is, possible intuitions, are bent to fit the concepts, in place of the concepts being bent to fit possible intuitions (whereon their objective validity rests, and on nothing else). The cause of that, in its turn, is that apperception, and thinking along with it, runs ahead of every possible determinate arrangement of the representations. So we think something as such {{ 346 }} and, on one side, determine it sensibly, while yet setting the universal object pictured _in abstracto_ apart from this manner of intuiting it; and there stays behind for us a manner of determining it by thinking alone, which is, granted, a bare logical form void of content, but which strikes us none the less as a manner in which the object is in itself {{{ 232 }}} (_Noumenon_), with no glance at the intuition penned within our senses.

***

Before quitting the transcendental analytic, we ought to append one thing further, which, trifling though it be in itself, may yet look to be called for if the system is to be complete. The topmost concept from which a transcendental philosophy is wont to set out is, as a rule, the split into the possible and the impossible. But because each division takes for granted a concept being divided, one higher still has to be named, and this is the concept of an object as such (taken problematically, and left unsettled whether it be something or nothing). Since the categories are the sole concepts bearing on objects as such, the sorting of an object—whether it be something or nothing—will run along the order and lead of the categories.
+ 1) {{ 347 }} Over against the concepts of all, of many, and of one stands the concept that cancels everything, that is, ***none***; and thus the object of a concept to which no specifiable intuition answers is nothing—a concept, that is, lacking an object, as the noumena are, which may not be reckoned among the possibilities, though neither on that account need they be given out as impossible (_ens rationis_), or as certain new fundamental forces are that one entertains in thought, free of contradiction, to be sure, but likewise free of any instance drawn from experience, and so not to be reckoned among the possibilities.
+ 2) Reality is ***something***, negation is ***nothing***—a concept, namely, of an object's absence, as with the shadow, with the cold (_nihil privativum_).
+ 3) The bare form of intuition, apart from substance, is of itself no object but only the formal condition of one (as appearance)—as pure space and pure time are, which, though they are something as forms for intuiting, are not themselves the objects intuited (_ens imaginarium_).
+ 4) The object of a self-contradicting concept {{ 348 }} is nothing, since the concept itself is nothing—the impossible, as, for instance, the two-sided rectilinear figure (_nihil negativum_).

One notices that the thought-thing (no. 1) parts from the non-thing (no. 4) in this: the first is not to be reckoned among the possibilities, being a mere invention (albeit not a self-contradicting one), whereas the second stands against possibility, in that its concept even cancels {{ 349 }} itself. The two of them, though, are concepts standing empty. By contrast, empty data for concepts are furnished by the _nihil privativum_ (no. 2) and the _ens imaginarium_ (no. 3). Had light never been given to the senses, no darkness could be pictured either; and had no extended beings been perceived, no space could be pictured. Both negation and the bare form of intuition, lacking anything real, are no objects at all.

[^*]: Were one minded here to fall back on the customary way out—that at least _realitates noumena_ cannot work against one another—one would still owe an example of such pure, sense-free reality, so that it might be understood whether a thing of the kind presents anything at all or nothing whatever. But an example can be drawn from no source but experience, which never holds out more than _Phaenomena_; and so this proposition amounts to nothing beyond saying that a concept made up of affirmations alone holds nothing negative—a proposition we have never once doubted.
