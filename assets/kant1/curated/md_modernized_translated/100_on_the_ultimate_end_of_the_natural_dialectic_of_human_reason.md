## {{ 697 }} On the ultimate end of the natural dialectic of human reason

In themselves the ideas of pure reason can never be dialectical; a deceptive semblance arises in us from them solely through their misapplication, for it is the nature of our reason that imposes them on us, and that highest court over every entitlement and claim belonging to our speculation could not conceivably harbor deceptions and illusions of its own. In all likelihood, then, within the natural predisposition of our reason they serve some good and purposive function. The mob of would-be reasoners, however, raises its customary outcry over incoherence and contradiction, and vilifies the regime whose deepest designs it cannot fathom, though to that regime's benevolent workings it should owe its own survival, and even the cultivation that first enables it to find fault and pass sentence.

No concept can be used _a priori_ with any assurance until its transcendental deduction has been carried through. It is true that the ideas of pure reason admit no deduction of the sort available for the categories; but should they possess even the slightest objective validity—however indeterminate—and not amount merely to {{ 698 }} vacuous entities of thought (_entia rationis ratiocinantis_), a deduction of them must at all events be feasible, however far it may depart from what can be undertaken for the categories. Herein lies the culmination of pure reason's critical task, and this is what we now mean to take in hand.

Whether something is presented to my reason as an ***object pure and simple*** or merely as an ***object in the idea*** makes for a considerable difference. In the first case my concepts are directed at determining the {{{ 443 }}} object; in the second there is really nothing but a schema, to which no object is conceded directly—not even on a hypothesis—and which functions only to let us represent other objects indirectly, through their relation to this idea under the aspect of their systematic unity. So I maintain that the concept of a supreme intelligence amounts to a mere idea, i.e., its objective reality is not supposed to lie in its being referred straightforwardly to an object (on that reading we could offer no justification of its objective validity); it is instead simply a schema—arranged under conditions of the utmost unity of reason—for the concept of a thing as such, whose sole function is to preserve, throughout our reason's empirical employment, the utmost systematic unity, in that the object of experience is derived, so to speak, from the fictive object of this idea taken as its basis or cause. The claim then runs, for instance: the things of the world are to be viewed ***as if*** their existence derived from a supreme intelligence {{ 699 }}. On this footing the idea is really no more than a heuristic, rather than an ostensive, concept: it points out, not what constitution an object has, but rather how, guided by it, we ***ought to seek*** the constitution and interconnection of objects of experience generally. Suppose, now, it can be established that although the three kinds of transcendental idea (the ***psychological***, the ***cosmological***, and the ***theological***) bear on no object directly answering to them, nor on any such object's ***determination***, still, once such an ***object in the idea*** is presupposed, every rule for reason's empirical exercise yields systematic unity and invariably enlarges cognition drawn from experience, never running afoul of it: then to work by ideas of this sort is a ***maxim*** of reason that cannot be dispensed with. And this constitutes the transcendental deduction of all the ideas belonging to speculative reason—not as ***constitutive*** principles by which our cognition might be extended to a greater range of objects than experience affords, but as ***regulative*** ones governing the systematic unity of the manifold that empirical cognition presents, a manifold thereby better cultivated and rectified within its own limits than the bare use of the understanding's principles, unaided by such ideas, could achieve.

{{{ 444 }}} {{ 700 }} Let me put this more plainly. Taking the ideas just mentioned as principles, we shall, ***first*** (in psychology), tie together all the appearances, activities, and receptivity of our mind along the thread of inner experience ***as if*** it were one simple substance persisting (during life, at any rate) with personal identity, while the states belonging to it—among which those of the body count merely as external conditions—undergo continuous change. We are bound, ***second*** (in cosmology), to trace the conditions of natural appearances, inner no less than outer, in an inquiry that can nowhere reach an end, ***as if*** that inquiry led into an infinite regress lacking any first or highest member—though this does not make us deny, beyond all appearances, their purely intelligible first grounds, which nonetheless we may never introduce into the fabric of natural explanation, since they lie wholly outside our acquaintance. Lastly and ***third***, we must (as regards theology) regard whatever might ever enter the fabric of possible experience ***as if*** experience made up a unity that is absolute, yet dependent through and through and still conditioned within the sensible world—while regarding it at the same time ***as if*** the totality of appearances (the sensible world itself) rested on one sole, supreme, and wholly sufficient ground beyond its own extent, namely a reason that is, so to speak, self-standing, primordial, and creative, in {{ 701 }} regard to which we so orient every empirical use of our reason, at its widest reach, ***as if*** the objects had one and all issued from that prototype of all reason. Which is to say: not to derive the soul's inner appearances from a simple thinking substance, but rather to derive them from one another under the guidance of the idea of a simple being; and not to trace the world's order and its systematic unity back to a supreme intelligence, but rather to borrow from the idea of a supremely wise cause the rule by which reason, in linking causes with effects across the world, may be turned to best account for its own contentment.

As things stand, nothing whatever stops us from ***taking up*** these ideas as objective and hypostatized as well—nothing save the cosmological idea alone, in whose case reason collides with an antinomy the moment it seeks to carry this through (the psychological and the theological involve no such thing). For they contain no contradiction; how, then, might anyone contest their objective reality against us, when he knows just as little of their possibility {{{ 445 }}}—too little to deny it—as we know in order to affirm it! Still, to assume something it is not yet enough that nothing positively stands in the way; nor can we be allowed to bring in figments of thought—which outrun all our concepts, though they clash with none—as real and determinate objects, merely on the credit of a speculative {{ 702 }} reason keen to see its work done. Hence they are not to be assumed in their own right; only their reality as a schema of that regulative principle—the systematic unity of all cognition of nature—should be granted standing, so that they serve as bases merely by way of analogues of real things, and never as those things taken in themselves. What we remove from the object of the idea are the conditions restricting our concept of the understanding—the very conditions, however, that alone put us in a position to possess any determinate concept of a thing whatever. And now we frame the thought of a certain something whose intrinsic nature is entirely beyond our concept, yet to which we do attribute a relation to the whole assemblage of appearances—one analogous to the relation the appearances bear to one another.

If, therefore, we posit ideal beings of this kind, what we actually broaden is not our cognition past the objects that possible experience allows, but only the empirical unity of those objects, and this by means of the systematic unity of which the idea supplies the schema—so that the idea counts not as a constitutive principle but strictly as a regulative one. For in setting down a thing that answers to the idea—a something, or a real being—we are by no means saying that our aim is to widen our cognition of things through transcendent concepts; this being gets posited within the idea alone and not in its own right, and thus solely to give expression to the systematic unity that is meant to guide us in reason's {{ 703 }} empirical employment, while leaving wholly open what the ground of that unity might be, or what inner character belongs to such a being upon which, as cause, it depends.

So the transcendental concept of God—the one determinate concept that merely speculative reason yields—is, taken in the strictest sense, ***deistic***: that is, reason does not so much as supply that concept's objective validity, but hands over only the idea of a something in which every empirical reality finds its supreme and necessary unity grounded, a something we can conceive in no way other than by analogy to a real substance that, under laws of reason, would be the cause of all things {{{ 446 }}}—assuming, that is, that we go so far as to conceive it at all as a distinct object, and do not instead, resting satisfied with the bare idea of reason's regulative principle, lay aside, as extravagant for the human understanding, the bringing-to-completion of all the conditions of thinking; a course that, however, cannot stand together with the aspiration to a thoroughgoing systematic unity in our cognition—an aspiration on which reason at least imposes no bounds.

And so it comes to pass that, in assuming a divine being, I possess not the slightest concept either of the inner possibility of its supreme perfection or of the necessity of its existence, {{ 704 }} yet am thereafter able to meet every other question bearing on what is contingent, and to afford reason the fullest contentment regarding the greatest unity it pursues in its empirical employment—though none regarding that presupposition itself; which goes to show that it is reason's speculative interest, not any insight of its own, that licenses it to depart from a point situated so far above its province, so as from there to take in its objects as one complete whole.

At this juncture a divergence in ways of thinking emerges under one and the same presupposition—a divergence rather fine-grained, and yet, for transcendental philosophy, of considerable weight. I may well have adequate grounds for positing a thing in relative fashion (_suppositio relativa_) and yet lack any warrant to posit it without qualification (_suppositio absoluta_). This distinction holds wherever the matter concerns nothing but a regulative principle whose necessity we do recognize in itself, though not the wellspring of that necessity, and for the sake of which we take on a supreme ground with the sole intention of conceiving the principle's universality all the more determinately—as, say, when I conceive a being to exist that answers to an idea that is merely, and indeed transcendentally, an idea. For in that instance the thing's existence in its own right can never be something I assume, given that {{ 705 }} no concepts by which I might determinately conceive any object ever reach so far, and given that the very idea rules out the conditions under which my concepts would have objective validity. Apart from the employment in which they render possible the empirical cognition of an object, the concepts of reality, of substance, of causality, and even that of necessity as regards existence, carry no meaning whatever by which any object might be determined. They will therefore serve to explain the possibility {{{ 447 }}} of things within the sensible world, yet never how a world-whole in its own right could be possible, seeing that the ground of such an explanation would necessarily lie beyond the world, and hence be no object of any possible experience. Even so, a being of this incomprehensible sort—the object of a mere idea—I can indeed posit relatively to the sensible world, albeit not in its own right. For suppose an idea (that of a systematically complete unity, of which I shall shortly speak with more precision) underlies the widest empirical employment my reason can have—an idea never as such to be exhibited adequately in experience, and yet strictly indispensable if empirical unity is to be brought as near as may be to the highest degree: then I shall find myself not merely licensed but positively required to make this idea real, i.e., to set a genuine object under it, though only as a something in general, one I have no acquaintance with in itself, and to which, purely as the ground of that systematic unity, I ascribe, in {{ 706 }} relation to the latter, such characteristics as run parallel to the understanding's concepts in their empirical employment. Accordingly, on the model of the realities found in the world—of substances, of causality, of necessity—I shall think a being in which all of this is possessed at the peak of perfection; and because this idea rests on my reason alone, I shall be able to think that being as a self-standing reason which, by way of ideas of supreme harmony and unity, is the cause of the world-whole, so that I drop every condition that would bound the idea, with the single purpose of making possible—under the safeguard of such a primordial ground—a systematic unity of the manifold within the world-whole, and, through that unity, the widest empirical use of reason attainable, in that I look upon every connection as if it were the ordinance of a supreme reason of which our own is but a feeble replica. This highest being I then think through nothing but concepts whose proper application lies only within the sensible world; but seeing that I put that transcendental presupposition to no use except a relative one—namely, that it should furnish the substratum for the greatest unity of experience attainable—I am fully entitled to think a being distinguished by me from the world by means of characteristics that pertain to the sensible world and to it alone. For it is in no way my demand—nor have I any right to demand it—that I should cognize this object of my idea as it may be in its own nature; I have no concepts fit for that, and even the {{ 707 }} concepts of reality, substance, and causality—indeed of necessity {{{ 448 }}} in existing—forfeit all meaning and become hollow labels for concepts wholly devoid of content, once I hazard beyond the field of the senses with them. All I think is the relation that a being, in itself wholly unknown to me, bears to the greatest systematic unity of the world-whole, and this only so as to make it the schema of the regulative principle governing my reason's widest possible empirical employment.

Turning our gaze now upon the transcendental object of our idea, we recognize that its actuality is not something we can presuppose in itself by the concepts of reality, substance, causality, and the like, for such concepts find not the least foothold in anything set entirely apart from the sensible world. Reason's supposition of a supreme being as the highest cause is thus purely relative, framed on behalf of the sensible world's systematic unity, and is nothing more than a something in the idea, of whose intrinsic nature we hold no concept. This likewise makes clear why, in reference to what is presented to the senses as existent, we do require the idea of a primordial being necessary in itself, and yet can at no point form the slightest concept of that being and of its absolute necessity.

We are now in a position to display plainly the upshot of the whole transcendental {{ 708 }} dialectic, and to fix with precision the ultimate end of those ideas of pure reason that turn dialectical solely through misconstrual and want of caution. Pure reason is in truth busied with nothing beyond itself, nor can it have any other occupation, for what gets handed to it is not the objects that would go to the unity of the concept of experience, but rather the understanding's cognitions, which are to make up the unity of the concept of reason—that is, their coherence within a single principle. Reason's unity is the unity belonging to a system, and such systematic unity does not furnish reason with an objective principle for spreading it across the objects, but serves subjectively, as a maxim, for spreading it across every empirical cognition of objects that is possible. All the same, the systematic linkage that reason is able to impart to the understanding's empirical use not only furthers that use's expansion but at once vouches for its correctness as well; and the principle underlying such a systematic unity is likewise objective, though after an indeterminate manner (_principium vagum_): it operates not as a constitutive principle settling anything about its direct object, but as a merely regulative principle and maxim, one that carries reason's empirical use {{{ 449 }}} onward toward the infinite (the indeterminate) by throwing open fresh routes unknown to the understanding, and makes it secure, never in the least running counter to the laws that govern that empirical use.

{{ 709 }} Reason, however, has no way of conceiving this systematic unity except by furnishing its idea, at the same time, with an object—one that no experience is capable of furnishing; for at no time does experience offer up an instance of complete systematic unity. Granted, this being of reason (_ens rationis ratiocinatae_) is a mere idea, and hence is not posited outright and in its own right as anything actual, but is set down only problematically (since it eludes every concept of the understanding), the aim being to view all interconnection among things in the sensible world as if such things found their ground in this being of reason—yet solely with the design of erecting upon it the systematic unity that reason cannot do without, a unity in every respect conducive to the understanding's empirical cognition and at no point an obstacle to it.

The sense of this idea is misjudged at once if one treats it as the affirmation—or so much as the assumption—of some real thing to which one would think to credit the ground of the world's systematic makeup; on the contrary, one leaves it altogether open what character that ground, which slips away from our concepts, may possess in itself, and one merely erects an idea as the vantage point from which alone that unity, so indispensable to reason and so wholesome for the {{ 710 }} understanding, admits of being spread abroad; in short, this transcendental thing is nothing but the schema of the regulative principle in question, whereby reason, to the extent it can, diffuses systematic unity across all experience.

The primary object of an idea of this kind is I myself, taken purely as a thinking nature (soul). Should I set about searching out the properties by which a thinking being exists in itself, experience is what I must interrogate, and of all the categories not one can I bring to bear on this object save so far as its schema stands given within sensible intuition. By this route, however, I never attain any systematic unity across all the appearances of inner sense. In lieu of the concept drawn from experience, then (the concept of what the soul really is), which can take us no great distance, reason lays hold of the concept of the empirical unity of all thought, and, by thinking that unity as unconditioned and {{{ 450 }}} primordial, fashions out of it a concept of reason (an idea)—the idea of a simple substance that, in itself unalterable (personally identical), stands in community with other real things beyond it; in short, the idea of a simple, self-standing intelligence. In this it keeps in view nothing else than principles of systematic unity for explaining the soul's appearances—namely, to regard every determination as inhering in one single subject, every power, so far as can be managed, as issuing from a single basic power, all variation as pertaining to the states {{ 711 }} of one selfsame abiding being, and to represent every appearance in space as thoroughly distinct from the acts of thinking. That simplicity of the substance, and the rest, was meant to be no more than the schema for this regulative principle, and is not laid down on the supposition that it forms the real ground of the soul's properties. For those properties may equally repose on grounds of a quite different sort, entirely unknown to us; and indeed, even were we to let these assumed predicates count of the soul without qualification, they would not really yield cognition of the soul as it is in itself, seeing that they amount to a mere idea incapable of any presentation _in concreto_. From a psychological idea so understood nothing but benefit can follow, provided only one is on guard against letting it pass for anything beyond a mere idea—that is, as holding merely in relation to the systematic use of reason concerning our soul's appearances. For on these terms no empirical laws of bodily appearances, which belong to an altogether different order, get mixed into the explanation of what pertains to inner sense alone; no airy hypotheses about the generation, the perishing, and the palingenesis of souls, and so forth, are let in; the examination of this object of inner sense is thus conducted quite purely, unadulterated by disparate properties, and beyond this the inquiry of reason is aimed at carrying the grounds of explanation within this subject, so far as may be, {{ 712 }} up to one sole principle—all of which is accomplished best, indeed accomplished only, by a schema of this sort, as if it were a real being. Nor can the psychological idea stand for anything but the schema of a regulative concept. For were I even to pose the question whether the soul might not, in itself, be of spiritual nature, the question would carry no sense at all. For by a concept of that kind I sweep away {{{ 451 }}} not corporeal nature merely but nature in its entirety—that is, every predicate of any possible experience, and with it every condition under which an object could be thought for such a concept; and it is precisely this that alone gives ground for saying a concept has sense.

Reason's second regulative idea, in its merely speculative capacity, is the concept of the world taken as a whole. For nature is, strictly speaking, the one given object with regard to which reason stands in need of regulative principles. This nature comes in two forms—the thinking and the corporeal. But for the corporeal, in order to conceive it as to its inner possibility—which is to say, to settle how the categories apply to it—no idea is called for, no representation that would outrun experience; nor, indeed, is any such idea possible here, since within this domain we are led by sensible intuition alone, and not, as with the basic psychological concept (the I), by something that harbors a definite form of thinking, namely its unity _a priori_. Thus for pure reason there is left to us {{ 713 }} nothing beyond nature at large and the completeness, on some principle or other, of the conditions within it. That the series of these conditions should form an absolute totality, when their members are traced out in derivation, is an idea that can, to be sure, never fully materialize within reason's empirical use, though it does furnish a rule for how we are to proceed in that regard: namely, in accounting for given appearances (whether descending or ascending) ***as if*** the series ran on infinitely in itself, i.e., _in indefinitum_; but wherever reason itself falls to be regarded as a determining cause (in freedom), and hence with practical principles, we proceed as though what lay before us were an object not of the senses but of the pure understanding, where the conditions admit of being placed no longer within the series of appearances but beyond it, and where the series of states may be looked upon ***as if*** it had commenced without qualification (through an intelligible cause); the whole of which goes to prove that the cosmological ideas are but regulative principles, far indeed from constitutively positing, as it were, any real totality of such series. Everything further may be sought out where it belongs, under the Antinomy of Pure Reason.

Reason's third idea, which harbors a merely relative supposition of a being counted as the single, all-sufficing cause behind every cosmological series, is the concept of ***God***. The {{{ 452 }}} {{ 714 }} object of this idea we have not the slightest ground for assuming outright (***for supposing it in itself***); for what could ever move us, or so much as authorize us, out of a being's bare concept to credit or affirm it in itself as one of supreme perfection and, by its very nature, unconditionally necessary—were it not the world, in whose regard alone such a supposition can be needed; and here it comes plainly to light that the idea of that being, like every speculative idea, purports to say no more than that reason enjoins us to view all the world's interconnection by principles of a systematic unity, hence ***as if*** the whole had sprung, one and all, from a single all-embracing being as its supreme and all-sufficing cause. From this it stands out clearly that here reason can be after nothing but its own formal rule in enlarging its empirical use, and never after an ***extension beyond all boundaries of the empirical use***, so that no constitutive principle for its use as directed upon possible experience lies concealed beneath this idea.

The supreme formal unity, resting on concepts of reason alone, is the ***purposive*** unity of things, and reason's ***speculative*** interest makes it requisite to look upon all arrangement in the world as though it had grown out of the design of a reason that is highest of all {{ 715 }}. For a principle of this sort throws open to our reason, once it is brought to bear on the field of experience, wholly new prospects of knitting the world's things together by teleological laws and thereby arriving at their greatest systematic unity. To presuppose a highest intelligence as the one and only cause of the world-whole—though, admittedly, only within the idea—can accordingly at all times profit reason and yet at no time do it harm. For if, concerning the earth's figure (round, yet a little flattened)[^*], concerning its mountains and seas, and so on, we posit in advance nothing but the wise designs of an author, then we can {{{ 453 }}}, along this very path, make a great many discoveries. So long as we hold to this presupposition as a merely ***regulative*** principle, not even error can do us harm. For the most that could ever follow from it is this: that in a place where we had counted on a teleological linkage (_nexus finalis_), one merely mechanical or physical (_nexus effectivus_) turns up, whereby in such a case {{ 716 }} we do no more than forgo one further unity, without wrecking the unity of reason in its empirical use. Yet even such a countercheck is powerless to touch the law itself as regards its general, teleological bearing overall. For though an anatomist may be shown to have erred when he assigns some limb of an animal body to an end that one can distinctly demonstrate does not issue from it, it remains utterly impossible to establish, in any given case, that an ordering of nature, whatever it might be, serves no end whatsoever. This is why physiology as well (that of the physicians) stretches its highly restricted empirical acquaintance with the ends served by the limb-structure of an organic body, by means of a principle that pure reason alone prompted, so far that within it one presumes—quite boldly, and with the assent of every judicious mind—that everything in the animal has its use and its good design; a presupposition which, were it to be constitutive, reaches out much farther than observation up to now could warrant, from which it may in turn be gathered that it is but a regulative principle of reason, one meant to bring us to the highest systematic unity through the idea of a purposive causality lodged in the supreme world-cause, and as though that cause, being a highest intelligence, were the source of everything by the wisest of designs.

{{ 717 }} Should we, however, abandon this confinement of the idea to a purely regulative use, reason is misled in ever so many ways, for it then forsakes the soil of experience—which must, after all, hold the markers of its course—and hazards itself above that soil toward the incomprehensible and the unsearchable, at whose altitude it inevitably grows giddy, since from that vantage it finds itself wholly severed from every use that squares with experience.

The first blunder springing from a use of the idea of a {{{ 454 }}} highest being that is not merely regulative but (in defiance of an idea's very nature) constitutive is ***lazy reason*** (_ignava ratio_)[^**]. That name may be given to any principle which leads one to {{ 718 }} treat one's inquiry into nature, wherever it may stand, as flatly finished, so that reason lays itself down to rest, as though it had discharged its task in full. Hence even the psychological idea, once it is pressed into service as a constitutive principle for explaining our soul's appearances, and then indeed for stretching what we cognize of this subject clear past all experience (its condition after death), does make things exceedingly convenient for reason, but it likewise corrupts and undermines outright all use of reason in nature under experience's guidance. Thus the dogmatic spiritualist accounts for the person's unity, abiding unchanged through every shift of state, out of the unity of the thinking substance that he fancies he perceives immediately in the I; the concern we bring to matters that are to befall us only once we are dead, out of an awareness of the immaterial character of our thinking subject, and so forth; and he spares himself all inquiry, from physical grounds of explanation, into the cause of these inner appearances of ours, in that he brushes past the immanent sources of cognition supplied by experience—by what amounts to the decree of a transcendent reason—for his own ease, but at the forfeit of every insight. This detrimental upshot leaps still more sharply into view with the dogmatism attaching to our idea of a supreme intelligence, and to the theological system of nature (physico-theology) that is thereupon {{ 719 }} mistakenly built. For at that point every purpose displaying itself in nature—often enough purposes we ourselves have foisted upon it—serves to make the search for causes thoroughly convenient for us: in place of seeking them within the universal laws of matter's mechanism, one appeals straightaway to the unsearchable counsel of a highest wisdom and reckons the labor of reason concluded just when one dispenses with reason's use—a use that nonetheless finds its guiding thread nowhere except where {{{ 455 }}} the arrangement of nature and the succession of alterations, following their inner and universal laws, place it in our hands. This blunder is escapable if we decline to view merely a handful of nature's parts—say, the spread of the solid land, the way it is built, or the makeup and placement of the mountains, or perhaps only the organization within the plant and animal realms—from the standpoint of purposes, and instead render this systematic unity of nature, in its bearing on the idea of a highest intelligence, ***entirely universal***. For then what we set as our basis is a purposiveness conforming to nature's universal laws, from which no single arrangement is exempted, each being merely picked out for us as more or less discernible, and we possess a regulative principle for the systematic unity of a teleological linkage—one that, however, we do not fix beforehand, but are entitled only to track, in anticipation of it, along the physico-mechanical linkage that follows universal {{ 720 }} laws. For it is only thus that the principle of purposive unity can perpetually widen reason's use with respect to experience, without curtailing it in any instance.

The second blunder, one issuing from a misreading of the principle of systematic unity just discussed, is precisely the case of ***perverted reason*** (_perversa ratio_, _hysteron proteron_ rationis). The idea of systematic unity ought to have served no other function than, as a regulative principle, to make us seek that unity in the connection of things under nature's universal laws, and, to whatever extent something of it can be come upon by the empirical route, to make us believe accordingly that we have drawn nearer the completeness of its use, though reach it we assuredly never shall. In the reverse of this, one inverts the matter and starts out by laying down as its basis, hypostatically, the actuality of a principle of purposive unity; one settles the concept of such a highest intelligence anthropomorphically—since in itself it is utterly unsearchable—and thereupon presses purposes on nature by force and by fiat, rather than seeking them, as would be proper, along the road of physical investigation: with the result that teleology, whose office was merely to round out nature's unity under universal laws, now works instead to remove it, while reason {{ 721 }} on top of this robs itself of its very purpose, namely ***to prove***, on this footing, from nature, that such an intelligent supreme cause exists. For if the supreme purposiveness in nature cannot be presupposed _a priori_—as belonging, that is, to nature's very essence—by what means is one {{{ 456 }}} to be set on the way to seek it out and, climbing its scale of degrees, to draw near the topmost perfection of an author as a perfection unconditionally necessary and therefore knowable _a priori_? What the regulative principle requires is that systematic unity, in the guise of ***unity of nature***—a unity cognized not by experience merely but presupposed _a priori_, indeterminately though it be—be presupposed without qualification, and so as flowing from the essence of things. But if I first place a highest ordering being at the base, the unity of nature is thereby, in effect, removed. For that unity then stands wholly alien and adventitious to the nature of things, and cannot be cognized from their universal laws either. And so there springs up a vicious circle in the proof, inasmuch as one takes as given the very thing that was supposed to be proved.

To mistake the regulative principle of nature's systematic unity for a constitutive one, and to set up hypostatically, as a cause, that which is placed at the base solely in the idea for reason's concordant use, {{ 722 }} is nothing else than to throw reason into confusion. Investigation of nature holds to its course entirely on its own, following the chain of natural causes by their universal laws—guided, to be sure, by the idea of an author, yet not so as to derive from him the purposiveness it everywhere pursues, but so as to cognize his existence out of that purposiveness, sought as it is within the essences of natural things, and, where feasible, within the essences of all things whatever, and thus as absolutely necessary. Whether that last should succeed or not, the idea stays correct throughout, and its use no less so, provided the use has been kept within the bounds of a merely regulative principle.

Purposive unity in its completeness is perfection (regarded without qualification). Supposing we fail to locate this within the essence of those things that constitute the whole object of experience—the whole, that is, of all cognition of ours that holds objectively—and so within universal and necessary laws of nature, how are we to draw from that, of all things, the idea of a supreme and unconditionally necessary perfection belonging to an original being that is the fountainhead of all causality? The greatest systematic unity, and with it the purposive unity as well, is the training-ground—indeed the very ground of possibility—for the fullest exercise of human reason. Its idea, then, {{ 723 }} is knit indissolubly to the very essence of our reason. This same idea is therefore lawgiving ***for us***, and it is only natural to posit a lawgiving reason answering to it (_intellectus archetypus_), {{{ 457 }}} the source from which every systematic unity of nature, as our reason's object, must be derived.

In connection with the Antinomy of Pure Reason we remarked that every question pure reason raises must admit of an unqualified answer, and that the plea of our cognition's boundaries—a plea that in many a question about nature is no less inevitable than warranted—can find no admission here, since the questions set before us here proceed not from the nature of things but purely from the nature of reason, and bear on nothing beyond its own inner constitution. We are now able to make good this claim—bold though it appears at first sight—as regards the two questions on which pure reason's interest is greatest, and thereby to carry our reflection on its dialectic through to complete conclusion.

So if one asks (with an eye to a transcendental theology)[^***], ***first***, whether there exists anything set apart from the world that holds the {{ 724 }} ground of the world's order and of its interconnection under universal laws, the reply runs: ***beyond doubt***. For the world amounts to a sum of appearances, and so some ground of it must exist that is transcendental—thinkable, that is, for the pure understanding alone. If, ***second***, the question runs whether this being is a substance, possessed of the greatest reality, necessary, and so on: then my answer is ***that the question is wholly without significance***. For all the categories by which I attempt to frame for myself a concept of an object of that sort are of no use but the empirical, and are utterly without sense unless brought to bear on objects of possible experience—on the sensible world, that is. Away from this field they are but labels for concepts, which one might concede, yet by which nothing whatever can be understood. If, ***third*** and last, the question runs whether we may not at any rate conceive this being, set apart from the world, on an ***analogy*** with objects of experience: then the reply is ***yes, indeed***, but only as an {{ 725 }} object within the idea, not within reality—that is, only so far as it {{{ 458 }}} constitutes a substratum, unknown to us, of the systematic unity, the order, and the purposiveness of the world's constitution, a substratum reason is bound to adopt as the regulative principle of its investigation of nature. More than that, within this idea we may grant—unabashed and beyond reproach—certain anthropomorphisms conducive to the regulative principle just spoken of. For at bottom it is never more than an idea, one referred not straight to a being set apart from the world at all, but to the regulative principle of the world's systematic unity—and even this only by way of a schema of that principle, namely a supreme intelligence that, on wise designs, is the author of the said unity. What this primordial ground of the world's unity might be in itself was never to be thought through the idea; only how we are to employ it—or rather its idea—relatively to reason's systematic use regarding the world's things.

In this manner, though (the questioning will press on), ***can*** we, after all is said, take for granted a single, wise, and omnipotent author of the world? ***Without a shadow of doubt***; and not this alone—we ***must*** lay down such a one as a presupposition. But in that manner, surely, we broaden our cognition past the field of possible experience? ***By no means***. For what we have laid down {{ 726 }} is merely a something whose intrinsic nature is wholly beyond our concept (a purely transcendental object); but with a view to the systematic, purposive order of the world's fabric—an order we are obliged to presuppose whenever we make a study of nature—we have conceived that being, unknown to us, solely ***by analogy*** with an intelligence (an empirical concept); which is to say, in respect of the ends and the perfection resting upon it, we have equipped it with just those properties that, under the conditions of our reason, are able to contain the ground of a systematic unity of that kind. This idea, accordingly, rests wholly on a footing ***relative to the world-directed use*** of our reason. Were we, however, to confer objective validity on it outright, we should lose sight of the fact that what we are thinking is nothing but a being in the idea; and by setting out then from a ground not determinable in the slightest through any contemplation of the world, we should thereby be rendered incapable of applying this principle in a manner suited to reason's empirical use.

But (the further question will come) can I, in this fashion, put to use the concept and the presupposition of a highest being when I contemplate the world rationally? Yes—that, in truth, is the very end for which {{{ 459 }}} reason laid this idea at the base. Yet am I now permitted {{ 727 }} to regard purpose-like arrangements as intentions, deriving them, though by way of special predispositions set up for this within the world, from the divine will? Yes, that too you may do, but in such a manner that it must come to the same for you whether someone declares that divine wisdom has disposed all things thus toward its highest ends, or that the idea of highest wisdom is a regulative for probing nature and a principle of nature's systematic, purposive unity under universal natural laws—even where those ends escape our notice; that is, wherever you do notice it, it must be a matter of complete indifference to you to say: God willed it so in his wisdom, or nature ordered it so with equal wisdom. For that greatest systematic, purposive unity which your reason called for as the regulative principle underlying all study of nature was the very thing that gave you the right to lay the idea of a highest intelligence at the base as a schema of that regulative principle; and however much purposiveness in the world you then meet with in keeping with it, so much confirmation do you have of your idea's rightfulness; but since the principle in question aimed at nothing else than to seek out a necessary and largest attainable unity of nature, we shall indeed, to the extent that we reach it, owe that unity to the idea of a highest being, and yet we cannot—without landing in contradiction {{ 728 }} with our own selves—pass over the universal laws of nature, with a view to which alone the idea was laid down at the base, so as to regard nature's purposiveness as contingent and, in its origin, hyperphysical, seeing that we were never entitled to assume a being over nature endowed with the properties in question, but only to place its idea at the base, in order, on the analogy of a causal determination, to regard the appearances as bound together systematically among themselves.

For this same reason we are likewise entitled to conceive the world-cause, within the idea, not merely on a more refined anthropomorphism (apart from which nothing at all of it could be conceived)—namely as a being endowed with understanding, with liking and disliking, and, to match, with a corresponding appetite and will, and so on—but rather to invest it with an infinite perfection, one that thereby reaches far past whatever the empirical acquaintance with the world's order could entitle us to. For what the regulative law of systematic unity demands is that we should study nature as though a systematic, purposive {{{ 460 }}} unity, joined to the greatest possible manifoldness, were to be met with everywhere, on and on without end. For although we shall spy out, or lay hold of, but little of this perfection of the world, it belongs nonetheless to our reason's legislation to hunt for it, and surmise it, on every side; and to carry on the study of nature by {{ 729 }} this principle must always work to our advantage and can at no time turn to our detriment. Yet under this representation of the idea of a highest author taken as basis, it is likewise clear that what I lay at the base is neither the existence nor the knowledge of such a being, but only the idea of it, and hence that I derive, in strictness, nothing from this being, but only from its idea—which is to say, from the nature of the world's things, as construed on an idea of this sort. There seems, moreover, to be a certain consciousness—undeveloped though it be—of the authentic use of this concept of reason of ours that has prompted the modest and equitable idiom of philosophers in every age, when they speak of nature's wisdom and forethought and of divine wisdom as interchangeable expressions, and indeed give the former expression preference, so long as it is merely speculative reason that is in play, because it reins in the presumption of a claim greater than any we have warrant to make, and at once directs reason homeward to the field peculiarly its own, nature.

So it is that pure reason—which at the outset appeared to hold out to us nothing short of an extension of knowledge past every boundary of experience—contains, once we grasp it aright, nothing but regulative principles that do, to be sure, prescribe a unity greater than the understanding's empirical use can reach, and yet, precisely by displacing so far outward the goal of that use's approximation {{ 730 }}, raise its accord with itself, through systematic unity, to the highest pitch—whereas, should one misconstrue them and take them for constitutive principles of transcendent cognitions, they beget, by an illusion at once dazzling and deceptive, persuasion and a knowledge merely imagined, and with these unending contradictions and quarrels.

***

Thus every cognition of the human sort takes its start from intuitions, moves on from these to concepts, and terminates in ideas. Granted that, in respect of all three of these elements, it commands sources of cognition _a priori_ which at a first look seem to hold in contempt the bounds of all experience, still a {{{ 461 }}} critique carried to completion carries the conviction that no reason, in speculative use, can with these elements ever pass out beyond the field of possible experience, and that the true vocation of this supreme faculty of cognition consists in availing itself of all its methods and their principles for one purpose only—to trace nature into its innermost depths along every possible principle of unity, of which the principle of ends is the most eminent—but never to soar past its limit, outside of which, ***for us***, there is nothing save vacant space. The critical scrutiny, in the Transcendental Analytic, of every {{ 731 }} proposition capable of stretching our cognition past actual experience has, it is true, satisfied us sufficiently that such propositions can conduct us to nothing beyond a possible experience; and had we not been mistrustful even toward the plainest of abstract and universal doctrines, had no alluring and plausible vistas tempted us to fling off the discipline of the former, we might well have been excused the wearisome cross-examination of all those dialectical witnesses whom a transcendent reason parades in furtherance of its pretensions; for we knew beforehand already, with entire certainty, that all its professions, honestly meant though they might perhaps be, could not but be flatly void, since they touched upon an intelligence no human being is ever able to come by. But because talk finds no end so long as one fails to reach behind the true cause of the illusion by which even the most rational may be taken in, and because the resolving of all our transcendent cognition into its constituent elements (as an inquiry into our own inner nature) is of no slight worth in itself, and is for the philosopher a positive duty besides, it was necessary not merely to track this entire, albeit fruitless, labor of speculative reason back in detail to its earliest springs; rather, since the dialectical {{ 732 }} illusion here is not deceptive to the judgment alone, but, given the interest one takes here in the judgment, is enticing and forever natural—and will so continue for all time to come—it was advisable to draw up, as it were, a full record of these proceedings and to lodge it in the archive of human reason as a safeguard against errors of a like kind in time to come.
