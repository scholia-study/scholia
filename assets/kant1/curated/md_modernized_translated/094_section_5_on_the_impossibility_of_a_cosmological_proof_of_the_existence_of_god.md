## {{ 631 }} 5. Section. On the impossibility of a cosmological proof of the existence of God.

It was a thoroughly unnatural procedure, and nothing but a novelty of the schoolmen's ingenuity, to try to pick out, from an idea sketched altogether at will, the existence of the corresponding object itself. In fact this route would never have been tried had it not been preceded by our reason's need to posit, for existence as such, something necessary (a point at which the ascent might come to rest), {{{ 404 }}} and had reason not found itself forced—given that such necessity has to be unconditioned and certain _a priori_—to look for a concept capable, if it could be managed, of meeting that requirement and of making an existence something cognized entirely _a priori_. This concept, now, one supposed oneself to discover in the idea of a being most real of all, and thus that idea came to serve merely as a more precise acquaintance with what one was, on independent grounds, already convinced or talked into holding must exist—that is, with the necessary being. This natural progression of reason was, however, kept out of sight, and rather than terminating in the concept, one set about starting from it, so as to draw out of it the necessity of existence that the concept was in truth appointed merely to {{ 632 }} round out. And so there arose that miscarried ontological proof, which offers nothing to satisfy either sound and natural understanding or a properly scholastic scrutiny.

***The cosmological proof***, which is now to occupy us, keeps intact the linkage of absolute necessity with supreme reality; but rather than reasoning, as the earlier one did, from supreme reality to necessity in existence, it reasons instead from the antecedently granted unconditioned necessity of some being or other to the unbounded reality of that being, and to this extent it at least steers everything onto the rails of a mode of inference that is—I cannot tell whether reasonable or merely reason-aping, but at all events natural—and that carries the most persuasion for the common understanding no less than for the speculative; just as it plainly sketches the opening contours of every proof in natural theology, contours that have been traced in every age and will be traced hereafter, however lavishly one may care to trim and conceal them beneath foliage and curlicues. This proof, to which Leibniz likewise gave the name _a contingentia mundi_, we shall now set before us and put to the test.

Its wording is this: if anything at all exists, then a downright necessary being must exist as well. Now, at the very least, I myself exist: hence there exists an absolutely necessary being. The minor premise contains an experience, {{ 633 }} the major the inference, from an experience in general, to the existence of the necessary.[^*] So the proof genuinely {{{ 405 }}} takes its start from experience, and is therefore not carried through wholly _a priori_, or ontologically; and since it is the world that we call the object of all possible experience, the proof is on that score labeled the ***cosmological*** one. And because it likewise abstracts from every special property of the objects of experience through which this world might differ from any other possible one: its very designation already marks it off, too, from the physico-theological proof, which presses observations of the peculiar makeup of this sensible world of ours into service as grounds of proof.

The proof presses on: the necessary being admits of determination in only one manner, i.e., with regard to all possible opposed predicates only through one of them, and so must be, through and through, determined by its concept. Now, of a thing there is but a single possible concept determining it _a priori_ all the way through—namely, that of the _entis realissimi_. The concept of the being most real of all is therefore the sole one {{ 634 }} by means of which a necessary being can be thought—that is, a supreme being exists of necessity.

So many ratiocinative principles converge in this cosmological argument that speculative reason looks here to have mustered the whole of its dialectical artistry so as to produce the largest transcendental semblance it can. Let us, though, lay its assessment aside for a while, simply to bring to light one of its tricks, whereby it puts forward an old argument, dressed up, as if new, and invokes the agreement of two witnesses—one a witness of pure reason, the other of empirical warrant—whereas really it is the first alone that has merely swapped its coat and its voice in order to pass for a second. To make its foundation really secure, this proof plants itself on experience and thereby assumes the look of differing from the ontological proof, which stakes its entire confidence on nothing but pure concepts _a priori_. Yet the cosmological proof draws on this experience for one single step only, namely the step to the existence of a necessary being as such. Which properties it may possess is something the {{{ 406 }}} empirical ground of proof is unable to teach; at that juncture reason parts company with it altogether and probes behind sheer concepts: namely, which properties {{ 635 }} an absolutely necessary being as such would have to possess—that is, which among all possible things holds within it the conditions required (_requisita_) for an absolute necessity. Now she fancies she encounters these requisites in the concept of a being most real of all, and there alone, and thereupon concludes: this is the downright necessary being. But plainly one is here taking for granted that the concept of a being of supreme reality does full justice to the concept of absolute necessity in existence—that is, that the one lets us infer the other; a claim the ontological argument put forward, and which one accordingly embraces and makes the basis in the cosmological proof, notwithstanding that one had wished to steer clear of it. For absolute necessity is existence out of bare concepts. If I now assert: the concept of the _entis realissimi_ is a concept of that sort, and indeed the only one suited and adequate to necessary existence, then I am bound to concede as well that from it the latter may be inferred. It is therefore, strictly, the ontological proof alone—drawn from bare concepts—that holds all the probative force within the so-called cosmological one; and the purported experience does no work at all, serving perhaps only to conduct us to the concept of absolute necessity, but never to exhibit that necessity in some determinate thing. For the moment we make that our aim, we have straightaway to forsake all experience and hunt among pure concepts for whichever of them might hold the conditions of the possibility of an {{ 636 }} absolutely necessary being. But once, in this fashion, the mere possibility of such a being has been discerned, its existence too stands demonstrated; for that comes to the same as saying: among everything possible there is a single thing that brings absolute necessity along with it, i.e., this being exists downright necessarily.

Every piece of sleight-of-hand in inference is exposed most readily when one displays it in properly scholastic form. Here is one such display.

Should the proposition hold good—every simply necessary being is at once the being most real of all (this being the _nervus probandi_ of the cosmological proof)—then, like all affirmative judgments, it must at least admit of conversion _per accidens_; and so: some beings most real of all are at once simply necessary beings. But now, since one _ens realissimum_ {{{ 407 }}} differs in no point from another, whatever is true of ***some*** of the items falling under this concept is true likewise of ***all***. Hence I shall (in the present case) be able to convert it ***without restriction*** as well, i.e., every being most real of all is a necessary being. And since this proposition stands settled _a priori_ from its concepts alone, the naked concept of the being most real of all must itself bring along that being's absolute necessity too—the very thing the ontological proof maintained and the cosmological {{ 637 }} declined to own, yet all the same slipped, in covert fashion, beneath its inferences.

The second route, then, that speculative reason follows in order to establish the existence of the supreme being is not merely as fallacious as the first, but bears this additional fault, that it perpetrates an _ignoratio elenchi_, in that it holds out to us the promise of leading along a fresh footpath yet, after a brief roundabout, delivers us back onto the old one we had abandoned for its sake.

I remarked a moment ago that a whole nest of dialectical pretensions lies hidden in this cosmological argument, one that transcendental critique can readily uncover and demolish. Here I mean only to enumerate them, leaving it to the reader already in practice to pursue the fallacious principles further and to remove them.

Thus there is, for instance, 1) the transcendental principle of arguing from the contingent to a cause—a principle that carries weight only within the sensible world and, beyond it, does not so much as possess a sense. For the purely intellectual concept of the contingent can bring forth no synthetic proposition of the kind that causality is, and the principle of causality carries no significance and no token of its employment save within the sensible world alone; here, however, it was meant to do precisely the service of carrying us out past the sensible world {{ 638 }}. 2) The principle of concluding, from the impossibility, within the sensible world, of an infinite series of causes given one atop another, to a first cause—an inference the principles of reason's use do not license us to draw even within experience, still less permit us to stretch this principle out beyond experience (to which the chain admits of no prolongation whatever). 3) Reason's spurious complacency regarding the completion of this series, achieved by finally clearing away every condition—though without a condition no concept of a necessity can obtain—and then, since nothing further can be grasped, treating this as a completion of one's concept. 4) The {{{ 408 }}} conflation of the logical possibility of a concept comprising all reality combined (with no inner contradiction) with transcendental possibility, which stands in need of a principle of the feasibility of such a synthesis—a principle that, once more, can reach only into the domain of possible experiences, and so forth.

The whole trick of the cosmological proof is directed merely at dodging the proof, _a priori_ and through bare concepts, of the existence of a necessary being—a proof that would have to proceed ontologically, something for which we feel ourselves wholly unequal. With that in view we argue, from an actual existence placed at the ground (an experience in general), as well as it can be managed, to some downright necessary condition of that existence. In that case we have no call to account for the possibility {{ 639 }} of this condition. For once it has been shown that it is there, the question of its possibility becomes wholly needless. Suppose now we want to pin down this necessary being more nearly as to its constitution; then we are not after that which suffices to make the necessity of existence comprehensible from its concept—for could we do so, no empirical premise would be called for; no, we are after nothing but the negative condition (_conditio sine qua non_), in the absence of which a being would fail to be absolutely necessary. In every other species of inference from a given consequence back to its ground this would proceed well enough; but the mischance here is that the condition demanded for absolute necessity can be encountered in one being only, which for that reason would have to embrace in its concept all that absolute necessity requires and so render possible an _a priori_ inference to it; that is, I should have to be able to argue the reverse as well: to whatever thing this concept (of supreme reality) belongs, that thing is downright necessary; and if I cannot argue thus (as I must indeed confess if I mean to avoid the ontological proof), then on my new road too I have failed, and I find myself back at the point from which I departed. The concept of the supreme being does, to be sure, give _a priori_ satisfaction to every question that can be raised about the inner determinations of a thing, and it is for that reason {{ 640 }} an ideal beyond compare, since the universal concept singles the same thing out at once as an individual among all possible things. It gives, however, no satisfaction whatever to the question about its own existence—the very thing that was really the point at issue—and to the query of someone who admitted the existence of a necessary being and merely wished to learn {{{ 409 }}} which among all things must be taken for it, no reply could be given: this one here is the necessary being.

One may perhaps be allowed to ***posit*** the existence of a being of the highest adequacy, as cause of all possible effects, in order to make easier for reason the unity of explanatory grounds that it is seeking. But to arrogate so much as even to declare: ***such a being exists necessarily***, is no longer the unassuming voicing of an admissible hypothesis, but the impudent presumption of an apodictic certainty; for whatever one pretends to cognize as simply necessary must have a cognition that likewise brings absolute necessity along with it.

The entire problem of the transcendental ideal amounts to this: to locate either a concept to go with absolute necessity, or, given the concept of some thing, that thing's absolute necessity. If one succeeds at the one, one must succeed at the other as well; for reason cognizes as simply necessary only what is necessary from its {{ 641 }} concept. Yet both alike outrun entirely every last exertion to content our understanding on this head, and equally every endeavor to quiet it over this incapacity of its own.

The unconditioned necessity that we find so indispensably requisite as the ultimate support of all things is the genuine abyss of human reason. Eternity itself, for all the shudder-inducing sublimity with which a Haller might portray it, falls far short of the same vertiginous effect upon the mind; for it does no more than measure the duration of things—it does not uphold them. One can neither fend off the thought nor yet endure it, that a being we picture to ourselves as the very highest among all possible ones should, so to speak, address itself thus: I am from everlasting to everlasting, apart from me there is nothing save what is something solely through my will; but from where, then, do I come? At this point everything gives way beneath us, and the greatest perfection no less than the least dangles unsupported before merely speculative reason, to which it costs nothing to let the one vanish exactly like the other, without the least obstacle.

Many of nature's forces, which announce their existence through certain effects, stay unfathomable to us; for observation cannot track them far enough. The transcendental object at the base of the appearances, and along with it the reason why our {{ 642 }} sensibility carries these rather than other supreme conditions {{{ 410 }}}, are and stay unfathomable to us, even though the matter itself is in fact given—only not seen into. An ideal of pure reason, though, cannot be styled ***unfathomable***, seeing that it has no other credential of its reality to display than reason's need to bring all synthetic unity to completion by its means. Since, accordingly, it is not so much as given as a thinkable object, neither is it unfathomable as one; rather, being a mere idea, it must find its abode and its dissolution within the nature of reason, and so must be capable of being fathomed; for in just this reason consists, that we can render account of all our concepts, opinions, and assertions—be it on objective grounds or, where they amount to mere semblance, on subjective ones.

[^*]: This mode of inference is far too familiar to require any elaborate setting-out here: It rests on the ostensibly transcendental natural law of causality: that everything ***contingent*** possesses its cause, which cause, being itself contingent in turn, is bound to possess a cause every bit as much, until the chain of causes ranked one beneath another has to terminate in a simply necessary cause, in the absence of which the chain would lack all completeness.
