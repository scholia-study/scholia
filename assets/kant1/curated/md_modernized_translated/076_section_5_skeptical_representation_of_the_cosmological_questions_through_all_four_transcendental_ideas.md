## {{ 513 }} Section 5. Skeptical representation of the cosmological questions through all four transcendental ideas.

We would happily abandon our insistence on having these questions settled dogmatically, could we but see beforehand this much: that no matter how the answer came out, it would do nothing but deepen our ignorance, tumbling us from one unintelligibility into the next, from one darkness into a darkness deeper still, and possibly even into outright contradictions. Where a question admits only of a yes or a no, the wiser course is to hold in reserve, for the time being, the supposed grounds on either side, and to weigh first of all what would actually be won should the verdict fall one way, and what should it fall the other. Should it emerge that either verdict yields pure vacuity of sense (nonsense), we then have solid warrant for turning a critical eye on the question itself, asking whether it may not rest upon some baseless assumption, trifling with an idea whose falsehood shows itself more plainly in the use one makes of it, and in what follows from it, than when the idea is entertained on its own. Herein lies the signal advantage of the skeptical way {{ 514 }} of handling those questions that pure reason addresses to pure reason, whereby a vast dogmatic tangle may be cleared away at slight cost, and replaced with a temperate critique that, working like a genuine purgative, will thoroughly flush out the delusion and its retinue, the craving to know everything.

Suppose, then, that of some cosmological idea I could discern at the outset the following: that whichever side of the unconditioned in the regressive synthesis {{{ 336 }}} of appearances it happened to take, it would in any event turn out, measured against every ***concept of the understanding***, to be either ***too great*** or ***too small***: I should then see that, given how this idea deals only with an object of experience meant to answer to a possible concept of the understanding, it must be wholly vacant and devoid of meaning, since no object squares with it, however I might contrive to fit one to it. And such is indeed the situation with every world-concept, which for this very reason drags reason, so long as it stays attached to them, into an inescapable antinomy. For only take

***first***, ***the world to have no beginning***: then, set against your ***concept***, it comes out ***too great***; for that concept, resting as it does on a step-by-step regress, can never overtake the whole of an eternity already elapsed. Grant instead ***that it does have a beginning***: then once more, for the concept your understanding forms in {{ 515 }} the requisite empirical regress, it proves ***too small***. For a beginning still supposes some earlier time lying before it, and so is not yet unconditioned; the rule governing the empirical employment of the understanding lays on you the duty of inquiring after a yet higher temporal condition, and by that rule the world plainly falls out ***too small***.

No differently does it go with the two competing answers to the question of the world's magnitude as regards space. For ***should it be infinite and without bound***, then relative to any possible empirical concept it is ***too great***. ***Is it finite and bounded***, then you are within your rights to press further: what fixes that limit? Vacant space is no correlate of things subsisting in its own right, and can furnish no condition at which you might halt, and even less an empirical condition forming any part of a possible experience. (For of the utterly void, who could ever have any experience?) Yet the absolute totality of an empirical synthesis invariably demands that the unconditioned itself amount to a concept of experience. And so a ***bounded world*** proves, measured by your concept, ***too small***.

***Secondly***: let every appearance in space (matter) be composed ***of infinitely many parts***, and the regress of dividing them stands, for your concept, forever ***too great***; but let the ***dividing*** of space ***come to rest*** at some one member of the series (the simple), and it stands, for the idea {{ 516 }} of the unconditioned, ***too small***. For that member always leaves standing a further regress to the several parts it contains.

***Thirdly***: grant that whatever comes to pass in the world is nothing but consequence under the laws of ***nature***, and the causality of any cause {{{ 337 }}} is itself, once again, an occurrence, one that makes your regress to a still higher cause—and thus the lengthening of the chain of conditions _a parte priori_—unending and unavoidable. Bare operative ***nature***, then, outstrips your every concept in the synthesis of what befalls the world, being ***too great***.

Opt here and there for happenings brought about ***spontaneously***, and so for an origination ***out of freedom***: at once the "why" dogs you under an inescapable law of nature, driving you past this point by the causal law of experience, and you find a totality of linkage of that kind to ***be too small*** for the empirical concept you cannot forgo.

***Fourthly***. Posit an ***utterly necessary*** being (whether the world at large, or some item within it, or the world's cause): you thereby lodge it in a time infinitely far removed from any moment you care to name, for otherwise it would hang upon some further, more ancient existence. But such an existence then lies beyond the reach of your empirical concept, indeed ***too great*** for you to arrive at by any prolongation of the regress whatever.

Should you hold, however, that everything comprised in the world (whether as conditioned {{ 517 }} or as its condition) is ***contingent***: then any existence handed to you falls, for your concept, ***too small***. For it forces you to go on casting about for some other existence upon which this one leans.

In each of these cases our claim has been that the ***idea of the world*** stands to the empirical regress, and therewith to any possible concept of the understanding, either as too great or else as too small. Why have we not put it the reverse way, saying: that in the first case the empirical concept is on every occasion too small for the idea, and in the second too great, so that the fault, so to speak, sticks to the empirical regress; rather than arraigning the cosmological idea for straying, by excess or by deficiency, from its aim, which is possible experience? Our reason ran thus. It is possible experience, and this alone, that can lend our concepts reality; failing that, a concept is nothing but an idea, bereft of truth and of any bearing on an object. The possible empirical concept therefore served as the measuring-rod against which the idea had to be assessed—whether it be a bare idea and creature of thought, or whether out in the world it does light upon its object. For we pronounce a thing too great or too small in comparison with something else only where it is posited purely for {{{ 338 }}} that other's sake and has to be tailored to it. {{ 518 }} One of the toys of the old dialectical schools was this puzzle: when a ball will not pass through a hole, how is one to put it—is the ball too big, or is the hole too small? Here it makes no difference which way you choose to phrase it; for you have no way of telling which of the pair exists for the sake of the other. You will not, on the other hand, declare the man too tall for his coat; you will say the coat is too short for the man.

We are thus led, at the very least, to a warranted suspicion: that the cosmological ideas—and with them every one of those subtly reasoned assertions pitted against each other—may rest at bottom on an empty, purely fancied concept of the manner in which the object of these ideas comes to be given us; and this suspicion is already enough to set us on the right scent, so that we may expose the sham that has misled us for so long.
