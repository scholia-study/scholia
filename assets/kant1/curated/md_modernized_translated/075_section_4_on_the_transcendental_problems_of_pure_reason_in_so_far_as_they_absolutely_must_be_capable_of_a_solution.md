## Fourth Section. On the transcendental problems of pure reason, in so far as they absolutely must be capable of a solution.

To set out to solve every problem and to answer every question would amount to a brazen grandiloquence and to a conceit so unbridled that one would immediately strip oneself of all trust. And yet certain sciences are so constituted by their very nature that each question raised within them must, without qualification, admit of an answer drawn from what one already knows, since the answer is bound to issue from the same sources out of which the question issues, and where it is on no account permissible to fall back on inescapable ignorance—rather, a resolution may be insisted upon. In every conceivable case, what counts as ***right*** or ***wrong*** must, as a rule, be something we are able to know, since it touches our obligation, and where ***we cannot know***, no obligation binds us either. {{ 505 }} When it comes to explaining the appearances of nature, by contrast, a great deal is bound to stay uncertain for us and more than one question insoluble, for what we know of nature falls far short, in many a case, of what we are called upon to explain. The question that now arises is this: whether, within transcendental philosophy, there is any question bearing on an object set before reason that this same pure reason proves unable to answer, and whether one is entitled to excuse oneself from giving it a conclusive answer by classing it—as something flatly uncertain (among all {{{ 331 }}} that lies within our power to cognize)—together with those matters of which we do indeed grasp enough to pose a question, while yet altogether lacking the means or the faculty to resolve it at any time.

My claim, then, is that transcendental philosophy holds, amid all speculative cognition, a peculiarity all its own: that not a single question touching on an object given to pure reason lies beyond solution for this very human reason, and that no appeal to an inescapable ignorance, or to some unfathomable depth in the task, can discharge us from the duty of answering it soundly and in full, seeing that the very concept enabling us to raise the question must without fail equip us as well to reply to it, inasmuch as the object is to be encountered nowhere apart from the concept itself (as happens with right and wrong).

{{ 506 }} Within transcendental philosophy, however, the only questions in whose case one may justly insist on a satisfactory answer touching the constitution of the object—without the philosopher being at liberty to slip away from it by pleading an impenetrable darkness—are the cosmological ones; and such questions can bear on nothing but cosmological ideas. For the object has to be something given in experience, and all that is asked is how far it matches an idea. Where the object is transcendental and therefore itself unknown—say, whether that something whose appearance (in our own case) is thinking, namely the soul, is a being simple in itself; whether there exists a cause of all things taken together that is absolutely necessary; and so forth—there we are to look, for our idea, to an object which we may grant is unknown to us and yet is not on that score impossible.[^*] {{ 507 }} It is a feature belonging to the cosmological ideas, and to these alone, {{{ 332 }}} that they can take their object—together with the empirical synthesis that its concept demands—as already given; and the question issuing from them bears solely on the advance of this synthesis, in so far as that advance is to embrace absolute totality, a totality which is no longer anything empirical, since it can be furnished in no experience. Now since what is at issue here is merely a thing regarded as the object of a possible experience, and not as a thing existing in itself, the transcendent cosmological question can find its answer in nothing beyond the idea, for it has to do with no object taken in itself; and as regards possible experience, what is being asked after is not that which _in concreto_ could be furnished in some experience, but that which resides in the idea, toward which the empirical synthesis is merely to draw nearer: and so it must admit of resolution from the idea by itself; for the idea is nothing but a product of reason, which accordingly cannot disown the accountability and load it onto the unknown object.

{{ 508 }} It is less remarkable than it may first appear that a science should be able, with respect to every question falling within its own compass (_quaestiones domesticae_), to insist upon and look forward to solutions that are one and all certain, even if for the time being they may perhaps still lie undiscovered. Apart from transcendental philosophy there remain two pure sciences of reason, the one merely speculative in content, the other practical: ***pure mathematics*** and ***pure morals***. Has anyone ever heard it maintained that, owing as it were to a necessary ignorance of the conditions, it was pronounced uncertain what precise ratio the diameter holds to the circle, whether in rational or in irrational numbers? Since by the former it cannot be given congruently at all, while by the latter it has yet to be found, the verdict was that the impossibility of any such solution can at least be cognized with certainty, and of this Lambert furnished a proof. In the universal principles of morality there can be nothing uncertain, for the propositions are either wholly null and void of sense, or else must proceed simply from our concepts of reason. In the study of nature, on the other hand, conjectures are infinite in number, and for none of them may one ever look to attain certainty, {{{ 333 }}} because the appearances of nature are objects handed to us in a manner independent of our concepts, so that the key to them resides not in us and our pure thinking but beyond us, and precisely on that account cannot, in many {{ 509 }} cases, be tracked down, so that no reliable disclosure is to be looked for. I do not include here the questions of the transcendental analytic that concern the deduction of our pure cognition, since our present business is solely the certainty of judgments about objects, not the certainty about the origin of our concepts themselves.

We shall accordingly find no escape from the duty of at least a critical resolution of the rational questions placed before us by voicing laments over the cramped bounds of our reason and professing, with the air of a modest self-knowledge, that it surpasses our reason to settle whether the world has been from eternity or took a beginning; whether the space of the world is filled to infinity with beings or shut up within certain limits; whether anything at all in the world is simple, or whether everything must undergo division to infinity; whether there is a begetting and producing out of freedom, or whether everything is strung upon the chain of nature's order; and lastly whether some being exists that is utterly unconditioned and necessary in itself, or whether everything, in respect of its existence, is conditioned and hence dependent from without and contingent in itself. For every one of these questions concerns an object that admits of being given nowhere save in our thoughts—namely, the utterly unconditioned totality belonging to the synthesis of appearances. Should we prove unable to state and settle anything certain about it from our own concepts, {{ 510 }} we are not at liberty to lay the fault on the thing that hides itself from us; for no thing of that kind can be given to us at all (since outside our idea it is met with nowhere), and we must instead seek the cause in our idea itself—an idea that is a problem admitting of no resolution, and one to which we nonetheless obstinately suppose that an actual object answers. Were the dialectic residing within our very concept laid out with clarity, it would soon bring us to full certainty as to how a question of this sort ought to be judged on our part.

Against your plea of uncertainty regarding these problems one may set, to begin with, this question, which you are bound at the very least to answer plainly: from where do these ideas come to you, whose resolution here embroils you in such difficulty? Are they, perhaps, appearances {{{ 334 }}} that stand in need of explanation, and for which, following out these ideas, you have merely to search for the principles, or the rule, of their exposition? Grant that nature lay entirely disclosed before you, that nothing were concealed from your senses and from your awareness of all that is set before your intuition: even so, no single experience would let you cognize the object of your ideas _in concreto_ (for beyond this complete intuition {{ 511 }} there would further be demanded a synthesis carried to completion, along with awareness of its absolute totality, which no empirical cognition can possibly yield); and thus your question can in no way be necessary for explaining any appearance that turns up, nor, so to speak, be assigned to you by the object itself. For the object can at no time present itself to you, since it can be given through no possible experience. Throughout every possible perception you stay hemmed in under ***conditions***, be it in space or in time, and never reach anything unconditioned by which to settle whether this unconditioned should be looked for in the synthesis's absolute beginning or in an absolute totality of the series lacking any beginning whatever. The whole, though, taken in an empirical sense, is always merely comparative. As for the absolute whole of magnitude (the world-whole), of division, of descent, of the condition of existence as such—together with every question whether it is to be brought off through a finite synthesis or one to be prolonged to infinity—this lies wholly outside the concern of any possible experience. You could not, for example, account for the appearances of a body one jot better, nor even so much as otherwise, by supposing it to be composed of simple parts rather than, throughout, of composite ones; for neither a simple appearance nor, no less, an infinite composition can ever come before you. Appearances call for explanation only to the extent that the conditions for explaining them are furnished within perception; {{ 512 }} yet whatever may at any time be given in them, gathered up into a single ***absolute whole***, is not itself a perception. Yet it is exactly this whole whose explanation the transcendental problems of reason call for.

Since, then, not even the solving of these problems can ever crop up within experience, you are in no position to maintain that it remains uncertain what should be attributed to the object on this point. For your object resides solely within your brain and admits of being given nowhere outside it; and so your only task is to reach agreement with yourself and to head off the amphiboly that would turn your idea into a purported representation {{{ 335 }}} of an object given empirically and therefore to be cognized in keeping with the laws of experience. The dogmatic solution, accordingly, is not so much uncertain as it is impossible. The critical one, on the other hand—which is capable of being wholly certain—regards the question not objectively at all, but in the light of the ground of the cognition on which it is founded.

[^*]: To be sure, to the question what constitution a transcendental object may possess—that is, what it is—no answer can be given; yet one may well answer that the question itself is nothing, and this precisely because no object corresponding to it has been provided. For that reason every question of the transcendental doctrine of the soul is likewise answerable, and has in fact been answered: for such questions bear on the transcendental subject of all inner appearances, a subject that is not itself appearance and hence is not given as an object, and one to which none of the categories (upon which the question is, after all, properly framed) find conditions for their application. Here, then, we have the case in which the common saying applies that no answer is itself an answer—that is, that an inquiry into the constitution of that something which can be thought through no determinate predicate, being posited altogether outside the sphere of the objects capable of being given to us, is utterly null and empty.
