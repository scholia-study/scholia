## 1. Axioms of Intuition

***Their principle is: All intuitions are extensive magnitudes.***

## Proof.

All appearances contain, as regards their form, an intuition in space and time, which lies at the basis of them all _a priori_. They can therefore not be apprehended, i. e., taken up into empirical consciousness {{{ 149 }}}, otherwise than through the synthesis of the manifold, whereby the representations of a determinate space or time are generated, i. e., through the composition of the homogeneous and the consciousness {{ 203 }} of the synthetic unity of this manifold homogeneous. Now the consciousness of the manifold homogeneous in intuition in general, insofar as the representation of an object thereby first becomes possible, is the concept of a ***magnitude*** (quanti). Thus even the perception of an object as appearance is possible only through the very same synthetic unity of the manifold of the given sensible intuition whereby the unity of the composition of the manifold homogeneous is thought in the concept of a magnitude; i. e., appearances are collectively magnitudes, and indeed ***extensive magnitudes,*** because as intuitions in space or time they must be represented through the same synthesis as that whereby space and time in general are determined.

I call an extensive magnitude that in which the representation of the parts makes the representation of the whole possible (and therefore necessarily precedes the latter). I cannot represent to myself any line, however small it may be, without drawing it in thought, i. e., successively generating all parts from a point, and thereby first of all delineating this intuition. The same is also the case with every time, even the smallest. I think therein only of the successive progress from one moment to another, whereby through all the parts of time and their addition a determinate magnitude of time is finally generated. Since the mere intuition in all appearances {{ 204 }} is either space or time, every appearance as intuition is an extensive magnitude, in that it can be cognized in apprehension only through successive synthesis (from part to part). All appearances are accordingly already intuited as aggregates {{{ 150 }}} (a multitude of previously given parts), which is not the case with every kind of magnitude, but only with those that are represented and apprehended by us ***extensively*** as such.

On this successive synthesis of the productive imagination in the generation of figures is grounded the mathematics of extension (geometry) with its axioms, which express the conditions of sensible intuition _a priori_ under which alone the schema of a pure concept of outer appearance can come about: e.g., between two points only one straight line is possible; two straight lines do not enclose a space. These are the axioms that properly concern only magnitudes (quanta) as such.

But as regards magnitude (quantitas), i. e., the answer to the question of how great something is, although several of these propositions are synthetic and immediately certain (indemonstrabilia), there are nevertheless, in the proper sense, no axioms. For that equals added to equals or subtracted from them yield equals are analytic propositions, since I am immediately conscious of the identity of the one generation of magnitude {{ 205 }} with the other; axioms, however, are supposed to be synthetic propositions _a priori_. On the other hand, the evident propositions of numerical relation, although indeed synthetic, are yet not universal, like those of geometry, and for this very reason also not axioms, but can be called numerical formulas. That 7 + 5 = 12 is not an analytic proposition. For I think the number 12 neither in the representation of 7, nor of 5, nor in the representation of the ***composition of both*** (that I should think this in the ***addition of both*** is not the question here; for in the case of an analytic proposition the only question is whether I actually think the predicate in the representation of the subject). But although it is synthetic, it is nevertheless only a singular proposition. Insofar as here regard is had merely to the synthesis of the homogeneous (of units), the synthesis here can occur in only a single way, although the ***use*** of these numbers is subsequently universal. When I say: with three lines, two of which taken together are greater than the third, a triangle can be drawn: I have here the mere function of the productive imagination, which can draw the lines larger and smaller, and likewise can let them meet at all kinds of arbitrary angles. On the contrary, the number 7 is possible in only a single way, and likewise the number 12, which is generated through the synthesis of the former with 5. Such {{{ 151 }}} {{ 206 }} propositions must therefore not be called axioms (for otherwise there would be infinitely many of them), but rather numerical formulas.

This transcendental principle of the mathematics of appearances gives our _a priori_ cognition a great extension. For it is this alone which makes pure mathematics in all its precision applicable to objects of experience, which without this principle would not be so obvious of itself, and indeed has caused many a contradiction. Appearances are not things in themselves. Empirical intuition is possible only through pure intuition (of space and time); what geometry says of the latter therefore holds also, without dispute, of the former, and the evasions, as if objects of the senses did not have to be conformable to the rules of construction in space (e.g., the infinite divisibility of lines or angles), must fall away. For thereby one denies objective validity to space, and with it at the same time to all mathematics, and no longer knows why and how far it is to be applied to appearances. The synthesis of spaces and times, as the essential form of all intuition, is that which at the same time makes possible the apprehension of the appearance, and therewith every outer experience, consequently also all cognition of its objects; and what mathematics in its pure use proves of the former holds also of necessity {{ 207 }} of the latter. All objections against this are only the chicanery of a falsely instructed reason, which erroneously thinks to detach the objects of the senses from the formal condition of our sensibility, and represents them, although they are mere appearances, as objects in themselves, given to the understanding; in which case, indeed, absolutely nothing could be synthetically cognized of them _a priori_, and therefore not even through pure concepts of space, and the science that determines them, namely geometry, would itself not be possible.
