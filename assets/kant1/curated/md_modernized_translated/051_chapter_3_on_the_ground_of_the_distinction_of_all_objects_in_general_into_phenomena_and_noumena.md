## Chapter 3. On the ground of the distinction of all objects in general into Phaenomena and Noumena

By now we have not merely journeyed across the territory of the pure understanding, giving each portion of it a careful inspection; we have also paced it out and fixed the position of every thing that lies within it. This territory, however, is an island that nature herself has locked within limits that cannot be altered. It is the territory of truth (a charming {{ 295 }} name), and around it lies a broad and tempestuous ocean, the very home of semblance, where more than one fog-bank, and more than one patch of ice on the verge of thawing, counterfeits fresh coastlines and, forever taunting with idle hopes the voyager who cruises about hunting for discoveries, draws him into escapades from which he is unable to desist and which he is nevertheless equally unable to see through to their close. Yet before we hazard ourselves upon this sea, in order to comb it across every latitude and to satisfy ourselves whether there is anything in those regions worth hoping for, it will pay us to throw one more look at the chart of the territory we are on the point of quitting, and to ask, in the first place, whether its contents might not after all suffice us, or whether sheer necessity forces us to rest content with them, should there prove to be no other soil anywhere for us to settle upon; and, in the second place, on what title it is that we hold this territory of ours and may count ourselves safe against every hostile pretension. These questions have, to be sure, been given a satisfactory answer {{{ 203 }}} already as the Analytic proceeded; even so, a brief recapitulation of how they were resolved may fortify our conviction, in that it gathers the several moments of the matter into one focus.

What we have found, in effect, is this: whatever the understanding produces out of its own resources, rather than taking it on loan from experience, is possessed by it all the same for one end only, namely to be applied within experience. The pure understanding's principles {{ 296 }}—be they _a priori_ constitutive, as with the mathematical, or nothing more than regulative, as with the dynamical—amount to no more than, so to speak, the bare schema for a possible experience; for such experience owes its unity entirely to that synthetic unity which the understanding, spontaneously and from the first, imparts to the imagination's synthesis with respect to apperception, a unity toward which the appearances, serving as data for a cognition that is possible, must already stand _a priori_ in a relation of accord. Now granted that these rules belonging to the understanding are not merely true _a priori_ but are indeed the very wellspring of all truth—that is to say, of the concord between our cognition and its objects, inasmuch as they harbor within themselves the ground on which experience, taken as the entire compass of cognition wherein objects can come to be given to us, is possible—it still strikes us as insufficient merely to have set before us what is true; what one craves, rather, is to know it. Suppose, then, that this critical inquiry teaches us nothing beyond what, in the plainly empirical employment of the understanding, we would have carried out well enough on our own even without so fine-grained an investigation: in that case the gain to be had from it would seem not to repay the outlay and the elaborate apparatus. To this, admittedly, one might retort that nothing does more harm to the enlargement of our cognition than a certain officious curiosity which {{ 297 }} insists on knowing the benefit beforehand, every time, prior to embarking on any research and before it could so much as frame the faintest notion of that benefit, even were the latter set right before its eyes. Still, there is after all one benefit that can be brought home, and made a matter of concern, even to the most obstinate and least willing apprentice in such transcendental research, and it is this: that an understanding busied solely with its empirical exercise, one that never ponders the wellsprings of its own cognition, can indeed make excellent headway, yet is incapable of a single task—that of marking out for its own sake how far its exercise reaches, and of knowing what falls inside and what outside the whole of its province; for it is precisely to this end that the searching {{{ 204 }}} investigations we have conducted are demanded. Should it prove unable to tell whether given questions fall within its horizon or fall outside it, then it can never rest assured of its entitlements or its holdings, and has to brace itself for repeated, mortifying reprimands whenever it transgresses the frontiers of its domain—as inevitably it does—and strays off into fancy and deceptive show.

The thesis, then, that the understanding is competent to put none but an empirical use, and never a transcendental one, to any of its _a priori_ principles—indeed to any of its concepts whatsoever—is one which, once it can be recognized with {{ 298 }} conviction, opens onto consequences of weight. What the transcendental employment of a concept within any given principle consists in is this: that the concept bears upon things ***in general*** and ***in themselves***; whereas its empirical employment consists in its bearing merely upon ***appearances***, that is, upon objects of an ***experience*** that is possible. That in fact only the latter is ever admissible becomes evident as follows. Every concept calls, to begin with, for the logical form of a concept (of thought) as such, and then, in addition, for the possibility of supplying it with an object upon which it might bear. Lacking this second requirement, it carries no sense and stands wholly void of content, however much it may yet retain the logical function of shaping a concept out of whatever data may be to hand. But an object gets furnished to a concept in no way save through intuition, and even where a pure intuition is available _a priori_ in advance of the object, this pure intuition too can secure an object of its own—and hence objective validity—only by way of empirical intuition, of which it constitutes nothing more than the form. And so every concept—and with it every principle, no matter how far they may be possible _a priori_—still points back to empirical intuitions, that is, to data for a possible experience. Without that, they possess no objective validity whatever, and are a mere game—whether the imagination's or the understanding's—played, in each case, with their respective representations. Take just the concepts of mathematics for {{ 299 }} an example, and, to begin with, in their pure intuitions. Space is of three dimensions; between two points no more than a single straight line is possible; and so forth. Even though the whole of these principles, together with the representation of the object that this science takes as its business, arises entirely _a priori_ within the mind, they would yet signify precisely nothing were we not in a position always to display what they mean in appearances (empirical objects). This is why one demands, of an abstracted concept, that it be {{{ 205 }}} ***made sensible***—in other words, that the object answering to it be displayed in intuition—since, failing that, the concept would (as the saying goes) be left without ***sense***, which is to say without significance. Mathematics meets this demand by constructing the figure, which is an appearance laid before the senses (though one produced _a priori_). Within that very science the concept of magnitude looks for its footing and its sense in number, and number in turn looks for these in the fingers, in the beads of the abacus, or in the strokes and dots that are put before the eyes. The concept, along with the synthetic principles or formulas drawn from such concepts, stays _a priori_ in its origin throughout; but the deploying of them, and their bearing on supposed objects, can in the last resort be tracked down nowhere except in experience, whose possibility (so far as its form goes) those very principles hold _a priori_.

{{ 300 }} That the very same holds, moreover, of all the categories and of the principles spun out of them is likewise borne out by this: that not so much as one of them can be really defined by us—that is, have the possibility of its object rendered intelligible—without our stooping at once to conditions of sensibility, and thus to the form of appearances, beyond which, as their only objects, these categories must accordingly be confined; for once that condition is struck out, all significance, that is, all bearing on an object, vanishes, and no example can bring home to oneself just what sort of thing is really intended under concepts of this kind.

Nobody can spell out the concept of magnitude in general otherwise than roughly thus: that it is that determination of a thing whereby we are enabled to think how often a unit has been posited within it. This 'how often', however, rests upon successive repetition, and therewith upon time and upon the synthesis (of what is homogeneous) carried out in time. Reality, as set over against negation, admits of explanation solely on condition that one conceives a time (as the whole compass of all being) which is either occupied by something or else stands empty {{{ 206 }}}. Should I drop persistence (which is existence throughout all time), then for the concept of substance I am left with nothing but the logical representation of a subject, and this I fancy myself to realize by picturing to myself something capable of figuring purely as subject (never as the predicate of anything else) {{ 301 }}. But quite apart from the fact that I am acquainted with no conditions under which this logical privilege would attach to any thing at all, there is furthermore nothing to be got out of it and not the least conclusion to be drawn, since by its means no object is fixed for the use of this concept, so that one has no idea whether it means anything anywhere. As for the concept of cause, were I to set aside the time in which one thing succeeds another according to a rule, I should discover in the bare category nothing further than that it is a something from which the existence of a further something may be inferred; and by this means not only would cause and effect be left wholly indistinguishable from each other, but—since this licence to infer soon calls for conditions of which I know nothing—the concept would be furnished with no determination as to how it might fit any object. The alleged principle that everything contingent has a cause makes its entrance, to be sure, with a certain gravity, as though it bore its own dignity within itself. But if I put the question—what do you mean by contingent? and you reply, that whose non-being is possible—then I would gladly learn by what token you propose to make out this possibility of non-being, unless within the series of appearances you picture to yourselves a succession, and within that succession an existence supervening upon a non-existence (or the reverse), and hence a change; for the claim that the non-being of a thing does not conflict with itself is a limp {{ 302 }} appeal to a logical condition which, necessary though it be for the concept, falls far short of sufficing for real possibility; just as I am able to cancel in thought any substance whatever that exists, without falling into self-contradiction, and yet cannot on that basis draw any inference to its objective contingency in its existence—that is, to the possibility of its non-being taken in itself. Turning to the concept of community, it is readily gauged that, seeing as the pure categories both of substance and of causality allow of no explanation that would pin down the object, the mutual causality obtaining among substances in their relation to one another (commercium) is every bit as little susceptible of one. Possibility, existence, and necessity—no one has ever managed to explain these save through patent tautology, supposing one wished to derive their definition purely from the bare {{{ 207 }}} understanding. For the sleight of hand whereby the logical possibility of the ***concept*** (in that it does not clash with itself) is slipped in for the transcendental possibility of ***things*** (in that an object answers to the concept) can hoodwink and content none but the untried.[^*]

{{ 303 }} From this it now issues past all contradiction: that the pure concepts of the understanding admit ***never*** of a ***transcendental***, but ***always*** only of an ***empirical*** use; and that the principles of pure understanding may be brought to bear on objects of the senses solely under the universal conditions of a possible experience, but at no time on things in general (leaving out of account the mode in which we might happen to intuit them).

The transcendental Analytic thus yields this weighty upshot: that _a priori_ the understanding can achieve nothing beyond anticipating the form of a possible experience as such, and that, given that whatever is no appearance can be no object of experience, it is never able to break past the barriers of sensibility, inside which alone objects come to be given to us. Its principles are no more than principles for expounding appearances, and the haughty title of an ontology—which lays claim to delivering, in a systematic doctrine, synthetic cognitions _a priori_ about things in general (for instance the principle of causality)—must cede its place to the unassuming title of a mere analytic of the pure understanding.

{{ 304 }} Thought is the act by which a given intuition gets referred to an object. Where the kind of that intuition is in no wise given, the object remains merely transcendental, and the understanding's concept carries no use other than a transcendental one—namely, the unity of the thinking of a manifold as such. Through a pure category, then—one in which every condition of sensible intuition {{{ 208 }}}, as the sole kind open to us, is left out of account—no object gets determined at all; what gets expressed is merely the thinking of an object in general, under differing modes. Now the employment of a concept demands, in addition, a function of the power of judgment, by means of which an object comes to be subsumed beneath it—hence at the least that formal condition under which something is capable of being given in intuition. Where this condition of the power of judgment (the schema) is wanting, every subsumption collapses; for nothing is on offer that could be ranged under the concept. The purely transcendental employment of the categories is therefore, in truth, no employment whatever, and it possesses no object that is determinate, nor even one determinable as to its form. From this it results that the pure category likewise falls short of any synthetic principle _a priori_, that the principles of pure understanding hold only of an empirical, never of a transcendental {{ 305 }} employment, and that once one is out beyond the field of possible experience there can nowhere be synthetic principles _a priori_ at all.

At the root of things here, however, lies an illusion not easily escaped. In point of origin the categories do not rest on sensibility, as do the ***forms of intuition***, space and time, and they seem for that reason to license an application reaching out over all objects of the senses. {{{ 209 }}} Yet they for their own part are, once again, nothing but ***forms of thought***, holding no more than the logical capacity to gather up _a priori_, into a single consciousness, whatever is given in {{ 306 }} intuition as manifold; and there, if the one intuition available to us be taken from them, they can carry even less import than those pure sensible forms, which do at any rate furnish an object—whereas a manner of combining the manifold that is proper to our understanding imports nothing whatever, so long as the intuition wherein alone that manifold can be given fails to be added to it. — And yet it already resides within our concept, once we name certain objects, qua appearances, entities of sense (_Phaenomena_) and thereby mark off the way we intuit them from their character as it is in itself, that we either set that self-same thing, taken by this latter character—though we do not intuit it under that character—or else set other possible things, no objects at all of our senses, over against the former, as objects thought through the understanding alone, and dub these entities of the understanding (_Noumena_). The question that then poses itself is whether our pure concepts of the understanding might not carry meaning as regards these latter, and count as a mode of cognizing them?

Right from the start, though, an ambiguity surfaces here, one apt to breed grave misunderstanding: namely that the understanding, whenever it labels an object mere appearance in one relation, at the same time frames for itself, outside that relation, a representation of an ***object in itself*** {{ 307 }}—and so imagines that it may frame ***concepts*** of such an object too, and, since the understanding hands over nothing save the categories, that the object in the latter meaning must at any rate be thinkable by way of these pure concepts of the understanding—and is thereby seduced into treating the wholly ***indeterminate*** concept of an entity of the understanding, as a something-in-general lying outside our sensibility, as though it were a ***determinate*** concept of an entity that we might, through the understanding, cognize in some fashion.

Taking a noumenon to mean a thing ***insofar as it is not an object of our sensible intuition***, in that we leave our manner of intuiting it out of account, we have a noumenon in the ***negative*** acceptation. If, on the other hand, we take it to mean an ***object*** of a ***non-sensible intuition***, we are thereby assuming a peculiar kind of intuition, the intellectual namely, which is not ours, however, and into {{{ 210 }}} whose possibility we can gain no insight either—and this would be the noumenon in its ***positive*** import.

The teaching about sensibility is at one and the same time the teaching about noumena in the negative acceptation—about things, that is, that the understanding is bound to think apart from this bearing on our manner of intuiting, and so not as mere appearances but as things in themselves, though of them it grasps, in this very act of setting them apart, that it can put no use of its {{ 308 }} categories to work in considering them after this fashion; for these categories, having meaning only with reference to the unity of intuitions within space and time, are able to fix that unity itself, and this precisely on account of the sheer ideality of space and of time, solely through universal concepts of connection _a priori_. Wherever this unity of time is nowhere to be met with—and thus in the case of the noumenon—there the whole use of the categories, indeed their whole meaning, lapses utterly; for even the possibility of the things meant to answer to the categories eludes all insight, on which score I may simply fall back on what I put forward, right at its outset, in the general remark appended to the foregoing chapter. But the possibility of a thing is never to be established out of the mere non-contradictoriness of a concept of it; it can be established only by underpinning that concept with an intuition answering to it. Were we, then, to seek to apply the categories to objects not regarded as appearances, we should have to put down as our basis an intuition of some kind other than the sensible, and in that event the object would be a noumenon in the ***positive*** import. Since such an intuition—intellectual intuition, that is—falls flatly beyond our cognitive faculty, the categories' use can in no way stretch past the boundary that the objects of experience mark out; and although entities of the understanding do of course answer to the entities of sense, {{ 309 }} and although there may well be entities of the understanding to which our sensible faculty of intuiting bears no relation whatever, still our concepts of the understanding, being mere forms of thought meant for our sensible intuition, do not reach out to these in the slightest; whatever, then, gets called noumenon by us must, as such, be understood in the ***negative*** import alone.

Should I strip from an empirical cognition {{{ 211 }}} the whole of thought (namely, thought through categories), then no cognition at all of any object is left over; for through bare intuition nothing gets thought whatsoever, and the circumstance that this affection of sensibility resides in me establishes no bearing of a representation of that kind upon any object at all. If, by contrast, I dispense with all intuition, there yet abides the form of thought—the manner, that is, of settling an object for the manifold of a possible intuition. To that extent, therefore, the categories range wider than sensible intuition does, for they think objects in general without as yet looking to the special way (belonging to sensibility) in which such objects might get given. They do not, however, thereby stake out a larger sphere of objects; for that objects of this sort can be given is something one is in no position to grant unless one first posits as possible a kind of intuition other than the sensible—an assumption to which we are by no means entitled.

{{ 310 }} A concept I term problematic when it holds no contradiction, when it also hangs together with other cognitions as a bounding of given concepts, and yet its objective reality is in no manner open to being cognized. The concept of a ***noumenon***—that is, of a thing meant to be thought not in the least as an object of the senses, but rather as a thing in itself (by means of a pure understanding alone)—carries no contradiction whatever; for one is surely not entitled to maintain of sensibility that it is the sole possible manner of intuition. This concept is, further, indispensable if sensible intuition is not to be spread out over things in themselves as well, and so if the objective validity of sensible cognition is to be kept within bounds (for the remainder, upon which such intuition has no purchase, are given the name noumena for that very reason, so as to signal thereby that cognitions of that sort cannot push their territory out over all that the understanding thinks). In the last analysis, though, the possibility of such _Noumenorum_ resists all insight, and the expanse beyond the sphere of appearances is, for us, void—which is to say, we are furnished with an understanding that reaches ***problematically*** further than the appearances do, but with no intuition, nor even so much as the concept of a possible intuition, whereby objects might be given to us outside the field of sensibility, and whereby, out past that field, the understanding might be exercised ***assertorically***. The concept of a noumenon {{ 311 }}, accordingly, is no more than a ***boundary concept***, serving to curb the presumption of sensibility, and hence is of a merely negative use. For all that, it is not fabricated at will, but hangs together with the curbing of {{{ 212 }}} sensibility, though without being competent to posit anything positive outside the compass of the latter.

For this reason the sorting of objects into _Phaenomena_ and _Noumena_, and of the world into, on the one hand, a world of sense and, on the other, a world of understanding, cannot in any positive meaning be conceded, even granting that concepts do indeed permit a sorting into the sensible and the intellectual ones; for to the latter no object can be assigned, and hence they cannot be passed off as objectively valid either. Once one takes leave of the senses, how is one to render intelligible that our categories (which would be the sole concepts left over for noumena) still signify anything at all, seeing that, for them to bear on any object whatever, something beyond the bare unity of thought—namely, over and above it, a possible intuition—must be supplied for those categories to be applied to? Taken merely in its problematic character, the concept of a noumenon stays, notwithstanding all this, not only permissible but, as a concept that fences sensibility about with limits, downright inescapable. But in that case there is no special ***intelligible object*** here for our understanding; rather, an understanding to which such an object would pertain is itself a problem—the problem, that is, of cognizing its object not by way of discourse, through categories {{ 312 }}, but by way of intuition, in a non-sensible intuiting—an understanding of whose very possibility we can form for ourselves not the faintest representation. In this way our understanding comes by an enlargement that is negative—which is to say, far from being hemmed in by sensibility, it rather hems sensibility in, precisely by giving the name noumena to things in themselves (not viewed as appearances). But at the same stroke it draws limits about itself, to cognize them through no categories, and thus to think them under nothing but the name of an unknown something.

In the writings of the moderns, meanwhile, I come upon a wholly different use of the terms mundus sensibilis and intelligibilis,[^**] one that {{{ 213 }}} parts ways entirely with the ancients' meaning, and that, while it presents no difficulty of course, likewise yields nothing but empty haggling over words. On this usage it has pleased some to give the sum total of appearances, so far as it is intuited, the name world of sense, but, so far as the interconnection of that total is thought according to universal laws of understanding, the name {{ 313 }} world of understanding. Theoretical astronomy, which sets forth the bare observation of the star-strewn sky, would exhibit the first of these, whereas contemplative astronomy (expounded, say, along the lines of the Copernican world-system, or indeed of Newton's laws of gravitation) would exhibit the second—an intelligible world, namely. But a wrenching of words of this kind is a merely sophistical shift, meant to sidestep an awkward question by tuning its sense down to suit one's own comfort. Where appearances are concerned, understanding and reason are certainly of service; but the question is whether they retain any service once the object is no appearance (a noumenon), and it is in this sense that one takes the object when it is thought in its own right as bare intelligible—which is to say, as consigned to the understanding alone and in no way to the senses. So the question comes to this: whether, over and above that empirical employment of the understanding (even in the Newtonian representation of the world's fabric), a transcendental one is still possible, one bearing on the noumenon as an object—a question we have settled in the negative.

If, accordingly, we say that the senses set objects before us ***as they appear***, but the understanding ***as they are***, the second phrase is to be understood not in a transcendental but in a plainly empirical meaning—namely, as those objects, being objects of experience, have to be represented within the thoroughgoing {{ 314 }} interconnection of appearances, and not in terms of what they may be apart from any relation to possible experience, and hence to the senses at large, that is, as objects of the pure understanding. For this will forever stay hidden from us—so much so that it stays hidden even whether a transcendental (out-of-the-ordinary) cognition of that sort is so much as possible, at any rate as one falling under our customary categories. ***Understanding*** and ***sensibility***, in our case, can determine objects ***only in conjunction***. Sever them, and we are left with intuitions devoid of concepts, or concepts devoid of intuitions, but in either case with representations that we are unable to refer to any determinate object.

Should someone, in spite of all these expositions, still scruple to forswear the {{{ 214 }}} merely transcendental employment of the categories, let him put them to the test in some synthetic assertion or other. For an analytic assertion carries the understanding no further along, and since it busies itself with nothing except what is thought already within the concept, it leaves it unsettled whether that concept, in its own right, bears on objects or signifies merely the unity of thought as such (a unity that abstracts wholly from the way in which an object may get given); to know what lies within its concept suffices the understanding; toward what the concept may itself be directed is, to it, a matter of {{ 315 }} indifference. Let him accordingly have a go with some synthetic and ostensibly transcendental principle, such as: whatever there is exists either as substance or as a determination inhering in one; all that is contingent exists as the effect of some other thing, that is, of its cause, and so on. Now I put the question: whence does he mean to fetch these synthetic propositions, given that the concepts are meant to hold good not with reference to possible experience but of things in themselves (noumena)? Where, in this case, is the third thing that a synthetic proposition invariably demands, so as to knit together within it concepts that share no logical (analytic) affinity at all? He will never make his proposition good—indeed, what is more, he will prove unable so much as to justify the very possibility of such a pure assertion—without taking the empirical use of understanding into account, and thereby forswearing outright the pure, sense-free judgment. And so the concept of pure, merely intelligible objects turns out to be utterly bereft of any principles for its application, since there is no devising a way in which such objects ought to be given; and the problematic thought, which for all that keeps a spot open for them, does duty only after the fashion of a vacant space, hedging in the empirical principles, yet without holding within itself, or bringing to light, any further object of cognition beyond the sphere of those principles.

[^*]: To put it in a word, once all sensible intuition—the sole kind at our disposal—has been withdrawn, there is nothing left by which these concepts might be underpinned and their real possibility thereby exhibited, and all that then survives is the logical possibility, namely that the concept (the thought) be possible—though that is not what is at issue, but rather whether it bears on an object and so signifies something or other.

[^**]: In place of this expression one ought not to employ that of an ***intellectual*** world, as is the customary habit in German discourse; for it is the ***cognitions*** alone that are intellectual or sensitive. But whatever can be no more than an ***object*** of the one or the other kind of intuition—the objects, then—must (harsh though the sound may be) be termed intelligible or sensible.
