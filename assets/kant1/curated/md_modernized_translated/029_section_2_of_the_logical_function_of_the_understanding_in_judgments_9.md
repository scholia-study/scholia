{{ 96 }} Since this division seems to deviate in some points, though not essential ones, from the customary technique of logicians, the following cautions against any feared misunderstanding will not be unnecessary.

1. Logicians say with justice that, in the use of judgments in syllogisms, singular judgments can be treated like universal ones. For for this very reason, because they have no extension at all, their predicate cannot be related merely to a part of that which is contained under the concept of the subject, and excluded from another part. It is therefore valid of that concept without exception, just as if it were a common concept that had an extension, to the entire meaning of which the predicate applied. If, on the contrary, we compare a singular judgment with a universally valid one, merely as cognition in respect of quantity, the former stands to the latter as unity to infinity, and is therefore in itself essentially distinct from it. Therefore, if I estimate a singular judgment (_judicium singulare_) not merely according to its inner validity, but also as cognition in general according to the quantity it has in comparison with other cognitions, then it is indeed distinct from universally valid judgments (_judicia communia_) and deserves a special place in a complete table of the moments of thought in general {{{ 88 }}} {{ 97 }} (although, to be sure, not in logic limited merely to the use of judgments in relation to one another).

2. Likewise, in a transcendental logic, ***infinite judgments*** must still be distinguished from ***affirmative*** ones, even though in general logic they are rightly classed with the latter and do not constitute a special member of the division. For the latter abstracts from all content of the predicate (even if it be negative) and attends only to whether it is attributed to the subject or opposed to it. The former, however, considers the judgment also in respect of the value or content of this logical affirmation by means of a merely negative predicate, and what benefit this yields in respect of the sum total of cognition. If I had said of the soul, "it is not mortal," I would at least have kept off an error by means of a negative judgment. Now, through the proposition: "the soul is non-mortal," I have indeed actually affirmed as regards logical form, by placing the soul in the unlimited extension of non-mortal beings. Since now within the entire extension of possible beings the mortal contains one part, and the non-mortal the other, nothing else is said by my proposition than that the soul is one of the infinite multitude of things that remain when I take away the mortal in its entirety. But by this the infinite sphere of all possible things is only so far limited that the {{ 98 }} mortal is separated from it and the soul is placed in the remaining space of its extension. This space, however, still remains infinite even with this exclusion, and more parts of it can be taken away without the concept of the soul growing in the least or being affirmatively determined thereby. These infinite judgments, therefore, in respect of logical extension, are actually merely limiting in respect of the content of cognition in general, and as such they must not be omitted from the transcendental table of all moments of thought in judgments, because the function of the understanding exercised here may perhaps be important in the field of its pure cognition _a priori_.

3. All relations of thought in judgments are: a) that of the predicate to the subject, b) that of the ground to the consequence, c) that of the divided cognition and of the collected members of the division to one another. In the first kind of judgments only two concepts are considered, in the second two judgments, in the third several judgments in relation to one another {{{ 89 }}}. The hypothetical proposition: "if there is perfect justice, then the obstinately wicked will be punished," contains actually the relation of two propositions: "there is perfect justice," and "the obstinately wicked will be punished." Whether both of these propositions are in themselves true remains here undecided. It is only the consequence that is thought through this judgment. Finally, the disjunctive judgment contains a {{ 99 }} relation of two or more propositions to one another, not of consequence, but of logical opposition, insofar as the sphere of one excludes that of the other, but yet at the same time of community, insofar as they together fill the sphere of the proper cognition, thus a relation of the parts of the sphere of a cognition, since the sphere of each part is a complement of the sphere of the other to the whole sum of the divided cognition; e.g., the world is either there through a blind chance, or through internal necessity, or through an external cause. Each of these propositions occupies a part of the sphere of the possible cognition concerning the existence of a world in general, all together the entire sphere. To remove the cognition from one of these spheres means to place it in one of the others, and, conversely, to place it in one sphere means to remove it from the others. There is therefore in a disjunctive judgment a certain community of cognitions, which consists in this, that they mutually exclude one another, but thereby yet ***in the whole*** determine the true cognition, since taken together they constitute the entire content of a single given cognition. And this is also all that I find it necessary to remark here for the sake of what follows.

4. The modality of judgments is a quite particular function of them, {{ 100 }} which has the distinctive feature that it contributes nothing to the content of the judgment (for besides quantity, quality, and relation there is nothing more that constitutes the content of a judgment), but rather concerns only the value of the copula in relation to thought in general. ***Problematical*** judgments are those where one takes the affirmation or negation as merely ***possible*** (arbitrary); ***assertorical***, where it is considered as ***actual*** (true); ***apodictical***, in which one regards it as ***necessary***.[^*] Thus the two judgments whose relation {{{ 90 }}} constitutes the hypothetical judgment (the _antec._ and _consequ._), as well as those in whose reciprocal interaction the disjunctive consists (members of the division), are altogether only problematical. In the above example, the proposition: "there is perfect justice," is not stated assertorically, but is only thought as an arbitrary judgment, which it is possible for someone to assume; and only the consequence is assertorical. Hence such judgments can also be obviously false and yet, taken problematically, serve as conditions for the cognition of truth. Thus the judgment: ***the world is there through blind chance***, is in the disjunctive judgment of only problematical significance, namely, that someone might assume this proposition for {{ 101 }} a moment, and yet it serves (like the mapping of a false path among all those that one can take) to find the true one. The problematical proposition is therefore that which expresses only logical possibility (which is not objective), i.e., a free choice to let such a proposition be valid, a merely arbitrary reception of it into the understanding. The assertorical speaks of logical actuality or truth, as, for example, in a hypothetical syllogism the antecedens occurs problematically in the major premise and assertorically in the minor premise, and indicates that the proposition is already connected with the understanding according to its laws. The apodictical proposition thinks the assertorical as determined by these laws of the understanding itself and thereby as asserting _a priori_, and in this way expresses logical necessity. Since here everything incorporates itself into the understanding step by step, so that one first judges something problematically, then accepts it assertorically as true, and finally asserts it as inseparably connected with the understanding, i.e., as necessary and apodictical, one can call these three functions of modality so many moments of thought in general.

[^*]: Just as if thought in the first case were a function of the ***understanding***, in the second of the ***power of judgment***, and in the third of ***reason***. A remark that only awaits its elucidation in the sequel.
