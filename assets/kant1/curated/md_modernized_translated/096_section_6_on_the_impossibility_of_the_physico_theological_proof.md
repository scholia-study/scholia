## Section 6. On the impossibility of the physico-theological proof

Given, then, that what is demanded can be furnished neither by the concept of things as such nor by experience of any ***existence in general***, one further recourse is still left: to test whether a ***determinate experience*** — that, namely, of the things making up the present world, their makeup and their ordering — may not supply a ground of proof able to lead us reliably to the conviction that a highest being exists. A proof of this sort we would term ***physico-theological*** {{{ 414 }}}. Should even this turn out to be impossible, then nowhere does there remain, drawn from merely speculative reason, any sufficient proof for a being's existing that would match our transcendental idea.

{{ 649 }} In view of everything remarked above, one will readily see that the reply to this demand can be looked for quite easily and tidily. For how should an experience ever come to be given that ought to fit an idea? This is exactly what marks off the latter: that no experience whatever can ever match it. So exorbitantly great is the transcendental idea of a necessary, wholly self-sufficing primordial being, so loftily raised above all that is empirical (which is invariably conditioned), that on the one hand one can never dredge up material enough within experience to fill such a concept, and on the other hand one keeps fumbling among conditioned things, forever seeking in vain the unconditioned, for which no law of any empirical synthesis provides us so much as an instance or the least guidance.

Were the highest being to stand inside this chain of conditions, it would itself figure as one member of that same series and, exactly like the lower members over which it is set, would require yet further investigation on account of its own still higher ground. Should one instead sever it from that chain and, as a being that is purely intelligible, decline to reckon it among the series of natural causes: what bridge, then, can reason manage to build so as to arrive at it, seeing that all the laws by which one crosses from effects back to their causes — indeed every synthesis and every widening of our cognition at all — are pinned to nothing beyond possible experience, and hence solely to {{ 650 }} objects of the world of sense, and can carry meaning only in regard to these?

The world now before us throws open a theater so boundless — of variety, of order, of fitness to purpose, and of beauty, whether one tracks these through space's infinity or through its unlimited divisibility — that even by the lights our feeble understanding has managed to gather concerning it, all speech forfeits its force in the face of marvels so numerous and beyond all surveying, all numbers forfeit their power to measure, and our very thoughts forfeit every boundary, so that our judgment on the whole cannot but melt into an astonishment that, though wordless, is for that very reason the more eloquent. On every side there meets us a chain of effects and their causes, of ends and the means, of regularity in arising or in perishing; and since nothing has of its own accord entered the state {{{ 415 }}} it now occupies, each thing keeps pointing onward toward some other thing as its cause, which cause makes that very same further inquiry unavoidable all over again, so that the entire cosmos would in this fashion have to founder in the abyss of nothingness, did one not posit something that — subsisting beyond this endless contingent realm, primordial and self-standing in its own right — sustained it and, being the cause of its arising, secured for it at the same time its lasting persistence. This supreme cause (in regard to every thing in the world) — of what greatness are we to conceive it? The world we do not know as to its whole content, {{ 651 }} and still less can we appraise its magnitude by holding it up against everything that is possible. Yet what is to stop us — once, with respect to causality, we do stand in need of an outermost and topmost being — from at the same time placing it, by degree of perfection, ***above every other possibility***? a thing we can bring off easily enough (though, to be sure, only through the delicate outline of an abstract concept) provided we picture within it, as within one solitary substance, every possible perfection gathered together; and this concept is favorable to reason's demand for thrift in principles, is liable in itself to no contradictions, and even assists the extension of reason's use in the very thick of experience, by way of the direction that such an idea lends toward order and purposiveness, while nowhere clashing outright with any experience.

This proof merits, on every occasion, to be named with respect. It is the most ancient, the most lucid, and the one most fitted to the common reason of humankind. It quickens the study of nature, even as it draws its own existence from that study and thereby continually acquires fresh strength. It transports ends and designs into regions where our observation would never have lit upon them unaided, and it enlarges our acquaintance with nature by the guiding line of a distinctive unity whose principle lies beyond nature. But this acquaintance recoils {{ 652 }} in turn upon its cause — that is, the idea that gave rise to it — and builds up belief in a supreme originator to the point of an irresistible conviction.

It would accordingly be not merely cheerless but also wholly pointless to try to strip anything away from the credit this proof enjoys. Reason, borne up without cease by grounds of proof this potent — grounds that swell ever larger in its handling, empirical though they remain — can be pressed down by no misgivings of refined, rarefied speculation so far {{{ 416 }}} that a single look it throws upon nature's wonders and the grandeur of the world's fabric would fail to wrench it out of every fretful indecision, as if out of a dream, and lift it up from one grandeur to the next as far as the utmost, from the conditioned onward to its condition as far as the topmost and unconditioned originator.

Now, although we have nothing to bring against the reasonableness and the usefulness of this procedure — indeed have every cause to commend and to spur it on — we cannot for all that grant the pretensions that this manner of proving would fain advance to apodictic certainty and to an approval standing in need of no favor and no foreign prop; and it can do the good cause no harm at all to pitch the dogmatic idiom of a sneering pseudo-reasoner down to the key of temperance and modesty proper to a belief that suffices for calm, even if it does not command unconditional {{ 653 }} submission. I contend, therefore, that the physico-theological proof is never able by itself to establish that a highest being exists, but must at all times hand the task over to the ontological proof (for which it merely furnishes the introduction) of supplying this shortfall, so that it is still the ontological that carries the ***sole possible ground of proof*** (supposing a speculative proof to be available anywhere at all), one past which no human reason can make its way.

The chief points of the physico-theological proof under discussion run as follows: 1) All across the world one meets with plain marks of an arrangement conforming to a definite design, executed with great wisdom and set within a whole whose content is of indescribable variety and whose compass is of unbounded size. 2) To the things of the world this purposive arrangement is entirely alien, clinging to them only by chance — that is to say, the natures of sundry things could never of themselves, through such manifold mutually converging means, have fallen into agreement toward definite final designs, had they not, in strict accordance with underlying ideas, been quite deliberately chosen and laid out to that end by an arranging rational principle. 3) There exists, then, an exalted and wise cause (or several) which must be the source of the world not simply as a blindly operating, all-powerful nature by way of ***fecundity***, but as an intelligence by way of ***freedom***. 4) That this cause is one may be gathered — with certainty as regards {{ 654 }} whatever our observation {{{ 417 }}} manages to reach, and beyond that, on all the principles of analogy, with probability — from the oneness of the mutual interrelation among the world's parts taken as members of a contrived edifice.

Without picking a quarrel here with natural reason over its conclusion — for out of the analogy between certain products of nature and the things human artifice turns out (when it forces nature and constrains it to work not toward nature's own ends but to bend to ours), their likeness, that is, to houses, ships, and clocks, it concludes that just such a causality, namely understanding and will, must underlie nature, once it goes on to derive the inner possibility of self-active nature (which is what first makes possible all artifice and perhaps even reason itself) from a further art, superhuman though it be, a style of inference that might well fail to survive the keenest transcendental critique — one has nonetheless to admit that, if a cause is once to be named by us, we can go about it here no more surely than along the analogy with such purposive productions, the only ones whose causes and manner of operating are fully known to us. Reason could never square it with itself were it minded to move across from the causality it is acquainted with to murky and unprovable grounds of explanation with which it is not acquainted.

On the strength of this inference, the purposiveness and neat congruity {{ 655 }} of so many natural provisions would establish merely that the form is contingent, not that the matter — that is, the substance within the world — is so; for this latter would over and above demand a proof that the world's things would of themselves be unsuited to any such order and concord under universal laws, unless they were, even ***in respect of their substance***, the handiwork of a supreme wisdom — something, however, that would call for grounds of proof altogether other than those taken from the analogy with human artifice. The proof could thus at the very most make out a ***builder of the world***, forever hemmed in narrowly by how serviceable the material he shapes happens to be, but never a ***creator of the world*** to whose idea all things lie subject — which comes nowhere near answering to the lofty aim held before one's eyes, namely to prove a primordial being sufficient unto all. Did we mean to prove the contingency of the matter itself, we should have to seek shelter in a transcendental argument — the very thing that was here supposed to be steered clear of.

The conclusion therefore runs from the order and purposiveness so thoroughly observable across the world, treated as an out-and-out contingent set-up, {{{ 418 }}} to there existing a cause ***proportioned to it***. The concept of this cause, however, has to let us cognize something quite ***determinate*** about it, and so it can be none other than that of a being possessed of all power, all wisdom, and so forth — in short, of every perfection {{ 656 }} — as a being self-sufficing in all respects. For the predicates of ***very great***, of amazing, of measureless power and superbness furnish no determinate concept whatever and do not, properly speaking, state what the thing may be in itself, but are merely relative representations of the object's size, which the observer (of the world) sets beside himself and beside his own power of grasp, and which come out just as full of praise whether one enlarges the object or, relatively to it, diminishes the observing subject. Wherever it is a question of the greatness (of perfection) belonging to a thing in general, no determinate concept is to be had except the one that takes in the entire span of possible perfection, and it is the whole (_omnitudo_) of reality alone that stands through and through determined within its concept.

Now I should not like to suppose that anyone would take it upon himself to see into the ratio of the world-magnitude he has observed (in compass as well as in content) to omnipotence, of the world-order to the highest wisdom, of the world-unity to the absolute unity of the originator, and so on. Physico-theology is therefore in no position to yield any determinate concept of the topmost cause of the world, and hence falls short of serving as a principle of theology — which is itself meant to make up the groundwork of religion.

To take the step to absolute totality is by the empirical road wholly and altogether out of the question. And yet, in the {{ 657 }} physico-theological proof, one does take it. By what device, then, does one contrive to get across so broad a gulf?

Having reached the point of marveling at the greatness of the wisdom, of the power, and so on of the world's originator, and being unable to press on further, one all at once quits this argument that has been steered by empirical grounds of proof, and turns back to the contingency of the world, which was inferred at the very start from its order and purposiveness. From this contingency by itself one now advances, purely by way of transcendental concepts, to the existence of an absolutely necessary something, and from the concept of the first cause's absolute necessity onward to the thoroughly determinate, or determining, concept belonging to it — the concept, namely, of an all-embracing reality. The physico-theological proof, then, had stuck fast in its enterprise and, in this predicament, abruptly vaulted across to the cosmological proof, {{{ 419 }}} and since the latter is nothing but a concealed ontological proof, it in fact carried its purpose through by pure reason alone, notwithstanding that at the outset it had renounced all connection with pure reason and had let everything ride on plain proofs from experience.

The physico-theologians, then, have no ground at all to act so aloof toward the transcendental style of proof and to peer down on it, with the conceit of clear-sighted experts on nature, as though on the gossamer of murky brooders. For were they willing merely to test themselves, they would {{ 658 }} find that, having gone forward a good way over the soil of nature and experience, and yet seeing themselves still just as distant from the object that appears to confront their reason, they all at once desert this soil and pass over into a kingdom of bare possibilities, where, upon the wings of ideas, they hope to come near to that which had slipped away from every one of their empirical searches. Once they at length fancy that, by so forceful a leap, they have secured firm footing, they broadcast the by-now determinate concept (which they have come to possess without knowing how) over the whole field of creation, and they elucidate the ideal — which was nothing but a product of pure reason — by means of experience, meagerly enough, to be sure, and far below the dignity of its object, all the while unwilling to confess that they reached this knowledge, or this presupposition, along some footpath other than experience's.

Thus at bottom the cosmological proof props up the physico-theological, and the ontological proof — concerning the existence of a single primordial being as the highest being — props up the cosmological; and since, outside these three routes, none further stands open to speculative reason, the ontological proof, wrought from nothing but pure concepts of reason, is the one and only possible proof, if indeed any proof at all is possible of a proposition raised this far above every empirical use of the understanding.
