## {{{ 27 }}} I. Of the difference between pure and empirical cognition.

That all our cognition begins with experience, there is no doubt whatsoever; for by what else should the cognitive faculty be awakened into exercise, if not by means of objects that affect our senses and partly of themselves produce representations, partly rouse our understanding's activity to compare these, to connect or separate them, and thus to work up the raw material of sensible impressions into a cognition of objects that is called experience? ***In time***, therefore, no cognition within us precedes experience, and with it all cognition begins.

But even though all our cognition commences **with** experience, it does not for that reason all arise **from** experience. For it could well be that even our empirical cognition is a composite of that which we receive through impressions and that which our own cognitive faculty (merely prompted by sensible impressions) provides out of itself, an addition which we cannot distinguish from that {{ 2 }} fundamental material until long practice has made us attentive to it and skilled in separating it out.

{{{ 28 }}} It is therefore at least a question still in need of closer investigation, and not to be dismissed at first glance: whether there is any such cognition independent of experience and even of all impressions of the senses. One calls **such** ***cognitions*** _a priori_, and distinguishes them from the ***empirical*** ones, which have their sources _a posteriori_, namely in experience.

That expression, however, is not yet determinate enough to adequately denote the whole meaning of the proposed question. For one is accustomed to say of many a cognition derived from experiential sources that we are capable of it or partake in it _a priori_, because we do not derive it immediately from experience, but from a general rule, which we have nevertheless ourselves borrowed from experience. Thus one says of someone who undermined the foundation of his house: he could have known _a priori_ that it would collapse, i.e., he did not need to wait for the experience of its actual collapse. Yet he could not have known this entirely _a priori_ either. For that bodies are heavy and therefore fall when their support is withdrawn, must first have become known to him through experience.

We shall therefore, in what follows, understand by cognitions _a priori_ not those {{ 3 }} that occur independently of this or that experience, but rather those that occur absolutely independently of all experience. Opposed to them are empirical cognitions, or those that are possible only _a posteriori_, i.e., through experience. Among the cognitions _a priori_, however, those are called ***pure*** with which nothing empirical at all is intermixed. Thus, for example, the proposition: every alteration has its cause, is a proposition _a priori_, but not pure, because alteration is a concept that can only be drawn from experience.
