## {{{ 27 }}} I. On the difference between pure and empirical cognition.

That all our cognition starts with experience admits of no doubt at all; for by what else would the faculty of cognition be roused into activity, were it not by objects that affect our senses and partly produce representations by themselves, partly set the activity of our understanding in motion to compare these, to combine or to separate them, and so to process the raw stuff of sensory impressions into a cognition of objects that goes by the name of experience? ***In respect of time***, therefore, no cognition within us comes before experience, and it is with experience that all cognition starts.

But even if all our cognition sets out **with** experience, it still does not for that reason all spring **from** experience. For it might well be that even our experiential cognition is something compounded out of what we receive through impressions and what our own faculty of cognition (merely occasioned by sensory impressions) supplies from within itself — an addition that we do not tell apart from that {{ 2 }} basic material until long practice has drawn our attention to it and rendered us adept at sorting it out.

{{{ 28 }}} This is, then, at the least a question requiring closer investigation, and one not to be dismissed at once on first appearance: whether any cognition of this kind exists that is independent of experience and even of all the impressions of the senses. **Such** ***cognitions*** are termed _a priori_ and are distinguished from ***empirical*** ones, whose sources lie _a posteriori_, that is, in experience.

That expression, however, is still not definite enough to indicate appropriately the full sense of the question posed here. For of many a cognition drawn from sources in experience one is accustomed to say that we are capable of it, or share in it, _a priori_, since we derive it not directly from experience but from a general rule — a rule which, all the same, we have ourselves taken over from experience. Thus of a man who dug away the foundation of his house one says: he could know _a priori_ that it would cave in, i.e., he had no need to wait for the experience of its actually caving in. Yet wholly _a priori_ he could not know this either. For that bodies have weight, and accordingly fall once their support is removed, had first to be made known to him by experience.

In what follows, then, we shall mean by cognitions _a priori_ not those {{ 3 }} that take place independently of this or that experience, but those that take place independently of absolutely all experience. Set over against them are empirical cognitions, or those possible only _a posteriori_, that is, through experience. Among the cognitions _a priori_, however, those are termed ***pure*** to which nothing empirical whatsoever is admixed. Thus, for example, the proposition: every alteration has its cause, is a proposition _a priori_, though not a pure one, since alteration is a concept that can be obtained only from experience.
