## First Book. Of the Concepts of Pure Reason

Whatever may be the case regarding the possibility of concepts from pure reason: they are nevertheless not merely reflected, but inferred concepts. Concepts of the understanding are also thought _a priori_, before {{ 367 }} experience and for the sake of the same; but they contain nothing further than the unity of reflection on appearances, insofar as they are necessarily to belong to a possible empirical consciousness. Through them alone is cognition and determination of an object possible. They therefore first provide the material for inferring, and no concepts _a priori_ of objects precede them, from which they could be inferred. On the other hand, their objective reality nevertheless rests solely upon this: that, because they constitute the intellectual form of all experience, their application must always be capable of being shown in experience.

The designation of a concept of reason, however, already indicates provisionally that it does not wish to be limited within experience, because it concerns a cognition of which every empirical cognition is only a part (perhaps the whole of possible experience or of its empirical synthesis), to which, indeed, no actual experience is ever completely sufficient, but which nevertheless always belongs to it. Concepts of reason serve for ***comprehending***, just as concepts of the understanding serve for ***understanding*** (of perceptions). If they contain the unconditioned, they concern something under which all experience belongs, but which itself is never an object of experience: something to which reason leads in its inferences from experience, and according to which it estimates and {{ 368 }} {{{ 245 }}} measures the degree of its empirical use, but which never constitutes a member of the empirical synthesis. If such concepts nevertheless possess objective validity, they can be called _conceptus ratiocinati_ (rightly inferred concepts); if not, they are at least surreptitiously obtained through an illusion of inference, and may be called _conceptus ratiocinantes_ (ratiocinating concepts). Since, however, this can only be decided in the chapter on the dialectical inferences of pure reason, we cannot yet take it into consideration, but will provisionally, just as we called the pure concepts of the understanding categories, designate the concepts of pure reason by a new name and call them transcendental ideas, and now explain and justify this designation.
