## Section 2. Of transcendental ideas

The transcendental analytic gave us an example of how the mere logical form of our cognition can contain the origin of pure concepts _a priori_, which represent objects prior to all experience, {{ 378 }} or rather indicate the synthetic unity that alone makes an empirical cognition of objects possible. The form of judgments (converted into a concept of the synthesis of intuitions) produced categories that guide all use of the understanding in experience. Just so we can expect that the form of syllogisms, if we apply it to the synthetic unity of intuitions in accordance with the categories, will contain the origin of special concepts _a priori_ that we can call pure concepts of reason or ***transcendental ideas***, and which will determine the use of the understanding in the whole of entire experience according to principles.

The function of reason in its inferences consisted in the universality of cognition according to concepts, and the syllogism itself {{{ 251 }}} is a judgment which is determined _a priori_ in the entire scope of its condition. The proposition: Cajus is mortal, I could also draw from experience merely through the understanding. But I seek a concept that contains the condition under which the predicate (assertion in general) of this judgment is given (i.e., here, the concept of man), and after I have subsumed under this condition, taken in its entire scope (all men are mortal): I then determine the cognition of my object accordingly (Cajus is mortal).

Accordingly, in the conclusion of a syllogism {{ 379 }} we restrict a predicate to a certain object, after we have previously thought it in the major premise in its entire scope under a certain condition. This completed magnitude of the scope in relation to such a condition is called ***universality*** (_Universalitas_). To this corresponds, in the synthesis of intuitions, ***allness*** (_Universitas_) or the ***totality of conditions***. Thus, the transcendental concept of reason is no other than that of the ***totality*** of conditions for a given conditioned. Now, since the ***unconditioned*** alone makes the totality of conditions possible, and conversely the totality of conditions is at all times itself unconditioned: a pure concept of reason in general can be defined by the concept of the unconditioned, insofar as it contains a ground of the synthesis of the conditioned.

As many kinds of relation as there are which the understanding represents to itself by means of the categories, so many pure concepts of reason will there also be; and we shall therefore have to seek, first, an ***unconditioned*** of the ***categorical*** synthesis in a ***subject***; ***secondly***, of the ***hypothetical*** synthesis of the members of a ***series***; ***thirdly***, of the ***disjunctive*** synthesis of the parts in a system.

For there are just as many kinds of syllogisms, each of which proceeds through prosyllogisms to the unconditioned: the first to a subject that is itself no longer a predicate; the second to a presupposition {{ 380 }} that presupposes nothing further; and the third to an aggregate of the members of a division, to which nothing further is required in order to complete the division of a concept. Hence, the pure concepts of reason regarding the totality in the synthesis of conditions are necessary and grounded in the nature of human {{{ 252 }}} reason, at least as problems, in order to extend the unity of the understanding, where possible, to the unconditioned; even though, moreover, these transcendental concepts may lack an use appropriate to them _in concreto_, and they may consequently have no other utility than to direct the understanding into a course in which its use, while being extended to the utmost, is at the same time made thoroughly consistent with itself.

But in speaking here of the totality of conditions and of the unconditioned as the common title of all concepts of reason, we again meet with an expression which we cannot dispense with, and yet, because of an ambiguity attached to it by long misuse, we cannot use with safety. The word ***absolute*** is one of the few words which, in their original meaning, were adapted to a concept to which subsequently no other word of the very same language precisely fits, and the loss of which, or, what {{ 381 }} is the same thing, its fluctuating use, must therefore also entail the loss of the concept itself—and indeed of a concept which, because it occupies reason so much, cannot be dispensed with without great detriment to all transcendental judgments. The word ***absolute*** is now frequently used to indicate merely that something is valid of a thing considered ***in itself***, and therefore ***internally***. In this sense, ***absolutely possible*** would mean that which is possible in itself (_interne_), which is indeed the ***least*** that can be said of an object. On the other hand, it is also sometimes used to indicate that something is valid in every relation (unrestricted) (e.g., absolute rule), and in this sense ***absolutely possible*** would mean that which is ***possible in every respect, in every relation***, which again is the most that I can say of the possibility of a thing. Now, it is true that these meanings sometimes coincide. Thus, for example, what is internally impossible is also impossible in every relation, and therefore absolutely impossible. But in most cases they are infinitely far apart, and I can by no means infer that because something is possible in itself, it is therefore also possible in every relation, and consequently absolutely possible. Indeed, of absolute necessity I shall show in the sequel that it by no means depends in all cases on internal necessity, and must therefore not be regarded as synonymous with {{ 382 }} it. That whose opposite is internally impossible, its opposite is of course also impossible in every respect, and therefore it is itself absolutely necessary; but I cannot conversely infer that what {{{ 253 }}} is absolutely necessary, its opposite is ***internally impossible***, i.e., that the ***absolute necessity*** of things is an ***internal necessity***; for this ***internal necessity*** is in certain cases a completely empty expression with which we cannot connect the least concept, whereas that of the necessity of a thing in every relation (to everything possible) carries with it very special determinations. Since, then, the loss of a concept of great application in speculative philosophy can never be indifferent to the philosopher, I hope that the determination and careful preservation of the expression on which the concept depends will also not be indifferent to him.

In this wider sense, I shall therefore employ the word ***absolute***, and oppose it to what is valid merely comparatively, or in a particular respect; for the latter is restricted to conditions, whereas the former is valid without restriction.

Now, the transcendental concept of reason is at all times directed only to the absolute totality in the synthesis of conditions, and never terminates except in the absolutely, i.e., in every respect, unconditioned. For {{ 383 }} pure reason leaves everything to the understanding, which relates first to the objects of intuition or rather to their synthesis in the imagination. The former reserves to itself solely the absolute totality in the use of the concepts of the understanding, and seeks to carry the synthetic unity, which is thought in the category, up to the absolutely unconditioned. We can therefore call the former the ***unity of reason*** of appearances, just as the latter, which the category expresses, may be called ***unity of the understanding***. Accordingly, reason relates itself only to the use of the understanding, and this indeed not insofar as the latter contains the ground of possible experience (for the absolute totality of conditions is not a concept usable in experience, since no experience is unconditioned), but in order to prescribe to it the direction toward a certain unity of which the understanding has no concept, and which aims at comprehending all acts of the understanding, in respect of every object, into an ***absolute whole***. Therefore, the objective use of the pure concepts of reason is at all times ***transcendent***, while that of the pure concepts of the understanding must by its nature at all times be ***immanent***, insofar as it restricts itself solely to possible experience.

{{{ 254 }}} I understand by idea a necessary concept of reason for which no congruent object can be given in the senses. Consequently, our pure concepts of reason which we have just been considering are ***transcendental ideas***. {{ 384 }} They are concepts of pure reason; for they regard all empirical cognition as determined by an absolute totality of conditions. They are not arbitrarily invented, but are posed by the nature of reason itself, and therefore relate of necessity to the entire use of the understanding. They are, finally, transcendent and overstep the limits of all experience, in which, therefore, no object can ever appear that is adequate to the transcendental idea. When we speak of an idea, we say a great deal as regards the object (as an object of the pure understanding), but very little as regards the subject (i.e., in respect of its reality under empirical conditions), for the very reason that, as the concept of a maximum, it can never be congruently given _in concreto_. Since, now, the latter is properly the whole aim in the merely speculative use of reason, and since the approximation to a concept which, however, is never actually reached in practice, is the same as if the concept were completely missed, it is said of such a concept: it is ***only an idea***. Thus one might say: the absolute whole of all appearances is ***only an idea***, for since we can never project a representation of it in an image, it remains a problem without any solution. On the other hand, because in the practical use of the understanding we are concerned solely with the {{ 385 }} execution according to rules, the idea of practical reason can at all times be actually given _in concreto_, though only in part, and indeed it is the indispensable condition of every practical use of reason. Its execution is at all times limited and defective, but within indeterminable limits, and thus always under the influence of the concept of an absolute completeness. Consequently, the practical idea is at all times highly fruitful and, in respect of actual actions, indispensably necessary. In it pure reason even possesses causality actually to produce what its concept contains; hence one cannot speak of wisdom, as it were, disparagingly, saying: it is ***only an idea***; but rather, precisely because it is the idea of the necessary unity of all possible ends, it must serve as a rule for everything practical, as an original, or at least a limiting, condition.

{{{ 255 }}} Although we must say of the transcendental concepts of reason: ***they are only ideas***, we shall yet by no means have to regard them as superfluous and empty. For even if no object can be determined by them, they can still, at bottom and unnoticed, serve the understanding as a canon of its extended and consistent use, whereby it indeed cognizes no more objects than it would cognize according to its concepts, yet is better and further guided {{ 386 }} in this cognition. Not to mention that they perhaps make possible a transition from the concepts of nature to the practical, and can in this way provide the moral ideas themselves with support and connection with the speculative cognitions of reason. On all of this, we must await explanation in the sequel.

In accordance with our purpose, however, we set the practical ideas aside here, and therefore consider reason only in its speculative, and in this even more narrowly, namely only in its transcendental use. Here we must now enter upon the same path that we took above in the deduction of the categories: namely, to consider the logical form of the cognition of reason and see whether reason does not thereby also become a source of concepts, so as to regard objects in themselves as synthetically determined _a priori_ in respect of one or another function of reason.

Reason, considered as the faculty of a certain logical form of cognition, is the faculty of inferring, i.e., of judging mediately (by the subsumption of the condition of a possible judgment under the condition of a given one). The given judgment is the universal rule (major premise, _major_). The subsumption of the condition of another possible judgment under the condition of the rule is the minor premise (_minor_). The actual judgment, which expresses the assertion of the rule {{ 387 }} ***in the subsumed case***, is the conclusion (_conclusio_). The rule indeed says something universally under a certain condition. Now, in an occurring case, the condition of the rule takes place. Thus, what was universally valid under that condition is also regarded as valid in the occurring case (which carries this condition with it). One easily sees that reason, through acts of the understanding which constitute a series of conditions, arrives at a cognition. If I arrive at the proposition: all bodies are alterable, only by {{{ 256 }}} starting from the more remote cognition (in which the concept of body does not yet occur, but which nevertheless contains the condition of it): everything composite is ***alterable***; passing from this to a closer one that stands under the condition of the former: bodies are composite; and from this only arriving at a third, which now links the remote cognition (alterable) with the one at hand: consequently, bodies are alterable—then I have arrived at a cognition (conclusion) through a series of conditions (premises). Now, any series whose exponent (of the categorical or hypothetical judgment) is given can be continued; consequently, the very same act of reason leads to the _ratiocinatio polysyllogistica_, which is a series of syllogisms that can be continued to indefinite lengths, either on the side of conditions (_per prosyllogismos_) {{ 388 }} or on that of the conditioned (_per episyllogismos_).

But one soon becomes aware that the chain or series of prosyllogisms, i.e., of inferred cognitions on the side of grounds or of conditions for a given cognition—in other words, the ***ascending series*** of syllogisms—must relate itself to the faculty of reason differently from the ***descending series***, i.e., the progress of reason on the side of the conditioned through episyllogisms. For since in the former case the cognition (_conclusio_) is given only as conditioned, one cannot arrive at it by means of reason except at least under the presupposition that all members of the series on the side of conditions are given (totality in the series of premises), because only under this presupposition is the judgment at hand possible _a priori_; whereas, on the side of the conditioned or of consequences, only a ***becoming*** series, and not one already completely presupposed or ***given***—consequently, only a potential progress—is thought. Hence, when a cognition is regarded as conditioned, reason is constrained to regard the series of conditions in the ascending line as completed and as given in its totality. But if {{ 389 }} the very same cognition is at the same time regarded as a condition of other cognitions which among themselves constitute a series of consequences in a descending line, reason can be completely indifferent as to how far this progress extends _a parte posteriori_, and whether a totality of this series is possible at all; because it does not need such a series for the conclusion lying before it, since this conclusion is already sufficiently determined and secured by its grounds {{{ 257 }}} _a parte priori_. It may be that, on the side of conditions, the series of premises has a ***first*** member as the supreme condition, or has none, and is therefore without limits _a parte priori_; yet it must nevertheless contain the totality of the condition, even granted that we could never succeed in grasping it; and the entire series must be unconditionally true if the conditioned, which is regarded as a consequence arising from it, is to count as true. This is a requirement of reason, which announces its cognition as determined _a priori_ and as necessary: either in itself, and then it requires no grounds, or, if it is derived, as a member of a series of grounds which is itself unconditionally true.
