## Section 2. On transcendental ideas

In the transcendental analytic we were shown, by way of illustration, how the merely logical form belonging to our cognition is able to harbor the source of pure _a priori_ concepts—concepts that present objects in advance of any experience, {{ 378 }} or that rather flag the synthetic unity through which alone an empirical cognition of objects becomes possible at all. Once the form of judgments had been transformed into a concept of the synthesis of intuitions, it yielded up the categories, which govern every employment of the understanding within experience. By the same token, we have reason to anticipate that the form of the inferences of reason—applied to that synthetic unity of intuitions in conformity with the categories—will likewise harbor the source of distinctive _a priori_ concepts; these we are entitled to name pure concepts of reason, or ***transcendental ideas***, and it is they that will fix, in accordance with principles, the employment of the understanding across experience regarded in its entirety.

What reason accomplishes in drawing its inferences lay in rendering cognition universal by way of concepts, and a syllogism, taken on its own, {{{ 251 }}} is a judgment determined _a priori_ across the full extent of its condition. Take the proposition, Cajus is mortal: this I might just as well draw straight from experience by means of the understanding alone. Yet what I am after is a concept holding within it the condition on which the predicate of this judgment (its assertion, generally speaking) rests (that is, in the present instance, the concept of the human being); and having subsumed under that condition, taken across its entire extent (all human beings are mortal), I then determine, on that basis, the cognition of my object (Cajus is mortal).

And so it is that, in the conclusion of a syllogism, {{ 379 }} we confine a predicate to some particular object, having earlier entertained it in the major premise across its whole extent and under a certain condition. Such a completed magnitude of extent, referred to a condition of this kind, we term ***universality*** (_Universalitas_). Its counterpart, over on the side of the synthesis of intuitions, is ***allness*** (_Universitas_), that is, the ***totality of conditions***. Consequently, the transcendental concept of reason turns out to be none but the concept of the ***totality*** of conditions belonging to some given conditioned thing. Since it is the ***unconditioned*** that alone renders any totality of conditions possible, while conversely a totality of conditions is at every point already unconditioned in itself: it follows that a pure concept of reason as such admits of explication by means of the concept of the unconditioned, provided this concept carries within it a ground for synthesizing the conditioned.

However many sorts of relation there are that the understanding pictures to itself through the categories, that same number of pure concepts of reason will there prove to be as well; and so we shall have to look, first, for an ***unconditioned*** belonging to the ***categorical*** synthesis within a ***subject***, ***secondly***, to the ***hypothetical*** synthesis of the members making up a ***series***, and ***thirdly***, to the ***disjunctive*** synthesis of the parts within a system.

For there exist correspondingly many species of syllogism, each of them pressing on through prosyllogisms toward the unconditioned: one toward a subject that is itself no longer a predicate at all, a second toward a presupposition {{ 380 }} presupposing nothing beyond itself, and a third toward an aggregate of the members of a division such that nothing further is called for to bring the division of a concept to completion. This is why the pure concepts of reason bearing on totality in the synthesis of conditions are indispensable—if only as problems set for us, whereby the understanding's unity is to be prolonged, so far as may be, right up to the unconditioned—and why they take root in the very nature of human {{{ 252 }}} reason; granted, these transcendental concepts may for the rest want any use befitting them _in concreto_, so that their sole utility consists in steering the understanding in a direction wherein its own employment, stretched to the very limit, is at once brought into thoroughgoing accord with itself.

But in speaking here of a totality of conditions, and of the unconditioned, under a heading shared by every concept of reason, we run up once more against a term that we can ill spare and yet, on account of an ambiguity that long abuse has fastened onto it, cannot deploy with confidence. The word ***absolute*** ranks among the handful of terms that in their primordial sense came to fit a concept so precisely that no other word of the very same language afterward suits it exactly—and whose loss, or what {{ 381 }} comes to the same thing, whose unsteady use, must on that account drag along with it the forfeiting of the concept itself, a concept, moreover, that, since it exercises reason to no small degree, can hardly be given up without serious damage to every transcendental appraisal. Nowadays the word ***absolute*** frequently serves simply to signal that something obtains of a thing taken ***in itself*** and therefore ***internally***. On that reading, ***absolutely possible*** would designate whatever is possible in its own right (_interne_)—which is, in point of fact, the ***least*** that can be affirmed of any object. Now and then, by contrast, it is put to work to signal that something holds good under every relation (without restriction)—absolute sovereignty, say—and on this reading ***absolutely possible*** would pick out whatever is ***possible in every intention, in every relation***, and this, in turn, is the utmost I am in a position to assert regarding the possibility of any thing. Granted, these two senses do on occasion converge. Whatever is internally impossible, to take one case, will likewise be impossible under every relation and therefore impossible in the absolute sense. For the most part, though, they stand infinitely far apart, and there is no route by which I might infer that a thing's being possible in itself makes it, for that reason, possible under every relation and so possible absolutely. Regarding absolute necessity, indeed, I shall establish further on that it by no means depends in all cases upon inner necessity, and must accordingly not be treated as saying the same {{ 382 }} thing as the latter. Where a thing's opposite is internally impossible, that opposite is of course impossible under every intention as well, so that the thing is itself necessary absolutely; but the converse inference is barred to me—that whatever is {{{ 253 }}} necessary absolutely has an opposite that is ***internally impossible***, that is, that the ***absolute necessity*** of things amounts to an ***inner necessity***; for such ***inner necessity*** is, in certain instances, an utterly hollow phrase to which we can attach not the slightest concept, whereas the necessity of a thing under every relation (with respect to all that is possible) brings along with it determinations of a quite distinctive kind. And since a philosopher can never look with indifference upon the loss of a concept of broad application within speculative philosophy, my hope is that he will likewise not regard with indifference the fixing and the careful safekeeping of the term to which that concept clings.

It is in this enlarged sense, accordingly, that I shall avail myself of the word ***absolute***, setting it over against what holds only comparatively or under some special consideration; for the latter stands restricted to conditions, whereas the former is valid with no restriction whatever.

The transcendental concept of reason, then, is invariably directed at nothing but the absolute totality in the synthesis of conditions, and it comes to rest nowhere save at the unconditioned in the strict sense—unconditioned, that is, under every relation. For {{ 383 }} pure reason hands everything over to the understanding, which in the first place bears upon the objects of intuition, or rather upon their synthesis within the imagination. To itself alone reason keeps back the absolute totality in the employment of the concepts of the understanding, striving to carry the synthetic unity that gets thought in the category all the way out to the strictly unconditioned. The former, accordingly, may be styled the ***unity of reason*** among appearances, much as the latter, the one that the category articulates, may be styled the ***unity of the understanding***. And so reason bears upon the employment of the understanding and upon nothing else—yet not insofar as this employment holds the ground of a possible experience (an absolute totality of conditions being no concept of any use within experience, seeing that no experience is ever unconditioned), but rather so as to lay down for it a bearing toward a certain unity that lies beyond any concept the understanding possesses, a unity that comes down to drawing together all the acts of the understanding, in regard to each and every object, into one ***absolute whole***. For this reason the objective employment of the pure concepts of reason remains at all times ***transcendent***, while that of the pure concepts of the understanding, being what it is by nature, must at all times stay ***immanent***, confined as it is to possible experience alone.

{{{ 254 }}} What I mean by an idea is a necessary concept of reason for which the senses can furnish no congruent object. The pure concepts of reason we have just been weighing are, therefore, ***transcendental ideas***. {{ 384 }} They count as concepts of pure reason; for they view all cognition drawn from experience as determined by way of an absolute totality of conditions. Far from being fabricated at will, they are laid upon us as a charge by the very nature of reason, and for that reason bear of necessity upon the whole employment of the understanding. They are, in the last place, transcendent, overshooting the bounds of all experience, within which, then, no object can ever turn up that would be adequate to the transcendental idea. To call something an idea is to claim, as measured by the object (as of an object of the pure understanding), a great deal, yet, as measured by the subject (in regard, that is, to its actuality under an empirical condition), on precisely that ground very little, since a maximum, of which it is the concept, can never be delivered congruently _in concreto_. And since it is this latter that, in the merely speculative employment of reason, really constitutes the entire aim—and since drawing near to a concept never in fact attained in the exercise counts for just as much as missing that concept altogether—one says of a concept of this sort: it is ***nothing but an idea***. One might put it thus: the absolute whole of all appearances is ***nothing but an idea***, for since we are never able to sketch its like in an image, it stays a problem admitting of no solution at all. By contrast, since in the practical employment of the understanding everything turns solely upon {{ 385 }} the carrying of rules into practice, the idea of practical reason can at all times be genuinely delivered, if only in part, _in concreto_—indeed, it is the condition, not to be dispensed with, of every practical employment of reason. The exercise of it is at every point bounded and deficient, yet the bounds themselves elude determination, so that it always stands under the sway of the concept of a completeness that is absolute. The practical idea, on this showing, is at every point supremely fruitful and, where actual actions are concerned, necessary past all avoidance. Within it pure reason possesses even a causality for actually bringing forth what its concept holds; and so of wisdom one may not, in a manner of speaking, say slightingly that it is ***nothing but an idea***—rather, on the very ground that it is the idea of the necessary unity of all possible ends, it has to serve as the rule for everything practical, as an original condition, or at the least a limiting one.

{{{ 255 }}} However true it may be that of the transcendental concepts of reason we are bound to say ***they are nothing but ideas***, we are in no way therefore to look upon them as idle and void. For even granting that no object gets determined through them, they can still, deep down and without being noticed, serve the understanding as a canon for the extended and concordant employment of itself—whereby it comes to cognize, true enough, no object over and above what its concepts would already let it cognize, and yet finds itself, within that very cognition, guided further and better {{ 386 }}. To say nothing of how they may perhaps open up a passage leading from the concepts of nature over to the practical ones, and in just this way secure for the moral ideas themselves a footing, together with a linkage to reason's speculative cognitions. Clarification on all of this we must look for as the inquiry proceeds.

As our purpose requires, however, we here lay the practical ideas to one side and so take reason up only in its speculative bearing, and, narrower still within that, only in its transcendental employment. The very road we struck out on earlier, in deducing the categories, we must now strike out on again: namely, to weigh up the logical form of rational cognition and to find out whether reason might not, by that route, itself become a wellspring of concepts under which objects, taken in themselves, come to be regarded as determined synthetically _a priori_ in respect of one function of reason or another.

Reason, viewed as the capacity answering to a certain logical form of cognition, is the capacity for drawing inferences—that is, for judging in a mediated fashion (by subsuming the condition of some possible judgment beneath the condition of a judgment already given). This given judgment supplies the universal rule (the premise stated first, _major_). Placing the condition of some further possible judgment beneath the condition of the rule yields the premise stated below (_minor_). The actual judgment, which pronounces the rule's assertion {{ 387 }} ***in the subsumed case***, is the closing proposition (_conclusio_). For the rule asserts something in a universal way under a certain condition. Now, in a case that comes up, the rule's condition is met. And so whatever held universally under that condition is taken to hold in the case that comes up as well (the case carrying this condition along with it). It is readily seen that reason reaches a cognition by way of acts of the understanding which together make up a series of conditions. Suppose I reach the proposition all bodies are alterable solely by {{{ 256 }}} setting out from the more distant cognition (one in which the concept of body has yet to appear, though it does hold the condition of that concept): everything composite is ***alterable***; then move on from this to a nearer cognition, standing under the condition of the first: bodies are composite; and from this, only then, on to a third, which at last ties the distant cognition (alterable) to the one now before me: bodies, consequently, are alterable: in that case I have made my way, through a series of conditions (premises), to a cognition (conclusion). Now any series whatever whose exponent (that of the categorical or hypothetical judgment) is given admits of being carried on; and thus the selfsame act of reason issues in the _ratiocinatio polysyllogistica_, which is a chain of inferences extendable into indeterminate reaches either on the side of the conditions (_per prosyllogismos_) {{ 388 }} or on that of the conditioned (_per episyllogismos_).

One soon comes to notice, however, that the chain, or series, of prosyllogisms—that is, of cognitions inferred over on the side of the grounds or the conditions for a given cognition, or, to phrase it otherwise, the ***ascending series*** of syllogisms—must after all stand in another relation to the faculty of reason than does the ***descending series***, i.e. reason's advance over on the side of the conditioned by way of episyllogisms. For seeing that, in the first case, the cognition (_conclusio_) is given merely as conditioned, one can arrive at it by means of reason in no other way than on the assumption, at the very least, that all the members of the series on the side of the conditions are given (a totality in the series of premises)—only on that assumption being the judgment at hand possible _a priori_; whereas on the side of the conditioned, or of the consequences, what gets thought is a series merely ***coming to be***, not one already presupposed in full or ***given***, and hence no more than a progression held in potential. For this reason, once a cognition is looked upon as conditioned, reason finds itself constrained to look upon the series of conditions along the ascending line as brought to completion and as given in respect of its totality. But if this {{ 389 }} very cognition is at the same time looked upon as the condition of other cognitions, which among themselves compose a series of consequences along the descending line, then it may be all one to reason how far this advance reaches out _a parte posteriori_, and whether a totality of the series is so much as possible anywhere; for reason has no need of such a series in reaching the conclusion set before it, that conclusion being already, through its grounds, {{{ 257 }}} sufficiently determined and secured _a parte priori_. It may well be that, on the side of the conditions, the series of premises does possess a ***first*** as its supreme condition, or that it does not and so runs on _a parte priori_ without any limit; even so, it has to comprise a totality of the condition, granting that we could never manage to lay hold of it; and the whole series has to be true without condition if that conditioned thing which is viewed as a consequence springing from it is to pass for true. This is a demand made by reason, which proclaims its cognition to be determined _a priori_ and to be necessary: whether in its own right, in which case no grounds are wanted, or, should it be derived, then as one link within a chain of grounds that is itself true in an unconditioned manner.
