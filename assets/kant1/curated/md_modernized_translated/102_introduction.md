## Introduction

{{{ 465 }}} {{ 735 }} If I regard the sum total of all cognition of pure and speculative reason as a building for which we have at least the idea in ourselves, then I can say that in the transcendental doctrine of elements we have made an estimate of the building materials and determined for what sort of building, of what height and sturdiness, they would suffice. To be sure, we found that although we had a tower in mind that was to reach to the heavens, the supply of materials was sufficient only for a dwelling house that was just roomy enough for our business on the plane of experience and high enough to survey it; but that that bold undertaking had to fail for lack of materials, quite apart from the confusion of languages that inevitably divided the laborers over the plan and dispersed them all over the world, each to build separately according to his own design. Now we are concerned not so much with the materials as with the plan, and since we are warned not to venture on a random, blind project that might exceed our entire capacity, yet we cannot well desist from the erection of a secure dwelling, we must draw up the estimate for a building in relation to the supply that is given to us and at the same time is appropriate to our needs.

By a transcendental doctrine of method, therefore, I understand the {{ 736 }} determination of the formal conditions of a complete system of pure reason. In this design we will have to do with a ***discipline***, a ***canon***, an ***architectonic***, and finally a ***history*** of pure reason, and, in a transcendental design, we will accomplish that which is sought in the schools under the name of a ***practical logic*** in respect of the use of the understanding in general, but is poorly {{{ 466 }}} accomplished, because general logic, not being restricted to any particular kind of cognition of the understanding (e.g., not to pure cognition) nor to certain objects, can do nothing more than present titles of ***possible methods*** and technical expressions of which one makes use in respect of the systematic in all kinds of sciences, without borrowing acquaintance from other sciences, which only makes the apprentice acquainted beforehand with names whose meaning and use he will first learn only in the future.
