## Introduction

{{{ 465 }}} {{ 735 }} If I view the entire stock of cognition belonging to pure and speculative reason as a building, of which we do at least carry the idea within ourselves, then I can say this: in the transcendental Doctrine of Elements we took stock of the building materials and settled for what sort of edifice, and of what height and solidity, they would be sufficient. It turned out, admittedly, that although we had had a tower in view, one designed to reach up to heaven, the stock of materials was only enough for a dwelling-house — one just roomy and high enough for our affairs on the plane of experience to take them in; but that this bold venture was destined to fail for want of material, and this without even reckoning the confusion of tongues that could not but divide the workmen over the plan and disperse them throughout the world, each to build separately, following his own draft. Now what matters to us is not so much the materials as, rather, the plan itself, and though we have been cautioned against risking any arbitrary, blind draft that might well surpass our entire means, yet, being unable properly to abstain from raising a secure place of residence, we must frame our estimate for a building in keeping with the stock supplied to us and, at once, matched to what we require.

By the transcendental Doctrine of Method, then, I mean the {{ 736 }} determination of the formal conditions of a complete system of pure reason. For this purpose we shall be occupied with a ***discipline***, a ***canon***, an ***architectonic***, and lastly a ***history*** of pure reason, and shall achieve, from a transcendental standpoint, what has been pursued in the schools under the title of a ***practical logic*** concerning the employment of the understanding as such, though poorly {{{ 466 }}} achieved, because, since general logic is not confined to any special sort of cognition of the understanding (not, for instance, to pure cognition), nor to any particular objects, it can, without drawing on knowledge borrowed from other sciences, do nothing more than put forward headings for ***possible methods*** together with technical terms of the kind used, in matters of systematic arrangement, across all manner of sciences — terms that make the names known to the apprentice beforehand, whose sense and use he is destined only afterwards to learn.
