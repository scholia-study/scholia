## Discovery and explanation of the dialectical illusion in all transcendental proofs of a necessary being's existence.

The two proofs conducted up to this point were both attempted transcendentally, that is, independently of empirical principles. For although the cosmological one takes an experience in general as its basis, it is nonetheless carried out not from any particular constitution of that experience but from pure principles of reason in relation to an existence {{ 643 }} furnished through empirical consciousness as such, and it goes so far as to forsake this guidance in order to rest on nothing but pure concepts. In these transcendental proofs, then, what gives rise to the dialectical—yet natural—illusion that ties together the concepts of necessity and of highest reality, and that turns into a real, hypostatized entity something which can, after all, be no more than an idea? What is the cause of the inevitability of assuming something among existing things to be necessary in itself and yet at the same time of recoiling from the existence of such a being as from an abyss; and how does one manage it so that reason may come to understand itself on this point and pass from the wavering condition of a timid assent, forever taken back again, to a settled insight?

It is something exceedingly remarkable that, once one presupposes that something exists, one cannot evade the inference that something also exists necessarily. The cosmological argument rested on this wholly natural (though not therefore reliable) inference. {{{ 411 }}} By contrast, whatever concept of a thing I may adopt, I discover that I can never represent its existence to myself as utterly necessary, and that nothing stops me—let whatever will exist—from thinking the non-being of that thing; hence, while I must indeed assume something necessary for the existing in general, I can nonetheless think no single thing as necessary in itself. That {{ 644 }} is to say: I can never ***complete*** the regress to the conditions of existing without assuming a necessary being; yet I can never ***begin*** from such a being.

If, for existing things in general, I am obliged to think something necessary, yet am not entitled to think any thing as necessary in itself, then it follows inevitably that necessity and contingency cannot pertain to and bear upon the things themselves, since otherwise a contradiction would arise; and hence that neither of these two principles is objective, but that they can at most be merely subjective principles of reason—namely, on the one hand, to seek for everything given as existing something that is necessary, that is, never to stop anywhere except at an explanation completed _a priori_, and, on the other hand, never to hope for this completion, that is, to accept nothing empirical as unconditioned and thereby to exempt oneself from any further derivation. In this sense both principles, as merely heuristic and ***regulative***, attending to nothing but the formal interest of reason, can perfectly well coexist. For the one says: you shall philosophize about nature ***as if*** there were, for everything belonging to existence, a necessary first ground, for the sole purpose of introducing systematic unity into your cognition, and this by pursuing such an idea, namely an imagined supreme ground; the other, however, warns you to assume no single {{ 645 }} determination concerning the existence of things as such a supreme ground, that is, as absolutely necessary, but rather to keep the path to further derivation always open and therefore to treat it at all times as still conditioned. But if everything perceived in things must be regarded by us as conditionally necessary, then no thing (whatever may be given empirically) can be viewed as absolutely necessary either.

It follows from this, however, that you must assume the absolutely necessary ***outside the world***: because it is meant to function solely as a principle securing the greatest attainable unity among appearances, in the role of their supreme ground, {{{ 412 }}} and because ***within the world*** you can never arrive at it, since the second rule commands you to regard all empirical causes of unity as at all times derived.

The philosophers of antiquity regarded all form of nature as contingent, but matter, according to the judgment of common reason, as original and necessary. Had they, however, considered matter not relatively, as the substratum of appearances, but ***in itself*** with respect to its existence, the idea of absolute necessity would at once have vanished. For nothing whatever binds reason unconditionally to this existence; rather, it can at any time and without contradiction cancel it in thought—and in thought alone did the absolute necessity reside as well. {{ 646 }} So a certain regulative principle must have underlain this persuasion. Indeed, extension and impenetrability (which together make up the concept of matter) are the supreme empirical principle of the unity of appearances and, insofar as this principle is empirically unconditioned, have in themselves a property of the regulative principle. Nevertheless, since every determination of matter that constitutes its real element, and hence impenetrability too, is an effect (an action) that must have its cause and is therefore always still derived, matter is nonetheless ill-fitted for the idea of a necessary being taken as a principle of all derived unity: for each of its real properties, being derived, is only conditionally necessary and can thus be cancelled in itself, whereby, however, the entire existence of matter would be cancelled, whereas, were this not to occur, we should have attained the highest ground of unity by empirical means—something the second regulative principle prohibits. It follows, then, that matter and, more generally, everything belonging to the world does not fit the idea of a necessary primordial being conceived as a mere principle of the greatest empirical unity, but that this being has instead to be posited outside the world; for we may then, with untroubled confidence, trace the world's appearances and their existence back to other appearances, just as if no necessary being existed at all, while still striving without cease toward the completeness of that derivation, just as if such a being were presupposed to be a supreme {{ 647 }} ground.

In view of these considerations, the ideal of the highest being is nothing other than a ***regulative*** principle of reason for viewing every connection within the world ***as if it*** sprang from an all-sufficient necessary {{{ 413 }}} cause, and for grounding on that basis the rule of a systematic unity, necessary according to universal laws, in the explanation of that connection; and it is not an assertion of an existence necessary in itself. But it is at the same time unavoidable to represent this formal principle, by means of a transcendental subreption, as constitutive, and to think this unity hypostatically. For just as space, because it originally makes possible all shapes, which are merely different limitations of it, is—although it is only a principle of sensibility—nonetheless taken for that very reason to be an absolutely necessary something subsisting on its own and an object given _a priori_ in itself: so it likewise comes about quite naturally that, since the systematic unity of nature can in no way be set up as a principle of the empirical use of our reason except insofar as we ground it on the idea of a most real being serving as the highest cause—whereby this idea gets represented as an actual object, and that object in its turn, being the supreme condition, gets represented as necessary, so that a ***regulative*** principle is converted into a ***constitutive*** one; which {{ 648 }} substitution reveals itself thus: once I go on to consider this supreme being—which, relative to the world, was absolutely (unconditionally) necessary—as a thing on its own, this necessity is capable of no concept and hence could only have been encountered in my reason as a formal condition of thought, not as a material and hypostatic condition of existence.
