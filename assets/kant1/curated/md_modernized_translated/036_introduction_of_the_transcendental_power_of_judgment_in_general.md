## Introduction. Of the transcendental power of judgment in general

If the understanding in general is explained as the faculty of rules, then the power of judgment is the faculty of ***subsuming*** under rules, i.e., of distinguishing whether something stands under a given rule (_casus datae legis_) or not. General logic contains no instructions at all for the power of judgment, and indeed cannot contain them. For ***since it abstracts from all content of cognition***: nothing remains to it but the business of analytically dissecting the mere form of cognition in concepts, judgments, and {{ 172 }} inferences, and thereby bringing about formal rules for all use of the understanding. If it wanted to show generally how one is to subsume under these rules, i.e., distinguish whether something stands under them or not, this could not happen otherwise than through a rule once again. But this latter, precisely because it is a rule, requires once again an instruction for the power of judgment; and thus it is shown that although the understanding is capable of being instructed and equipped by rules, the power of judgment is a particular talent that cannot be instructed at all, but wants only to be practiced. Hence this is also the specific character of so-called mother-wit, the lack of which no school can replace; for although a school can provide and, as it were, graft onto a limited understanding plenty of rules borrowed from foreign insight: yet the faculty of making correct use of them must belong to the apprentice himself, and no rule that one {{{ 132 }}} might wish to prescribe to him for this purpose is, in the absence of such a {{ 173 }} natural gift, safe from misuse.[^*] A physician, therefore, a judge, or a statesman can have many fine pathological, juridical, or political rules in his head, even to the degree that he can himself become a thorough teacher in them, and will nevertheless easily blunder in their application, either because he lacks natural power of judgment (although not understanding), and can indeed see the general _in abstracto_, but cannot distinguish whether a case _in concreto_ belongs under it, or else because he has not been sufficiently trained for this judgment by examples and actual business. This is also the sole and great utility of examples: that they sharpen the power of judgment. For as regards the correctness and precision of the insight of the understanding, they rather commonly do it some harm, because they only rarely satisfy the condition of the rule adequately (as _casus in terminis_), and moreover they often weaken that effort of the understanding to see rules in general and independently of the particular circumstances of experience according to their sufficiency, and therefore eventually accustom one to use them more as formulas than as principles. Thus {{ 174 }} examples are the go-cart of the power of judgment, which he who lacks its natural talent can never do without.

But although ***general logic*** can give no instructions to the power of judgment, the case is quite otherwise with ***transcendental*** logic, so much so that it seems the latter has as its proper business to correct and secure the power of judgment in the use of the pure understanding through determinate rules. For to provide expansion for the understanding in the field of pure cognitions _a priori_, and hence as a doctrine, philosophy seems not to be necessary at all, or rather to be ill-suited, because after all previous attempts little or no ground has been won with it; but as a {{{ 133 }}} critique, in order to prevent the blunders of the power of judgment (_lapsus judicii_) in the use of the few pure concepts of the understanding that we have, to this end (although the utility then is only negative) philosophy is summoned with all its acuteness and art of examination.

But transcendental philosophy has this peculiarity: that besides the rule (or rather the universal condition for rules) which is given in the pure concept of the understanding, it can at the same time indicate _a priori_ {{ 175 }} the case to which they are to be applied. The cause of the advantage that it has in this respect over all other instructive sciences (except mathematics) lies precisely in this: that it deals with concepts that are to relate to their objects _a priori_, and hence their objective validity cannot be demonstrated _a posteriori_—for that would leave their dignity entirely untouched—but rather it must at the same time set forth, in universal but sufficient marks, the conditions under which objects can be given in agreement with those concepts, since otherwise they would be without all content, and hence mere logical forms and not pure concepts of the understanding.

This ***transcendental doctrine of the power of judgment*** will now contain two chapters: the ***first***, which deals with the sensible condition under which alone pure concepts of the understanding can be used, i.e., with the schematism of the pure understanding; the ***second***, however, with the synthetic judgments that flow _a priori_ from pure concepts of the understanding under these conditions and lie at the basis of all other cognitions _a priori_, i.e., with the principles of the pure understanding.

[^*]: The lack of the power of judgment is properly what is called stupidity, and for such a failing there is no remedy at all. A dull or limited mind, which lacks nothing but a proper degree of understanding and its own concepts, can indeed be equipped by learning, even to the point of erudition. But since in such cases there is commonly also a lack of the power of judgment (the _secunda Petri_), it is nothing unusual to meet highly learned men who, in the use of their science, frequently reveal that never-to-be-remedied lack.
