## Section 1. System of cosmological ideas

In order now to be able to enumerate these ideas according to a principle with systematic precision, we must ***first*** remark that it is only the understanding from which pure and transcendental concepts can arise, that reason actually produces no concept at all, but at most only frees the concept of the understanding from the unavoidable limitations of a possible experience, and thus seeks to extend it beyond {{ 436 }} the limits of the empirical, yet in connection with it. This happens by its demanding absolute totality for a given conditioned on the side of the conditions (under which the understanding subjects all appearances to synthetic unity), and thereby making the category into a transcendental idea, in order to give absolute completeness to the empirical synthesis by continuing it to the unconditioned (which is never met with in experience, but only in the idea). Reason demands this according to the principle: ***if the conditioned is given, then the entire sum of conditions, and consequently the absolutely unconditioned, is also given***, through which alone the former was possible. Thus ***first*** the transcendental ideas will actually be nothing but categories extended to the unconditioned, and they will allow of being brought into a table arranged according to the titles of the latter. ***Secondly***, however, not all categories will be fit for this, but only those in which the synthesis constitutes a ***series***, and indeed of subordinated (not coordinated) conditions to a conditioned. Absolute totality is demanded by reason only insofar as it concerns the ascending series of conditions for a given conditioned, and consequently not when the descending line of consequences is under discussion, nor when the aggregate of coordinated conditions for {{{ 284 }}} {{ 437 }} these consequences is concerned. For in regard to a given conditioned, conditions are already presupposed and are to be regarded as given along with it, whereas, since consequences do not make their conditions possible but rather presuppose them, in the progress to the consequences (or in descending from the given condition to the conditioned) one can be unconcerned whether the series ceases or not, and in general the question regarding their totality is not a presupposition of reason at all.

Thus one necessarily thinks of a time completely elapsed up to the given moment as also given (even if not determinable by us). But as regards future time, since it is not the condition for arriving at the present, it is, for the sake of comprehending the latter, completely indifferent how we wish to treat future time, whether we want to make it stop somewhere or let it run on to infinity. Let there be the series m, n, o, wherein n is given as conditioned in regard to m, but at the same time as a condition of o; let the series go upward from the conditioned n to m (l, k, i etc.), and likewise downward from the condition n to the conditioned o (p, q, r etc.): then I must presuppose the former series in order to regard n as given, and according to reason (the totality of conditions) n is possible only by means of that series, but its possibility does not rest {{ 438 }} on the subsequent series o, p, q, r, which therefore could not be regarded as given, but only as _dabilis_.

I will call the synthesis of a series on the side of the conditions, that is, starting from the one nearest to the given appearance and so on to the more remote conditions, the ***regressive*** synthesis, but that which on the side of the conditioned proceeds from the nearest consequence to the more remote ones, the ***progressive*** synthesis. The former goes in _antecedentia_, the latter in _consequentia_. The cosmological ideas, therefore, occupy themselves with the totality of the regressive synthesis and go in _antecedentia_, not in _consequentia_. When this latter occurs, it is an arbitrary and not a necessary problem of pure reason, because for the complete comprehensibility of what is given in appearance we indeed require grounds, but not consequences.

In order now to arrange the table of ideas according to the table of categories, we first take the two original _Quanta_ of all our intuition, time and space. Time is in itself a series (and the formal condition of all series), and therefore in it, in regard to a given present, the _antecedentia_ as conditions (the past) {{{ 285 }}} are to be distinguished _a priori_ from the _consequentibus_ (the future). Consequently, the transcendental idea of the absolute totality of the series {{ 439 }} of conditions for a given conditioned goes only to all past time. According to the idea of reason, the whole of elapsed time, as a condition of the given moment, is necessarily thought of as given. But as regards space, in it in itself there is no difference between progressus and regressus, because it constitutes an ***aggregate***, but ***no series***, since its parts are all simultaneous. I could regard the present point of time in view of past time only as conditioned, but never as its condition, because this moment arises only through elapsed time (or rather, through the elapsing of the preceding time) in the first place. But since the parts of space are not subordinated to one another, but are coordinated, one part is not the condition of the possibility of the other, and it does not, as time in itself does, constitute a series. Yet the synthesis of the manifold parts of space, through which we apprehend it, is nevertheless successive, thus occurs in time and contains a series. And since in this series of aggregated spaces (e.g., of feet in a perch) those thought of beyond a given one are always the ***condition of the boundary*** of the former, the ***measurement*** of a space is also to be regarded as a synthesis of a series of conditions for a given conditioned; except that the side of the conditions is in itself not distinguished from the side toward which the conditioned lies, {{ 440 }} and consequently _regressus_ and _progressus_ in space seem to be one and the same. Because, however, one part of space is not given through another, but is only bounded by it, we must therefore regard every bounded space as also conditioned, which presupposes another space as the condition of its boundary, and so on. In regard to limitation, therefore, progress in space is also a regressus, and the transcendental idea of the absolute totality of the synthesis in the series of conditions also concerns space, and I can ask just as well about the absolute totality of appearance in space as about that in elapsed time. Whether, however, an answer to this is possible at all will allow of being determined in the future.

Secondly, the reality in space, i.e., ***matter***, is a conditioned whose inner conditions are its parts, and the parts of the parts are the remote conditions, so that here a regressive synthesis {{{ 286 }}} takes place, the absolute totality of which is demanded by reason, and which can take place in no other way than through a completed division, whereby the reality of matter vanishes either into nothing or at least into that which is no longer matter, namely, the simple. Consequently, there is here also a series of conditions and a progress to the unconditioned.

{{ 441 }} Thirdly, as regards the categories of real relation among appearances, the category of substance with its accidents is not suited to a transcendental idea; i.e., reason has no ground, in regard to substance, to go regressively to conditions. For accidents (so far as they inhere in a single substance) are coordinated with one another and do not constitute a series. In regard to substance, however, they are not actually subordinated to it, but are the way of existing of substance itself. What might still seem to be an idea of transcendental reason here would be the concept of the ***substantial***. But since this means nothing other than the concept of the object in general, which subsists insofar as one thinks in it merely of the transcendental subject without any predicates, whereas here we are speaking only of the unconditioned in the series of appearances, it is clear that the substantial can constitute no member in it. Exactly the same applies also to substances in community, which are mere aggregates and have no exponent of a series, since they are not subordinated to one another as conditions of their possibility, which could indeed be said of spaces, whose boundary was never determined in itself, but always through another space. There remains, therefore, only the category of ***causality***, which offers a series of causes for a given {{ 442 }} effect, in which one can ascend from the latter as the conditioned to the former as conditions, and answer the question of reason.

Fourthly, the concepts of the possible, actual, and necessary do not lead to any series, except insofar as the ***contingent*** in existence must at all times be regarded as conditioned, and, according to the rule of the understanding, points to a condition under which it is necessary, and which in turn points to a higher condition, until reason encounters unconditioned ***necessity*** only in the totality of this series.

There are accordingly no more than four cosmological ideas, according to the four titles of the categories, if one selects those that necessarily carry a series with them in the synthesis of the manifold.

First, it is to be noted here that the idea of absolute totality concerns nothing other than the exposition of ***appearances***, and consequently not the pure concept of the understanding of a whole of things in general. Here, therefore, appearances are regarded as given, and reason demands the absolute completeness of the conditions of their possibility, insofar as these constitute a series, and consequently an absolutely (i.e., in every respect) complete synthesis, whereby the appearance can be expounded according to laws of the understanding.

Secondly, it is actually only the unconditioned that reason seeks in {{ 444 }} this synthesis of conditions, continued serially and indeed regressively—as it were, the completeness in the series of premises, which together presuppose no other. This ***unconditioned*** is indeed always contained ***in the absolute totality of the series*** when one represents it to oneself in the imagination. But this absolutely completed synthesis is in turn only an idea; for one cannot know, at least beforehand, whether such a synthesis is even possible in the case of appearances. If one represents everything to oneself through mere pure concepts of the understanding, without conditions of sensible intuition, one can say directly: that to a given conditioned the entire series of conditions subordinated to one another is also given; for the former is given solely through {{{ 288 }}} these conditions. But in the case of appearances, a special limitation is to be met with in the way conditions are given, namely, through the successive synthesis of the manifold of intuition, which is supposed to be complete in the regressus. Whether this completeness is now sensibly possible is still a problem. Yet the idea of this completeness lies nonetheless in reason, regardless of the possibility or impossibility of connecting empirical concepts adequately with it. Thus, since the unconditioned is necessarily contained in the absolute totality of the regressive synthesis of the manifold in appearance (guided by the categories, which represent it as a series of conditions for a {{ 445 }} given conditioned), even if one leaves it undecided whether and how this totality is to be brought about: reason here takes the path of starting from the idea of totality, although its actual ultimate purpose is the ***unconditioned***, whether of the whole series or of a part of it.

This unconditioned can now be thought: either as consisting merely in the entire series, in which therefore all members without exception are conditioned and only their whole is absolutely unconditioned, and then the regressus is called infinite; or the absolutely unconditioned is only a part of the series, to which the remaining members of the series are subordinated, but which itself stands under no other condition.[^*] In the former case, the series is _a parte priori_ without boundaries (without a beginning), i.e., infinite, and yet entirely given, but the regressus in it is never completed and can be called infinite only _potentialiter_. In the {{ 446 }} second case, there is a first member of the series, which in regard to past time is called the ***beginning of the world***, in regard to space the ***boundary of the world***, in regard to the parts of a whole given within its boundaries the ***simple***, in regard to causes absolute ***self-activity*** (freedom), and in regard to the existence of alterable things absolute ***natural necessity***.

We have two expressions: ***world*** and ***nature***, which sometimes run into one another. The first signifies the mathematical whole of all appearances {{{ 289 }}} and the totality of their synthesis in the great as well as in the small, i.e., both in the progress of the same through composition and through division. But this very same world is called nature[^**] insofar as it is considered as a dynamic whole, and one looks not at the {{ 447 }} aggregation in space or time to bring it about as a magnitude, but rather at the unity in the ***existence*** of appearances. Here, then, the condition of that which happens is called the cause, and the unconditioned causality of the cause in appearance is called freedom, whereas the conditioned cause is called, in the narrower sense, a natural cause. The conditioned in existence in general is called contingent, and the unconditioned is called necessary. The unconditioned necessity of ***appearances*** can be called natural necessity.

The ideas with which we are now occupying ourselves I have above called cosmological ideas, partly because by 'world' is understood the sum total of all appearances, and our ideas are directed solely to the unconditioned among appearances, partly also because the word 'world' in the transcendental sense signifies the absolute totality of the sum total of existing things, and we direct our attention solely to the completeness of the synthesis (although actually only in the regressus to the conditions). Considering, moreover, that these ideas are collectively transcendent and, although they indeed do not exceed their object, namely appearances, ***as regards its kind***, but have to do solely with the world of sense (not with noumena), yet they carry the synthesis to a ***degree*** that surpasses all possible experience, one can, in my opinion, quite appropriately call them collectively ***world-concepts***. In regard {{ 448 }} to the difference between the mathematically and the dynamically unconditioned toward which the regressus aims, I would nevertheless call the first two 'world-concepts' in a narrower sense (of the world in the great and in the small), but the other two ***transcendent concepts of nature*** {{{ 290 }}}. This distinction is not of any particular importance for now, but it can become more important in the course of progress.

[^*]: The absolute whole of the series of conditions for a given conditioned is at all times unconditioned, because outside it there are no more conditions in regard to which it could be conditioned. But this absolute whole of such a series is only an idea, or rather a problematic concept, whose possibility must be investigated, namely in relation to the way in which the unconditioned, as the actual transcendental idea which is at issue, may be contained therein.

[^**]: Nature, taken _adjectively_ (_formaliter_), signifies the connection of the determinations of a thing according to an inner principle of causality. On the other hand, by nature taken _substantively_ (_materialiter_), one understands the sum total of appearances, insofar as these are thoroughly connected by virtue of an inner principle of causality. In the former sense, one speaks of the nature of fluid matter, of fire, etc., and makes use of this word only _adjectively_; on the other hand, when one speaks of things of nature, one has an existing whole in mind.
