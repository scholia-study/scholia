## Section 1. System of cosmological ideas

Now, if we are to enumerate these ideas according to a principle and with systematic precision, we must ***first*** observe that pure and transcendental concepts can spring from the understanding alone, that reason of itself brings forth no concept whatever, but does no more than free a concept of the understanding from the inescapable restrictions of a possible experience, seeking thereby to enlarge it beyond {{ 436 }} the limits of the empirical, though still in conjunction with the empirical. This comes about as follows: on the side of the conditions (those beneath which the understanding brings every appearance under synthetic unity), reason lays claim, for some given conditioned, to absolute totality, and in this way a category is raised into a transcendental idea, the aim being to endow the empirical synthesis with absolute completeness by prolonging it up to the unconditioned (which is met with never in experience but solely in the idea). Reason's demand here proceeds from this principle: ***once the conditioned is posited, the entire sum of its conditions is likewise given, and therewith the utterly unconditioned as well***, through which the conditioned was alone possible. Thus the transcendental ideas will, ***first***, amount to nothing more than categories enlarged up to the unconditioned, and they will let themselves be set out in a table ordered by the titles of the categories. ***Secondly***, however, not every category will serve this purpose, but only those whose synthesis makes up a ***series***—and indeed a series of conditions subordinated one to another (not coordinated) running toward a conditioned. Reason exacts absolute totality only to the degree that it bears upon the ascending series of conditions belonging to a given conditioned, and thus not where what is at issue is the descending line of consequences, or the aggregate of coordinated conditions leading to {{{ 284 }}} {{ 437 }} those consequences. For with respect to the given conditioned the conditions are already presupposed and must be taken as given together with it; whereas, seeing that consequences do not render their conditions possible but on the contrary presuppose them, one may, as one advances toward the consequences (or descends from a given condition to what it conditions), stay unconcerned whether the series terminates or not, so that the question of its totality is in general no presupposition of reason whatever.

In just this way one necessarily represents a stretch of time, wholly run out up to the present moment, as itself given as well (albeit not determinable by us). But as regards time still to come, since it forms no condition for reaching the present, it is—for the purpose of comprehending the present—entirely indifferent what we care to make of future time, whether we would have it stop somewhere or run on without end. Take the series m, n, o, where n is given as conditioned in relation to m and yet at the same time as the condition of o; let the series ascend from the conditioned n up to m (l, k, i etc.) and equally descend from the condition n down to the conditioned o (p, q, r etc.): then I must presuppose the first series if I am to regard n as given, and, according to reason (the totality of conditions), n is possible only through that series, whereas its possibility in no way depends {{ 438 }} upon the following series o, p, q, r, which for that reason could be regarded not as given but merely as _dabilis_.

The synthesis of a series on the side of the conditions—starting from the condition nearest the given appearance and proceeding to ever more remote conditions—I shall term the ***regressive*** synthesis; the one that goes forward on the side of the conditioned, from the nearest consequence out to the more remote ones, I shall term the ***progressive*** synthesis. The former of these runs off into the _antecedentia_, the latter into the _consequentia_. The cosmological ideas, accordingly, concern themselves with the regressive synthesis in its totality, running off into the _antecedentia_ and not into the _consequentia_. Should the latter be the case, we have an arbitrary and by no means necessary problem of pure reason, for to comprehend fully what is given in the appearance we require its grounds, but not its consequences.

Now, to set up the table of ideas in correspondence with the table of categories, we begin with the two primordial _Quanta_ of all our intuition, namely time and space. Time is in and of itself a series (indeed the formal condition of every series), and so within it, relative to a given present, the _antecedentia_ as conditions (the past) {{{ 285 }}} must be told apart _a priori_ from the _consequentibus_ (the future). It follows that the transcendental idea of an absolute totality in the series {{ 439 }} of conditions for a given conditioned reaches only over all bygone time. In keeping with the idea of reason, the entire course of elapsed time is of necessity thought as given, being the condition of the given moment. Space, on the other hand, contains in itself no distinction between progressus and regressus, for it makes up an ***aggregate*** and ***no series***, its parts being all together at once. The present point in time I could, relative to time past, view only as conditioned and never as a condition of that past, since this moment arises in the first place solely through elapsed time (or rather through the elapsing of the time that went before). But because the parts of space stand not in subordination but in coordination to one another, no one part is the condition of another's possibility, and space does not, as time does in itself, make up a series. Still, the synthesis of space's manifold parts, whereby we apprehend it, does proceed successively; it therefore takes place in time and harbors a series. And since in this series of aggregated spaces (the feet in a rod, say), reckoning from a given one, each further space added in thought is invariably the ***condition of the boundary*** of the one before, the ***measuring*** of a space too must count as a synthesis of a series of conditions running to a given conditioned; save only that the side of the conditions is not, in itself, distinguished {{ 440 }} from the side on which the conditioned lies, so that in space _regressus_ and _progressus_ appear to coincide. But since one part of space is not given by means of another, only bounded by it, we must to that extent regard every bounded space as itself conditioned as well, presupposing a further space as the condition of its boundary, and so onward without end. In respect of bounding, then, advance in space is likewise a regressus, and the transcendental idea of the absolute totality of synthesis in the series of conditions applies to space as well; I may therefore ask after the whole of appearance in space no less than after the whole of it in time gone by. Whether an answer to any of this is possible at all will be something to settle later on.

Secondly, the reality in space—that is, ***matter***—is a conditioned; its inner conditions are its parts, and the parts of those parts its more distant conditions, so that here a regressive synthesis {{{ 286 }}} obtains, the absolute totality of which reason demands—a totality attainable in no other way than through a completed division, by which the reality of matter disappears either into nothing or else into something that is matter no longer, namely the simple. Here too, then, we have a series of conditions and an advance toward the unconditioned.

{{ 441 }} Thirdly, as for the categories of the real relation among appearances, the category of substance together with its accidents does not lend itself to a transcendental idea; that is to say, reason has no ground to travel back regressively toward conditions in its case. For accidents, so far as they inhere in one and the same substance, stand coordinate with one another and form no series. And in relation to the substance they are not, strictly speaking, subordinate to it; they are rather the very mode in which the substance itself exists. The one thing here that might still look like an idea of transcendental reason would be the concept of the ***substantiale***. Yet since this means nothing beyond the concept of an object as such that subsists—so far as one thinks in it the mere transcendental subject stripped of all predicates—while what is under discussion here is only the unconditioned within the series of appearances, it is evident that the substantiale can form no link in that series. The very same holds of substances in mutual community, which are mere aggregates and possess no exponent of a series, inasmuch as they are not subordinated one to another as conditions of their possibility—as could well be said of spaces, whose limit was never fixed in itself but always by another space. There remains, then, only the category of ***causality***, which sets before us a series of causes for a given {{ 442 }} effect, one in which we can climb from that effect, as conditioned, up to those causes, as its conditions, and so give answer to the question of reason.

Fourthly, the concepts of the possible, the actual, and the necessary give rise to no series, except only to the extent that the ***contingent*** in existence must at all times be viewed as conditioned and, by the rule of the understanding, points back to a condition, under which it becomes necessary to point on to a still higher one, until reason lights upon the unconditioned ***necessity*** solely within the totality of this series.

Thus there are, all told, no more than four cosmological ideas, answering to the four titles of the categories, once one picks out those categories that necessarily bring a series with them in the synthesis of the manifold.

The first point to be noted is this: that the idea of absolute totality has to do with nothing beyond the exposition of ***appearances***, and thus not with the pure concept of the understanding of a whole of things as such. Appearances, then, are here taken as given, and reason calls for the conditions of their possibility to be supplied in absolute completeness, so far as those conditions make up a series—hence for a synthesis that is complete without qualification (complete, that is, in every respect), by means of which the appearance admits of being expounded under laws of the understanding.

Secondly, what reason genuinely seeks in {{ 444 }} this synthesis of conditions—carried on step by step through the series, and regressively at that—is really nothing but the unconditioned: the completeness, so to speak, of the row of premises that, taken together, presuppose no further premise. This ***unconditioned*** is, at every turn, already contained ***within the absolute totality of the series*** whenever one pictures that series to oneself in imagination. Yet a synthesis thus brought to unqualified completion is, once again, no more than an idea; for one cannot tell, at least beforehand, whether anything of the kind is even possible in the case of appearances. If one pictures everything by means of pure concepts of the understanding alone, apart from the conditions of sensible intuition, then one may say straight out that, given a conditioned, the whole series of conditions subordinate to one another is given along with it; for the former is had only through the latter {{{ 288 }}}. With appearances, however, we meet a peculiar restriction on the way in which their conditions come to be given—namely, through a successive synthesis running across the manifold of intuition, one that is supposed to reach completeness in the regress. Whether such completeness is in fact possible on the side of sensibility remains a problem. The idea of that completeness, all the same, resides in reason quite apart from whether or not empirical concepts adequate to it can be attached. So, since the unconditioned is necessarily embraced within the regressive synthesis of appearance's manifold when taken in absolute totality (a synthesis guided by the categories, which present that manifold as a series of conditions running to a {{ 445 }} given conditioned)—and here one may leave undetermined whether and how such totality is to be achieved—reason adopts the course of setting out from the idea of totality, although its genuine final aim is the ***unconditioned***, whether of the whole series or of a part of it.

This unconditioned now admits of being thought in either of two ways: either as residing simply in the entire series, in which case every member without exception would be conditioned and only the series as a totality would be unconditioned outright—and then the regress is termed infinite; or else the absolutely unconditioned is only some one part of the series, to which the remaining members are subordinate, while it itself falls under no further condition.[^*] In the former case the series, _a parte priori_, is boundless (without beginning), i.e. infinite, and nonetheless given in full, though the regress within it is never completed and can be styled infinite only _potentialiter_. In the {{ 446 }} latter case the series possesses a first member—one called, with respect to time elapsed, the ***beginning of the world***; with respect to space, the ***boundary of the world***; with respect to the parts of a whole given within its limits, the ***simple***; with respect to causes, absolute ***self-activity*** (freedom); and with respect to the existence of alterable things, absolute ***necessity of nature***.

We have two terms at our disposal—***world*** and ***nature***—which now and then run into each other. The first denotes the mathematical whole of all appearances {{{ 289 }}} together with the totality of their synthesis alike in the great and in the small, that is, in the progress of that synthesis both by composition and by division. This very same world, however, we call nature[^**] once it is regarded as a dynamic whole—when we look not to the {{ 447 }} aggregation of things in space or time (a way of constituting it as a magnitude) but to the unity in the ***existence*** of appearances. At this point the condition of whatever happens we name the cause; the unconditioned causality of that cause within the appearance we name freedom; and the conditioned causality, by contrast, is what, in the narrower sense, we name a cause in nature. The conditioned in existence generally we call contingent, the unconditioned we call necessary. The unconditioned necessity that belongs to ***appearances*** may be termed a necessity of nature.

The ideas now occupying us I termed cosmological a moment ago, partly because "world" signifies the sum of all appearances and our ideas likewise aim only at the unconditioned among appearances, and partly too because the word "world", taken transcendentally, denotes the absolute totality of the sum of existing things, and it is on the completeness of the synthesis alone (though strictly only in the regress toward the conditions) that we fix our attention. Bearing in mind, moreover, that these ideas are one and all transcendent, and that, although they do not overstep their object—appearances—***as to kind***, having to do merely with the world of sense (not with noumena), they nonetheless push the synthesis to a ***degree*** exceeding every possible experience, I think one may quite aptly call them, collectively, ***world-concepts***. In view {{ 448 }} of the difference between the mathematically and the dynamically unconditioned toward which the regress is directed, I would, even so, reserve the term world-concepts (of the world in the great and the small) for the first two in a narrower sense, calling the remaining pair ***transcendent concepts of nature*** {{{ 290 }}} instead. For the present this distinction carries no special weight, but as we go on it may take on greater importance.

[^*]: For any given conditioned, the entire series of its conditions, taken as an absolute whole, is unfailingly unconditioned, because beyond that whole there lie no further conditions relative to which it might itself count as conditioned. This absolute whole belonging to such a series, however, is nothing but an idea—or rather a problematic concept—whose very possibility has to be investigated, and investigated precisely with regard to how the unconditioned, which is the real transcendental idea at issue, might be held within it.

[^**]: Nature, taken _adjective_ (_formaliter_), means the interconnection of a thing's determinations according to an inner principle of causality. By nature in the _substantive_ (_materialiter_) sense, on the other hand, one understands the sum of appearances so far as these hang together throughout by virtue of an inner principle of causality. In the former sense one speaks of the nature of fluid matter, of fire, etc., making use of the word only _adjective_; whereas when one speaks of the things of nature, one has in view a whole that subsists on its own.
