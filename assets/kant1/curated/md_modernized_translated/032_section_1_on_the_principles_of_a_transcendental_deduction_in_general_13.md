## Section 1. §13 Of the principles of a transcendental deduction in general.

Jurists, when they speak of rights and claims, distinguish in a legal dispute the question of what is of right (_quid iuris_) from that which concerns the fact (_quid facti_), and since they demand proof of both, they call the former, which is to demonstrate the right or also the legal claim, the ***deduction***. We make use of a multitude of empirical concepts without anyone's objection, and consider ourselves justified, even without deduction, in attributing to them a sense and imaginary significance, because we always have experience at {{ 117 }} hand to prove their objective reality. There are, however, also usurped concepts, such as ***fortune*** or ***fate***, which to be sure circulate with almost universal indulgence, but are yet occasionally challenged by the question: _quid iuris_; since one then falls into no small embarrassment regarding their deduction, because one can adduce no clear legal ground, whether from experience or from reason, by which the right to their use would become clear.

Among the various concepts, however, which make up the very mixed web of human cognition, there are some which are also destined for pure use _a priori_ (completely independent of all experience), {{{ 100 }}} and the right to this use always requires a deduction: because proofs from experience are not sufficient for the lawfulness of such a use, but one must nevertheless know how these concepts can relate to objects that they yet do not derive from any experience. I therefore call the explanation of the way in which concepts _a priori_ can relate to objects their ***transcendental deduction***, and distinguish it from the ***empirical*** deduction, which shows the way in which a concept has been acquired through experience and reflection upon it, and which therefore concerns not the lawfulness, but the fact from which the possession arose.

{{ 118 }} We already have two kinds of concepts of completely different sorts, which yet agree with each other in that they both relate to objects completely _a priori_, namely the concepts of space and time as forms of sensibility, and the categories as concepts of the understanding. To attempt an empirical deduction of them would be entirely lost labor, because the distinguishing feature of their nature lies precisely in this, that they relate to their objects without having borrowed anything from experience for their representation. If, therefore, a deduction of them is necessary, it must always be transcendental.

One can, however, seek out for these concepts, as for all cognition, if not the principle of their possibility, yet the occasional causes of their generation in experience; where then the impressions of the senses give the first occasion to open the entire cognitive power in respect of them and to bring about experience, which contains two very heterogeneous elements, namely a ***matter*** for cognition from the senses and a certain ***form*** to order it, from the inner source of pure intuiting and thinking, which on the occasion of the former are first brought into exercise and produce concepts. Such a tracing of the first endeavors of our cognitive power, in order {{ 119 }} to rise from singular perceptions to general concepts, has without doubt its great utility, and we have the famous Locke to thank for having first opened the way to this. But a ***deduction*** of pure concepts _a priori_ never comes about in this way, for it does not lie on this path at all, because in respect of their future use, which is to be completely independent of experience, they must have to show a completely different birth certificate from that of descent {{{ 101 }}} from experiences. This attempted physiological derivation, which properly cannot be called a deduction at all because it concerns a _quaestionem facti_, I will therefore call the explanation of the ***possession*** of a pure cognition. It is therefore clear that of these alone can there be a transcendental deduction and by no means an empirical one, and that the latter in respect of pure concepts _a priori_ is nothing but idle attempts, with which only he can occupy himself who has not understood the completely peculiar nature of these cognitions.

But although the only kind of a possible deduction of pure cognition _a priori_, namely that by the transcendental way, is conceded, it is yet not thereby clear that it is so indispensably necessary. We have above traced the concepts of space and time to their sources by means of a transcendental deduction, {{ 120 }} and explained and determined their objective validity _a priori_. Nevertheless, geometry proceeds with a secure step through nothing but cognitions _a priori_, without needing to request from philosophy a certificate of credentials regarding the pure and lawful descent of its fundamental concept of space. But the use of the concept in this science also goes only to the outer world of the senses, of which space is the pure form of its intuition, in which therefore all geometrical cognition, because it is grounded on intuition _a priori_, has immediate evidence, and the objects are given in intuition through the cognition itself _a priori_ (as to their form). On the contrary, with the ***pure concepts of the understanding*** there begins the indispensable need to seek the transcendental deduction not only of themselves but also of space, because, since they speak of objects not through predicates of intuition and sensibility but of pure thinking _a priori_, they relate to objects generally without any conditions of sensibility, and since they are not grounded on experience, they cannot present in intuition _a priori_ any object on which, prior to all experience, they might ground their synthesis; and they therefore not only excite suspicion regarding the objective validity and limits of their use, but also render that ***concept of space*** {{ 121 }} ambiguous, by being inclined to use it beyond the conditions of sensible {{{ 102 }}} intuition, on which account a transcendental deduction of it was also necessary above. Thus the reader must be convinced of the indispensable necessity of such a transcendental deduction before he has taken a single step in the field of pure reason, because he will otherwise proceed blindly, and after he has wandered about in many ways, he must yet return to the ignorance from which he had started. He must also see clearly in advance the inevitable difficulty, so that he may not complain of obscurity where the matter itself is deeply enveloped, or become too soon discouraged over the clearing away of obstacles, since it is a matter of either completely giving up all claims to insights of pure reason as the most favorite field, namely that beyond the limits of all possible experience, or bringing this critical investigation to perfection.

We were able above, in the case of the concepts of space and time, with little trouble, to make it comprehensible how these, as cognitions _a priori_, must yet necessarily relate to objects, and made possible a synthetic cognition of them independent of all experience. For since only by means of such pure forms of sensibility can an object appear to us, i.e., be an object of empirical intuition, space and time are pure intuitions which {{ 122 }} contain the condition of the possibility of objects as appearances _a priori_, and the synthesis in them has objective validity.

The categories of the understanding, on the contrary, do not represent to us the conditions under which objects are given in intuition; consequently, objects can indeed appear to us without their having to relate necessarily to functions of the understanding, and without the latter containing the conditions of these _a priori_. Hence a difficulty here shows itself which we did not encounter in the field of sensibility, namely, how ***subjective conditions of thinking*** should have ***objective validity***, i.e., yield conditions of the possibility of all cognition of objects: for without functions of the understanding, appearances can indeed be given in intuition. I take, for example, the concept of cause, which signifies a special kind of synthesis, since upon something A something completely different B is posited according to a rule. It is not clear _a priori_ why appearances should contain anything of the sort (for experiences cannot be adduced as proof, {{{ 103 }}} because the objective validity of this concept must be capable of being demonstrated _a priori_); and it is therefore _a priori_ doubtful whether such a concept is not perhaps entirely empty and meets with no object at all among appearances. For that objects of sensible intuition {{ 123 }} must be in agreement with the formal conditions of sensibility lying _a priori_ in the mind is clear from the fact that they would otherwise not be objects for us; but that they must also over and above this agree with the conditions which the understanding requires for the synthetic unity of thinking is a conclusion whose deduction is not so easy to see. For appearances could, after all, be so constituted that the understanding would not find them in agreement with the conditions of its unity at all, and everything would lie in such confusion that, e.g., in the succession of appearances nothing offered itself that would provide a rule of synthesis and thus correspond to the concept of cause and effect, so that this concept would therefore be entirely empty, null, and without meaning. Appearances would nevertheless still present objects to our intuition, for intuition does not in any way require the functions of thinking.

If one thought to extricate oneself from the troublesomeness of these investigations by saying that experience incessantly offers examples of such regularity among appearances, which give sufficient occasion for abstracting the concept of cause from them and thereby at the same time validating the objective validity of such a concept, one does not notice that in this way the concept of cause cannot arise at all, but that it must either be grounded completely _a priori_ in the understanding, {{ 124 }} or be entirely abandoned as a mere figment of the brain. For this concept absolutely requires that something A be of such a kind that something else B follows from it ***necessarily and according to an absolutely universal rule***. Appearances indeed provide cases from which a rule is possible according to which something usually happens, but never that the consequence is ***necessary***: hence there also attaches to the synthesis of cause and effect a dignity that cannot be expressed empirically at all, namely, that the effect is not merely added to the cause, but is posited ***by*** it and ***follows*** from it. The strict universality of the rule is also not a property of empirical rules at all, which through induction can obtain nothing but comparative universality, i.e., extensive usefulness. But now the use of the pure concepts of the understanding {{{ 104 }}} would be completely altered if one wanted to treat them only as empirical products.
