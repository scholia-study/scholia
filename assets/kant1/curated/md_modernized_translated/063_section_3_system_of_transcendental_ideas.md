## {{ 390 }} Section 3. System of transcendental ideas

What occupies us here is not a logical dialectic, one that abstracts from all content of cognition and merely lays bare the false semblance in the form of the inferences of reason, but a transcendental one, which is meant to contain, wholly _a priori_, the origin of certain cognitions springing from pure reason together with inferred concepts whose object can never be given empirically at all, and which for that reason lie entirely outside the capacity of the pure understanding. We have inferred, from the relation that the transcendental employment of our cognition must naturally bear to its logical employment—in judgments no less than in inferences—that dialectical inferences can be of only three kinds, answering to the three ways of inferring by which reason is able to reach cognitions from principles, and that in every case their task consists in climbing up from that conditioned synthesis, to which the understanding stays perpetually tied, toward an unconditioned one that it can never attain.

Now, the universal element in every relation that our representations are {{ 391 }} capable of having is this: 1) their relation to the subject, 2) their relation to objects, and here either as appearances or as objects {{{ 258 }}} of thinking in general. If this subdivision is combined with the one given above, then every relation of the representations—of which we are able to frame either a concept or an idea—turns out to be threefold: 1. its relation to the subject, 2. to the object's manifold in appearance, 3. to all things whatsoever.

Now, pure concepts as such all have to do with the synthetic unity of representations, whereas concepts of pure reason (transcendental ideas) are directed at the unconditioned synthetic unity embracing all conditions whatsoever. It follows that every transcendental idea can be assigned to one of ***three classes***: the ***first*** takes in the ***thinking subject's*** absolute (unconditioned) ***unity***; the ***second***, the absolute ***unity*** belonging to the ***series*** of the ***conditions of appearance***; the ***third***, the absolute ***unity*** proper to the ***condition of all objects of thinking in general***.

The thinking subject forms the object of ***psychology***; the sum of all appearances (the world), that of ***cosmology***; and that thing which holds the highest condition for the possibility of all that admits of being thought (the being of all beings), that of ***theology***. In this way pure reason hands over the idea of a transcendental doctrine of the soul (_psychologia rationalis_), of a transcendental {{ 392 }} science of the world (_cosmologia rationalis_), and lastly of a transcendental cognition of God (_theologia transscendentalis_). Even the mere sketch of the one or the other of these sciences in no way stems from the understanding, even were it bound up with reason's highest logical use, i.e., with every conceivable inference, so as to move from one object of the understanding (an appearance) to all the rest, right down to the remotest members of the empirical synthesis; rather, it is merely a pure and genuine product or problem of pure reason.

Which _modi_ of the pure concepts of reason stand under these three headings of all transcendental ideas will be set forth in full in the chapter that follows. They proceed along the guiding thread of the categories. For pure reason never relates directly to objects, but rather to the understanding's concepts of them. In the same way, only the complete working-out can show how reason, simply by the synthetic use of that very function which serves it in the categorical syllogism, is bound to {{{ 259 }}} come upon the concept of the ***thinking subject's*** absolute unity; how, in the hypothetical case, its logical procedure yields the idea of what is unconditioned outright ***in a series*** of given conditions; and how, lastly, the {{ 393 }} bare form of the disjunctive syllogism cannot but drag along with it the supreme rational concept of a ***being of all beings***—a notion that strikes one, at first sight, as utterly paradoxical.

Of these transcendental ideas no ***objective deduction*** is, strictly speaking, possible, of the kind we were able to supply for the categories. For in truth they stand in no relation to any object that might be given as congruent with them, and this precisely because they are nothing but ideas. A subjective derivation of them from the nature of our reason, however, we could indeed undertake; and that has in fact been carried out in the present chapter.

One readily sees that pure reason aims at nothing other than the absolute totality of the synthesis ***on the side of the conditions*** (whether of inherence, of dependence, or of concurrence), and that it has no business with the absolute completeness ***on the side of the conditioned***. For reason requires the former alone, so as to lay down in advance the entire series of conditions and thus supply them to the understanding _a priori_. Yet the moment a completely (and unconditionally) given condition is present, no concept of reason is any longer called for as regards the continuation of the series; for on its own the understanding accomplishes every step {{ 394 }} downward from the condition to the ***conditioned***. Accordingly, the transcendental ideas are of service solely for the ***ascent*** through the series of conditions up to the unconditioned, that is, to the principles. As regards the ***descent*** to the conditioned, by contrast, our reason does make a far-reaching logical use of the laws of the understanding, yet no transcendental use whatever; and should we frame an idea of the absolute totality of such a synthesis (the _progressus_)—say, of the entire sequence of all ***future*** changes in the world—this is a thought-thing (_ens rationis_), one thought up merely at will and not necessarily presupposed by reason. For while the totality of a thing's conditions is presupposed for the possibility of the conditioned, the totality of its consequences is not. Such a concept, therefore, is no transcendental idea—and it is transcendental ideas alone that occupy us here.

{{{ 260 }}} Finally, one also comes to notice that a certain coherence and unity shines out among the transcendental ideas themselves, and that by means of them pure reason arranges the whole of its cognitions into a system. To advance from cognition of oneself (the soul) to cognition of the world, and by its means to the primordial being, is a progression so natural that it looks like the logical passage of reason from {{ 395 }} premises to conclusion [^*]. Now whether a kinship of the same sort as that between the logical and the transcendental procedure really does lie secretly at the ground here is likewise one of those questions whose answer one may expect only as these investigations are pursued further. For the moment we have already gained our end, {{ 396 }} in that we have managed to lift the transcendental concepts of reason—which are otherwise wont to mingle, in the theorizing of the philosophers, among other concepts, without these philosophers even properly telling them apart from concepts of the understanding—out of this equivocal position, to specify their origin and, at one stroke, their determinate number, past which none can ever be added, and to exhibit them within a systematic coherence, whereby a special field is marked off and bounded for pure reason.

[^*]: Metaphysics has, as the proper aim of its inquiry, only three ideas: ***God***, ***freedom and immortality***, so arranged that the second concept, once combined with the first, is supposed to yield the third as a necessary conclusion. Everything else that this science otherwise busies itself with serves it merely as a means for reaching these ideas and their reality. It requires them not for the ends of natural science, but in order to pass beyond nature. Insight into them would make ***theology, morals***, and, through the union of the two, ***religion***, and hence the highest ends of our existence, depend solely on the speculative faculty of reason and on nothing else at all. In a systematic presentation of those ideas the order given above, as the ***synthetic*** one, would be the most suitable; but in the elaboration that must necessarily go before it, the ***analytic*** one, which turns this order around, will fit the aim better, so that, by starting out from what experience puts directly into our hands, the ***doctrine of the soul***, moving on to the ***doctrine of the world***, and from there up to the cognition of ***God***, we may bring our great design to completion.
