## {{{ 28 }}} II. We are in possession of certain cognitions _a priori_, and even the common understanding is never without them

Everything here turns on a mark that lets us tell a pure cognition apart from empirical ones with certainty. Experience, to be sure, teaches us that a thing has this or that character, yet never that it could be no other way. So, **firstly**, should we come upon a proposition whose very thinking carries its ***necessity*** along with it, we have an _a priori_ judgment; and should it in addition {{{ 29 }}} rest on no premise except one that is itself valid as a necessary proposition, then it is _a priori_ without qualification. **Secondly**: to the judgments it yields experience grants no genuine or strict, but merely presumed and comparative ***universality*** (through induction), so {{ 4 }} that the proper formulation must run: so far as we have thus far observed, this or that rule admits of no exception. Hence whenever a judgment is conceived with strict universality, that is, so that not a single exception is allowed to count as possible, it has not been drawn from experience but holds with unconditional _a priori_ validity. Empirical universality, then, is nothing but an arbitrary raising of a validity that obtains in most instances to one that obtains across the board, as in the proposition all bodies are heavy; where, on the other hand, strict universality is integral to a judgment, it marks out a peculiar cognitive source for that judgment, namely a capacity for cognizing _a priori_. Necessity and strict universality, accordingly, are reliable indicators of an _a priori_ cognition, and the two are moreover inseparably bound to one another. Yet because, in applying them, it is now and then easier to point out a judgment's empirical restrictedness than its contingency, and at other times clearer to exhibit the unrestricted universality we attach to a judgment than to exhibit its necessity, one does well to deploy each of these two criteria on its own, since either taken singly is infallible.

It is a simple matter to show that judgments of this kind—necessary, universal in the strictest sense, and therefore pure and _a priori_—do genuinely occur within human cognition. Should an illustration from the sciences be wanted, the whole body of mathematical propositions supplies it; should one {{ 5 }} be wanted from the most ordinary exercise of understanding, the proposition that any alteration must have a cause will answer; indeed in this latter case the concept of a cause itself so manifestly harbors the concept of a necessary linkage to an effect, and of a rule holding with strict universality, that it would collapse altogether if, in Hume's manner, one sought to trace it back to a repeated pairing of what occurs with what came before, and to a habit springing from this (a necessity, therefore, that is merely subjective) of tying representations together. One might equally, with no need of such examples to establish that pure _a priori_ principles are actual in our cognition, exhibit their indispensability to the very possibility of {{{ 30 }}} experience, and so exhibit it _a priori_. For from where could experience itself draw its certainty, were all the rules by which it advances themselves empirical through and through, and thus contingent; on which account one could scarcely allow them to pass for first principles. For the present, however, it suffices that we have exhibited, as a fact and together with its distinguishing marks, the pure use of our cognitive faculty. Yet an _a priori_ origin for some of them shows itself not merely in judgments but even in concepts. Strip from your empirical concept of a ***body***, one feature after another, all that is empirical in it, its color, its hardness or softness, its weight, its impenetrability even, and what still remains is the ***space*** that this body (now vanished entirely) {{ 6 }} had occupied, and that you are unable to strip away. In the same way, once you strip from your empirical concept of any object at all, bodily or not, every property that experience has taught you: there is still one you cannot take from it, the property whereby you think it as ***substance*** or as ***inhering*** in a substance (even though this concept carries more determination than does the concept of an object as such). Compelled, then, by the necessity with which this concept presses itself upon you, you must admit that its seat lies _a priori_ within your cognitive faculty.
