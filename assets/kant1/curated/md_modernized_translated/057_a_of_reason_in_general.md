## A. Of reason in general

All our cognition starts from the senses, goes from there to the understanding, and ends with reason, beyond which nothing higher is found in us to work up the matter of intuition and bring it under the highest unity of thought. Since I am now to give an explanation of this supreme cognitive power, I find myself in some embarrassment. There is of it, as of the understanding, a merely formal, i.e., logical use, where reason abstracts from all content of cognition, but also a real use, since it itself contains the origin of certain concepts and principles which it borrows neither from the senses nor from the understanding. The former faculty has, to be sure, long since been explained by logicians as the faculty of drawing mediate inferences (in distinction from immediate inferences, _consequentiis immediatis_); but the second, which itself generates concepts, is thereby not {{{ 238 }}} yet understood. Since, then, a division of reason into a {{ 356 }} logical and a transcendental faculty occurs here, a higher concept of this source of cognition must be sought which comprises both concepts under itself, while we can expect, according to the analogy with the concepts of understanding, that the logical concept will at the same time provide the key to the transcendental one, and the table of functions of the former will at the same time provide the ancestral registry of the concepts of reason.

We explained the understanding in the first part of our transcendental logic as the faculty of rules; here we distinguish reason from it by the fact that we wish to call it the ***faculty of principles***.

The expression of a principle is ambiguous, and commonly signifies only a cognition that can be used as a principle, although in itself and according to its own origin it is no _principium_. Every general proposition, even if it be taken from experience (by induction), can serve as the major premise in a syllogism; but it is not for that reason itself a _principium_. Mathematical axioms (e.g., between two points there can be only one straight line) are even general cognitions _a priori_, and are therefore rightly called principles, relatively to the cases which can be subsumed under them. But I cannot for all that say that I cognize this property {{ 357 }} of straight lines in general and in itself from principles, but only in pure intuition.

I would therefore call cognition from principles that in which I cognize the particular in the universal through concepts. Thus, every syllogism is a form of derivation of a cognition from a principle. For the major premise always gives a concept which has the effect that everything that is subsumed under its condition is cognized from it according to a principle. Since, then, every general cognition can serve as the major premise in a syllogism, and the understanding presents such general propositions _a priori_, these can indeed also be called principles in respect of their possible use.

But if we consider these principles of pure understanding in themselves according to their origin, they are by no means cognitions from concepts. For they would not even be possible _a priori_ if we did not bring in pure intuition (in mathematics), or conditions of a possible experience in general. That everything {{{ 239 }}} which happens has a cause, cannot at all be inferred from the concept of that which happens in general; rather, the principle shows how one can first of all obtain a determinate empirical concept of that which happens.

Synthetic cognitions from concepts, therefore, the understanding cannot {{ 358 }} provide at all, and it is these which I properly call principles without qualification: whereas all general propositions in general may be called comparative principles.

It is an ancient wish, which, who knows how late, may perhaps one day be fulfilled: that one might for once, instead of the endless multiplicity of civil laws, search out their principles; for in this alone can the secret consist of simplifying, as the saying is, legislation. But the laws here are also only limitations of our freedom to conditions under which it thoroughly agrees with itself throughout; consequently they refer to something which is entirely our own work, and of which we can ourselves be the cause through those concepts. But how objects in themselves, how the nature of things should stand under principles and be determined according to mere concepts, is, if not something impossible, at least very absurd in its demand. But however this may be (for we still have this investigation before us), this much at least is clear: that cognition from principles (in itself) is something entirely different from mere cognition of the understanding, which indeed can also precede other cognitions in the form of a principle, but in itself (insofar as it is synthetic) does not rest on mere thinking, nor does it contain within itself a universal according to concepts.

{{ 359 }} The understanding may be a faculty of the unity of appearances by means of rules, so reason is the faculty of the unity of the rules of the understanding under principles. It therefore never goes first to experience or to any object, but to the understanding, in order to give unity _a priori_ through concepts to its manifold cognitions, which unity may be called unity of reason, and is of an entirely different kind from that which can be achieved by the understanding.

This is the general concept of the faculty of reason, so far as it has been possible to make it comprehensible in the total absence of examples (such as are only to be given in the sequel).
