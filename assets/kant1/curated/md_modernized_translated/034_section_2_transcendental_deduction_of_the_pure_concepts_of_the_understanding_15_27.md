## {{{ 107 }}} Section 2. Transcendental deduction of the pure concepts of the understanding. §15–27

## §15. Of the possibility of a combination in general.

The manifold of representations can be given in an intuition which is merely sensible, i.e., nothing but receptivity, and the form of this intuition can lie _a priori_ in our faculty of representation, without yet being anything other than the way in which the subject is affected. But the ***combination*** (_conjunctio_) of a manifold in general can never come into us through the senses, and can {{ 130 }} therefore also not be simultaneously contained in the pure form of sensible intuition; for it is an act of the spontaneity of the power of representation, and since one must call the latter understanding to distinguish it from sensibility, all combination—whether we are conscious of it or not, whether it be a combination of the manifold of intuition or of various concepts, and in the case of the former, of sensible or non-sensible intuition—is an action of the understanding, which we would designate with the general title of ***synthesis***, in order to make it simultaneously noticeable that we can represent nothing as combined in the object without having previously combined it ourselves, and among all representations ***combination*** is the only one that cannot be given through objects but can only be performed by the subject itself, because it is an act of its self-activity. One easily perceives here that this action must originally be unitary and equally valid for all combination, and that dissolution, ***analysis***, which seems to be its opposite, nevertheless always presupposes it; for where the understanding has not previously combined anything, there it can also dissolve nothing, because it is only ***through it***, as combined, that anything could be given to the power of representation.

{{{ 108 }}} But the concept of combination carries with it, besides the concept of the manifold and its synthesis, also that of its unity. Combination is the representation of the ***synthetic*** unity of the {{ 131 }} manifold.[^*] The representation of this unity can therefore not arise from the combination; rather, by being added to the representation of the manifold, it makes the concept of combination possible in the first place. This unity, which precedes _a priori_ all concepts of combination, is not, say, that category of unity (§10); for all categories are grounded on logical functions in judgments, but in these combination, and therefore unity of given concepts, is already thought. The category therefore already presupposes combination. Thus we must seek this unity (as qualitative, §12) even higher, namely in that which itself contains the ground of the unity of different concepts in judgments, and hence of the possibility of the understanding even in its logical use.

## §16. Of the originally-synthetic unity of apperception.

The: ***I think***, ***must*** be able to accompany all my representations; {{ 132 }} for otherwise something would be represented in me that could not be thought at all, which is as much as to say that the representation would either be impossible or at least nothing for me. That representation which can be given prior to all thinking is called ***intuition***. Thus all manifold of intuition has a necessary relation to the: ***I think***, in the same subject in which this manifold is found. But this representation is an act of ***spontaneity***, i.e., it cannot be regarded as belonging to sensibility. I call it the ***pure apperception***, in order to distinguish it from the ***empirical***, or also the ***original apperception***, because it is that self-consciousness which, by producing the representation: ***I think***, {{{ 109 }}} which must be able to accompany all others and is one and the same in all consciousness, can be accompanied by no further representation. I also call its unity the ***transcendental*** unity of self-consciousness, in order to designate the possibility of _a priori_ cognition from it. For the manifold representations given in a certain intuition would not altogether be my representations if they did not altogether belong to one self-consciousness, i.e., as ***my*** representations (even if I am not conscious of them as such) they must still necessarily conform to the condition under which alone they ***can*** stand together in a universal self-consciousness, because otherwise they would not thoroughly belong to me. From this original combination much can be inferred.

Namely, this thoroughgoing identity of the apperception of a manifold given in intuition contains a synthesis of representations and is possible only through the consciousness of this synthesis. For the empirical consciousness that accompanies different representations is in itself dispersed and without relation to the identity of the subject. This relation therefore does not yet occur by my accompanying every representation with consciousness, but rather by my ***adding*** one to the other and being conscious of the synthesis of them. Thus, only because I can combine a manifold of given representations in ***one consciousness*** is it possible for me to represent the ***identity of consciousness in these representations*** itself; i.e., the ***analytic*** unity of apperception is only possible under the presupposition {{ 134 }} of some ***synthetic*** unity.[^**] The thought: these representations given in intuition {{{ 110 }}} altogether belong to ***me***, therefore means as much as that I unite them in one self-consciousness, or can at least unite them therein; and although it is not itself yet the consciousness of the synthesis of representations, it still presupposes the possibility of the latter, i.e., only because I can comprehend their manifold in one consciousness do I call them altogether my representations; for otherwise I would have a self as multicolored and diverse as I have representations of which I am conscious. Synthetic unity of the manifold of intuitions, as given _a priori_, is therefore the ground of the identity of apperception itself, which precedes _a priori_ all ***my*** determinate thinking. But combination does not lie in objects and cannot, say, be borrowed from them through perception and thereby first {{ 135 }} received into the understanding; rather, it is solely a performance of the understanding, which itself is nothing more than the faculty of combining _a priori_ and bringing the manifold of given representations under unity of apperception, which principle is the supreme one in the whole of human cognition.

This principle of the necessary unity of apperception is, to be sure, itself identical, and therefore an analytic proposition, yet it explains a synthesis of the manifold given in an intuition as necessary, without which that thoroughgoing identity of self-consciousness cannot be thought. For through the 'I' as simple representation nothing manifold is given; in intuition, which is distinct from it, the manifold can alone be given and thought through ***combination*** in one consciousness. An understanding in which all manifold would be simultaneously given through self-consciousness would ***intuit***; ours can only ***think*** and must seek intuition in the senses. I am therefore conscious of the identical self in regard to the manifold of representations given to me in an intuition because I call them altogether ***my*** representations, which constitute ***one*** representation. But that is as much as to say that I am conscious _a priori_ of a necessary synthesis of them, which is called the original synthetic unity {{ 136 }} of apperception, under which stand all representations given to me, but under which they must also be brought by means of a synthesis.

## {{{ 111 }}} §17. The principle of the synthetic unity of apperception is the supreme principle of all use of the understanding.

The supreme principle of the possibility of all intuition in relation to sensibility was, according to the Transcendental Aesthetic, that all its manifold stand under the formal conditions of space and time. The supreme principle of the very same in relation to the understanding is that all manifold of intuition stand under conditions of the originally-synthetic unity of apperception.[^***] Under the former stand all manifold representations of intuition insofar as they are ***given*** to us, under the latter insofar as they must be able to be ***combined*** in {{ 137 }} one consciousness; for without this nothing can be thought or recognized thereby, because the given representations do not have in common the act of apperception: ***I think***, and would thereby not be grasped together in one self-consciousness.

***Understanding*** is, to speak generally, the faculty of ***cognitions***. These consist in the determinate relation of given representations to an object. But an ***object*** is that in the concept of which the manifold of a given intuition is ***united***. Now, however, all unification of representations requires unity of consciousness in their synthesis. Consequently, the unity of consciousness is that which alone constitutes the relation of representations to an object, hence their objective validity, and consequently their becoming cognitions, and on which consequently even the possibility of the understanding rests.

The first pure cognition of understanding, therefore, on which its entire remaining use is grounded, which is also at the same time entirely independent of all conditions of sensible intuition, is now the principle of the original ***synthetic*** unity of apperception. Thus the mere form of outer sensible intuition, space, is not yet cognition at all; it only supplies the manifold of intuition _a priori_ for a {{{ 112 }}} possible cognition. In order to cognize anything in space, {{ 138 }} e.g., a line, I must ***draw*** it and thus synthetically bring about a determinate combination of the given manifold, so that the unity of this action is at the same time the unity of consciousness (in the concept of a line), and thereby first an object (a determinate space) is cognized. The synthetic unity of consciousness is therefore an objective condition of all cognition, not one of which I merely stand in need myself in order to cognize an object, but rather under which every intuition must stand ***in order to become an object for me***, because in any other way, and without this synthesis, the manifold would ***not*** unite in one consciousness.

This latter proposition is, as has been said, itself analytic, even though it makes synthetic unity the condition of all thinking; for it says nothing more than that all ***my*** representations in any given intuition must stand under the condition under which alone I can ascribe them as ***my*** representations to the identical self and can therefore grasp them together as synthetically combined in one apperception through the general expression: ***I think***.

But this principle is still not a principle for every possible understanding in general, but only for that one through whose pure apperception in the representation: ***I am***, nothing manifold is yet given. That understanding through whose self-consciousness the {{ 139 }} manifold of intuition would be simultaneously given, an understanding through whose representation the objects of this representation would at the same time exist, would not require a special act of synthesis of the manifold for the unity of consciousness, of which the human understanding, which merely thinks but does not intuit, stands in need. But for the human understanding it is still unavoidably the first principle, so that it cannot form the least concept of any other possible understanding, either of one that itself intuited, or of one that, while possessing a sensible intuition, yet possessed one of a different kind than that lying at the ground in space and time.

## {{{ 113 }}} §18. What objective unity of self-consciousness is.

The ***transcendental unity*** of apperception is that unity through which all manifold given in an intuition is united in a concept of the object. It is therefore called ***objective***, and must be distinguished from the ***subjective unity*** of consciousness, which is a ***determination of inner sense***, through which that manifold of intuition is empirically given for such a combination. Whether I can be ***empirically*** conscious of the manifold as simultaneous or successive depends on circumstances or empirical conditions {{ 140 }}; hence the empirical unity of consciousness, through association of representations, itself concerns an appearance and is entirely contingent. By contrast, the pure form of intuition in time, merely as intuition in general, which contains a given manifold, stands under the original unity of consciousness solely through the necessary relation of the manifold of intuition to the one: ***I think***, and thus through the pure synthesis of the understanding, which lies _a priori_ at the ground of the empirical synthesis. That unity alone is objectively valid; the empirical unity of apperception, which we do not consider here, and which is only derived _in concreto_ from the former under given conditions, has only subjective validity. One person combines the representation of a certain word with one thing, another person with another thing; and the unity of consciousness in that which is empirical is, in regard to what is given, not necessary and universally valid.

## §19. The logical form of all judgments consists in the objective unity of the apperception of the concepts contained therein.

I have never been able to be satisfied with the explanation that logicians give of a judgment in general: it is, as they say, the representation of a relation between two concepts. Without now {{ 141 }} quarreling with them here about the defectiveness of this explanation—that it at best fits only ***categorical***, but not hypothetical and disjunctive judgments (which latter contain not a relation of concepts but rather of judgments themselves) (notwithstanding that many troublesome consequences have arisen {{{ 114 }}} from this oversight of logic)[^****], I remark only that it is not here determined in what this ***relation*** consists.

But if I investigate more closely the relation of given cognitions in every judgment, and distinguish it, as belonging to the understanding, from the relation according to laws of the reproductive imagination (which has only subjective validity), then I find that a judgment is nothing other than the way to bring given cognitions to the ***objective*** unity of apperception. That is the aim of the copula {{ 142 }} ***is*** in them, in order to distinguish the objective unity of given representations from the subjective. For this designates the relation of the representations to the original apperception and its ***necessary unity***, even if the judgment itself is empirical, and therefore contingent, e.g., 'bodies are heavy'. By this, to be sure, I do not mean to say that these representations ***necessarily belong to one another*** in empirical intuition, but rather that they belong to one another ***by virtue of the necessary unity*** of apperception in the synthesis of intuitions, i.e., according to principles of the objective determination of all representations insofar as cognition can come from them, which principles are all derived from the principle of the transcendental unity of apperception. Only through this does this relation become ***a judgment***, i.e., a relation that is ***objectively valid***, and is sufficiently distinguished from the relation of these very same representations in which there would be merely subjective validity, e.g., according to laws of association. According to the latter I would only be able to say: 'If I carry a body, I feel a pressure of weight'; but not: 'It, the body, ***is*** heavy'; which is as much as to say that these two representations are combined in the object, i.e., without distinction of the state of the subject, and not merely together in perception (however often it may be repeated).

## {{{ 115 }}} {{ 143 }} §20. All sensible intuitions stand under the categories as conditions under which alone their manifold can come together in one consciousness.

The manifold given in a sensible intuition belongs necessarily under the original synthetic unity of apperception, because through this alone is the ***unity*** of intuition possible (§17). But that action of the understanding through which the manifold of given representations (whether they be intuitions or concepts) is brought under an apperception in general is the logical function of judgments (§19). Thus all manifold, insofar as it is given in ***one*** empirical intuition, is ***determined*** in regard to one of the logical functions for judging, through which, namely, it is brought to a consciousness in general. But now the ***categories*** are nothing other than these very functions for judging, insofar as the manifold of a given intuition is determined in regard to them (§10). Consequently, the manifold in a given intuition also stands necessarily under categories.

## {{ 144 }} §21. Remark.

A manifold contained in an intuition that I call mine is represented through the synthesis of the understanding as belonging to the ***necessary*** unity of self-consciousness, and this occurs through the category.[^*****] This therefore indicates: that the empirical consciousness of a given manifold of an intuition stands just as much under a pure self-consciousness _a priori_ as empirical intuition stands under a pure sensible intuition, which likewise takes place _a priori_. In the above proposition, therefore, the beginning of a ***deduction*** of the pure concepts of the understanding is made, in which, since the categories arise merely in the understanding, ***independently of sensibility***, I must still abstract {{{ 116 }}} from the way in which the manifold for an empirical intuition is given, in order to look only at the unity that is added to the intuition through the understanding by means of the category. In the what follows (§26) it will be shown from the way in which the empirical {{ 145 }} intuition is given in sensibility that its unity is none other than that which the category (according to the preceding §20) prescribes to the manifold of a given intuition in general, and thereby, since its validity _a priori_ in regard to all objects of our senses is explained, the aim of the deduction will first be fully achieved.

Yet from one piece I could not abstract in the above proof, namely, from this: that the manifold for intuition must be ***given*** prior to the synthesis of understanding and independently of it; but how, remains here undetermined. For if I wanted to think of an understanding that itself intuited (such as a divine one, which would not represent given objects, but through whose representation the objects themselves would be simultaneously given or produced), then the categories would have no significance at all in regard to such cognition. They are only rules for an understanding whose entire faculty consists in thinking, i.e., in the action of bringing the synthesis of the manifold, which has been given to it from elsewhere in intuition, to the unity of apperception, which understanding therefore ***cognizes*** nothing at all by itself, but only combines and orders the matter for cognition, the intuition, which must be given to it through the object. But for the peculiarity of our understanding, of bringing about unity of apperception _a priori_ only by means of the categories {{ 146 }} and only precisely through this kind and number of them, no further ground can be given, any more than why we have precisely these and no other functions for judging, or why space and time are the only forms of our possible intuition.

## §22. The category has no other use for the cognition of things than its application to objects of experience.

To ***think*** an object and to ***cognize*** an object are therefore not one and the same. For to cognition belong two parts: {{{ 117 }}} first, the concept through which an object is thought at all (the category), and second, the intuition through which it is given; for if a corresponding intuition could not be given to the concept at all, then it would be a thought as far as form is concerned, but without any object, and through it no cognition of any thing at all would be possible, because, as far as I know, there would be nothing, nor could there be anything, to which my thought could be applied. Now all intuition possible for us is sensible (Aesthetic); therefore, the thinking of an object in general through a pure concept of the understanding can become cognition in us only insofar as this concept is related to objects of the senses. Sensible {{ 147 }} intuition is either pure intuition (space and time) or empirical intuition of that which is immediately represented in space and time as actual, through sensation. Through the determination of the former we can obtain _a priori_ cognitions of objects (in mathematics), but only as far as their form is concerned, as appearances; whether there can be things that must be intuited in this form remains thereby still undecided. Consequently, all mathematical concepts are not by themselves cognitions, except insofar as one presupposes that there are things that can be presented to us only in accordance with the form of that pure sensible intuition. ***Things in space*** and ***time*** are, however, given only insofar as they are perceptions (representations accompanied by sensation), and thus through empirical representation. Consequently, the pure concepts of the understanding, even when they are applied to intuitions _a priori_ (as in mathematics), provide cognition only insofar as these, and thus also the concepts of the understanding by means of them, can be applied to empirical intuitions. Consequently, the categories, by means of intuition, also do not provide us with any cognition of things except through their possible application to ***empirical intuition***, i.e., they serve only for the possibility of ***empirical cognition***. But this latter is called ***experience***. Consequently, the categories have no {{ 148 }} other use for the cognition of things except insofar as these are accepted as objects of possible experience.

## §23.

The above proposition is of the greatest importance; for it determines the limits of the use of the pure concepts of the understanding in {{{ 118 }}} regard to objects, just as the Transcendental Aesthetic determined the limits of the use of the pure form of our sensible intuition. Space and time are valid as conditions of the possibility of how objects can be given to us, no further than for objects of the senses, and thus only of experience. Beyond these limits they do not represent anything at all; for they are only in the senses and have no reality outside them. The pure concepts of the understanding are free from this limitation and extend to objects of intuition in general, whether it be similar to ours or not, as long as it is sensible and not intellectual. But this wider extension of concepts beyond our sensible intuition does not help us at all. For they are then empty concepts of objects, of which we cannot judge at all through them whether they are even possible or not—mere forms of thought without objective reality, because we have no intuition at hand to which the synthetic unity of apperception, which they alone {{ 149 }} contain, could be applied, so that they might thereby determine an object. ***Our*** sensible and empirical intuition alone can provide them with sense and significance.

If, therefore, one assumes an object of a ***non-sensible*** intuition as given, one can to be sure represent it through all those predicates that already lie in the presupposition ***that nothing belonging to sensible intuition pertains to it***: data, that it is not extended or in space, that its duration is not time, that no change (succession of determinations in time) is found in it, etc. But that is yet no proper cognition at all if I merely indicate how the intuition of the object ***is not***, without being able to say what is then contained in it; for then I have not represented the possibility of an object for my pure concept of the understanding at all, because I could not give any intuition corresponding to it, but could only say that ours is not valid for it. But the most important thing here is that not even a single category could be applied to such a something; e.g., the concept of a substance, i.e., of something that can exist only as a subject but never as a mere predicate, of which I do not know at all whether there can be any thing corresponding to this determination of thought if empirical intuition does not give me the case for its application. But more of this in what follows.

## {{{ 119 }}} {{ 150 }} §24. Of the application of the categories to objects of the senses in general.

The pure concepts of the understanding relate, through the mere understanding, to objects of intuition in general, whether it be ours or any other, as long as it is sensible, but are for this very reason mere ***forms of thought***, through which no determinate object is yet cognized. The synthesis or combination of the manifold in them related merely to the unity of apperception, and was thereby the ground of the possibility of cognition _a priori_ insofar as it rests on the understanding, and was therefore not only transcendental but also merely purely intellectual. But because a certain form of sensible intuition _a priori_ lies at our ground, which rests on the receptivity of the capacity for representation (sensibility), the understanding, as spontaneity, can determine the inner sense through the manifold of given representations in accordance with the synthetic unity of apperception, and thus think _a priori_ the synthetic unity of apperception of the manifold of ***sensible intuition***, as the condition under which all objects of our (human) intuition must necessarily stand, whereby the categories as mere {{ 151 }} forms of thought then obtain objective reality, i.e., application to objects that can be given to us in intuition, but only as appearances; for of these alone are we capable of intuition _a priori_.

This ***synthesis*** of the manifold of sensible intuition, which is possible and necessary _a priori_, can be called ***figurative*** (_synthesis speciosa_) to distinguish it from that which would be thought in regard to the manifold of an intuition in general in the mere category and is called combination of understanding (_synthesis intellectualis_); both are ***transcendental***, not merely because they themselves proceed _a priori_, but also because they ground the possibility of other cognition _a priori_.

Yet the figurative synthesis, when it relates merely to the originally-synthetic unity of apperception, i.e., this transcendental unity which is thought in the categories, must be called the ***transcendental synthesis of imagination*** to distinguish it from the merely intellectual combination. **Imagination** is the faculty {{{ 120 }}} of representing an object even ***without its presence*** in intuition. Now since all our intuition is sensible, the imagination, because of the subjective condition under which alone it can give a corresponding intuition to the concepts of the understanding, belongs to ***sensibility***; yet insofar as its synthesis is still an exercise of {{ 152 }} spontaneity, which is determining and not, like sense, merely determinable, and can thus determine _a priori_ the form of sense in accordance with the unity of apperception, the imagination is to that extent a faculty of determining sensibility _a priori_, and its synthesis of intuitions, ***in accordance with the categories***, must be the transcendental synthesis of ***imagination***, which is an action of the understanding on sensibility and its first application (as well as the ground of all the rest) to objects of the intuition possible for us. As figurative, it is distinguished from the intellectual synthesis without any imagination, through the mere understanding. Insofar as the imagination is now spontaneity, I also sometimes call it the ***productive*** imagination, and thereby distinguish it from the ***reproductive*** imagination, whose synthesis is subject solely to empirical laws, namely those of association, and which therefore contributes nothing to the explanation of the possibility of cognition _a priori_, and on that account belongs not to transcendental philosophy but to psychology.

***

Here is now the place to make intelligible the paradox that must have struck everyone in the exposition of the form of inner sense (§6): namely, how this sense represents even ourselves to consciousness only as we appear to ourselves, not as we are in ourselves, since {{ 153 }} we namely intuit ourselves only as we are internally ***affected***, which seems contradictory, since we would have to relate to ourselves as passive; hence one usually prefers to treat ***inner sense*** as identical with the faculty of ***apperception*** (which we carefully distinguish) in systems of psychology.

That which determines inner sense is the understanding and its original faculty of combining the manifold of intuition, i.e., of bringing it under an apperception (on which its possibility itself rests). Now because the understanding in us humans is not itself {{{ 121 }}} a faculty of intuitions, and cannot receive them ***into itself*** even if they were given in sensibility, in order as it were to combine the manifold of ***its own*** intuition, its synthesis, if it is considered by itself alone, is nothing other than the unity of action of which it is conscious as such even without sensibility, but through which it is capable of determining sensibility internally in regard to the manifold that may be given to it in accordance with the form of its intuition. It therefore exercises, under the name of a ***transcendental synthesis of imagination***, that action on the ***passive*** subject whose ***faculty*** it {{ 154 }} is, of which we say with right that the inner sense is thereby affected. Apperception and its synthetic unity are so far from being one and the same with inner sense that the former, rather, as the source of all combination, relates to the manifold of ***intuitions in general***, under the name of the categories, prior to all sensible intuition, to objects in general; by contrast, inner sense contains the mere ***form*** of intuition, but without combination of the manifold in it, and thus does not yet contain any ***determinate*** intuition, which is possible only through the consciousness of its determination by the transcendental action of imagination (synthetic influence of the understanding on the inner sense), which I have called the figurative synthesis.

This we also perceive in ourselves at any time. We cannot think of a line without ***drawing*** it in thought, nor think of a circle without ***describing*** it, nor represent the three dimensions of space at all without ***setting*** three lines perpendicular to one another from the same point, and we cannot even represent time without, in ***drawing*** a straight line (which is to be the outer figurative representation of time), attending merely to the action of the synthesis of the manifold through which we successively determine inner sense, and thereby attending to the succession of this determination in it. Motion as action {{ 155 }} of the subject (not as determination of an object)[^******], and consequently the synthesis {{{ 122 }}} of the manifold in space, if we namely abstract from space and attend merely to the action through which we determine the ***inner sense*** in accordance with its form, even produces the concept of succession in the first place. The understanding therefore does ***not*** find some such combination of the manifold already in inner sense, but rather ***produces it*** by ***affecting*** that sense. But how the 'I' that I think is distinct from the ***I*** that intuit itself (since I can represent other modes of intuition as at least possible) and yet is identical with the latter as the same subject, and how I can therefore say: I, as intelligence and ***thinking*** subject, cognize myself as a ***thought*** object, insofar as I am given to myself in intuition over and above that, only (just like other phenomena) not as I am before the understanding but as I appear to myself, has no more and no less difficulty {{ 156 }} in it than how I can be an object for myself at all, and indeed an object of intuition and inner perceptions. But that it must really be so can be clearly shown—if one accepts space as a mere pure form of appearances of outer senses—by the fact that we cannot make time, which is yet not an object of outer intuition at all, representable to ourselves in any other way than under the image of a line, insofar as we draw it, without which mode of representation we could not cognize the unity of its dimension at all; likewise, that we must always take the determination of the length of time, or also of positions in time for all inner perceptions, from what outer things present to us as changeable, and consequently must order the determinations of inner sense as appearances in time in exactly the same way as we order those of the outer senses in space; consequently, if we concede of the latter that we cognize objects through them only insofar as we are externally affected, we must also admit of inner sense that we thereby intuit ourselves only as we are internally affected ***by ourselves***, i.e., as far as inner intuition is concerned, we cognize our own subject only as appearance, but not according to what it is in itself.[^*******]

## {{{ 123 }}} {{ 157 }} §25.

On the contrary, I am conscious of myself in the transcendental synthesis of the manifold of representations in general, and thus in the synthetic original unity of apperception, not as I appear to myself, nor as I am in myself, but ***only that*** I am. This ***representation*** is a ***thinking***, not an ***intuiting***. Now since for the ***cognition*** of ourselves, besides the action of thinking (which brings the manifold of every possible intuition to the unity of apperception), a determinate kind of intuition is also required, through which this manifold is given, my own existence is indeed not appearance (much less mere illusion), but the determination {{ 158 }} of my existence[^********] can occur only in accordance with the form of inner sense, according to the particular way in which the manifold that I combine is given in inner intuition; and I have therefore accordingly ***no cognition*** of myself ***as I am***, but merely as I ***appear*** to myself. The consciousness of oneself is therefore still far from being a cognition of oneself, notwithstanding all the categories that constitute the thinking of an object in general through combination of the manifold in an apperception. Just as for the cognition of an object distinct from myself I require, besides the thinking of an ***object in general*** (in the category), still an intuition through which I determine that general concept, so for the cognition of myself I also require, besides the consciousness or besides my thinking myself, still an {{{ 124 }}} intuition of the manifold in me, through which I determine this thought; and I exist as an intelligence that is merely conscious of its faculty of combination, but in regard to the manifold {{ 159 }} that it is to combine, is subject to a limiting condition that it calls inner sense, of making that combination intuitable only according to relations of time (which lie entirely outside the proper concepts of the understanding), and which can therefore still cognize itself only as it merely appears to itself in respect of an intuition (which cannot be intellectual and given through the understanding itself), not as it would cognize itself if its ***intuition*** were intellectual.

## §26. Transcendental deduction of the universally possible empirical use of the pure concepts of the understanding.

In the ***metaphysical deduction*** the origin of the categories _a priori_ was in general demonstrated through their complete agreement with the universal logical functions of thought, but in the ***transcendental*** deduction their possibility as _a priori_ cognitions of objects of an intuition in general (§20, 21) was presented. Now the possibility—of cognizing _a priori_ ***through categories*** objects ***that may ever come before our senses***, and indeed not as regards the form of their intuition but according to the laws of their combination, thus as it were prescribing the law to nature {{ 160 }} and even making it possible—is to be explained. For without this suitability of theirs, it would not be clear how everything that may ever come before our senses must stand under laws that arise _a priori_ from the understanding alone.

First of all, I remark that by ***synthesis of apprehension*** I understand the composition of the manifold in an empirical intuition, through which perception, i.e., empirical consciousness of it (as appearance), becomes possible.

We have _a priori_ ***forms*** of outer as well as inner sensible intuition in the representations of space and time, and to these the synthesis of apprehension of the manifold of appearance must always conform, because it can itself occur only in accordance with this form. But space and time are represented _a priori_ not merely as ***forms*** of sensible intuition, {{{ 125 }}} but as ***intuitions*** themselves (which contain a manifold), and thus with the determination of the ***unity*** of this manifold in {{ 161 }} them (see Transc. Aesthetic).[^*********] Thus even the ***unity of synthesis*** of the manifold outside or in us, and consequently also a ***combination*** to which everything that is to be represented as determined in space or time must conform, is already given _a priori_ as a condition of the synthesis of all ***apprehension***, simultaneously with (not in) these intuitions. But this synthetic unity can be no other than that of the combination of the manifold of a given ***intuition in general*** in an original consciousness, in accordance with the categories, only applied to our ***sensible intuition***. Consequently, all synthesis through which even perception becomes possible stands under the categories; and since experience is cognition through connected perceptions, the categories are conditions of the possibility of experience, and are therefore valid _a priori_ of all objects of experience as well.

***

{{ 162 }} If, therefore, I for example make the empirical intuition of a house into a perception through apprehension of its manifold, then the ***notnecessary unity*** of space and of outer sensible intuition in general lies at my ground, and I as it were draw its shape in accordance with this synthetic unity of the manifold in space. But this very same synthetic unity, if I abstract from the form of space, has its seat in the understanding, and is the category of the synthesis of the homogeneous in an intuition in general, i.e., the {{{ 126 }}} category of magnitude, to which that synthesis of apprehension, i.e., the perception, must therefore conform through and through.[^**********]

If (in another example) I perceive the freezing of water, I apprehend two states (fluidity and solidity) as such that stand in a relation of time to each other. But {{ 163 }} in time, which I lay at the ground of the appearance as ***inner intuition***, I necessarily represent a synthetic ***unity*** of the manifold, without which that relation could not be given as determined in an intuition (in regard to the temporal succession). Now, however, this synthetic unity, as an _a priori_ condition under which I combine the manifold of an ***intuition in general***, if I abstract from the constant form of my inner intuition, time, is the category of ***cause***, through which, when I apply it to my sensibility, ***I determine everything that happens in time in general according to its relation***. Consequently, the apprehension in such an occurrence, and therefore this occurrence itself as regards possible perception, stands under the concept of the ***relation*** of ***effects and causes***, and so in all other cases.

***

Categories are concepts that prescribe laws _a priori_ to appearances, and thus to nature as the sum total of all appearances (_natura materialiter spectata_); and now the question arises—since they are not derived from nature and do not follow it as their model (because they would otherwise be merely empirical)—how it is to be conceived that nature must conform to them, i.e., how they can determine the combination of the manifold of nature _a priori_ without taking it from the latter. Here is the solution to this riddle.

{{ 164 }} It is no more surprising how the laws of appearances in nature must agree with the understanding and its _a priori_ form, i.e., its faculty of combining the manifold in general, than how the appearances themselves must agree with the _a priori_ form of sensible intuition {{{ 127 }}}. For laws exist just as little in appearances, but rather only relative to the subject in which the appearances inhere, insofar as it has understanding, as appearances do not exist in themselves, but rather only relative to the same being, insofar as it has senses. To things in themselves their conformity to law would necessarily pertain even outside of an understanding that cognizes them. But appearances are only representations of things that are present without being recognized according to what they may be in themselves. As mere representations, however, they stand under no law of connection at all other than that which the connecting faculty prescribes. Now that which connects the manifold of sensible intuition is imagination, which depends on the understanding for the unity of its intellectual synthesis and on sensibility for the manifoldness of apprehension. Since, now, all possible perception depends on the synthesis of apprehension, but this empirical synthesis itself depends on the transcendental synthesis, and thus on the categories, all possible perceptions, and thus also everything that can ever reach empirical {{ 165 }} consciousness, i.e., all appearances of nature, as regards their combination, must stand under the categories, on which nature (considered merely as nature in general) depends as the original ground of its necessary conformity to law (as _natura formaliter spectata_). But the pure faculty of the understanding is not sufficient to prescribe _a priori_ laws to appearances through mere categories beyond more laws than those on which a ***nature in general***, as conformity to law of appearances in space and time, rests. Particular laws, because they concern empirically determined appearances, cannot be ***completely derived*** from them, although they all collectively stand under them. Experience must be added in order to get to know the latter ***at all***; but concerning experience in general, and what can be recognized as an object of it, those _a priori_ laws alone provide the instruction.

## §27. Outcome of this deduction of the concepts of the understanding.

We cannot ***think*** any object except through categories; we cannot ***cognize*** any thought object except through intuitions that correspond to those concepts. Now all our intuitions are {{{ 128 }}} sensible, and this cognition, insofar as its object {{ 166 }} is given, is empirical. But empirical cognition is experience. Consequently, ***no _a priori_ cognition is possible for us except solely of objects of possible experience***.[^***********]

But this cognition, which is limited merely to objects of experience, is not on that account all borrowed from experience, but rather, as regards both the pure intuitions and the pure concepts of the understanding, they are elements of cognition that are found in us _a priori_. Now there are only two ways in which a ***necessary*** agreement of experience with the concepts of its objects can be thought: either experience makes these concepts, {{ 167 }} or these concepts make experience possible. The former does not occur in regard to the categories (nor in regard to pure sensible intuition); for they are concepts _a priori_, and thus independent of experience (the assertion of an empirical origin would be a sort of _generatio aequivoca_). Consequently, only the second way remains (as it were, a system of the ***epigenesis*** of pure reason): namely, that the categories on the part of the understanding contain the grounds of the possibility of all experience in general. How they make experience possible, however, and what principles of its possibility they provide in their application to appearances, will be taught in detail in the following chapter on the transcendental use of the power of judgment.

Were someone to propose a middle way between the two only designated ways, namely, that they were ***neither self-thought*** _a priori_ first principles of our cognition, nor yet borrowed from experience, but rather subjective dispositions for thinking, implanted in us simultaneously {{{ 129 }}} with our existence, which were so arranged by our creator that their use agreed precisely with the laws of nature along which experience runs (a kind of ***preformation system*** of pure reason), then (besides the fact that with such a hypothesis no end can be seen to how far one might drive the presupposition of predetermined dispositions for future judgments) this would be decisive against {{ 168 }} the said middle way: that in such a case the categories would lack the ***necessity*** that essentially belongs to their concept. For for example, the concept of cause, which asserts the necessity of an event under a presupposed condition, would be false if it rested only on an arbitrary subjective necessity, implanted in us, of combining certain empirical representations according to such a rule of relation. I would not be able to say: 'The effect is combined with the cause in the object (i.e., necessarily)', but rather only 'I am so constituted that I cannot think of this representation otherwise than as so connected'; which is precisely what the skeptic most desires; for then all our insight through the supposed objective validity of our judgments is nothing but sheer illusion, and there would also be no lack of people who would not admit this subjective necessity (which must be felt) of themselves; at least one would not be able to dispute with anyone about that which rests merely on the way in which his subject is organized.

## Short outline of this deduction.

It is the presentation of the pure concepts of the understanding (and with them of all theoretical cognition _a priori_) as principles of the possibility of experience, but of the latter as ***determination*** of appearances in space and {{ 169 }} time ***in general***, — finally, of this determination from the principle of the ***original*** synthetic unity of apperception, as the form of the understanding in relation to space and time as original forms of sensibility.

***

Only up to this point do I hold the division into sections to be necessary, because we had to do with the elementary concepts. Now that we wish to make their {{{ 130 }}} use representable, the exposition may proceed in a continuous connection without them.

[^*]: Whether the representations are themselves identical, and thus one can be thought analytically through the other, is not here taken into consideration. The ***consciousness*** of the one is, as far as the manifold is under discussion, still always to be distinguished from the consciousness of the other, and on the synthesis of this (possible) consciousness depends everything here.
[^**]: The analytic unity of consciousness attaches to all common concepts as such; e.g., if I think of ***red*** in general, I thereby represent to myself a quality that (as a mark) can be found somewhere or other, or combined with other representations; thus only by virtue of a previously thought possible synthetic unity can I represent to myself the analytic unity. A representation that is to be thought of as common to ***different*** things is regarded as belonging to those that, besides it, have something ***different*** in themselves; consequently, it must previously be thought in synthetic unity with others (even if only possible representations) before I can think in it the analytic unity of consciousness that makes it a _conceptus communis_. And thus the synthetic unity of apperception is the highest point to which one must attach all use of the understanding, even the whole of logic, and, after it, transcendental philosophy; indeed, this faculty is the understanding itself.
[^***]: Space and time and all parts thereof are ***intuitions***, hence individual representations with the manifold that they contain within themselves (see the Transcendental Aesthetic), hence not mere concepts through which the very same consciousness is contained as in many representations, but rather many representations as contained in one and its consciousness, hence as composite, consequently the unity of consciousness is found as ***synthetic***, but yet original. This ***singularity*** of theirs is important in the application (see §25).
[^****]: The detailed doctrine of the four syllogistic figures concerns only categorical syllogisms; and although it is nothing more than an art of surreptitiously obtaining the illusion of more kinds of inference than that of the first figure, by concealing immediate inferences (_consequentiae immediatae_) among the premises of a pure syllogism, yet it would not have made any particular fortune through this alone if it had not succeeded in bringing categorical judgments into exclusive authority, as those to which all others must be related, which, however, according to §9, is false.
[^*****]: The ground of proof rests on the represented ***unity of intuition*** through which an object is given, which always includes in itself a synthesis of the manifold given for an intuition, and already contains the relation of the latter to unity of apperception.
[^******]: Motion of an ***object*** in space does not belong in a pure science, consequently also not in geometry, because that something is movable cannot be cognized _a priori_, but only through experience. But motion as the ***description*** of a space is a pure act of the successive synthesis of the manifold in outer intuition in general through the productive imagination, and belongs not only to geometry, but even to transcendental philosophy.
[^*******]: I do not see how one can find so much difficulty in inner sense being affected by ourselves. Every act of ***attention*** can give us an example of this. The understanding therein always determines inner sense in accordance with the combination it thinks, corresponding to the inner intuition that corresponds to the manifold in the synthesis of the understanding. How much the mind is commonly affected thereby, everyone will be able to perceive in himself.
[^********]: The: ***I think***, expresses the act of determining my existence. Existence is thereby already given, but the way in which I am to determine it, i.e., posit the manifold belonging to it in myself, is not yet given thereby. For that, self-intuition is required, which has at its ground an _a priori_ given form, i.e., time, which is sensible and belongs to the receptivity of the determinable. Now if I do not have yet another self-intuition, which gives the ***determining*** in me, of whose spontaneity alone I am conscious, prior to the act of ***determining***, in the same way that time gives the determinable, then I cannot determine my existence as that of a self-active being; rather, I represent to myself only the spontaneity of my thinking, i.e., of determining, and my existence remains always merely sensibly determinable, i.e., as the existence of an appearance. Yet this spontaneity is what makes me call myself ***intelligence***.
[^*********]: Space represented as ***object*** (as is really required in geometry) contains more than mere form of intuition, namely, the ***comprehension*** of the manifold given in accordance with the form of sensibility into an ***intuitive*** representation, so that the ***form of intuition*** gives merely the manifold, but the ***formal intuition*** gives unity of the representation. In the Aesthetic I had added this unity merely to sensibility, only in order to note that it precedes all concepts, although it indeed presupposes a synthesis that does not belong to the senses, but through which alone all concepts of space and time first become possible. For since through it (by the understanding determining sensibility) space or time are first ***given*** as intuitions, the unity of this intuition belongs _a priori_ to space and time, and not to the concept of the understanding (§24).
[^**********]: In such a way it is proved that the synthesis of apprehension, which is empirical, must necessarily conform to the synthesis of apperception, which is intellectual and contained entirely _a priori_ in the category. It is one and the same spontaneity that, there under the name of imagination, here of understanding, introduces combination into the manifold of intuition.
