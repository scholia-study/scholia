## {{ 620 }} Section 4. On the impossibility of an ontological proof of the existence of God.

From what has already been said one sees readily: the concept of an absolutely necessary being constitutes a pure concept of reason — that is, a mere idea — whose objective reality is far from established by reason's having need of it, an idea which also merely points the way toward a certain, albeit unreachable, completeness, and which in truth serves rather to circumscribe the understanding than to enlarge its reach toward new objects. Here we now come upon what is odd and paradoxical: that the inference from some given existence in general to an existence that is absolutely necessary appears at once pressing and correct, and that all the conditions the understanding requires for framing a concept of such a necessity nonetheless stand wholly against us.

At all times people have talked of the ***absolutely necessary*** being, and have labored not so much to understand whether and in what way a thing of this kind can even be thought, as rather to prove its existence. A nominal definition of this concept is admittedly quite easy: it is, namely, something whose non-being is impossible; but this makes one no wiser about the conditions that {{ 621 }} render it impossible to view the non-being of a thing as downright unthinkable, conditions which are in fact the very thing one wishes to know, namely whether by this concept anything whatsoever is being thought or not. For to discard, by means of the word ***unconditioned***, every condition that the understanding invariably needs in order to view something as {{{ 398 }}} necessary is far from making it intelligible to me whether, by a concept of an unconditionally necessary thing, I then still think something or perhaps nothing whatever.

More than this: this concept — hazarded at sheer random and by now grown thoroughly familiar — has furthermore been believed to be explained by a mass of examples, so that any further asking after its intelligibility has appeared entirely superfluous. Every proposition of geometry, for example that a triangle has three angles, holds with absolute necessity; and in this way people talked about an object lying entirely beyond the sphere of our understanding as though they grasped quite well what they meant to say by their concept of it.

The alleged examples are, every one of them, drawn from ***judgments*** alone, but not from ***things*** and their existence. The unconditioned necessity of judgments, however, is no absolute necessity of things. For the judgment's absolute necessity is nothing but a {{ 622 }} conditioned necessity of the thing, or of the predicate within the judgment. The proposition above did not assert that three angles are absolutely necessary; it said rather that, on condition that a triangle is there (is given), three angles too are there (in it) of necessity. All the same, this logical necessity has shown so great a power of illusion that, once one had formed a concept _a priori_ of a thing, so framed that in one's own opinion existence fell within its compass, one believed oneself able to conclude with assurance: because existence necessarily attaches to the object of this concept — that is, on condition that I posit this thing as something given (as existing), its existence too is posited necessarily (by the rule of identity) — this being is therefore itself absolutely necessary, since its existence is thought along in a concept adopted at pleasure and on condition that its object is posited.

If, in an identical judgment, I remove the predicate and retain the subject, a contradiction springs up, and hence I say: the former belongs of necessity to the latter. But if I remove the subject along with the predicate, no contradiction springs up; for there is ***nothing left over*** to which a contradiction could attach. Positing a triangle while removing its three angles is contradictory; but removing the triangle along with its three angles involves no contradiction. {{{ 399 }}} {{ 623 }} Matters stand in just the same way regarding the concept of an absolutely necessary being. Should you remove its existence, you remove the thing itself together with every one of its predicates; from where, then, is the contradiction supposed to arise? Externally there is nothing left that would be contradicted, since the thing is not meant to be necessary externally; nor is there anything internally, since in removing the thing itself you have removed everything internal along with it. God is almighty; the judgment is a necessary one. Omnipotence cannot be removed so long as you posit a deity — that is, an infinite being — with whose concept the former is identical. But should you say: ***God is not***, neither omnipotence nor any one of his other predicates is then given; for all of them have been removed along with the subject, and this thought exhibits not the slightest contradiction.

You have seen, then, that when I remove the predicate of a judgment together with its subject, no internal contradiction can ever spring up, be the predicate what it may. Now the only evasion left to you is to say: there are subjects that admit of no removal at all, and that must therefore remain. But that would be to say as much as: there are absolutely necessary subjects; a presupposition whose correctness is exactly what I have been doubting, and whose possibility it was that you meant to show me. For of a thing which, were it removed with all its predicates, would leave behind a contradiction, I can frame not the slightest concept; and {{ 624 }} without the contradiction I have, through nothing but pure concepts _a priori_, no mark whatever of impossibility.

In the face of every one of these general inferences (which nobody can refuse) you summon me with one case, put forward by you as a proof by deed: that there is after all one concept — indeed only this single one — whose object's non-being or removal is contradictory in itself; and it is the concept of the most real of all beings. It possesses, you say, all reality, and you are justified in assuming such a being to be possible (which I concede for the present, though a concept that does not contradict itself is a long way from demonstrating the possibility of its object).[^*] Now among all reality existence too is {{{ 400 }}} included: hence existence lies within the concept of a possible thing. {{ 625 }} Now if this thing is removed, the internal possibility of the thing has been removed, which is contradictory.

I reply: you have committed a contradiction already if, into the concept of a thing that you wished to think solely as to its possibility, you have brought — under whatever disguised name — the concept of its existence. Grant you this, and to all appearances you have won the game, though in fact you have said nothing; for what you have committed is a bare tautology. I put the question to you: is the proposition, ***this thing or that*** (which I allow you as possible, be it what it may) ***exists***; is, I say, this proposition an analytic one or a synthetic? If it is the former, then the thing's existence contributes nothing to your thought of it; but in that case either the thought present in you would have to be the very thing itself, or else you have taken an existence for granted as belonging to possibility, and have then, as you allege, inferred the existence from the internal possibility — which is nothing but a wretched tautology. The word: reality, which rings differently in the concept of the thing than existence does in the predicate's concept, does not decide the matter. For even if you give the name reality to every act of positing (leaving undetermined what it is you posit), still you have already posited the thing with all of its predicates in the subject's concept {{ 626 }} and assumed it to be actual, and in the predicate you merely repeat it. If you concede instead, as every reasonable person must in all fairness concede, that every existential proposition is synthetic: how then would you maintain that the predicate of existence admits of no removal without contradiction? since that advantage attaches distinctively to analytic propositions alone, whose character rests on just this.

I would indeed hope to demolish this over-subtle argumentation without any roundabout course, through an exact determination of the concept of existence, had I not found that the illusion that comes of mistaking a ***logical predicate*** for a real predicate (that is, for a determination {{{ 401 }}} of a thing) turns away nearly every attempt at instruction. As a logical predicate anything whatever may serve, even the subject may be predicated of itself; logic, after all, abstracts from every content. A ***determination***, by contrast, is a predicate that comes in over and above the subject's concept and enlarges it. It must therefore not be contained in that concept already.

***Being*** is evidently no real predicate, that is, no concept of something that could be added onto the concept of a thing. It is just the position of a thing, or of certain determinations, taken in themselves. In logical use it is nothing but the copula in a judgment. The proposition: ***God is almighty***, contains two concepts that have their objects: God and omnipotence; the small word: ***is***, is not some further predicate on top, but merely that which sets the predicate ***in its relation*** to the {{ 627 }} subject. If I now take the subject (God) together with all of its predicates (omnipotence being among them) and say: ***God is***, or that there is a God, then I posit no new predicate for the concept of God, but only the subject in itself together with all of its predicates — and indeed the ***object*** in relation to my ***concept***. The two must have exactly the same content, and hence nothing further can accrue to the concept — which expresses the mere possibility — from my thinking its object as absolutely given (through the expression: it is). And thus the actual holds no more within it than the merely possible. A hundred actual thalers contain nothing in the least beyond a hundred possible ones. For since the possible thalers signify the concept while the actual ones signify the object and its position in itself, my concept — in case the object contained more than the concept — would not then express the object entire, and so would not be the appropriate concept of it either. In the state of my finances, however, there is more with a hundred actual thalers than with their mere concept (that is, with their possibility). For given actuality, the object is not merely contained in my concept analytically, but is joined synthetically to my concept (itself a determination of my state), without these hundred thalers, as merely thought, being augmented in the slightest by this being outside my concept.

{{ 628 }} If, accordingly, I think a thing by means of whatever predicates I please, and by however many (even in its thoroughgoing determination), then my adding besides: this thing ***is***, brings not the slightest addition to the thing {{{ 402 }}}. For otherwise not the very same thing, but more than I had thought in the concept, would exist, and I could not say that it is precisely the object of my concept that exists. Even should I think every reality but one in some thing, the missing reality does not get added by my saying: so defective a thing exists; rather, the thing exists saddled with just the same defect with which I thought it, or else something other than what I had thought would exist. Should I now think a being as the highest reality (without any defect), the question still stands whether it exists or not. For though nothing whatever is lacking in my concept of a thing's possible real content in general, something is still lacking in its relation to the entire state of my thinking — namely, that cognition of that object should be possible _a posteriori_ as well. And here the source of the difficulty at work in this matter also comes to light. Were an object of the senses at issue, I would be unable to mistake the thing's existence for the bare concept of the thing. For through the concept the object is thought merely as according with the general conditions of a possible empirical cognition as such, whereas through existence it is thought {{ 629 }} as contained within the context of the whole of experience; through this linkage with the content of the whole of experience, the object's concept is not enlarged in the least, while our thinking gains by it one further possible perception. If, by contrast, we would think existence through the pure category alone, no wonder we can name no mark by which to tell it apart from mere possibility.

Let our concept of an object, then, contain what and as much as it will; we must still step outside the concept in order to bestow existence upon the object. With objects of the senses this comes about through their connection with one or another of my perceptions according to empirical laws; whereas for objects of pure thinking there exists no means at all of cognizing their existence, since it would have to be cognized wholly _a priori_; our consciousness of any existence, however (whether immediate, by way of perception, or mediated by inferences that tie something to perception), belongs wholly and entirely to the unity of experience; and while an existence beyond this field cannot be pronounced downright impossible, it is a presupposition that we lack any means to justify.

{{{ 403 }}} The concept of a highest being is an idea very useful in many a respect; but just because it is mere idea, it is wholly unable, by means of itself alone, to broaden our cognition with regard to what exists. It cannot manage even so much as to instruct us {{ 630 }} about the possibility of anything further. The analytic mark of possibility, consisting in this, that bare positions (realities) generate no contradiction, cannot indeed be denied it; but since the linking of all real properties within one thing is a synthesis on whose possibility no _a priori_ judgment is open to us — the realities not being given to us in their specific character, and, even were that to happen, no judging at all taking place there, because for synthetic cognitions the mark of possibility must always be looked for in experience alone, to which, though, an idea's object can never belong — the celebrated Leibniz came nowhere near accomplishing what he flattered himself he had, namely, achieving _a priori_ insight into the possibility of so lofty an ideal being.

On the celebrated ontological (Cartesian) proof, then, which would derive a highest being's existence from concepts, all pains and labor are spent in vain; and a human being might as little grow richer in insight from bare ideas as a merchant grows in wealth were he minded, in order to better his circumstances, to hang some zeros onto his cash balance.

[^*]: A concept is possible in every case where it does not contradict itself. This is the logical mark of possibility, and by it the concept's object is marked off from the _nihil negativum_. It can nonetheless remain an empty concept so long as the objective reality of the synthesis whereby the concept is brought forth is not separately established; and this, as was shown above, rests at all times on principles of possible experience, not on the principle of analysis (the law of contradiction). Here is a warning against inferring straightaway from the possibility of concepts (logical) to the possibility of things (real).
