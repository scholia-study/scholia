## {{ 536 }} Section 8. Regulative principle of pure reason in respect of the cosmological ideas.

Since, by the cosmological principle of totality, no greatest member of the conditions ranged in series within a sensible world—taken as a thing existing in its own right—is ever ***given***, but can at most be ***set as a task*** as we regress through them: the principle of pure reason under discussion, its sense once rectified in this manner, still keeps its full legitimacy—not, to be sure, as an ***axiom*** bidding us conceive the totality in the object as actual, {{{ 349 }}} but as a ***problem*** posed to the understanding, hence to the subject, requiring it to set going and pursue, answerably to the completeness lodged in the idea, the regress through the conditions leading up to a given conditioned.

For within sensibility—that is, in space and in time—each condition we can reach while expounding appearances that are given proves in turn to be itself conditioned: for such appearances are no self-subsistent objects wherein the wholly unconditioned might conceivably have its place, but are nothing more than empirical representations, which have invariably to locate their condition in intuition, this condition fixing their place in space or in time. Properly speaking, then, the principle of reason amounts to nothing but a ***rule*** that enjoins, across the conditions belonging in series to given appearances, a regress {{ 537 }} which is at no point allowed to come to rest upon anything utterly unconditioned. It is accordingly no principle governing how experience is possible, nor how objects of the senses come to be cognized empirically, and so it is no principle of the understanding, since any experience stays shut within the limits set for it (in keeping with the intuition that is given); neither is it a ***constitutive principle*** of reason for stretching the concept of the sensible world past every experience that could be had: it is, rather, a principle for pressing experience onward and widening it as far as ever it will go, one under which no boundary met with empirically need count as a boundary that is absolute—in short, a principle of reason that ***as a rule*** lays down what we are to do ***in the regress***, while it ***does not anticipate*** whatever may, ***in the object*** itself, stand given in advance of any regress at all. Hence I call it a ***regulative*** principle of reason—whereas the principle asserting an absolute totality of the conditions in their series, on the footing that it is given in itself within the object (that is, within the appearances), would come out as a constitutive cosmological principle, and it is exactly this drawing of a line that lets me expose the emptiness of such a principle, so as to head off what otherwise happens without fail (by way of transcendental subreption): the crediting of an idea, whose office is merely to serve as a rule, with objective reality of its own.

Now to pin down aright what this rule of pure reason means, one should observe at the outset that it is in no position to tell us ***what {{ 538 }} the object may be***, but only ***in what manner the empirical regress ought to be set going***, if we are to reach a complete concept of that object. For were the first of these the case, the rule would turn into a constitutive principle, and a thing of that sort can never issue from pure reason. One can therefore not in the least intend, by means of it, to pronounce the conditions that lead {{{ 350 }}} up to a given conditioned to be, taken by themselves, either finite or endless in their series; for so doing would let a bare idea of absolute totality, fashioned wholly out of itself, think an object that no experience can ever supply, since a run of appearances would thereby be endowed with an objective reality owing nothing to the empirical synthesis. All the idea of reason does, then, is lay down for the regressive synthesis running through the conditions a rule whereby that synthesis moves on from the conditioned, passing through every condition subordinate to another, toward the unconditioned—though the latter is at no time attained. For nowhere in experience does the strictly unconditioned come to be met with.

With this aim in view, the first thing is to fix with precision what the synthesis of a series comes to, given that such a synthesis is never brought to completion. For this end people ordinarily fall back on a pair of expressions supposed to mark off something here, though no one is really able to state on what the marking-off rests. Among mathematicians one hears exclusively of a _progressus in infinitum_. {{ 539 }} Those who inquire into concepts—the philosophers—would, in that phrase's stead, admit as valid only the term _progressus in indefinitum_. Rather than linger over weighing the misgiving that counseled the latter to draw such a line, or over whether the drawing of it proves serviceable or barren, I shall undertake to settle exactly what these concepts amount to as regards the end I have in view.

It is quite proper to say of a straight line that one may prolong it endlessly, and in this instance telling apart an infinite advance and one reaching indeterminably far (_progressus in indefinitum_) would amount to a hollow piece of hairsplitting. For granted that, when the direction runs: carry a line further, it does read more accurately to append _in indefinitum_ rather than _in infinitum_—the first amounting to no more than: lengthen it as far as you ***care*** to, whereas the second says: you ***must*** never leave off lengthening it (and that is just not what is meant here)—still, so long as the discussion turns solely on **what can be done**, the first expression hits the mark exactly; for you are able to keep on enlarging the line without any end. And matters stand the same way in every case where the talk concerns merely the progressus—the passing onward, that is, from what conditions to what is conditioned; among appearances this possible passage runs on to infinity. Starting from a couple who are parents, you may proceed endlessly downward along the line of begetting {{ 540 }} and may perfectly well suppose that in the world it really does run on in that way. For at this point reason has no call for any absolute totality {{{ 351 }}} of the series, seeing that it does not lay such totality down beforehand as a condition, as though it were handed over (_datum_), but only as a further conditioned item, one that is merely such as can be supplied (_dabile_) and gets appended without any end.

The task wears an altogether different look when the question is this: how far does the regress reach that climbs from the conditioned already given up to its conditions taken in series; may I pronounce it **a going-back into the infinite**, or only a going-back that stretches ***indeterminably far*** (_in indefinitum_); and may I therefore, beginning with people now alive, mount upward without end through the line of their forebears, or is all that can be said this: that however far back my steps have carried me, at no point does an empirical ground turn up for treating the line as limited somewhere, so that I stand both entitled and bound to go on hunting, beyond each of the earliest fathers, for a progenitor of his—yet without being entitled to take one for granted.

My contention, then, is this: once the whole has been furnished in empirical intuition, the regress across the inner conditions ranged within it runs on to infinity. But where merely a single link of the series is on hand, one from which the regress toward absolute totality has yet to get under way: there occurs nothing beyond a going-back into indeterminate breadth (_in indefinitum_). So {{ 541 }} of the dividing of a portion of matter (a body) presented within its own bounds one has to say: it goes on to infinity. For this matter lies before us entire, and hence, together with each of the parts it can have, is furnished in empirical intuition. Since, now, what conditions this whole is a part of it, and what conditions that part is again a part lying within the part, and so forth, while throughout this regress of taking-apart no unconditioned—that is, indivisible—link of these serial conditions is ever come upon, there is not merely nowhere any empirical warrant for halting the division; rather, the still further links yielded by the division to be carried on are themselves, in advance of that continued dividing, given empirically—which is to say, the dividing proceeds to infinity. The line of forebears belonging to some particular person, by contrast, is nowhere within a possible experience laid out in its absolute totality; and yet the regress does climb from any link of this begetting to a higher one, so that we light upon no empirical bound that would set out some link as unconditioned in the strict sense. Since, however, the links that might here supply the condition do not, ahead of the regress, already lie within the empirical intuition of the whole: this regress does not run to infinity (as the dividing of what is given does), but into {{{ 352 }}} an indeterminable breadth in the hunt for additional links to append to those given, which in their turn are always themselves given only as conditioned.

{{ 542 }} In neither of the two cases—the _regressus in infinitum_ no more than the _in indefinitum_—does one look upon the conditions in their series as given in the object as infinite. What we have here are not things existing in their own right but simply appearances, which, as one another's conditions, get furnished nowhere except in the regress itself. The question, accordingly, is no longer how large these serial conditions are in their own right, whether finite or infinite—for in their own right they are nothing at all—but rather how we go about the empirical regress and how far we ought to press it on. And on this head a considerable difference shows itself as regards the rule governing such advance. Where the whole has been supplied empirically, it is ***possible*** to make one's way back ***into the infinite*** through the inner conditions ranged within it. But where that whole is not supplied, and is instead to be furnished only through an empirical regress, all I can say is: it stays ***possible without end*** to move on to conditions higher still in the series. In the earlier case my statement could run: there stand present, empirically furnished, always more links than I ever get to by the regress (the taking-apart); in the second, however: within the regress I can push on yet further still, for no link is furnished empirically as unconditioned in the strict sense, and so a higher link is left standing as always possible, and with it the demand for that link as something required. There it was needful to ***come upon*** more links of the series, whereas here it is always needful to ***ask after*** more of them, since no experience {{ 543 }} ***sets an absolute bound***. For either you possess no perception that draws a flat limit around your empirical regress—in which case you are not to hold that regress finished—or you do possess a perception of the sort that bounds your series, and then it cannot rank as one of the members already traversed (since what ***does the bounding*** has to be kept apart from what ***gets bounded by it***), so that you are obliged to prosecute your regress onward to this condition as well, and thus without cease.
