## Chapter 1. Of the clue to the discovery of all pure concepts of the understanding

When a faculty of cognition is brought into play, various concepts emerge according to the manifold occasions, which make this faculty recognizable and which allow themselves to be gathered in a more or less detailed essay, once their observation has been undertaken for a longer period of time or with greater astuteness. Where this investigation will be completed can never be determined with certainty according to this, as it were, mechanical procedure. Moreover, the concepts that one discovers only by occasion in this way do not reveal themselves in any order and systematic {{ 92 }} unity, but are instead ultimately only paired according to similarities and arranged in series according to the magnitude of their content, from the simple to the more composite, which are brought about in a way that is anything but systematic, although it is in a certain way methodical.

Transcendental philosophy has the advantage, but also the obligation, to seek out its concepts according to a principle, because they spring pure and unmixed from the understanding as an absolute unity, and therefore must themselves be connected with one another according to one concept or idea {{{ 85 }}}. Such a connection, however, provides a rule by which the proper place for every pure concept of the understanding and the completeness of all of them together can be determined _a priori_, all of which would otherwise depend on whim or chance.
