## Section 1. On ideas in general

For all the abundance our languages possess, a thinking mind is often left embarrassed for the very expression that would fit its concept exactly, and lacking which it can render itself, neither to others nor even to itself {{ 369 }}, properly intelligible. To coin new words is to lay claim to legislating in language—a pretension that rarely succeeds; and before one resorts to so desperate an expedient, the prudent course is to cast about in some dead and learned tongue to discover whether this concept, along with a fitting expression for it, may not already be present there; and even should the old usage of that word have been rendered somewhat wavering through the incaution of those who first framed it, it is still preferable to pin down the meaning that was distinctively its own (granting that it may remain uncertain whether precisely this same sense was intended back then) than to ruin one's enterprise outright by making oneself unintelligible.

For this very reason, should there happen to exist just one solitary word for a given concept—a word that in its already established usage fits that concept exactly, and whose separation from other cognate concepts carries great weight—the sensible thing is not to handle it wastefully, nor to deploy it merely for variety's sake as a synonym for others, but instead to guard carefully {{{ 246 }}} its own proper signification; for otherwise it readily comes about that, once the expression ceases to hold our attention in any special way and sinks from view amid the throng of others of widely differing meaning, the very thought is lost as well that it, and it alone, might have preserved.

{{ 370 }} ***Plato*** made use of the term ***idea*** in a manner that plainly reveals he meant by it something which is not merely never drawn from the senses, but which even rises far above those concepts of the understanding that engaged ***Aristotle***, seeing that in experience one never lights upon anything congruent with it. For Plato the ideas are archetypes of the things themselves, not merely keys to possible experiences in the way the categories are. On his view they streamed forth from the highest reason, from which they passed to human reason, which, however, no longer stands today in its pristine condition, but must with effort summon back the old ideas, now heavily darkened, by means of a recollection (which is what philosophy is). I do not wish here to embark on any literary inquiry with a view to establishing the sense the sublime philosopher bound up with his expression. I note only that it is nothing whatever out of the ordinary—in everyday talk no less than in written works—to understand an author better than he understood himself, through a comparison of the thoughts he voices about his subject, since he failed to fix his concept adequately and thus at times spoke, or indeed thought, in opposition to his own purpose.

Plato observed quite well that our faculty of cognition feels a need far loftier than merely, in keeping with synthetic unity {{ 371 }}, to spell appearances out so as to make them legible as experience, and that our reason, of its own nature, lifts itself toward cognitions that reach so far that no object experience is able to supply could ever prove congruent with them—cognitions that for all that possess a reality of their own and are in no way mere fabrications of the brain.

It was above all across the whole domain of the practical that Plato located his ideas,[^*] that is, in whatever rests upon freedom—freedom that on its side falls under cognitions constituting a {{{ 247 }}} distinctive product of reason. Anyone bent on drawing the concepts of virtue out of experience, anyone minded to take what can at most furnish an example for imperfect illustration and set it up as the model for the wellspring of cognition (as, in truth, no small number have done), would turn virtue into an equivocal monstrosity, shifting with time and circumstance and serviceable for no rule whatever. In contrast, everyone becomes inwardly aware that, {{ 372 }} when someone is set before him as a model of virtue, he yet retains the genuine original solely within his own head, against which he measures this alleged model and by reference to which alone he rates it. This, however, is the idea of virtue, in relation to which all conceivable objects of experience do duty indeed as examples (evidence that what the concept of reason demands is, to some degree, achievable), yet not as archetypes. The fact that no human being will ever conduct himself in a manner fully commensurate with what the pure idea of virtue holds within it does nothing at all to prove there is something chimerical in this thought. For all the same, every judgment upon moral worth or worthlessness is possible only through the mediation of this idea; consequently it lies of necessity at the basis of every advance toward moral perfection, however far the obstacles lodged in human nature—whose magnitude admits of no determination—may keep us at a distance from it.

***Plato's Republic*** has passed into a byword, taken as a glaring specimen of a perfection merely dreamed up, one able to lodge nowhere but in the brain of the idle thinker, and Brucker finds it laughable that the philosopher should have asserted a prince would never rule well without partaking of the ideas. Yet it would be the wiser course to follow up this thought more closely and (at those points where that admirable man abandons us to our own devices) to set it in the {{ 373 }} light through fresh endeavors, rather than to push it aside as worthless on the utterly paltry and pernicious pretext that the thing cannot be done. A constitution affording the ***greatest human freedom*** under laws so framed that ***the freedom of each can coexist with that of everyone else*** (not the greatest happiness, for that will ensue of its own accord anyway) is at the very least a necessary idea that must be laid at the foundation not only of the {{{ 248 }}} initial sketch of a political constitution but of every one of its laws, and in drawing which one must at the start leave out of account the present impediments, which perhaps arise not so much unavoidably from human nature as, rather, from having neglected the authentic ideas in the framing of law. For nothing more injurious, and nothing more unworthy of a philosopher, can be turned up than the mob-like appeal to a supposedly recalcitrant experience, which after all would have no existence whatever had those provisions been instituted in good time according to the ideas, and had crude concepts not—for the very reason that they were scooped up out of experience—thwarted, in their place, every good design. The more closely legislation and administration were fashioned in agreement with this idea, the scarcer punishments would surely grow, and it is then wholly rational (as Plato contends) to hold that under a flawless arrangement of them not a single such penalty would be called for. Now granted that this last may never {{ 374 }} be realized, the idea is nonetheless perfectly sound that erects this maximum as an archetype, so that by its measure the lawful constitution of humankind may be brought forever closer to the greatest attainable perfection. For what the supreme degree may be at which humankind must halt, and thus how wide the chasm that inevitably persists between the idea and its realization may turn out to be, is a thing that no one is able, or entitled, to fix—precisely because it is a matter of freedom, which can overstep whatever limit one might name.

But it is not only in the field where human reason exhibits a true causality—where ideas turn into operative causes (of deeds and of their objects), namely in the moral sphere—but with regard to nature itself as well that Plato justly discerns clear proofs of its origin from ideas. A growing plant, an animal, the orderly arrangement of the cosmic edifice (and so, presumably, the entire order of nature) make it plain that nothing but ideas could render them possible; that no single creature, taken under the particular conditions of its existence, does match the idea of the most perfect of its kind (any more than the human being matches the idea of humanity, which he even carries within his own soul as the archetype of his deeds); that those ideas are all the same, within the highest understanding, individual, immutable, through and through determinate, and the primordial causes of things, and that only the totality of their interconnection {{ 375 }} throughout the universe is fully adequate, wholly and solely, to that idea. If {{{ 249 }}} one strips away what is overdone in the phrasing, then the philosopher's flight of spirit—mounting up from the corporeal contemplation of the physical side of the world-order to its architectonic conjunction according to purposes, that is, according to ideas—represents an effort worthy of respect and of emulation; but as concerns whatever bears on the principles of morality, of legislation, and of religion, where the ideas are what first render experience itself (of the good) possible, though they can never be given full expression within it, there is a merit entirely its own, one that goes unacknowledged solely because it is appraised by those very empirical rules whose validity as principles was to have been cancelled by precisely those ideas. For where nature is in question, experience places the rule in our hands and is the wellspring of truth; where the moral laws are in question, however, experience is (alas!) the mother of mere semblance, and nothing is more culpable than to borrow the laws governing ***what I ought to do*** from, or to seek to hem them in by, that ***which is done***.

In lieu of all these reflections, whose proper carrying-through is what in fact makes up the peculiar dignity of philosophy, we now occupy ourselves with a less resplendent, though not therefore meritless {{ 376 }}, labor—namely, that of rendering level and structurally sound the ground beneath those majestic moral edifices—ground riddled with all manner of molehills thrown up by a reason that tunnels fruitlessly, though in high hopes of treasure, and that leaves the edifice above unsafe. It is therefore the transcendental employment of pure reason, its principles and its ideas, that we are now obliged to become precisely acquainted with, if we are to gauge and appraise, as we should, the influence of pure reason and its worth. Yet before I lay this preliminary introduction aside, I entreat those who hold philosophy dear (which says more than one ordinarily encounters), should they find themselves persuaded by the present remarks and by what is to follow, to take the term ***idea*** into their keeping in accordance with its original meaning, lest it drift henceforth in among the remaining expressions by which every sort of representation is habitually labelled in heedless disorder, and lest the science suffer a loss thereby. We are, after all, in no want of designations aptly matched to each kind of representation, without our needing to trespass upon the domain of another. Here is a graduated ladder of them. The genus is ***representation*** as such (_repraesentatio_). {{{ 250 }}} Beneath it falls representation accompanied by consciousness (_perceptio_). A ***perception*** bearing solely on the subject, as a modification of its state, is ***sensation*** (_sensatio_); an objective perception is {{ 377 }} ***cognition*** (_cognitio_). This last is either ***intuition*** or ***concept*** (_intuitus vel conceptus_). The one bears directly upon its object and is singular; the other only mediately, by way of a mark that may be shared among several things. A concept, again, is either ***empirical*** or a ***pure concept***, and a pure concept, to the extent that it takes its rise within the understanding alone (and not within any pure image belonging to sensibility), bears the name _Notio_. A concept assembled out of notions, one that outstrips the possibility of experience, is the ***idea***, or concept of reason. To anyone who has once grown at home with this distinction, it is bound to prove insufferable to hear the representation of the color red termed an idea. It cannot even properly be styled a notion (a concept belonging to the understanding).

[^*]: He did, to be sure, stretch his concept to cover speculative cognitions as well, so long as these were given in pure and thoroughly _a priori_ fashion, and even to cover mathematics, notwithstanding that the latter locates its object nowhere save in ***possible*** experience. In this I am unable to go along with him, no more than in his mystical derivation of these ideas or in the extravagances whereby he as good as hypostatized them, even though the elevated language he resorted to in this domain is perfectly amenable to a gentler reading, one better attuned to the nature of things.
