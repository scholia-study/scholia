## II. Solution of the cosmological idea of the totality of the division of a given whole in intuition.

In dividing a whole given in intuition, I advance from a conditioned thing back to the conditions on which its possibility rests. Dividing the parts in turn (_subdivisio_ or _decompositio_) amounts to a regress along the series formed by such conditions. Only if the regress could arrive at ***simple*** parts would the absolute totality of this ***series*** {{{ 358 }}} be given. But if all the parts remain divisible again and again throughout a continuously advancing decomposition, then the division — that is, the regress — proceeds from the conditioned to its conditions _in infinitum_, for the conditions (namely the parts) already lie {{ 552 }} within the conditioned itself, and since the latter is given entire in an intuition shut in between its boundaries, they too are, one and all, given right along with it. The regress here may accordingly not be styled a mere retreat _in indefinitum_, as the earlier cosmological idea alone allowed, where I had to pass from the conditioned to conditions that lay beyond it, conditions therefore not co-given with it at once but first supervening in the empirical regress. Despite this, one is in no way entitled to say, of a whole divisible to infinity in this manner: ***that it consists of infinitely many parts***. For even though every part is contained within the whole's intuition, that intuition does ***not*** contain the ***entire division*** within it, a division residing solely in the ongoing decomposition, or in the regress itself, which is what first renders the ***series*** actual. Since this regress is now infinite, all the members (parts) it reaches are indeed contained in the given whole as ***aggregates***, yet not the entire ***series*** of the ***division***, which is successively infinite and never ***whole***, and can therefore exhibit no infinite multitude, nor any combining of such a multitude into a whole.

This general reminder can, to begin with, be applied to space with great ease. Any space intuited within its limits is a whole of this kind, whose parts, under every decomposition, remain spaces once again, and for that reason it admits of division to infinity.

{{ 553 }} From this the second application follows just as naturally, namely to an outer appearance (body) shut in within its boundaries. The divisibility of the body is founded upon that of space, which is what makes a body possible as an extended whole. The body is thus divisible to infinity, yet without thereby consisting of infinitely many parts.

It does indeed appear that, because a body must be represented as a substance in space, it will differ from space in this very respect as regards the law of the divisibility of space; for one might at most concede that decomposition in the latter can never abolish all composition, since then even the whole of space, {{{ 359 }}} which apart from that has nothing self-subsistent about it, would cease (which is impossible); but that, were all composition of matter cancelled in thought, nothing whatever should remain over, seems not to square with the concept of a substance, which ought properly to be the subject of all composition and would have to persist in its elements even if the linkage of them in space, through which they constitute a body, were cancelled. Yet with that which ***in the appearance is termed substance***, matters stand otherwise than one would suppose of a thing in itself through a pure concept of the understanding. The former is no absolute subject, but an abiding image of sensibility and nothing but an intuition, in which nothing unconditioned {{ 554 }} is met with anywhere at all.

Now although this rule of progression to infinity applies beyond doubt when we subdivide an appearance regarded as nothing but a filling of space, it nevertheless cannot hold if we also mean to extend it to the multitude of parts already, in a certain manner, set off within the given whole, whereby these constitute a _quantum discretum_. To suppose that in every articulated (organized) whole each part is again articulated, and that in this way, on breaking the parts down to infinity, one keeps meeting fresh artfully formed parts — in a word, that the whole is articulated to infinity — simply cannot be thought, though it can well be thought that the parts of matter may be articulated to infinity in the course of their decomposition. For the fact that a given appearance in space divides without end rests entirely on this, that thereby merely the divisibility is given — that is, a multitude of parts in itself wholly indeterminate — while the parts themselves come to be given and determined solely by means of the subdivision; in brief, that the whole is not, in itself, already partitioned. Hence the division is able to fix within it a multitude reaching just as far as one cares to press on in the regress of the division. In an organic body articulated to infinity, on the other hand, the whole is, precisely through this {{ 555 }} concept, already represented as partitioned, and a multitude of parts determinate in itself, yet infinite, is encountered in it ahead of all regress of the division; whereby one falls into contradiction with oneself, since this infinite enfolding is taken to be a series that can never be brought to completion (infinite) and yet, all the same, is regarded as completed in a single act of comprehension. Infinite division marks out the appearance merely as a _quantum continuum_ {{{ 360 }}} and is inseparable from the filling of space, precisely because it is in this very filling that the ground of infinite divisibility lies. But the moment something is taken to be a _quantum discretum_, the multitude of unities in it stands determined, and is thus on every occasion equal to a number. How far, then, the organization in an articulated body may reach can be made out by experience alone, and even should it with certainty never attain any inorganic part, such parts must at any rate lie within possible experience. Yet just how far an appearance admits of transcendental division at all is in no way a matter of experience, but a principle of reason: never to regard the empirical regress in the breaking-down of what is extended — as the nature of this appearance requires — as being simply complete.
