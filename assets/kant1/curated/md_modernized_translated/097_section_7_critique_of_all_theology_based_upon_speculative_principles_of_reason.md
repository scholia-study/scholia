## {{{ 420 }}} {{ 659 }} Section 7. Critique of all theology based upon speculative principles of reason

Should I mean by theology the cognition of the primordial being, then such cognition arises either out of sheer reason (_theologia rationalis_) or out of revelation (_revelata_). The first of these conceives its object in one of two ways: purely by reason, through wholly transcendental concepts alone (_ens originarium_, _realissimum_, _ens entium_) — this being termed ***transcendental*** theology — or through a concept lifted from nature (from our own soul), namely as the highest intelligence, in which case it would have to bear the name ***natural*** theology. Whoever allows for a transcendental theology only is termed a ***deist***; whoever grants a natural theology as well, a ***theist***. The deist concedes that the existence of a primordial being may, at a stretch, be cognized by reason alone, our concept of it staying merely transcendental all the while — that is, the concept simply of a being possessed of all reality, though one admitting of no closer determination. The theist, by contrast, maintains that reason can specify the object further on an analogy with nature, namely as a being that, by way of understanding and freedom, holds within itself the original ground of all remaining things. Under this heading, then, the one pictures merely a ***world-cause*** (whether operating through its nature's necessity or {{ 660 }} through freedom is left open), the other an ***author of the world***.

Transcendental theology, for its part, is either the sort that means to trace the primordial being's existence back to experience as such (fixing nothing further about the world it belongs to), which earns the name ***cosmotheology***, or else it supposes that it can cognize that existence out of bare concepts unassisted by any experience whatever, and so is dubbed ***ontotheology***.

***Natural theology*** reasons to the attributes and existence of a world-author from the make-up, order, and unity met with in this world — a world within which a twofold causality, together with its rule, has to be granted, namely nature and freedom. {{{ 421 }}} From this world, accordingly, it climbs up to the highest intelligence, whether as the principle of all natural, or of all ethical, order and perfection. In the first case its name is ***physicotheology***, in the second ***moral theology***[^*].

Now since we habitually take the concept of God to mean not just some blindly-working eternal nature at the root of things, but a supreme being {{ 661 }} meant to be, through understanding and freedom, the originator of things — and since it is this concept alone that holds any interest for us — one might, on a strict reckoning, refuse the ***deist*** any belief in God at all, leaving him nothing beyond the assertion of a primordial being or highest cause. Still, seeing that a man ought not to be accused of meaning flatly to deny a thing simply because he does not dare affirm it, the gentler and fairer course is to put it thus: the ***deist*** places his belief in a ***God***, whereas the ***theist*** believes in a ***living God*** (_summam intelligentiam_). Let us now set about tracking down the possible wellsprings of all these ventures of reason.

I shall be satisfied here to characterize theoretical cognition as that whereby I cognize ***what there is***, and practical cognition as that whereby I represent to myself ***what ought to exist***. On this footing, the theoretical use of reason is the one through which I cognize _a priori_ (as necessary) that a thing is; the practical use, the one through which it is cognized _a priori_ what ought to take place. Now supposing that either the being or the coming-to-pass of something is beyond all doubt certain, and yet only conditioned, then the condition for it may be either strictly necessary in some determinate shape, or else assumed as no more than optional and contingent. In the first case the condition gets postulated (_per thesin_), in the second merely supposed (_per hypothesin_). Because practical laws do exist that are utterly {{ 662 }} necessary (the moral ones), it follows that, should these require as a necessary presupposition some existence to serve as the condition under which their ***binding*** power is possible, that existence has to be ***postulated*** — and this for the reason that the conditioned thing, from which one reasons to this determinate condition, is itself {{{ 422 }}} cognized _a priori_ to be flatly necessary. In what follows we shall establish, concerning the moral laws, that they do more than presuppose a supreme being's existence: standing, as they do, on wholly necessary footing in another regard, they likewise postulate that existence by right, albeit of course only in practice; for the moment, though, we lay this line of inference aside.

Given that, once the talk is solely of what there is (not of what ought to be), the conditioned handed to us in experience always gets thought of as contingent too, the condition attaching to it can never on that basis be cognized as flatly necessary, but figures only as a presupposition — relatively necessary, or better ***needful***, yet taken in itself and _a priori_ a wholly arbitrary one — serving reason's cognition of the conditioned. Hence, were the absolute necessity of some thing to be cognized within theoretical cognition, this could come about only out of concepts _a priori_, and in no way as that of a cause bearing on an existence handed over through experience.

A theoretical cognition counts as ***speculative*** when it bears on an object, or on concepts of an object, of a kind that {{ 663 }} no experience can ever reach. Set over against it stands ***cognition of nature***, which bears on no objects, and no predicates of them, save those a possible experience is able to supply.

To reason from what comes to pass (the empirically contingent), as an effect, back to a cause is a principle proper to cognition of nature, though not to the speculative kind. For if this principle be stripped of its office as one that carries within it the condition of possible experience in general, and — with everything empirical thrown out — one wishes to pronounce it of the contingent as such, then there survives not a shred of warrant for any such synthetic proposition by which one might make out how I am to move from a thing that is over to something wholly other than it (labelled cause); indeed, in a use so purely speculative, the concept of a cause, no less than that of the contingent, forfeits all the significance whose objective reality might be rendered intelligible _in concreto_.

When, therefore, one reasons from the existence of the ***things*** in the world back to their cause, this move belongs not to the ***natural*** but to the ***speculative*** employment of reason: for the former refers to a cause not the things themselves (substances) but only that ***which happens*** — hence their ***states*** — as empirically contingent; whereas that the substance itself (matter) should be contingent in respect of its existence would have to rank as a purely speculative rational cognition {{{ 423 }}} {{ 664 }}. But suppose the talk turned merely on the world's form, on the manner of its interconnection and the changes therein, while I nonetheless meant to reason from that to a cause set wholly apart from the world: here again the judgment would be one of purely speculative reason, since in this case the object is no object of any possible experience at all. And then the principle of causality, valid as it is only inside the field of experiences and, beyond that field, devoid of any use — indeed of any significance — would be wrenched entirely away from its proper vocation.

My contention, then, is this: that every attempt at a purely speculative employment of reason in matters of theology is utterly barren and, by its very make-up, null and void, while the principles governing reason's use in nature lead to no theology at all — so that, failing moral laws laid down as groundwork or taken as a guiding thread, a theology of reason simply cannot exist anywhere. For all the understanding's synthetic principles hold good only immanently; yet cognizing a supreme being calls for a transcendent deployment of them, and for that our understanding is in no way outfitted. Were the empirically valid law of causality to carry us up to the primordial being, that being would have to figure among the chain of objects of experience — in which case, like every appearance, it would once more be conditioned itself. But even granting the leap {{ 665 }} clear past experience's frontier by way of the dynamical law linking effects to their causes: what concept could a proceeding of that sort ever get us? Nothing remotely like a concept of a supreme being, seeing that experience never lays before us the largest of all possible effects (the one meant to testify to its cause). Were we to be allowed — purely so as to leave no void standing in our reason — to make good this want of complete determination by a bare idea of highest perfection and original necessity, that might well be conceded as an indulgence, yet cannot be exacted as the due of some proof there is no resisting. The physico-theological proof might thus, perhaps, lend some force to other proofs (should any be available), tying speculation as it does to intuition: but on its own it does more to ready the understanding for theological cognition, and to aim it straight and naturally that way, than it could ever ***single-handedly*** bring the business to completion.

{{{ 424 }}} One sees plainly enough from all this that transcendental questions allow of none but transcendental answers — that is, answers drawn from concepts _a priori_ alone, carrying not the least empirical alloy. Yet the question before us is plainly synthetic, calling for our cognition to be stretched past every frontier of experience — reaching, namely, to the existence of a being {{ 666 }} meant to answer to our bare idea, a being no experience can ever match. And by our earlier proofs, every synthetic cognition _a priori_ is made possible in one way only, by its giving voice to the formal conditions of a possible experience, so that all principles carry a validity that is merely immanent — that is, they bear solely on objects of empirical cognition, or appearances. So here too a transcendental procedure achieves nothing where the theology of a merely speculative reason is at stake.

But should someone prefer to throw all the foregoing proofs of the Analytic into doubt rather than surrender his settled trust in the weight of grounds so long relied upon, still he can hardly decline to meet the challenge when I ask that he at the very least give an account of how, and by what manner of illumination, he supposes he can vault over all possible experience on the strength of naked ideas. Fresh proofs, or refurbished versions of the old ones, I would beg to be spared. For granted that there is not, in truth, much choosing to be done here — since in the last resort all the merely speculative proofs run together into one, the ontological — so that I need harbor no special dread of being pestered by any fruitfulness on the part of that sense-free reason's dogmatic partisans; and granted, moreover, that I have no wish (without fancying myself much of a combatant on that score) to turn down the summons {{ 667 }} to lay bare the fallacy in each attempt of the kind and so thwart its presumption: even so, the hope of better luck is, among people once habituated to dogmatic persuasions, never wholly removed; and I therefore rest content with the one equitable requirement — that a person justify himself, in general terms and out of the very nature of the human understanding along with all its other sources of cognition, as to how he proposes to set about widening his cognition entirely _a priori_ and reaching it out to where no possible experience, and hence no means, avails to guarantee to any {{{ 425 }}} concept of our own devising its objective reality. By whatever road the understanding may have come to this concept, the existence of its object is still not to be dug out of it analytically, for cognition of the object's ***existence*** consists in just this: that the thing is set down, of itself, ***outside the thought***. It is, however, altogether out of the question to step, of one's own accord, beyond a concept and — short of following out the empirical linkage (which anyhow yields none but appearances at any turn) — to reach the discovery of fresh objects and extravagant beings.

Yet however far reason in its merely speculative employment may fall short of so lofty a design — the reaching, that is, of a supreme being's existence — it still renders here a very great {{ 668 }} service: to ***set right*** the cognition of that being, supposing such cognition can be had from some other quarter, to bring it into accord with itself and with every intelligible design, and to cleanse it of all that might run counter to the concept of a primordial being, as well as of every admixture of empirical restrictions.

Transcendental theology, for all its shortcomings, keeps nonetheless an important negative use, and it acts as a perpetual censor of our reason on every occasion when the latter is busy merely with pure ideas, which precisely on that account will tolerate no measuring-rod other than a transcendental one. For suppose that, at some point, in a further and perhaps practical bearing, the ***presupposition*** of a highest and all-sufficient being, as the supreme intelligence, were to make its validity good past all gainsaying: it would then be of the utmost consequence to fix this concept exactly on its transcendental side, as the concept of a necessary and supremely real being, to clear away whatever runs contrary to highest reality, whatever pertains to mere appearance (to anthropomorphism in the broader sense), and at one and the same time to sweep from the path every contrary assertion, be it ***atheistic*** or ***deistic*** or ***anthropomorphistic*** — a thing very readily done under such critical handling, since the very grounds that set {{ 669 }} before our eyes the impotence of human reason as regards asserting a being of the sort to exist are bound to serve equally for proving the ineptitude of each and every counter-assertion. For where is anyone to come by the insight, through reason's pure speculation, that no highest being exists as the primordial ground of all things {{{ 426 }}}, or that it bears none of those attributes which, judging by their effects, we picture to ourselves on an analogy with the dynamical realities of a thinking being, or that, on that last supposition, these attributes would have to lie under all the very limitations that sensibility inescapably fastens on the intelligences we are acquainted with through experience.

For reason's merely speculative use, therefore, the supreme being stays a bare, and yet ***faultless ideal*** — a concept that closes and crowns the whole of human cognition, whose objective reality can along this route be neither established nor overturned; and should a moral theology come to be, one able to make up this deficiency, then the transcendental theology that was hitherto merely problematic will prove how little it can be dispensed with, by fixing its concept and keeping up an unbroken censorship of a reason that sensibility deceives often enough and that does not always square with its own ideas. Necessity, infinity, unity, an existence outside the world (not as a world-soul), an eternity free of the conditions of time, an omnipresence free of {{ 670 }} the conditions of space, omnipotence, etc., are one and all transcendental predicates, and for that reason the purified concept of them, which every theology stands so badly in need of, is to be drawn from transcendental theology and nowhere else.

[^*]: Not, that is, theological morals; for the latter comprise ethical laws that ***presuppose*** the existence of a supreme ruler over the world, whereas moral theology, by contrast, is a conviction of the existence of a highest being, a conviction resting upon ethical laws.
