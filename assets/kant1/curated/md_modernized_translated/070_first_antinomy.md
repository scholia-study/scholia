## {{{ 294 }}} {{ 454 }} The Antinomy of Pure Reason

## First Conflict of the Transcendental Ideas.

## Thesis.

The world has a beginning in time, and as regards space it likewise lies confined within bounds.

## Proof.

For assume that, in respect of time, the world has no beginning: then, up to any given point in time, an eternity has run out, and with it an infinite series of the successive states of the world's things has gone by. Yet just here lies the infinity of a series — namely, that no successive synthesis can ever bring it to completion. An infinitely elapsed world-series is therefore impossible, and so a beginning of the world stands as a necessary condition of its existence — the point to be established first.

Turning to the ***second***, assume once more the reverse, and the world becomes an infinite given whole made up of things that exist all at once. Now the magnitude of a _quantum_ that no intuition supplies within certain bounds [^*] {{ 456 }} can be thought by us in no way but through a synthesis of the parts, and the totality of such a _quantum_ only through a synthesis carried to its end, or by adjoining the unit to itself over and over.[^**] Hence, to conceive the world that occupies every space as one whole, one would have to {{{ 296 }}} take the successive synthesis of an infinite world's parts to be finished — that is, an infinite time would have to count as spent in the tallying of all coexisting things; and this cannot be. It follows that an infinite aggregate of actual things can count neither as a given whole nor, therefore, as something ***given all at once***. A world is on this account ***not infinite*** in its spatial extension, but shut within its bounds — and this was the second point.

## {{{ 295 }}} {{ 455 }} Antithesis.

The world has neither a beginning nor spatial limits, but is infinite alike in respect of time and of space.

## Proof.

For posit the following: it has a beginning. Since a beginning is an existence ahead of which lies a time wherein the thing is not, some time must have run before it in which the world was not — that is, an empty time. But within an empty time nothing whatever can come to be: for no stretch of such a time carries in itself, ahead of any other, any distinguishing condition of existence over that of non-being (whether one supposes it to arise of itself or through some other cause). So although many a series of things may well begin within the world, the world itself can begin at no point, and is thus, in respect of past time, infinite.

As to the second point, grant first the reverse — namely, that the world is, in respect of space, finite and bounded: it then sits within an empty space that has no bounds. One would thereby meet with not merely a relation of things within ***space***, but also a relation of things ***to space***. But since the world is an absolute whole {{ 457 }} beyond which no object of intuition, and so no _correlatum_ of the world, is met with for it to stand toward, {{{ 297 }}} the world's relation to empty space would come to a relation of it ***toward no object at all***. Such a ***relation***, however — and with it the world's confinement by empty space — is nothing; the world is accordingly in no way bounded in space, that is, it is infinite as regards its extension.[^***]

## {{ 458 }} Remark on the First Antinomy.

## I. On the Thesis.

In framing these mutually opposed arguments I have looked for no sleight of hand by which to mount, as people say, a lawyer's proof — one that turns the adversary's incaution to its own profit, that is happy to let his appeal to a misread statute pass, and then rears its own unwarranted claims on the refutation of it. Every one of these proofs is instead derived from the nature of the case, with no use made of the advantage that the dogmatists' faulty inferences on either side might have afforded us.

I might equally have furnished a mere semblance of proof for the thesis by sending on ahead, in the dogmatists' habitual manner, a defective concept of the infinity of a given magnitude. ***Infinite*** names a magnitude than which none greater is possible (that is, none exceeding the multitude of a given unit that it contains). But no multitude is the largest, for one may forever adjoin a further unit or several. An infinite given magnitude, and thus a world infinite alike in its elapsed series and in its extension, {{{ 298 }}} cannot be; the world is therefore bounded on either hand. In this fashion I might have carried through my proof — save that the concept fails to square with what an infinite whole is taken to mean. It tells us nothing of how great the whole is, so that its concept is no concept of a maximum; {{ 460 }} it registers merely the whole's relation to some freely chosen unit, in respect of which it exceeds every number. As that unit is taken larger or smaller, the infinite would come out larger or smaller; but the infinitude itself, grounded as it is wholly in the relation to this given unit, would stay unaltered throughout — though the whole's absolute magnitude goes thereby quite uncognized, which is in any case not our concern here.

The genuine (transcendental) concept of infinity runs thus: that the successive synthesis of the unit in traversing a _quantum_ can never reach its end.[^****] What follows from this, and with full security, is that up to a given (the present) point in time no eternity of successive actual states can have run its course, and hence that the world must have had a beginning.

With the thesis's second part, the trouble of a series at once infinite and yet elapsed does fall away; for the manifold of a world infinite in extension is given ***all at once***. But to conceive the totality of such a multitude — since we can lean on no bounds that would of themselves mark this totality out in intuition — we are bound to render account {{{ 300 }}} of our concept, which here cannot pass from the whole down to the fixed multitude of the parts, but has to make out the possibility of a whole by way of the parts' successive synthesis. And because this synthesis would make up a series never to be finished: no totality can be conceived either ahead of it or, consequently, through it. For here the very concept of totality is the representation of a synthesis of the parts carried through to the end, and this carrying-through — hence the concept of it too — is impossible.

## {{ 459 }} II. On the Antithesis.

The proof that the given world-series and the sum-total of the world are infinite rests on this: that, were it otherwise, an empty time — and an empty space no less — would have to make up the world's boundary. Now I am well aware that shifts are looked for against this consequence, the claim being that a boundary of the world in respect of time and space is quite possible without one's needing to posit an absolute time before the world's beginning or an absolute space spread out beyond the actual world; which cannot be. With the latter half of this view, held by the philosophers of the Leibnizian {{{ 299 }}} school, I am altogether content. Space is nothing more than the form of outer intuition — no actual object open to outward intuition, and no _correlatum_ of the appearances, but the appearances' own form. Space, then, taken absolutely (on its own), can figure as nothing determinative in the existence of things, since it is no object whatever, but only the form that possible objects take. Things, then, as appearances do indeed determine space — that is, out of every predicate it might carry (magnitude and relation) they bring it about that this one or that belongs to actuality; conversely, though, space as a self-subsisting something cannot determine the actuality of things in respect of magnitude or shape, since it is in itself nothing actual. A space (full or empty)[^*****] may thus well be bounded by appearances, {{ 461 }} yet appearances cannot be bounded ***by an empty space*** lying outside them. Exactly this holds of time as well. Grant all of this, and it remains beyond dispute that one is bound to posit these two non-things — the empty space outside and the empty time before the world — the moment one posits a world-boundary, whether in respect of space or of time.

For the way out by which the consequence is dodged — the consequence, as we put it, that if the world has limits (in time and space) the infinite void must determine the existence of actual things as to their magnitude — comes down, on the quiet, to just this: that in place of a ***world of sense*** one imagines who knows what intelligible world, and in place of the first beginning (an existence ahead of which lies a time of non-being) one imagines an existence at large that ***presupposes no further condition within the world***, that in place of the boundary of extension one imagines ***bounds*** to the world-whole, and so slips clear of time and space. But here it is the _mundus phaenomenon_ alone, and its magnitude, that is at issue — something from whose supposed conditions of sensibility one cannot abstract in the slightest without cancelling its very being. The ***world of sense***, should it be bounded, {{{ 301 }}} lies of necessity within the infinite void. Should one wish to drop this, and thereby space as such, as the _a priori_ condition of the possibility of appearances, the whole ***world of sense*** drops with it. It is this, and this alone, that our problem hands us. The _mundus intelligibilis_ is nothing beyond the general concept of a world as such, in which one abstracts from every condition of intuiting it, and with respect to which, in consequence, not one synthetic proposition — affirming or denying — can be reached.

[^*]: An indeterminate _quantum_ we can intuit as a whole once it lies shut within bounds, without our having to build up its totality by measurement — that is, by the successive synthesis of its parts. For the bounds already determine the completeness, in that they lop off everything beyond.

[^**]: In this case the concept of totality comes to nothing beyond the representation of a synthesis of its parts carried to the end, since — being unable to lift the concept off the intuition of the whole (impossible here) — we can seize it only through the parts' synthesis, pressed to the completion of the infinite, at least in the idea.

[^***]: Space is nothing beyond the form of outer intuition (a formal intuition), and no actual object open to outward intuition. Space prior to all the things that determine it (that fill or bound it), or rather that yield an ***empirical intuition*** answering to its form, is — under the title of absolute space — nothing beyond the bare possibility of outer appearances, so far as these either exist in themselves or may still accrue to given appearances. ***Empirical intuition***, then, is no compound of appearances and space (of perception together with empty intuition). Neither is the _correlatum_ of the other within the synthesis; they are merely joined in one and the same empirical intuition, as its matter and its form. Should one set one of these two components apart from the other (space beyond every appearance), there spring from it assorted empty determinations of outer intuition that are yet no possible perceptions — for instance, the world's motion or rest in infinite empty space, a determination of how the two stand to one another that can never be perceived and is thus the predicate of a mere thing of thought.

[^****]: By this it holds a multitude (of a given unit) exceeding every number — which is the mathematical concept of the infinite.

[^*****]: One readily sees that the point meant here is this: ***empty space, so far as it is bounded by appearances***, and hence the space ***inside the world***, does not, at least, run counter to the transcendental principles, and may on their score be allowed (though its possibility is not for that reason at once asserted).
