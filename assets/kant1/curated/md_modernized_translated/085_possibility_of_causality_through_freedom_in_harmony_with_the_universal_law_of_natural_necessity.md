## {{ 566 }} Possibility of causality through freedom, in harmony with the universal law of natural necessity.

I term ***intelligible*** that which, in an object of the senses, is not itself an appearance. If, then, whatever has to be viewed within the sensory world as appearance possesses, in its own self, a power that is no object of sensible intuition and yet by which it can be the cause of appearances: then the causality of this being admits of being regarded from two sides—as ***intelligible*** with regard to its ***action***, this being a thing in itself, and as ***sensible*** with regard to its ***effects***, taken as an appearance within the sensory world. Of the power possessed by such a subject we would, on this basis, frame a concept of its causality that is at once empirical and intellectual, both aspects concurring in one and the same effect. To think the power of an object of the senses under such a twofold aspect conflicts with none of the concepts we are obliged to frame regarding appearances and regarding a possible experience. For, since these appearances, not being things in their own right, must rest upon a transcendental object that determines them as nothing more than representations, nothing stands in the way of our ascribing to this transcendental object, over and above the property {{ 567 }} whereby it appears, a ***causality*** as well that is no appearance, even though its ***effect*** is met with in the appearance nonetheless. Yet every efficient cause must possess a **character**—that is to say, a law of its causality—for lacking this it would be no cause whatever. And so, in a subject belonging to the sensory world, we would in the first place find an ***empirical character***, by which its actions, qua appearances, would hang together with other appearances entirely under unvarying laws of nature, could be derived from those appearances as their conditions, and would thereby, joined with them, form links in one single series of the order of nature. In the second place, one would have to concede to it {{{ 367 }}} an ***intelligible character*** as well, by virtue of which it is, to be sure, the cause of those same actions as appearances, while it itself stands under no conditions of sensibility and is no appearance at all. One might likewise name the former the character of such a thing as it is in appearance, and the latter the character of the thing as it is in itself.

Now this acting subject, taken according to its intelligible character, would be subject to no temporal conditions whatsoever, for time conditions only appearances and not things as they are in themselves. Within it {{ 568 }} no ***action*** would ***come to be*** or ***pass out of being***, and hence it would not fall under the law binding all determination of time and everything mutable—namely, that whatever ***happens*** finds its cause ***in the appearances*** (of the preceding state). To put it in a word, so far as its causality is intellectual, it would lie wholly outside the sequence of empirical conditions by which the occurrence in the sensory world is made necessary. This intelligible character, admittedly, could never be known directly, since nothing is perceptible to us save insofar as it appears; nevertheless it would have to be ***thought*** as answering to the empirical character, exactly as we are quite generally required to lay a transcendental object beneath appearances in our thought, notwithstanding that we know nothing of what it may be in its own self.

According to its empirical character, therefore, this subject, as appearance, would be subject to every law of determination proceeding by causal connection; and so far it would amount to nothing more than a portion of the sensory world, whose effects, no differently from any other appearance, would issue unfailingly from nature. The moment outer appearances flowed into it, and the moment its empirical character—the law, that is, governing its causality—had been cognized through experience, every one of its actions would have to admit of explanation by laws of nature, and whatever is requisite for their complete and necessary determination would have to be discoverable within a possible experience.

{{ 569 }} In respect of its intelligible character, however (albeit of this we can possess nothing beyond its general concept), that same subject would nonetheless have to be pronounced free of every influence of sensibility and of all determination by appearances; and because within it, so far as it counts as ***noumenon***, nothing ***happens***—no change calling for a dynamical determination of time, and consequently no linkage with appearances in the role of causes is to be met with—this active being would {{{ 368 }}} in that respect be, in its actions, independent of and free from all natural necessity, such as is encountered solely within the sensory world. One could say of it, with perfect correctness, that it initiates its effects in the sensory world ***of itself***, though the action does not begin ***in it itself***; and this would hold good without the effects in the sensory world being entitled on that account to begin of themselves, since within that world they are at every moment determined beforehand by empirical conditions in the antecedent time—yet only by way of the empirical character (which is nothing but the appearance of the intelligible)—and are possible merely as a prolongation of the chain of natural causes. In this way freedom and nature, each in its complete meaning, would be encountered at one and the same time and without any conflict whatever in the very same actions, according as they are compared with their cause taken as intelligible or as sensible.
