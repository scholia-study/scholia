## III. Philosophy stands in need of a science which shall determine the possibility, the principles, and the extent of all _a priori_ cognition

What means still far more than all the foregoing is this, that certain cognitions even leave the field of all possible experiences, and, through concepts to which no corresponding object can be given in experience anywhere, have the appearance of extending the scope of our judgments beyond all its boundaries.

And precisely in these latter cognitions, which go beyond the world of sense, where experience can yield neither a guiding thread nor correction, lie the investigations of our reason, which we hold, according to
{{{ 31 }}} {{ 7 }} their importance, to be far more excellent and their ultimate aim to be far more sublime than anything that the understanding can learn in the field of appearances, whereby we would even rather risk everything, even at the danger of erring, than give up such vital investigations from any ground of hesitation, or from disdain and indifference. These unavoidable problems of pure reason itself are ***God***, ***freedom***, and ***immortality***. The science, however, whose ultimate aim, with all its preparations, is strictly directed only to the solution of these, is called ***metaphysics***, whose procedure is in the beginning ***dogmatic***, i.e., it confidently takes upon itself the execution without a previous examination of the capacity or incapacity of reason for so great an undertaking.

Now it does indeed seem natural that, as soon as one has left the ground of experience, one would not immediately erect a building with cognitions that one possesses without knowing whence, and on the credit of principles whose origin one does not know, without first being assured of its foundation through careful investigations, that one would therefore rather have raised the question long ago as to how the understanding can arrive at all these cognitions _a priori_, and what extent, validity, and worth they may have. In fact, nothing is more natural, if by the word ***natural*** one understands that {{ 8 }} which ought to happen in a fair and reasonable manner; but if one understands by it that which usually happens, then again nothing is more natural and comprehensible than that this investigation had to be left undone for a long time. For one part of these cognitions, such as the mathematical, is in the ancient possession of reliability, and thereby gives rise to a favorable expectation for others as well, even though these may be of a completely different nature. Moreover, once one is beyond the circle of experience, one is sure of not being refuted by experience. The charm of expanding one's cognitions is so great that one can be stopped in one's progress only by a clear contradiction that one runs into. This, however, can {{{ 32 }}} be avoided if one only makes one's fictions cautiously, without their remaining any less fictions on that account. Mathematics gives us a brilliant example of how far we can go in _a priori_ cognition independently of experience. Now it is true that it occupies itself with objects and cognitions merely so far as such can be exhibited in intuition. But this circumstance is easily overlooked, because the aforementioned intuition can itself be given _a priori_, and consequently can hardly be distinguished from a mere pure concept. Captivated by such a proof of the power of reason, the drive for expansion sees no boundaries. The light dove, as it cleaves the air in its free flight, whose resistance it feels, could grasp the {{ 9 }} representation that it would succeed much better in airless space. Just so did Plato abandon the world of sense, because it sets such narrow limits for the understanding, and ventured beyond it on the wings of the ideas into the empty space of the pure understanding. He did not remark that he was making no headway by his efforts, for he had no resistance, as a support, as it were, upon which he could take a stand and to which he could apply his powers in order to move the understanding. But it is a common fate of human reason in speculation to finish its building as early as possible and only afterward to investigate whether the foundation for it has been well laid. But then all sorts of excuses are sought out to console us regarding its sturdiness, or even to refuse such a late and dangerous examination entirely. What keeps us free from all worry and suspicion during the building, however, and flatters us with apparent thoroughness, is this. A great part, and perhaps the greatest, of the business of our reason consists in ***analyses*** of the concepts that we already have of objects. This supplies us with a multitude of cognitions which, although they are nothing more than clarifications or elucidations of that which has already been thought in our concepts (though still in a confused manner), are yet valued at least in their form as equal to new insights {{{ 33 }}}, although in their matter or content they do not extend the concepts that {{ 10 }} we have, but only explicate them. Now since this procedure yields an actual cognition _a priori_ which has a secure and useful progress, reason stealthily, without itself noticing it, surreptitiously introduces under this pretense assertions of a completely different sort, where reason adds entirely foreign concepts to given concepts, and indeed _a priori_, without one knowing how it arrives at them, and without letting such a question even come to mind. I will therefore treat of the difference between this twofold kind of cognition right at the outset.
