## III. Philosophy stands in need of a science which shall determine the possibility, the principles, and the extent of all _a priori_ cognition

What carries far greater weight than everything preceding is this: that there are cognitions which even quit the field of every possible experience, and which, through concepts for which no answering object can be supplied anywhere in experience, seem to widen the reach of our judgments past every boundary of that field.

And it is exactly within these latter cognitions, the ones that pass beyond the sensible world—where experience offers neither a thread to follow nor any means of correction—that the researches of our reason lie; researches which, by their {{{ 31 }}} {{ 7 }} weight, we rate as far more distinguished, and whose final purpose we take to be far loftier, than whatever the understanding is able to learn in the field of appearances, and for whose sake we will hazard anything, even at the risk of going astray, rather than give up inquiries of such moment on any grounds of misgiving, or from slight regard and indifference. Pure reason's own inescapable tasks are these: ***God***, ***freedom***, and ***immortality***. The science, however, whose whole apparatus and final purpose are aimed in truth at nothing but the resolution of these tasks bears the name ***metaphysics***; and its method is ***dogmatic*** to begin with, that is, it takes the execution of so vast an enterprise confidently in hand without any preliminary test of whether reason is or is not equal to it.

Now it does look natural that, having once quit the soil of experience, one would hardly go straight to raising an edifice out of cognitions in one's keeping—cognitions whose source one cannot name—and on the strength of principles of unknown descent, before making sure by careful inquiry of the groundwork beneath it; and that one would, rather, have posed long ago the question of just how the understanding is to reach all these cognitions _a priori_, and what scope, validity, and value they may possess. And in fact nothing could be more natural, provided one takes the word ***natural*** to mean that {{ 8 }} which in fairness and reason ought to take place; yet if one takes it to mean what customarily does take place, then again nothing could be more natural or more intelligible than that such an inquiry should have gone so long neglected. For one portion of these cognitions, the mathematical, has from of old held secure possession, and so raises a hopeful expectation for the rest as well, however wholly different in kind these may be. Besides, once one has passed outside the compass of experience, one is safe from any refutation by experience. So strong is the temptation to enlarge one's cognitions that nothing short of a clear contradiction, met head-on, can arrest one's advance. Yet even this can {{{ 32 }}} be sidestepped, if one is merely cautious in framing one's inventions—which for all that stay inventions no less. Mathematics affords us a brilliant instance of how far, independently of experience, we may carry cognition _a priori_. True, it busies itself with objects and cognitions only to the extent that these can be displayed in intuition. But that fact is readily missed, since the intuition at issue can itself be furnished _a priori_, and thus is scarcely to be told apart from a mere pure concept. Taken with such a demonstration of reason's might, the urge to expand recognizes no limits. The light dove, cleaving the air in unimpeded flight and feeling its resistance, might conceive the {{ 9 }} representation that in empty space it would fare better still by far. Just so did Plato quit the sensible world, since it hems the understanding in with such narrow bounds, and struck out past it, borne on the pinions of the ideas, into the void of the pure understanding. He failed to see that with all his striving he was winning no ground, for he had nothing to press against by way of a footing, no resistance on which to stiffen himself and against which to bring his powers to bear, so as to move the understanding from its place. It is, though, a familiar destiny of human reason in its speculation to run its edifice up as quickly as it can, and only after the fact to look into whether the foundation was soundly laid. By then, though, every sort of extenuation is fetched up, either to console us over its soundness, or else to fend off such a tardy and perilous test altogether. But what shields us throughout the building from all misgiving and mistrust, and flatters us with an apparent rigor, is this. A great share of our reason's business—perhaps the greatest—consists in ***dissecting*** the concepts we already possess of objects. This yields us a wealth of cognitions which, though they are nothing beyond clarifications or expositions of what our concepts (confusedly, to be sure) already contained, are nonetheless rated, in point of form at least, as equal to fresh insights {{{ 33 }}}, even while, in point of matter or content, they do not enlarge the concepts we {{ 10 }} possess but only unfold them. Now because this procedure does deliver a real _a priori_ cognition, one that makes safe and profitable headway, reason, all unawares, smuggles in under this semblance claims of a quite different order, whereby it fastens onto given concepts others entirely alien to them, and does so _a priori_, without anyone's grasping how it manages this, and without so much as allowing such a question to cross one's mind. For that reason I mean to deal, from the very start, with the difference between these two sorts of cognition.
