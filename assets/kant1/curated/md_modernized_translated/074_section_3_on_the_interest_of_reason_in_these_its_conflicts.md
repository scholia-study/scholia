## {{{ 322 }}} {{ 490 }} Third Section. On the interest of reason in these its conflicts.

So now the whole dialectical game of the cosmological ideas lies before us, ideas that in no way allow a congruent object to be given them in any possible experience, indeed that do not even allow reason to think them in accord with universal laws of experience — laws that are, for all that, not arbitrarily contrived, but ones to which reason is of necessity led in the continuous progress of the empirical synthesis, whenever it wishes to release from every condition that which, by the rules of experience, can always be determined only conditionally, and to seize it in its unconditioned totality. These ratiocinating assertions are so many endeavors to resolve four natural and inescapable problems that reason poses, of which, accordingly, there can be exactly this many, no more and likewise no fewer, since there exist no further series of synthetic presuppositions that draw an _a priori_ limit around the empirical synthesis.

We have presented the brilliant pretensions of a reason that widens its territory past every boundary of experience only in dry formulas {{ 491 }} that contain merely the ground of its rightful claims, and, as befits a transcendental philosophy, we have divested them of all that is empirical, even though the entire magnificence of reason's assertions can blaze forth only in conjunction with that very element. In this application, however, and in the steadily advancing enlargement of reason's use, as it sets out from the field of experiences and by degrees soars upward to these sublime ideas, philosophy manifests a dignity that, could it only make good its pretensions, would leave the worth of all other human science far beneath itself, inasmuch as it holds out the foundation for our greatest expectations and prospects regarding the final ends in which every exertion of reason must at last come together. The questions — whether the world possesses a beginning and some boundary to its extension in space; whether anywhere, and perhaps within my thinking self, there is an indivisible {{{ 323 }}} and indestructible unity, or nothing but what is divisible and transitory; whether in my actions I am free, or, like other beings, am led along the thread of nature and of destiny; and whether, in the end, a supreme world-cause exists, or whether instead the natural things and their order make up the ultimate object at which we must come to rest in all our reflections: these are questions to whose resolution the mathematician would happily surrender his whole science; for that science, after all, can furnish him no contentment with respect to {{ 492 }} the highest and most pressing ends of humanity. Even the proper dignity of mathematics (this pride of human reason) rests on the fact that, since it gives reason the guidance to gain insight into nature, in the large as much as in the small, in respect of its order and lawful regularity, and likewise in the admirable unity of the forces that move it, far beyond every expectation of a philosophy that builds upon ordinary experience, it thereby itself furnishes occasion and encouragement for that use of reason extended past all experience, and likewise stocks the worldly wisdom occupied therewith with the finest materials for lending support, so far as the character of the inquiry permits, to its research by way of appropriate intuitions.

Unfortunately for speculation (though perhaps fortunately for the practical vocation of the human being), reason, in the midst of its greatest expectations, finds itself so hemmed in by a press of grounds and counter-grounds that, since for the sake both of its honor and even of its safety it is not advisable to draw back and to look on with indifference at this quarrel as at a mere mock combat, still less flatly to command peace, because the object of the strife is one of keen interest, nothing further is left to it than to ponder the origin of this dissension of reason with itself: whether perhaps some mere misunderstanding may be at fault {{ 493 }} in it, upon whose clearing up the proud claims on either side would indeed perhaps drop away, but in their stead a lasting and untroubled reign of reason over understanding and the senses would take its start.

For the present we will still hold this thorough treatment in abeyance and beforehand take into account the following: toward which side we would most readily incline, were we by chance compelled to take a side. Since in this case we consult not the logical touchstone of truth but merely our own interest, such an inquiry, even though it {{{ 324 }}} decides nothing regarding the contested right of the two parts, will nevertheless prove useful in rendering intelligible why those taking part in this dispute have thrown in with the one side rather than the other, without any superior insight into the object having in fact been the reason for it, and useful, likewise, in explaining still other incidental matters, for example the zealous heat of the one party and the cool assertion of the other, why they gladly cheer their joyous applause to the one and are irreconcilably prejudiced beforehand against the other.

There is, however, something that in this preliminary assessment fixes the standpoint from which alone it can be undertaken with due thoroughness, and this is the comparison of the principles from which the two parts proceed. Among the assertions of the antithesis one notices a perfect uniformity in the manner of thinking and a complete unity {{ 494 }} of maxim, namely a principle of unmixed ***empiricism***, not merely in explaining the appearances within the world, but also in resolving the transcendental ideas concerning the world-whole itself. The assertions of the thesis, on the other hand, place at the basis, over and above the empirical mode of explanation within the series of appearances, intellectual beginnings as well, and to that extent the maxim is not simple. But, taking my cue from its essential distinguishing mark, I will call it the ***dogmatism*** of pure reason.

On the side, then, of the ***dogmatism*** in the determination of the cosmological ideas of reason, or of the ***thesis***, there shows itself:

***First***, a certain ***practical interest***, in which everyone of good disposition, if he understands his own true advantage, gives a heartfelt assent. That the world had a beginning, that the self which thinks in me is of simple, and on that account imperishable, nature, that this same self is at once free in its voluntary actions and lifted above the constraint of nature, and that, in the end, the whole order of the things making up the world stems from an original being from which everything borrows its unity and purposive connection: these amount to just so many cornerstones for morality and for religion. The antithesis tears away from us every one of these supports, or at any rate appears to do so.

***Second***, a ***speculative interest*** of reason likewise makes itself felt on this side. For when the transcendental ideas {{ 495 }} are adopted and put to use in this fashion, one can grasp wholly _a priori_ the entire chain of conditions and comprehend the derivation of the conditioned, {{{ 325 }}} by starting from the unconditioned; something the antithesis does not accomplish, and thereby recommends itself very badly, in that it can offer no answer to the question about the conditions of its synthesis that would not leave over, endlessly, an ever further asking. On its account, from any given beginning one has to climb to a yet higher one, every part points to a yet smaller part, each occurrence has above it still another occurrence serving as its cause, and the conditions of existence taken generally brace themselves ever again upon others, without ever gaining, in some self-subsisting thing as original being, an unconditioned footing and support.

***Third***, this side has, over and above this, the advantage of ***popularity***, which surely makes up no small part of its recommendation. Ordinary understanding meets not the least difficulty in the ideas of the unconditioned beginning of all synthesis, since in any case it is more used to descending toward consequences than to climbing toward grounds, and in the concepts of the absolutely first (over whose possibility it does not brood) it possesses a comfort and, at the same time, a fixed point on which to fasten the guiding cord of its steps, whereas in the restless ascent from conditioned to condition, forever with one foot in the air, it can take no pleasure whatever.

{{ 496 }} On the side of ***empiricism*** in the determination of the cosmological ideas, or of the ***antithesis***, there is to be found:

***Firstly***, no such practical interest springing from pure principles of reason as morality and religion carry along with them. Rather, mere empiricism seems to strip both of all force and influence. If no original being set apart from the world exists, if the world has no beginning and hence no author either, if our will is not free and the soul shares the same divisibility and corruptibility as matter: then the ***moral*** ideas and principles too forfeit all validity and topple along with the ***transcendental*** ideas that made up their theoretical prop.

***By contrast***, however, empiricism holds out to the speculative interest of reason advantages that are highly alluring and far exceed anything the dogmatic instructor in the ideas of reason might promise. On the former view, the understanding remains at every moment upon its own native ground, that is, the field consisting of nothing but possible experiences, whose laws it can track and by means of which it can extend, {{{ 326 }}} without end, its secure and graspable cognition. Here it can, and ought to, present the object both as it is in itself and in its relations for intuition, or else in concepts whose image can be set out clearly and distinctly in given, similar intuitions. Not only does it have no need {{ 497 }} to forsake this chain of the natural order so as to cling to ideas whose objects it does not know, because, being things of thought, they can never be given; rather, it is not even allowed to quit its work and, on the pretext that this has now been carried to its end, to cross over into the province of idealizing reason and to transcendent concepts, where it no longer has any need to observe and to inquire in keeping with the laws of nature, but only to ***think*** and to ***invent***, assured that no facts of nature can refute it, since it is precisely not bound to their testimony, but may pass them by, or may even subordinate them to a higher authority, namely that of pure reason.

The empiricist will therefore never permit any epoch of nature to be taken as the absolutely first, or any limit of his outlook upon nature's compass to be viewed as the outermost, or a passage to be made from the objects of nature, which observation and mathematics enable him to resolve and to fix synthetically within intuition (the extended), to those that neither sense nor imagination can ever exhibit _in concreto_ (the simple); nor will he grant that ***in nature itself*** one may place at the basis a faculty of acting independently of nature's laws (freedom) and thereby curtail for the understanding its work of tracking, along the guiding thread of necessary rules, the arising of {{ 498 }} appearances; nor, finally, will he allow that for anything the cause is to be sought outside of nature (original being), since we know nothing besides nature, it being the one thing that offers us objects and can instruct us about their laws.

To be sure, if the empirical philosopher, with his antithesis, has no other aim than to beat down the presumption and the overreach of a reason that misjudges its true vocation, which makes a great show of ***insight*** and ***knowledge*** just where insight and knowledge actually give out, and that means to pass off, as a furthering of the speculative interest, what one lets stand with respect to the practical interest, so as, {{{ 327 }}} where it suits its own comfort, to snap the thread of physical investigations and, on a pretense of extending cognition, to fasten that thread to transcendental ideas, through which one really cognizes only ***that one knows nothing***; if, I say, the empiricist rested content with this, then his fundamental principle would amount to a maxim of restraint in its claims, of modesty in its assertions, and, at once, of the widest attainable enlargement of our understanding under the tutor genuinely appointed over us, namely experience. For under such circumstances we would not find ourselves robbed of intellectual ***presuppositions*** and ***belief*** for the benefit of our practical affairs; only these could not be allowed to appear under the title and the pomp of science and rational insight, {{ 499 }} because the properly speculative ***knowledge*** can nowhere light upon any object other than that of experience, and, once one crosses its boundary, the synthesis that ventures cognitions of a new sort, owing nothing to experience, is left without any substratum of intuition upon which it might be brought to bear.

But if, in this way, empiricism, with regard to the ideas, itself turns dogmatic (as for the most part it does) and brashly denies whatever stands above the reach of its intuiting cognitions, then it itself lapses into the fault of immodesty, which here is all the more to be censured because thereby an irreplaceable disadvantage is inflicted on the practical interest of reason.

Herein lies the opposition of ***Epicureanism***[^*] to ***Platonism***.

{{{ 328 }}} Both of them assert more than they know, yet in such a way that the ***former*** {{ 500 }} spurs on and advances knowledge, albeit to the detriment of the practical, while the ***latter*** does indeed put excellent principles for the practical into our hands, but on that very account, with regard to everything for which alone a speculative knowledge is granted us, allows reason to dwell on idealized explanations of natural appearances and, over this, to let physical research fall by the wayside.

As to the ***third*** moment that may be looked to in the preliminary choice between the two contending parts: it is quite remarkable that empiricism runs entirely counter to all popularity, however much one might have thought that ordinary understanding would eagerly seize upon a plan that undertakes to content it with nothing beyond experiential cognitions and the reason-governed coherence among them, rather than transcendental dogmatics forcing it to climb to concepts that far outreach the insight and the rational capacity of the heads most practiced {{ 501 }} in thinking. But precisely this is its motive. For it then finds itself in a condition in which not even the most learned man can presume any advantage over it. If it grasps little or nothing of the matter, then no one else can boast of grasping much more of it either, and although it cannot speak on this as academically as others, it can nonetheless ratiocinate about it beyond all measure, since it strolls about amid nothing but ideas, concerning which one is most eloquent precisely because one knows nothing of them; whereas over the investigation of nature it would have to grow wholly mute and confess its ignorance. Comfort, then, and vanity are already a powerful recommendation of these principles. And besides, although a philosopher finds it very hard to adopt anything as a principle when he cannot render an account of it to himself, or even to bring in concepts whose objective reality cannot be discerned, to ordinary understanding nothing is more familiar. It wants to have something with which it may confidently make a start. The trouble of grasping such a presupposition in its own right gives it no unease, because such a presupposition (given that it does not know what grasping means) never so much as enters its head, and it takes for known whatever, through frequent use, has grown habitual to it. In the end, however, all speculative interest fades away in it before the practical, and it {{{ 329 }}} fancies that it discerns and knows precisely what to assume or ***believe*** {{ 502 }} its anxieties or hopes drive it toward. Thus the empiricism of transcendentally idealizing reason is wholly stripped of all popularity, and however much that is prejudicial to the supreme practical principles it may harbor, there is nonetheless no cause whatever to fear that it will ever pass beyond the bounds of the school and gain, in the commonwealth at large, even a moderately notable standing or any favor with the great multitude.

Human reason is by its nature architectonic, that is, it views all cognitions as members of a possible system and therefore admits only such principles as at least do not make a cognition one has in view unfit to coexist with others within some system. The propositions of the antithesis, however, are of a sort that makes the completion of any structure of cognitions wholly impossible. On their showing, over any one state of the world there is always a still older one, in every part still further parts, again divisible, before every occurrence another that had likewise been produced in its turn from elsewhere, and, in existence generally, everything forever only conditioned, without any unconditioned and first existence being acknowledged. Since, therefore, the antithesis nowhere concedes a first and no beginning that might serve outright as the ground of the building, a finished edifice of cognition is, on presuppositions of this kind, wholly impossible. Hence the architectonic interest {{ 503 }} of reason (which calls for a rational unity that is not empirical but pure and _a priori_) brings with it a natural endorsement of the claims put forward by the thesis.

But could a human being renounce all interest and take the assertions of reason into consideration merely by the substance of their grounds, indifferent to all consequences, then such a person, granting that he knew of no way out of the press other than to profess himself to one or the other of the contending doctrines, would be in a ceaselessly wavering condition. On one day it would appear compelling to him that the human will is ***free***; on the next, upon weighing the unbreakable chain of nature, he would judge freedom to be nothing more than a self-delusion, and hold that all things are simply ***nature***. But once it came to deed and action, this game of merely speculative reason would dissolve like the phantom shapes of a dream, and he would settle his principles purely on the basis of the practical interest {{{ 330 }}}. Yet because it does become a reflective and inquiring being to devote certain seasons solely to the testing of its own reason, while therein casting off all partiality entirely and so imparting its remarks openly to others for their judgment, no one can be blamed, still less barred, for letting the propositions and counter-propositions {{ 504 }} step forward just as, frightened by no threat, they are able to defend themselves before jurors of their own estate (namely the estate of frail human beings).

[^*]: It remains, for all that, a question whether Epicurus ever advanced these fundamental claims as objective assertions at all. Had they amounted to no more than maxims governing the speculative employment of reason, he would have shown in them a truer philosophical spirit than any other sage of antiquity. That in explaining the appearances one has to proceed as though no boundary or worldly beginning severed the field of investigation; that one should assume the world's material to be precisely such as it must be, provided we wish to receive instruction concerning it from experience; that no generation of occurrences other than as they are determined by unalterable laws of nature, and, finally, no cause distinct from the world, may be brought into play: these are, even today, very sound though little heeded principles for enlarging speculative philosophy, and likewise for locating the principles of morality independently of extraneous aids, without, on that account, anyone who demands that those dogmatic propositions be ***ignored*** for as long as we are engaged in mere speculation being liable to the charge that he means to ***deny*** them.
