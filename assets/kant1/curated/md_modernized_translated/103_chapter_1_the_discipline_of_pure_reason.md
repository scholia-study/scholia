## Chapter 1. The Discipline of Pure Reason

Negative judgments — those that are negative not merely in their logical form but also in their content — enjoy little particular esteem in the eyes of human curiosity; indeed they are often regarded as envious enemies {{ 737 }} of our drive for cognition, which strives unceasingly toward expansion, and it takes almost an apology to secure for them so much as toleration, and still more to secure for them favor and high regard.

To be sure, one can express ***logically*** any proposition one wishes in negative form, yet as concerns the content of our cognition as such — whether a judgment enlarges or limits it — the negating ones have the peculiar office of doing nothing but ***warding off error***. Hence negative propositions designed to fend off a false cognition in a case where no error is ever possible are, though perfectly true, still empty — that is, not at all suited to their aim, and for precisely that reason frequently absurd; as with that rhetorician's dictum in the schools, that Alexander would have been unable to conquer any territory had he possessed no army.

Wherever, though, the bounds of the cognition available to us are exceedingly narrow, the temptation to judge strong, the semblance on offer highly deceptive, and the damage done by error substantial, there the ***negative*** side of instruction — which serves solely to secure us against errors — has still greater weight than many a positive lesson by which our cognition might be enlarged. We give the name ***discipline*** to the ***constraint*** by which the abiding inclination to stray from certain rules is held in check {{{ 467 }}} and at last stamped out. It is distinct from ***culture***, which is meant merely to impart a ***skill*** without thereby removing another one already present. To the cultivation of a talent that already has of itself an impulse toward expression, discipline will {{ 738 }} therefore make a ***negative***[^*] contribution, whereas culture and doctrine make a ***positive*** one.

That temperament, and likewise that talents which readily allow themselves a free and unrestricted movement (such as imagination and wit), stand in need of a discipline in more than one respect, everyone will readily grant. That reason, however, whose proper task it is to prescribe its discipline to all other endeavors, should itself still require one, may indeed seem strange; and in fact it has thus far escaped such a humiliation precisely because, given the solemnity and the thorough decorum with which it comes forward, no one could easily fall into suspecting it of a frivolous play with figments in place of concepts and words in place of things.

Reason requires no critique in its empirical use, because there its principles are subjected, on the touchstone of experience, to continual testing {{ 739 }}; and likewise none in mathematics, where its concepts must at once be exhibited _in concreto_ in pure intuition, so that everything groundless and arbitrary at once becomes manifest. But wherever reason is held on no visible course by intuition, whether empirical or pure — that is, in its transcendental employment operating on concepts alone — its need for a discipline becomes so acute, one that would curb its bent toward extending itself past the cramped confines of possible experience and restrain it from excess and error, that the whole philosophy of pure reason is occupied with nothing beyond this negative usefulness. Isolated aberrations admit of correction by ***censure*** {{{ 468 }}}, and the sources from which they spring by critique. Where, however, one meets — as happens in pure reason — with a whole system of illusions and deceptive semblances that are tightly bound up with one another and drawn together under shared principles, there a wholly peculiar and indeed negative legislation appears to be called for, one that, under the title of a ***discipline***, would raise up, from the very nature of reason and of the objects of its pure use, something like a system of circumspection and self-scrutiny, before which no fallacious, sophistical semblance could survive but would at once give itself away, whatever grounds it might muster to gloss itself over.

{{ 740 }} It should, however, be well noted that within this second principal part of the transcendental critique I aim the discipline of pure reason not at the content but solely at the method of cognition drawn from pure reason. The former was already accomplished in the Doctrine of Elements. Yet the exercise of reason bears so much resemblance to itself, no matter which object it is brought to bear upon, and is nonetheless, insofar as it is meant to be transcendental, at the same time so essentially set apart from everything else, that without the admonitory negative teaching of a discipline established expressly for it there is no averting the errors that must inevitably issue from an unfitting compliance with methods which do suit reason elsewhere, only not here.

[^*]: I am well aware that in Scholastic usage the name of ***discipline*** is commonly used as equivalent to that of instruction. But there are, on the other hand, so many other cases in which the former expression, as ***chastening***, is carefully distinguished from the second, as ***instruction***, and the nature of things itself demands that the only fitting expressions be preserved for this distinction, that I would wish that word never be permitted to be used in any but a negative sense.
