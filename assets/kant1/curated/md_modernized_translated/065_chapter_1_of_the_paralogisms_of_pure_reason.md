## {{ 399 }} Chapter 1. Of the Paralogisms of Pure Reason

The logical paralogism consists in the falsity of a syllogism as to its form, whatever its content may otherwise be. A transcendental paralogism, however, has a transcendental ground for drawing a logically false conclusion as to form. In this manner, such a fallacy will have its ground in the nature of human reason, and will bring with it an unavoidable, although not insoluble, illusion.

We now come to a concept which was not cataloged in the general list of transcendental concepts above, and yet must be reckoned among them, without, however, altering that table in the least or declaring it to be defective. This is the concept or, if {{{ 263 }}} one prefers, the judgment: ***I think***. But one easily sees that this concept is the vehicle of all concepts in general and therefore also of transcendental ones, and thus is always included among them, and hence is just as much transcendental, {{ 400 }} yet can have no special title, because it serves only to introduce all thinking as belonging to consciousness. Nevertheless, however pure it may be of the empirical (the impression of the senses), it still serves to distinguish two kinds of objects from the nature of our power of representation. ***I***, as thinking, is an object of the inner sense, and is called the soul. That which is an object of the outer senses is called body. Accordingly, the expression "I", as a thinking being, already signifies the object of psychology, which can be called rational psychology, if I do not require to know anything more of the soul than what can be inferred, independently of all experience (which determines me more closely and _in concreto_), from this concept ***I***, insofar as it occurs in all thinking.

The ***rational*** doctrine of the soul is indeed an undertaking of this kind; for if the least empirical element of my thought, any particular perception of my inner state, were mixed with the grounds of knowledge of this science, it would no longer be a rational, but an ***empirical*** doctrine of the soul. We have therefore before us an alleged science, which is built upon the single proposition: ***I think***, and the ground or unground of which we can here investigate quite properly and in accordance with the nature of a transcendental philosophy. One must not take offense at the fact that I do have an inner experience in this proposition, which expresses the perception of oneself, {{ 401 }} and that consequently rational psychology, which is built upon it, is never pure, but is in part founded on an empirical principle. For this inner perception is nothing more than the mere apperception: ***I think***, which even makes all transcendental concepts possible, in which we say: ***I think*** substance, cause, etc. For inner experience in general and its possibility, or perception in general and its relation to other perception, without any particular difference among them and determination being empirically given, cannot be regarded as empirical knowledge, but must be regarded as knowledge of the empirical in general, and belongs to the investigation of the possibility of any experience, which is indeed transcendental. The least object of perception (e.g., merely {{{ 264 }}} pleasure or pain) that was added to the general representation of self-consciousness would immediately convert rational psychology into an empirical one.

***I think*** is the sole text of rational psychology, from which it is to develop all its wisdom. One easily sees that this thought, if it is to be referred to an object (myself), can contain nothing but transcendental predicates of the same: because the least empirical predicate would spoil the rational purity and independence of the science from all experience.

{{ 402 }} Here, however, we shall have only to follow the guide of the categories; yet since a thing, the "I", is here first given as a thinking being, we shall indeed not alter the above order of the categories among themselves, as presented in their table, but we shall nevertheless begin here with the category of substance, by which a thing in itself is represented, and thus follow their series backward. The topic of the rational doctrine of the soul, from which everything else that it may contain must be derived, is accordingly the following:[^*]

{{{ 265 }}} {{ 403 }} From these elements arise all the concepts of the pure doctrine of the soul merely through combination, without acknowledging any other principle in the least. This substance, merely as an object of the inner sense, gives the concept of ***immateriality***; as a simple substance, that of ***incorruptibility***; its identity as an intellectual substance gives ***personality***; all these three parts together constitute ***spirituality***; its relation to objects in space gives the ***commercium*** with bodies; consequently, it represents the thinking substance as the principle of life in matter, i.e., as soul (_anima_) and as the ground of ***animality***, which latter, limited by ***spirituality***, yields ***immortality***.

To these elements now refer four paralogisms of a transcendental psychology, which is falsely held to be a science of pure reason concerning the nature of our thinking being. As the ground {{ 404 }} of this science, however, we can lay down nothing other than the simple and, in itself, completely empty representation "I", of which one cannot even say that it is a concept, but a mere consciousness that accompanies all concepts. Through this "I", or "he", or "it" (the thing) that thinks, nothing more is represented than a transcendental subject of thoughts = X, which is known only through the thoughts that are its predicates, and of which we, in isolation, can never have the least concept, so that we turn around it in a constant circle, since we must always make use of its representation in order to judge anything concerning it; an inconvenience that is inseparable from it, because consciousness in itself is not so much a representation distinguishing a particular object, but rather a form of representation in general, insofar as it is to be called knowledge; for of this alone can I say that through it I think anything.

But it must seem strange at the very beginning that the condition under which I think in general, and which is consequently merely a property of my subject, should at the same time be valid for everything that thinks, and that we can presume to base upon an empirically seeming proposition an apodictic and {{{ 266 }}} universal judgment, namely: that everything which thinks is so constituted as the voice of self-consciousness declares it to be in {{ 405 }} my own case. But the cause of this lies in the fact that we must of necessity attribute _a priori_ to things all the properties that constitute the conditions under which alone we think them. Now, of a thinking being I cannot have the least representation through any outer experience, but only through self-consciousness. Therefore, such objects are nothing more than the transfer of this consciousness of mine to other things, which can be represented as thinking beings only in this way. The proposition "I think" is, however, here taken only problematically, not insofar as it may contain a perception of an existence (the Cartesian _cogito, ergo sum_), but according to its mere possibility, in order to see what properties may flow from so simple a proposition to its subject (whether such a subject exists or not).

If more than the _cogito_ were laid as the ground of our pure rational knowledge of thinking beings in general, and if we were also to call to our aid the observations concerning the play of our thoughts and the laws of nature of the thinking self to be drawn from them, an empirical psychology would arise, which would be a kind of ***physiology*** of the inner sense, and might serve to explain the appearances of that sense, but could never serve to reveal such properties as do not belong to {{ 406 }} possible experience at all (such as that of the simple), nor to teach ***apodictically*** anything concerning the nature of thinking beings in general; it would therefore be no ***rational*** psychology.

Since the proposition ***I think*** (taken problematically) contains the form of every judgment of the understanding in general, and accompanies all categories as their vehicle, it is clear that the inferences from it can contain a merely transcendental use of the understanding, which excludes all admixture of experience; and of the progress of this use, after what we have shown above, we cannot in advance form any advantageous concept. We will therefore follow it with a critical eye through all the predicaments of the pure doctrine of the soul, yet {{{ 267 }}} for the sake of brevity allow their examination to proceed in an uninterrupted connection.

First of all, the following general remark can sharpen our attention to this mode of inference. It is not through the mere fact that I think that I know any object, but only by determining a given intuition in respect to the unity of consciousness, in which all thinking consists, that I can know any object. Consequently, I do not know myself through being conscious of myself as thinking, but only when I am conscious of the intuition of myself as determined in respect of the function of thought. All _modi_ of {{ 407 }} self-consciousness in thinking in itself are therefore not yet concepts of understanding of objects (categories), but mere logical functions, which give to thinking no object at all, and mithin do not give myself to be known as an object. It is not the consciousness of the ***determining*** self, but only that of the ***determinable*** self, i.e., of my inner intuition (insofar as its manifold can be conjoined in accordance with the universal condition of the unity of apperception in thought), that is the ***object***.

1) In all judgments, I am always the determining ***subject*** of that relation which constitutes the judgment. But that the "I" who thinks must in thought always hold as ***subject***, and as something that cannot be regarded as merely adhering to thought like a predicate, is an apodictic and indeed ***identical proposition***; but it does not mean that I as ***object*** am a ***self-subsisting being*** or ***substance***. The latter is a very far-reaching assertion, and therefore requires data that are not found in thought at all, perhaps (sofar as I regard the thinking being merely as such) more than I shall ever find anywhere in it.

2) That the "I" of apperception, and consequently in all thought, is a ***singular*** which cannot be resolved into a plurality of subjects, and therefore denotes a logically simple subject, lies already in the concept {{ 408 }} of thinking, and is consequently an analytic proposition; but this does not mean that the thinking "I" is a simple ***substance***, which would be a synthetic proposition. The concept of substance always relates to intuitions, which in my case cannot be other than sensible, and mithin lie entirely outside the field of the understanding and its thinking, of which alone we are actually speaking here when it is said that the {{{ 268 }}} "I" in thinking is simple. It would indeed be marvelous if that which otherwise requires so much preparation in order to distinguish, in what intuition presents, what is substance therein, and still more whether this substance can be simple (as with the parts of matter), were here given to me so directly, in the poorest of all representations, as if by a revelation.

3) The proposition of the identity of myself amidst all the manifold of which I am conscious is a proposition likewise lying in the concepts themselves, and therefore an analytic proposition; but this identity of the subject, of which I can become conscious in all its representations, does not concern its intuition, whereby it is given as an object, and can therefore also not signify the identity of the person, by which is understood the consciousness of the identity of one's own substance as a thinking being in all change of states, to prove which the mere analysis of the proposition "I think" is not sufficient, but {{ 409 }} various synthetic judgments, which ground themselves on the given intuition, would be required.

4) "I distinguish my own existence, as a thinking being, from other things outside of me (to which my body also belongs)" is likewise an analytic proposition; for ***other*** things are those which I think of as ***distinguished*** from myself. But whether this consciousness of myself would be at all possible without things outside of me, whereby representations are given to me, and whether I could therefore exist merely as a thinking being (without being a man), I do not know at all through this.

Thus, through the analysis of the consciousness of myself in thought in general, not the least is gained in respect of the knowledge of myself as an object. The logical exposition of thinking in general is falsely held to be a metaphysical determination of the object.

It would be a great, indeed the sole, stumbling block against our entire critique if there were a possibility of proving _a priori_ that all thinking beings are in themselves simple substances, and as such consequently (which is a consequence of the very same ground of proof) carry personality inseparably with them and are conscious of their existence as separate from all matter. For in this way we should indeed have taken a step beyond the world of sense, we should have entered the field of {{ 410 }} ***noumena***, and then no one could deny us the right to extend ourselves further in this field, to cultivate it, and, according as each one's lucky star favors him, {{{ 269 }}} to take possession of it. For the proposition: "Every thinking being as such is a simple substance", is a synthetic proposition _a priori_, because, first, it goes beyond the concept laid as its ground and adds the ***mode of existence*** to thinking in general, and, second, it adds to that concept a predicate (of simplicity) which cannot be given in any experience whatsoever. Therefore, synthetic propositions _a priori_ are not, as we have asserted, feasible and permissible only in relation to objects of possible experience, and indeed as principles of the possibility of this experience itself, but they can also apply to things in general and in themselves, which conclusion would put an end to this entire critique and command us to leave things as they were. However, the danger here is not so great if one looks closer at the matter.

In the procedure of rational psychology there reigns a paralogism, which is represented by the following syllogism.

***That which cannot be thought otherwise than as subject, also exists not otherwise than as subject, and is therefore substance.***

{{ 411 }} ***Now a thinking being, considered merely as such, cannot be thought otherwise than as subject.***

***Therefore it also exists only as such, i.e., as substance.***

In the major premise, we speak of a being that can be thought in general, in every respect, consequently also as it may be given in intuition. In the minor premise, however, we speak of it only insofar as it considers itself as subject only relatively to thinking and the unity of consciousness, but not at the same time in relation to the intuition whereby it is given as an object to thought. Consequently, the conclusion is drawn _per sophisma figurae dictionis_, and mithin through a fallacy.[^**]

{{{ 270 }}} {{ 412 }} That this resolution of the famous argument into a paralogism is entirely correct is clearly evident if one will consult the General Remark on the Systematic Representation of the Principles and the section on Noumena, where it was proved: that the concept of a thing which can exist for itself as subject, but not as mere predicate, carries with it no objective reality at all, i.e., that one cannot know whether an object can belong to it anywhere, since one does not understand the possibility of such a mode of existence, and consequently that it yields absolutely no knowledge. If it is therefore to indicate, under the name of a substance, an object that can be given; if it is to become a piece of knowledge: then a persistent intuition must be laid as the ground, as the indispensable condition of the objective reality of a concept, namely, that through which alone the object is given. Now we have in {{ 413 }} inner intuition nothing persistent at all, for the "I" is only the consciousness of my thinking; therefore, if we stop at mere thinking, we also lack the necessary condition for applying the concept of substance, i.e., of a self-subsisting subject, to oneself as a thinking being; and the simplicity of substance combined therewith falls away entirely along with the objective reality of this concept, and is transformed into a mere logical, qualitative unity of self-consciousness in thinking in general, whether the subject be composite or not.

## Refutation of Mendelssohn's Proof of the Permanence of the Soul.

This sharp-witted philosopher soon noticed, in the common argument by which it is to be proved that the soul (if one admits it to be a simple being) cannot cease to exist by ***disintegration***, a lack of sufficiency for the purpose of securing its necessary permanence, since one could still assume a cessation of its existence {{{ 271 }}} through ***vanishing***. In his _Phaedo_, he sought to ward off this perishability, which would be a true annihilation, from it by venturing to prove: that a simple being cannot cease to be at all, because, since it cannot be diminished and thus cannot gradually lose something of its existence and so ***gradually*** be converted in {{ 414 }} into nothing (having no parts and therefore no plurality in itself), no time would be found between an instant in which it is, and another in which it is no longer, which is impossible. — But he did not consider that, even if we grant the soul this simple nature, in that it contains no manifold ***outside one another*** and mithin no extensive magnitude, one can yet not deny to it, any more than to any existing thing, intensive magnitude, i.e., a degree of reality in respect of all its faculties, indeed of everything that constitutes existence, which degree can decrease through all the infinitely many smaller degrees, and thus the alleged substance (the thing whose permanence is not otherwise established), although not by disintegration, yet by gradual relaxation (_remissio_) of its powers (and mithin by elanguescence, if I may be permitted to use this expression) can be converted into nothing. For even consciousness always has a degree, which can always be further diminished {{ 415 }}[^***], and consequently also the faculty of being conscious of oneself, and so all other faculties. — Thus the permanence of the soul, as a mere object of the inner sense, remains unproved and indeed unprovable, although its permanence in life, since the thinking being (as man) is at the same time an object of outer senses, is clear of itself, with which, however, the rational psychologist is by no means satisfied, {{{ 272 }}} who undertakes to prove its absolute permanence even beyond this life from mere concepts[^****].

{{ 416 }} If we now take our above propositions, as they must be taken in a ***synthetic*** connection in rational psychology as a system so as to be valid for all thinking beings, and go from the {{{ 273 }}} {{ 417 }} category of relation with the proposition: "all thinking beings are as such substances", backward through the series of these categories until the circle closes, we hit at last upon their existence, of which in this system they are not only conscious independently of outer things, {{ 418 }} but which they can also determine of themselves (in respect of the permanence that of necessity belongs to the character of substance). From this, however, it follows that ***idealism*** is unavoidable in this very same rationalistic system, at least problematic idealism; and if the existence of outer things is not required at all for the determination of one's own existence in time, the former is assumed only quite gratuitously, without ever being able to give a proof of it.

If, on the contrary, we follow the ***analytic*** procedure, taking as given the "I think" as a proposition that already contains an existence in itself, so that modality lies at the ground, and analyze it in order to know its content—namely, whether and how this "I" in space or time determines its existence merely thereby—then the propositions of rational psychology would start not from the concept of a thinking being in general, but from a reality, and from the manner in which this {{ 419 }} is thought, after everything that is empirical therein has been separated, that which belongs to a thinking being in general would be inferred, as the following table shows.

{{{ 274 }}} Since it is not determined here in the second proposition whether ***I*** can exist and be thought of only as subject and not also as predicate of another, the concept of a subject is here taken merely logically, and it remains undetermined whether substance is to be understood by it or not. But in the third proposition, the absolute unity of apperception—the simple "I" in representation, to which all connection or separation that constitutes thinking is referred—becomes important for its own sake, even though I have not yet determined anything about the subject's constitution or subsistence. Apperception is something real, and its simplicity lies already in its possibility. Now, there is nothing real in space that is simple; for points (which constitute the only simple thing in space) are merely limits, and are not themselves something {{ 420 }} that serves to constitute space as a part. Consequently, there follows from this the impossibility of explaining the constitution of myself, as a merely thinking subject, from grounds of ***materialism***. But because my existence is regarded as given in the first proposition—since it does not say "every thinking being exists" (which would assert absolute necessity and therefore too much of them), but only: ***I exist*** thinking—this proposition is empirical, and contains the determinability of my existence merely in respect of my representations in time. Since I, however, again require for this first of all something persistent, the like of which is not given to me at all in inner intuition insofar as I think myself: it is not possible at all to determine, through this simple self-consciousness, the manner in which I exist, whether as substance or as accident. Therefore, if ***materialism*** is unfit for the mode of explanation of my existence, ***spiritualism*** is just as insufficient for it; and the conclusion is that we cannot in any way whatsoever know anything of the constitution of our soul that concerns the possibility of its separate existence in general.

And how should it indeed be possible, by means of the unity of consciousness—which we ourselves know only because we indispensably need it for the possibility of experience—to go beyond experience (our existence in life), and even to extend our knowledge to the nature {{ 421 }} of all thinking beings in general through the empirical but, in respect of every kind of intuition, undetermined proposition: "I think"?

There is therefore no rational psychology as a doctrine that would provide us with an addition to our self-knowledge, but only as a {{{ 275 }}} ***discipline***, which sets impassable limits to speculative reason in this field, on the one hand in order not to throw itself into the lap of soulless materialism, and on the other hand not to lose itself in a spiritualism that is groundless for us in this life, but rather reminds us to look upon this refusal of our reason to give a satisfying answer to the curious questions reaching beyond this life as a hint from it to turn our self-knowledge from fruitless, extravagant speculation to fruitful practical use, which, although it is always directed only to objects of experience, yet takes its principles from a higher source and determines our behavior as if our destination reached infinitely far beyond experience, and mithin beyond this life.

One sees from all this that a mere misunderstanding gives rise to rational psychology. The unity of consciousness, which lies at the ground of the categories, is here taken for an intuition of the {{ 422 }} subject as object, and the category of substance is applied to it. It is, however, only the unity in ***thinking***, by which alone no object is given, to which therefore the category of substance—which always presupposes a given ***intuition***—cannot be applied, and consequently this subject cannot be known at all. The subject of the categories can therefore, by the fact that it thinks them, not obtain a concept of itself as an object of the categories; for in order to think them, it must lay down its pure self-consciousness—which was the very thing to be explained—as ground. Likewise, the subject in which the representation of time originally has its ground cannot determine its own existence in time thereby; and if the latter cannot occur, then neither can the former take place as a determination of itself (as a thinking being in general) through categories.[^*****]

{{ 423 }} Thus, a piece of knowledge attempted beyond the limits of possible experience, and yet belonging to the highest interest of humanity, vanishes, so far as it is to be owed to speculative philosophy, {{{ 276 }}} {{ 424 }} into disappointed expectation; whereby, nevertheless, the severity of the critique, in that it at the same time proves the impossibility of making any dogmatic assertion concerning an object of experience beyond the limits of experience, performs a not unimportant service to reason in this interest of hers, by placing her in equal security against all possible assertions of the contrary; which cannot happen otherwise than by either proving one's proposition apodictically, or, if this does not succeed, seeking out the sources of this inability, which, if they lie in the necessary limits of our reason, must then subject every opponent to exactly the same law of renunciation of all claims to dogmatic assertion.

Nevertheless, not the least is lost hereby for the right, or even the necessity, of assuming a future life in accordance with principles of the practical use of reason combined with the speculative; for the merely speculative proof has in any case never been able to exert any influence on common human reason. It is balanced on so fine a point that even the schools can {{{ 277 }}} keep it standing on it only so long as they keep it spinning like a top, and thus in their own eyes it provides no lasting foundation upon which anything could be built. The proofs {{ 425 }} that are of use to the world all remain in their undiminished value, and rather gain in clarity and unstudied conviction through the removal of those dogmatic pretensions, by placing reason in its proper field—namely, the order of ends, which is at the same time an order of nature—but which, as a practical faculty in itself, without being limited to the conditions of the latter, is entitled to extend the former, and with it our own existence, beyond the limits of experience and of life. To judge according to the ***analogy with the nature*** of living beings in this world, in regard to which reason must of necessity adopt the principle that no organ, no faculty, no impulse—thus nothing superfluous or unproportioned to its use, and mithin purposeless—is to be met with, but rather that everything is exactly conformed to its destination in life, man, who yet alone can contain in himself the ultimate end of all this, would have to be the only creature that was excepted from it. For his natural endowments—not merely as regards the talents and impulses to make use of them, but chiefly the moral law in him—go so far beyond all utility and advantage that he could draw from them in this life, that the latter even teaches him to esteem above all things the mere consciousness of rectitude {{ 426 }} of disposition in the absence of all advantages, even including the shadow-play of posthumous fame, and he feels himself inwardly called to make himself fit, by his conduct in this world and by renunciation of many advantages, to be a citizen of a better world which he has in idea. This powerful, never to be refuted ground of proof, accompanied by a constantly increasing knowledge of the purposiveness in everything we see before us, and by a view into the immensity of creation, and mithin also by the consciousness of a certain boundlessness in the possible extension of our knowledge, together with an impulse conformed thereto, still remains over, even if we must give up the attempt to understand the necessary duration of our existence from the merely theoretical knowledge of ourselves.

## {{{ 278 }}} Conclusion of the Solution of the Psychological Paralogism.

The dialectical illusion in rational psychology rests on the confusion of an idea of reason (of a pure intelligence) with the, in all respects, undetermined concept of a thinking being in general. I think myself for the sake of a possible experience, while still abstracting from all actual experience, and I conclude therefrom that I can become conscious of my existence even outside of experience and the {{ 427 }} empirical conditions of the same. Consequently, I confuse the possible ***abstraction*** from my empirically determined existence with the supposed consciousness of a possible ***separate*** existence of my thinking self, and believe that I know the substantial in me as the transcendental subject, when I have in thought merely the unity of consciousness, which lies at the ground of all determining as the mere form of knowledge.

The problem of explaining the community of the soul with the body does not properly belong to that psychology of which we are here speaking, because its intention is to prove the personality of the soul even outside this community (after death), and mithin is in the proper sense ***transcendent***, although it occupies itself with an object of experience, but only insofar as it ceases to be an object of experience. Nevertheless, a sufficient answer can also be given to this according to our system of doctrine. The difficulty that has occasioned this problem consists, as is well known, in the presupposed heterogeneity of the object of the inner sense (the soul) and the objects of the outer senses, since to the former only time, to the latter also space, attaches as the formal condition of their intuition. But if one considers that both kinds of objects differ herein not inwardly, but {{ 428 }} only insofar as one ***appears*** outwardly to the other, and mithin that what lies at the ground of the appearance of matter as a thing in itself might perhaps not be so heterogeneous, then this difficulty vanishes, and none remains except that of how a community of substances is possible at all, the solution of which lies entirely outside the field of psychology, and, as the reader will easily judge from what was said in the Analytic concerning fundamental forces and faculties, lies without any doubt also outside the field of all human knowledge.

[^*]: The reader, who from these expressions in their transcendental abstraction will not so easily guess their psychological sense, and why the latter attribute of the soul belongs to the category of ***existence***, will find them sufficiently explained and justified in what follows. For the rest, with regard to the Latin expressions which, contrary to the style of good style, have crept in instead of equivalent German ones, I have to offer as an excuse, both in this section and in respect of the entire work: that I preferred to detract somewhat from the elegance of the language rather than to render scholastic usage more difficult by the least lack of intelligibility.

[^**]: Thinking is taken in a quite different meaning in the two premises: in the major premise, as it applies to an object in general (consequently, as it may be given in intuition); in the minor premise, however, only as it consists in relation to self-consciousness, wherein therefore no object at all is thought, but only the relation to oneself as subject (as the form of thinking) is represented. In the former, we speak of things that cannot be thought otherwise than as subjects; in the latter, however, we speak not of ***things***, but of ***thinking*** (in that we abstract from all objects), in which the 'I' always serves as the subject of consciousness; therefore, it cannot follow in the conclusion: "I cannot exist otherwise than as subject", but only: "I can, in thinking my existence, use myself only as the subject of the judgment", which is an identical proposition that absolutely does not reveal anything about the mode of my existence.

[^***]: Clarity is not, as the logicians say, the consciousness of a representation; for a certain degree of consciousness, which however does not suffice for recollection, must be present even in many obscure representations, because without all consciousness we should make no distinction in the connection of obscure representations, which we are yet able to do in the case of the characteristics of many concepts (such as those of right and equity, and of the musician when he strikes many notes simultaneously in improvising). Rather, a representation is clear in which consciousness is sufficient for the **consciousness** ***of the difference*** between it and others. If this is indeed sufficient for distinguishing, but not for the consciousness of the difference, then the representation would still have to be called obscure. Thus, there are infinitely many degrees of consciousness down to its vanishing.

[^****]: Those who, in order to introduce a new possibility, believe they have done enough if they rely on the fact that no contradiction can be shown in their presuppositions (as are all those who believe they understand the possibility of thought after its cessation, of which they have an example only in the empirical intuitions in human life), can be put into great embarrassment by other possibilities that are not in the least bolder. Such is the possibility of the division of a ***simple substance*** into several substances, and conversely, the coalescence (coalition) of several into one simple substance. For although divisibility presupposes a composite, it does not of necessity require a composite of substances, but only of degrees (of the various faculties) of one and the very same substance. Just as we can now think of all the forces and faculties of the soul, even that of consciousness, as having diminished by half, yet so that substance would still remain: so we can, without contradiction, conceive of this expired half as preserved, not indeed in it, but outside it, and that since everything in it that is at all real has consequently a degree, and mithin its entire existence has been halved without anything lacking, a special substance would then arise outside it. For the plurality which has been divided was already there beforehand, yet not as a plurality of substances, but of every reality as a quantum of existence in it; and the unity of substance was only a mode of existence which by this division alone has been transformed into a plurality of subsistence. But in the same way, several simple substances could coalesce again into one, whereby nothing would be lost but merely the plurality of subsistence, since the one would contain the degree of reality of all the former ones together in itself; and perhaps the simple substances which give us the appearance of matter might (not indeed through a mechanical or chemical influence upon one another, but yet through an unknown influence of which the former would be only the appearance) produce children-souls by such a ***dynamic*** division of parent-souls as ***intensive magnitudes***, while the latter replenished their loss again through coalition with new material of the same kind. I am far from allowing any value or validity to such figments of the brain, and the above principles of the Analytic have also sufficiently enjoined us to make no other than an empirical use of the categories (such as that of substance). But if the rationalist is bold enough to make a self-subsisting being out of the mere faculty of thought, without any persistent intuition whereby an object would be given, merely because the unity of apperception in thinking does not permit him to explain it from what is composite—instead of doing better to confess that he does not know how to explain the possibility of a thinking nature—why should the ***materialist***, although he can just as little adduce experience in favor of his possibilities, not be entitled to equal boldness in using his principle for the opposite purpose, while retaining the formal unity of the former?

[^*****]: The **"I think"** **is**, as has already been said, an empirical proposition, and includes the proposition "I exist" in itself. I cannot, however, say: "everything that thinks, exists"; for then the property of thinking would make all beings that possess it into necessary beings. Therefore, my existence cannot be regarded as inferred from the proposition "I think", as Cartesius held (since otherwise the major premise "everything that thinks, exists" would have to precede), but is identical with it. It expresses an undetermined empirical intuition, i.e., perception (consequently proving that sensation, which consequently belongs to sensibility, lies at the ground of this existential proposition), but precedes experience, which is to determine the object of perception through the category in respect of time; and existence is here not yet a category, as that has relation not to an undetermined given object, but only to such an object of which one has a concept and of which one wishes to know whether it is also posited outside this concept or not. An undetermined perception here signifies only something real that has been given, and indeed only to thinking in general, hence not as appearance, nor as a thing in itself (noumenon), but as something which in fact exists and is indicated as such in the proposition "I think". For it is to be noted that when I have called the proposition "I think" an empirical proposition, I do not mean to say thereby that the "I" in this proposition is an empirical representation; rather, it is purely intellectual, because it belongs to thinking in general. But without some empirical representation which provides the material for thinking, the actus "I think" would yet not take place, and the empirical is only the condition of the application or use of the pure intellectual faculty.
