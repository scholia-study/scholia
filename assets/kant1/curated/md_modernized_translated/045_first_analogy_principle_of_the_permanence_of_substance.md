## {{{ 162 }}} First Analogy. Principle of the permanence of substance

***In all change of appearances substance persists, and its quantum is neither increased nor diminished in nature.***

## Proof.

All appearances are in time, in which, as substrate (as persistent form of inner intuition), both ***coexistence*** and ***succession*** can alone be represented. Time itself, therefore, in which all {{ 225 }} change of appearances is to be thought, remains and does not change; because it is that in which succession or coexistence can be represented only as determinations of it. Now time cannot be perceived by itself. Consequently, in the objects of perception, i. e., the appearances, the substrate must be encountered which represents time in general, and in which all change or coexistence can be perceived in apprehension through the relation of the appearances to it. But the substrate of all that is real, i. e., of that which belongs to the existence of things, is ***substance***, in which everything that belongs to existence can be thought only as a determination. Consequently the permanent, in relation to which alone all time-relations of appearances can be determined, is the substance in the appearance, i. e., the real of the same, which as substrate of all change always remains the same. Since this, therefore, cannot change in existence, its quantum in nature can also be neither increased nor diminished.

{{{ 163 }}} Our ***apprehension*** of the manifold of appearance is at all times successive, and is therefore always changing. By this alone, therefore, we can never determine whether this manifold as object of experience is coexistent or successive, if there is not something at its basis ***which is at all times***, i. e., something ***abiding*** and ***persistent***, of which all change and coexistence are nothing but {{ 226 }} so many ways (_modi_ of time) in which the persistent exists. Only in the permanent, therefore, are time-relations possible (for simultaneity and succession are the only relations in time), i. e., the permanent is the substratum of the empirical representation of time itself, in which alone all time-determination is possible. Permanence, in general, expresses time as the constant correlatum of all existence of appearances, of all change and of all coexistence. For change does not affect time itself, but only the appearances in time (just as coexistence is not a _modus_ of time itself, since in it no parts are coexistent, but all are successive). If one wanted to attribute a succession in time to time itself, one would have to think yet another time in which this succession would be possible. Through the permanent alone does ***existence*** in different parts of the time-series successively acquire a ***magnitude***, which is called ***duration***. For in mere succession alone, existence is always vanishing and commencing, and never has the least magnitude. Without this permanent, therefore, there is no time-relation. Now time cannot be perceived in itself; consequently, this permanent in the appearances is the substratum of all time-determination, and therefore also the condition of the possibility of all synthetic unity of perceptions, i. e., of experience {{ 227 }}, and in this permanent all existence and all change in time can be regarded only as a _modus_ of the existence of that which remains and persists. Thus in all appearances the permanent is the object itself, i. e., the substance (_phaenomenon_), but everything that changes or can change belongs only to the way in which this substance or these substances exist, and consequently to their determinations.

I find that at all times not merely the philosopher, but even the common understanding has presupposed this permanence as a substratum of all change of appearances, and will also at all times assume it as beyond doubt, only that the philosopher expresses himself somewhat more definitely on this point by saying: in all alterations in the {{{ 164 }}} world the ***substance*** remains, and only the ***accidents*** change. However, I nowhere meet with even the attempt at a proof of this so synthetic proposition; indeed, it only seldom stands, as is yet its due, at the head of the pure laws of nature that exist entirely _a priori_. In fact, the proposition that substance is permanent is tautological. For it is merely this permanence which is the ground why we apply the category of substance to appearance, and it ought to have been proved that in all appearances there is something permanent, in which the changeable is nothing but a determination of its existence. {{ 228 }} Since, however, such a proof can never be conducted dogmatically, i. e., from concepts, because it concerns a synthetic proposition _a priori_, and since it was never thought that such propositions are valid only in relation to possible experience, and can therefore be proved only through a deduction of the possibility of the latter: it is no wonder that, although it has indeed been laid at the basis of all experience (since its need is felt in empirical cognition), it has yet never been proved.

A philosopher was asked: how much does smoke weigh? He answered: subtract from the weight of the burnt wood the weight of the remaining ashes, and so you have the weight of the smoke. He therefore presupposed as undeniable: that even in fire matter (substance) does not perish, but only its form suffers an alteration. In the same way, the proposition: nothing comes from nothing, was only another corollary from the principle of permanence, or rather of the everlasting existence of the proper subject in the appearances. For if that in the appearance which we choose to call substance is to be the proper substratum of all time-determination, then both all existence in the past and that in the future time must be capable of being determined solely and uniquely in it. Hence we can give the name of substance to an appearance only because we presuppose its existence {{ 229 }} at all times, which is not even well expressed by the word permanence, since this refers more to future time. Nevertheless, the inner necessity to persist is yet inseparably connected with the necessity of having always existed, and the expression may therefore remain. _Gigni de nihilo nihil, in nihilum nil posse reverti_, were two propositions which the ancients linked inseparably together, and which are now sometimes separated through a misunderstanding, because it is imagined that they concern things in themselves, and that the former might be opposed to the dependence of the world {{{ 165 }}} on a supreme cause (even as regards its substance); which apprehension is unnecessary, since we are here speaking only of appearances in the field of experience, the unity of which would never be possible if we were to allow new things (as regards their substance) to arise. For then that would fall away which alone can represent the unity of time, namely, the identity of the substratum, as that in which alone all change has thoroughgoing unity. This permanence, however, is yet nothing more than the way in which we represent to ourselves the existence of things (in the appearance).

The determinations of a substance, which are nothing else than particular ways in which it exists, are called ***accidents***. They are at all times real, because they concern the existence of the substance (negations are only determinations which express the non-existence of something in the substance). {{ 230 }} If we now attribute a particular existence to this real in the substance (e. g., to motion as an accident of matter), we call this existence inherence, in distinction from the existence of the substance, which we call subsistence. But from this many misunderstandings arise, and it is more precise and correct to speak of the accident only as the way in which the existence of a substance is positively determined. Nevertheless, by virtue of the conditions of the logical use of our understanding, it is yet unavoidable to isolate, as it were, that which can change in the existence of a substance, while the substance remains, and to consider it in relation to the proper permanent and radical element; hence also this category stands under the title of relations, rather as their condition than as containing a relation itself.

Upon this permanence is now also based the correction of the concept of ***alteration***. Coming into being and perishing are not alterations of that which comes into being or perishes. Alteration is a way of existing which succeeds another way of existing of the very same object. Therefore everything that alters is ***abiding***, and only its ***state changes***. Since this, therefore, affects only the determinations which can cease or begin, we can say, in an expression which seems somewhat paradoxical: only the permanent {{ 231 }} (substance) is altered; the changeable suffers no alteration, but only a ***change***, in so far as certain determinations cease and others begin.

{{{ 166 }}} Alteration can therefore be perceived only in substances, and coming into being or perishing as such, unless it concerns merely a determination of the permanent, cannot be any possible perception at all, because it is this very permanent which makes possible the representation of the transition from one state into another, and from non-existence to existence, which can therefore be empirically known only as changing determinations of that which remains. Assume that something absolutely begins to be, then you must have a point of time in which it was not. But to what will you attach this point, if not to that which is already there? For an empty time which would precede is no object of perception; but if you link this coming into being to things which were before and continue up to that which comes into being, then the latter was only a determination of the former as the permanent. It is just the same with perishing: for this presupposes the empirical representation of a time in which an appearance no longer exists.

Substances (in appearance) are the substrates of all time-determinations. The coming into being of some and the perishing of others of them would itself abolish the sole condition of the empirical unity of time {{ 232 }}, and appearances would then relate to two different times, in which existence would flow on side by side, which is absurd. For there is ***only one*** time, in which all different times must be placed not as coexistent, but as successive.

Thus, therefore, permanence is a necessary condition, under which alone appearances are determinable as things or objects in a possible experience. But as to what may be the empirical criterion of this necessary permanence, and with it of the substantiality of appearances, the sequel will afford us opportunity to note what is necessary.
