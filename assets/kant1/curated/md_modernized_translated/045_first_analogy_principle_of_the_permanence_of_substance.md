## {{{ 162 }}} First Analogy. Principle of the permanence of substance

***Substance persists through every change of the appearances, and the quantity of it present in nature is neither augmented nor reduced.***

## Proof.

All appearances are found in time, in which — serving as substrate (as the permanent form of inner intuition) — both ***coexistence*** and ***succession*** can be represented at all. Time itself, accordingly, wherein all {{ 225 }} change of appearances must be thought, abides and does not itself change, being that in which sequence, or coexistence, can be represented only as determinations belonging to it. Now time on its own cannot be perceived. It follows that the objects of perception — appearances, that is — must contain the substrate representing time as such, upon which, through the appearances' relation to it, every instance of change or coexistence becomes perceptible in apprehension. But that substrate of all that is real — of all, that is, that pertains to the existence of things — is ***substance***, and everything belonging to existence can be thought within it merely as a determination. The permanent, then — that whereby alone every temporal relation among appearances admits of determination — is substance within the appearance, that is, its real component, which, as the substrate underlying all change, stays ever the same. And because it can undergo no change in its existence, its quantity in nature likewise admits of neither increase nor decrease.

{{{ 163 }}} The ***apprehension*** whereby we take up the manifold of an appearance is invariably successive, and hence in constant flux. By that alone, therefore, we can never settle whether this manifold, taken as an object of experience, exists together or follows in sequence, unless something underlies it ***that is at all times***, i.e., something ***abiding*** and ***permanent***, in relation to which all change and coexistence amount to nothing more than {{ 226 }} the several modes (_modi_ of time) according to which the permanent exists. Temporal relations, therefore, are possible only within the permanent (since the sole relations obtaining in time are simultaneity and sequence), which is to say that the permanent furnishes the substratum on which the very empirical representation of time reposes, and in it alone can any determination of time occur. Permanence gives expression to time in general as the abiding correlate of every existence of appearances, of every change and of all concomitance. Change, after all, does not touch time itself but only the appearances lodged in time (much as coexistence is no _modus_ of time proper, given that in time no parts stand together but all lie one after another). If someone wished to attribute to time itself a succession one after another, he would be forced to posit a further time within which such a succession could take place. It is through the permanent alone that ***existence***, across the successive stretches of the time-series, takes on a ***magnitude*** one calls ***duration***. For in bare sequence taken by itself, existence is forever ceasing and starting up and never possesses even the slightest magnitude. Absent this permanent, therefore, no temporal relation obtains at all. Time as such, however, admits of no perception; and so this permanent in the appearances is what underlies every determination of time — and thus, too, the condition making possible any synthetic unity among perceptions, i.e., experience {{ 227 }} — while within this permanent all existence and all change in time can be regarded solely as a _modus_ of the existence of that which remains and persists. In every appearance, accordingly, the permanent is the object itself, i.e., the substance (_phaenomenon_), whereas all that changes, or is capable of changing, pertains merely to the manner in which this substance or these substances exist, and hence to their determinations.

At every period, I observe, this permanence has been taken for granted — not by the philosopher alone but by ordinary understanding as well — as a substratum for all change of appearances, and it will always continue to be accepted as beyond question; only that the philosopher puts the matter somewhat more precisely, saying: through all alterations in the {{{ 164 }}} world the ***substance*** endures, and the ***accidents*** alone change. Yet nowhere do I come upon so much as an attempt at proving this thoroughly synthetic proposition; indeed, it but seldom stands where it by rights belongs, at the forefront of the pure laws of nature that hold wholly _a priori_. The proposition that substance is permanent is, in truth, a tautology. For it is this permanence alone that grounds our applying the category of substance to appearance, and one would have needed to show that in all appearances something permanent is present, in relation to which the mutable counts as nothing more than a way in which its existence is determined. {{ 228 }} But since a proof of this kind can never be carried through dogmatically, i.e., out of mere concepts — for it concerns a synthetic proposition _a priori_ — and since it was never noticed that propositions of that sort hold good only in reference to possible experience, and can therefore be established only by a deduction of the possibility of the latter: it is no wonder that, though laid at the basis of all experience (one feels the need of it in empirical cognition), it has nevertheless never been proved.

A philosopher was once asked: how much does smoke weigh? He replied: from the weight of the burnt wood subtract the weight of the ash left behind, and the weight of the smoke is what remains. He thereby assumed as beyond dispute that even in fire matter (substance) does not perish, but only its form suffers a modification. In the same way, the maxim that nothing arises from nothing was but a further consequence drawn from the principle of permanence — or rather from the perpetual existence of the true subject underlying the appearances. For should whatever in an appearance we choose to call substance serve as the genuine substratum for every determination of time, then existence alike in time past and in time to come must be fixable in it, and in it alone. Hence we call an appearance a substance for one reason only: because we presuppose its existence {{ 229 }} at all times — which the word permanence does not even express particularly well, seeing as it bears more on time to come. Even so, the inner necessity of persisting is inseparably bound together with the necessity of having always been, and so the term may well be retained. _Gigni de nihilo nihil, in nihilum nil posse reverti_ were two propositions that the ancients linked indissolubly, and that, through a misunderstanding, are nowadays now and then pulled apart, because one fancies that they bear on things in themselves, and that the first might stand opposed to the world's dependence {{{ 165 }}} on a supreme cause (even in respect of its substance); a concern which is needless, since what is here in question is merely appearances in the field of experience, whose unity could never be possible were we to let new things (in respect of substance) come into being. For in that event the very thing that alone can represent time's unity would fall away — namely the self-identity of the substratum, as that wherein all change alone possesses thoroughgoing unity. This permanence, though, is nothing beyond the manner in which we represent to ourselves the existence of things (in the appearance).

The determinations of a substance, being nothing but particular ways in which it exists, are termed ***accidents***. They are in every case real, inasmuch as they bear on the substance's existence (negations being merely determinations that voice the non-being of something in the substance). {{ 230 }} Now if one assigns to this real element in the substance a distinct existence of its own (e.g., to motion as an accident of matter), then one names this existence inherence, as against the existence of the substance, which one names subsistence. From this, however, a good many misreadings arise, and one speaks with greater precision and correctness by designating the accident solely through the way a substance's existence is positively determined. Still, given the conditions attaching to the logical use of our understanding, it is unavoidable that we set apart, so to speak, whatever can vary in a substance's existence while the substance itself persists, and consider it in relation to the properly permanent and radical; and it is on this account that this category, too, falls under the heading of the relations, more as their condition than as itself containing any relation.

It is on this permanence, then, that the correction of the concept of ***alteration*** likewise rests. Coming-to-be and passing-away are not alterations of that which comes to be or passes away. Alteration, rather, is one manner of existing that succeeds upon another manner of existing in one and the very same object. Whatever undergoes alteration, then, is itself ***enduring***, and it is only its ***state that changes***. Since this change, then, bears solely on the determinations that can cease or likewise begin: we may put it in a somewhat paradoxical-sounding way and say that only the permanent {{ 231 }} (the substance) is altered, whereas the mutable undergoes no alteration but a ***change***, some determinations ceasing and others beginning.

{{{ 166 }}} Alteration, accordingly, is perceptible only in substances, and a sheer coming-to-be or passing-away that did not concern merely some determination of the permanent could never amount to a possible perception at all, for it is precisely this permanent that first renders representable the passage out of one state into another and out of non-being into being — a passage, then, that can be cognized empirically only as the shifting determinations of what abides. Suppose something began to be absolutely, then you would need a point in time at which it was not. But onto what would you fasten that point, if not onto something already present? For an empty time preceding it is no object of perception; whereas if you attach this coming-to-be to things that existed beforehand and last up to the thing that arises, then the latter was but a determination of the former as the permanent. And it stands the same with passing-away, for this presupposes the empirical representation of a time at which an appearance no longer is.

Substances (in the appearance) are the substrates of all determinations of time. Were some of them to come to be and others to pass away, the one and only condition of time's empirical unity would thereby be cancelled {{ 232 }}, and the appearances would then be referred to two distinct times in which existence ran its course side by side — which is absurd. For time is ***but one***, and within it all the different times must be posited not together but one after another.

Permanence, then, proves to be a condition necessarily required if appearances are to be determinable at all as things or objects within a possible experience. But as for what the empirical criterion of this necessary permanence — and with it of the substantiality of appearances — might be, the sequel will afford us occasion to note what is needful.
