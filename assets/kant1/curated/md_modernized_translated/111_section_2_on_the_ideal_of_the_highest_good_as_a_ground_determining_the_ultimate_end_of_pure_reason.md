## Section 2. On the ideal of the highest good, as a ground determining the ultimate end of pure reason

Reason, in its speculative employment, conducted us across the terrain of experience, and then—since it can nowhere find full satisfaction there—onward to speculative ideas; yet these in the end steered us back to experience once more, so that reason accomplished its purpose in a way that, however useful, fell quite short of what we had expected. One trial yet remains for us: whether pure reason may also be found at work in its practical employment; whether therein it conducts us to the ideas that reach those supreme ends of pure reason just enumerated above; and whether, viewed from the angle of its practical concern, it can after all bestow on us precisely what, on the speculative side, it denies us altogether.

Every concern of my reason, the speculative no less than the practical, converges upon the three following questions:
+ 1. What can I know? {{ 833 }}
+ 2. What ought I to do?
+ 3. What am I permitted to hope?

{{{ 523 }}} The first of these is purely speculative. As I flatter myself, we have run through every answer that it admits of and have finally lit upon the one at which reason must indeed come to rest and, provided it keeps its gaze off the practical, has good ground to feel content; yet we stand as remote as ever from the two great ends at which this whole striving of pure reason was really aimed—as remote as if we had, from sheer love of comfort, refused the task at the very start. If, then, what we are after is knowledge, this much at any rate stands firm and beyond dispute: that in regard to those two tasks it can never become ours.

The second question is purely practical. As such a question it may well fall to pure reason, and yet in that case it is not transcendental but moral, so that it cannot be a business for our critique taken by itself.

The third question—namely, if I now do what I ought, what am I then entitled to hope? is at once practical and theoretical, in such a way that the practical merely serves as a thread by which we arrive at the theoretical, and, where this reaches high, the speculative question. For all ***hoping*** aims at happiness, and, as concerns the practical and the moral law, it is exactly what knowledge and the law of nature are as concerns the theoretical cognition {{ 834 }} of things. The former issues at last in the inference that something ***is*** (that which fixes the final possible end) ***because something ought to happen***; the second, that something ***is*** (that which operates as supreme cause) ***because something happens***.

Happiness is the gratification of all our inclinations (whether _extensive_, in respect of their manifoldness, or _intensive_, in respect of degree, or again _protensive_, in respect of duration). A practical law whose moving ground is ***happiness*** I term pragmatic (a rule of prudence); but one—should any such exist—that takes for its moving ground nothing beyond the ***worthiness to be happy*** I term moral (the moral law). The first counsels what must be done if we would have a share in happiness; the second dictates how we must bear ourselves so as merely to grow worthy of it. The first is founded on empirical principles; for apart from the mediation of experience I have no way of knowing either which inclinations exist that press for satisfaction, {{{ 524 }}} or what the natural causes are through which their satisfaction might be brought about. The second leaves out of account both the inclinations and the natural means of gratifying them, attending solely to the freedom of a rational being as such together with the necessary conditions under which that freedom, and it alone, accords with a distribution of happiness by principles, and therefore it ***can*** at the least rest upon bare ideas of pure reason and admit of being cognized _a priori_.

{{ 835 }} I take it that there genuinely exist pure moral laws which, wholly _a priori_ (with no regard to empirical moving grounds, that is, to happiness), settle what is to be done and left undone, that is, govern how the freedom of a rational being in general is exercised, and that such laws command ***absolutely*** (not merely conditionally, on the supposition of further empirical ends) and are on that account necessary in every respect. This proposition I am entitled to lay down in advance, invoking not merely the demonstrations of the most enlightened among moralists, but the ethical judgment of any human being whatever, once he sets himself to conceive such a law with clarity.

Pure reason accordingly harbors—not in its speculative use, to be sure, but nonetheless in a certain practical, namely the moral, use—principles of the ***possibility of experience***, that is, of such deeds as ***could***, in keeping with ethical precepts, come to be found in the ***history*** of humankind. For inasmuch as it commands that such deeds ought to come about, they must also be capable of coming about, and hence a distinctive kind of systematic unity—the moral kind—has to be possible, while the systematic unity of nature could not be established ***on speculative principles*** of reason, since reason wields causality in the matter of freedom at large yet not in the matter of nature taken as a whole, and moral principles of reason are able to give rise to free deeds but not to laws of nature. In consequence, the principles of {{ 836 }} pure reason possess objective reality in their practical, and above all in their moral, use.

A world, to the extent that it should stand in accord with all ethical laws (as, by virtue of the ***freedom*** of rational beings, it ***can*** stand, and, by virtue of the necessary laws of ***morality***, it ***ought*** to stand), I name a **moral world**. So far forth it is conceived merely as an intelligible world, because within it one leaves aside all conditions (ends) and even every impediment to morality therein (the frailty or impurity of {{{ 525 }}} human nature). To that degree, then, it is a mere idea, though a practical one, which really can and should exert its influence upon the sensible world, in order to bring the latter into accord with this idea as far as may be. The idea of a moral world therefore possesses objective reality, not as though it bore upon an object of some intelligible intuition (a thing we are wholly unable to conceive), but as bearing upon the sensible world—though as an object belonging to pure reason in its practical exercise and as a _corpus mysticum_ of the rational beings within that world, in that their free choice, under moral laws, carries in itself a thoroughgoing systematic unity alike with its own self and with the freedom of each of the rest.

That constituted the reply to the earlier of the two questions that pure reason raised touching the practical concern: ***Do that through which*** {{ 837 }} ***you make yourself worthy to be happy***. The second now inquires: given that I so bear myself as not to be undeserving of happiness, may I likewise hope by that means to become capable of sharing in it? Everything, in answering it, turns on whether the principles of pure reason that lay down the law _a priori_ likewise bind up this hope with it of necessity.

My claim, then, is this: that precisely as the moral principles are necessary according to reason in its ***practical*** use, so it is no less necessary, according to reason in its ***theoretical*** use, to grant that each person has ground to hope for happiness to just the degree in which he has, by his conduct, rendered himself worthy of it, and hence that the system of ethical life is knit together with that of happiness indissolubly, though only within the idea of pure reason.

Now within an intelligible, that is, a moral, world—in the concept of which we set aside every impediment to ethical life (the inclinations)—such a system, wherein a proportioned happiness is joined to morality, admits of being thought as necessary as well, for freedom, in part impelled and in part curbed by ethical laws, would be the very source of the universal happiness, so that rational beings, guided by principles of this sort, would themselves author both their own lasting welfare and that of others at once. But this system of a self-recompensing morality {{ 838 }} is no more than an idea, the carrying-out of which hinges on the condition {{{ 526 }}} that ***each and all*** do as they ought—that is, that every deed of rational beings unfold as if it had issued from a sovereign will that gathers all private choice within or beneath itself. But since the bindingness that flows from the moral law holds good for every particular exercise of freedom, even where others should fail to conduct themselves in keeping with that law, nothing in the nature of the world's things, nor in the causality of the actions themselves and their bearing on ethical life, settles how their upshot will stand toward happiness; and the necessary linkage already noted—between the hope of being happy and the ceaseless endeavor to render oneself worthy of happiness—cannot be cognized by reason so long as one takes bare nature for one's foundation, but may only be hoped for once a ***supreme reason***, commanding in accordance with moral laws, is taken as at the same time the ground of nature.

The idea of such an intelligence—one in which a will of the utmost moral perfection, conjoined with the highest bliss, is the origin of all the happiness in the world, so far as that happiness stands in precise proportion to ethical life (as the worthiness to be happy)—this idea I name ***the ideal of the highest good***. Hence it is only within the ideal of that highest ***original*** good that pure reason can come upon the ground of the practically necessary linkage of the two {{ 839 }} constituents of the highest derived good—namely, an intelligible, that is, ***moral***, world. Since we are now bound, through reason, to picture ourselves as belonging to such a world, even though the senses set before us nothing but a world of appearances, we shall have to take that world to be a sequel of our conduct in the sensible world and—because the latter presents us with no such linkage—to be a world still to come for us. God, then, and a life to come are two suppositions that, by the principles of that very reason, cannot be sundered from the bindingness which pure reason lays upon us.

Ethical life taken by itself makes up a system, but happiness does not, save to the extent that it is meted out in exact fitness to morality. This, however, is possible solely within the intelligible world, beneath a wise originator and governor. Reason finds itself constrained to assume such an originator, along with life in such a world—one we must look upon as yet to come—or else to treat the moral laws as hollow chimeras of the mind, since the necessary success that this same {{{ 527 }}} reason ties to them would inevitably lapse were that supposition wanting. This is likewise why everyone looks upon the moral laws as ***commands***, which they could not be did they not, _a priori_, attach fitting consequences to their rule and thus carry ***promises*** and ***threats*** {{ 840 }} along with them. Yet even this they cannot do unless they have their seat in a necessary being—the highest good—that alone can make such a purposive unity possible.

Leibniz gave the world—so far as one heeds in it only the rational beings and their interconnection under moral laws beneath the government of the highest good—the name ***realm of grace***, marking it off from the ***realm of nature***, in which these same beings do indeed stand under moral laws yet look for no results of their conduct other than those that follow the ordinary course of nature in our sensible world. Thus to behold oneself within the realm of grace, where the whole of happiness lies in wait for us—save in so far as we ourselves cut down our share of it by an unworthiness to be happy—is an idea of reason that is practically necessary.

Practical laws, in so far as they come to be at the same time subjective grounds of the actions—that is, subjective principles—are termed ***maxims***. The ***assessment*** of ethical conduct as to its purity and its consequences takes place by ***ideas***; the ***following*** of its laws, by ***maxims***.

It is necessary that the whole conduct of our life be placed under ethical maxims; and yet it is at the same time impossible for this to happen unless reason attaches, to the moral law—which is a bare idea—an operative cause that assigns to conduct governed by that law an outcome answering exactly to our highest ends, be it in {{ 841 }} this life or in some other. Absent a God, then, and a world unseen by us at present but hoped for, the splendid ideas of ethical life are indeed objects of applause and wonder, but not springs of resolve and performance, for they do not fulfil the whole end which is native to every rational being and which that very same pure reason fixes and makes necessary _a priori_.

Happiness on its own is, in the eyes of our reason, very far from the complete good. Reason gives it no sanction (however keenly inclination may crave it) so long as it is not wedded to the worthiness to be happy, that is, to good ethical conduct. But morality on its own, and with it the mere ***worthiness*** to be happy, likewise falls well short of being the {{{ 528 }}} complete good. For this to be brought to completion, whoever has borne himself as not undeserving of happiness must be in a position to hope for a share in it. Even a reason liberated from every private aim, were it—taking no interest of its own into the reckoning—to place itself in the position of a being charged with apportioning all happiness to others, could reach no other verdict; for in the practical idea the two components are bound together essentially, though in such wise that the moral disposition, functioning as condition, is what first makes possible the share in happiness, and not the reverse—that the prospect of happiness should be what first renders the moral disposition possible. For in the latter case it would fail to be {{ 842 }} moral, and hence unworthy also of that entire happiness which, in the sight of reason, acknowledges no restriction other than the one springing from our own unethical conduct.

Happiness, then, in precise commensuration with the ethical worth of rational beings, whereby they are worthy of it, is what alone makes up the highest good belonging to a world into which we are wholly obliged to transport ourselves by the precepts of pure yet practical reason—a world that is, admittedly, no more than intelligible, since the sensible world holds out to us, from the nature of things, no such systematic unity of ends, and whose reality can moreover be founded on nothing else than the supposition of a highest original good, wherein a self-standing reason, furnished with all the sufficiency of a supreme cause, in keeping with the most consummate purposiveness founds, sustains, and brings to completion the universal order of things, though one hidden deep from us in the sensible world.

This moral theology enjoys, now, the singular advantage over the speculative kind that it leads without fail to the concept of a ***single, most perfect of all***, and ***rational*** primordial being—something toward which speculative theology, arguing from objective grounds, does not so much as ***direct*** us, still less could it ***persuade*** us of it. For neither in transcendental nor in natural theology, however far reason may carry us within them, do we come upon any weighty ground for positing so much as a single being that {{ 843 }} we might set over all natural causes and upon which we might have cause enough to make those causes dependent through and through. If, on the other hand, we weigh—from the vantage of ethical unity as a necessary law of the world—the cause that can lend this unity its fitting effect and therewith a force binding upon us, then it must be one sole supreme will that holds all these laws together within {{{ 529 }}} itself. For how could a complete unity of ends ever be found among a plurality of wills? This will must be all-powerful, so that the whole of nature, together with its bearing on ethical life in the world, may lie subject to it; all-knowing, so that it may cognize the inmost of the dispositions and their moral worth; everywhere present, so that it may stand immediately near to every need that the world's supreme good demands; everlasting, so that at no time may this concord of nature and freedom fall short, and so on.

But this systematic unity of ends within the said world of intelligences—which, taken as mere nature, amounts only to a world of sense, whereas as a system of freedom it may be styled an intelligible, that is, moral, world (_regnum gratiae_)—leads without fail to a purposive unity of all the things that compose this vast whole by universal laws of nature, exactly as the former unity does by universal and necessary ethical laws, and it fuses practical reason with the speculative. The world must {{ 844 }} be pictured as having sprung from an idea, if it is to fall into accord with that employment of reason apart from which we should count ourselves unworthy of reason—namely the moral employment, resting as it does wholly on the idea of the highest good. By this means every inquiry into nature receives a bent toward the form of a system of ends, and, at its widest reach, turns into physico-theology. This latter, however—since it set out, after all, from ethical order as a unity grounded in the very essence of freedom and not contingently instituted by outward commands—carries the purposiveness of nature back to grounds that must be _a priori_ and inseparably tied to the inner possibility of things, and thereby to a ***transcendental theology*** which fastens upon the ideal of the highest ontological perfection as its principle of systematic unity—a principle binding all things by universal and necessary laws of nature, since one and all they take their origin in the absolute necessity of a single primordial being.

What ***use*** are we to make of our understanding, even where experience is concerned, if we set ourselves no ends? But the loftiest ends are those of morality, and these it is pure reason alone that can bring us to cognize. Equipped with these ends now and led by their thread, we can put the knowledge of nature itself to no purposive use as regards cognition wherever {{ 845 }} nature has not herself laid down a purposive unity; {{{ 530 }}} for lacking this we should not so much as possess a reason of our own, since we would be without any school for it and without any cultivation by way of objects that might furnish the material for concepts of the kind. That purposive unity, however, is necessary and takes its ground in the very essence of free choice, and so the latter—holding, as it does, the condition for applying that unity _in concreto_—must be necessary too; and thus the transcendental heightening of our rational cognition would prove to be, not the cause, but merely the effect of the practical purposiveness that pure reason lays upon us.

We therefore likewise discover, in the history of human reason: that until the moral concepts had been purified and made determinate enough, and until the systematic unity of ends conforming to them had been grasped—and grasped, indeed, from necessary principles—the knowledge of nature, and even a respectable degree of cultivation of reason across a number of other sciences, was able in part to bring forth none but crude and roving concepts of the deity, and in part left standing an astonishing indifference toward this question altogether. A fuller working-out of moral ideas, rendered necessary by the utterly pure moral law of our religion, whetted reason upon the object through the interest it obliged reason to take therein; and without any contribution either from an enlarged acquaintance with nature or from sound and trustworthy transcendental insights (of which sort there has {{ 846 }} at every period been a dearth), these ideas brought to pass a concept of the divine being that we now regard as the right one—not because speculative reason persuades us of its rightness, but because it chimes perfectly with the moral principles of reason. And so, when all is said, it is ever only pure reason, though solely in its practical employment, that has the credit of binding to our highest concern a cognition which bare speculation may merely surmise yet cannot make good, and of thereby raising it—not, to be sure, into a demonstrated dogma, but nonetheless into a wholly indispensable supposition attaching to reason's most essential ends.

But now that practical reason has attained this lofty vantage—the concept, namely, of a single primordial being as the highest good—it may in no way take the liberty, as though it had lifted itself above all the empirical conditions of that concept's application and soared up to an immediate acquaintance with fresh objects, of setting out from this concept {{{ 531 }}} and deducing the moral laws themselves from it. For it was these very laws whose ***inner*** practical necessity drove us to the supposition of a self-standing cause, or of a wise governor of the world, so as to lend those laws effect; and for that reason we cannot, following upon this, turn round and regard them as contingent and drawn from a mere will—least of all {{ 847 }} from a will of which we should have no concept whatever, had we not shaped it in conformity with those very laws. So far as practical reason is entitled to lead us, we shall deem actions binding not on the ground that they are God's commands, but shall instead look on them as divine commands on the ground that we stand inwardly bound to them. We shall study freedom beneath the purposive unity by the principles of reason, and shall believe ourselves in accord with the divine will only to the extent that we hold sacred the moral law which reason teaches us out of the very nature of actions, and shall believe that we serve that will by this alone—that we further the world's good in ourselves and in others. Moral theology is thus of an immanent use only—namely, to discharge our vocation here in the world by fitting ourselves into the system of all ends—and not, in a rapturous or perhaps even sacrilegious spirit, to forsake the thread of a morally legislative reason within a good course of life in order to tie it directly onto the idea of that highest being—an idea whose adoption would furnish a transcendent use that, just like the use of bare speculation, cannot but pervert and thwart the ultimate ends of reason.
