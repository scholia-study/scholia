## On the regulative use of the ideas of pure reason

The outcome of every dialectical attempt made by pure reason does more than corroborate what the transcendental analytic had already established—namely that all inferences of ours which aspire to carry us past the field of possible experience are fallacious and without foundation; it further instructs us in something quite particular: that in this very matter human reason harbors a natural leaning to transgress that limit, that transcendental ideas come to it every bit as naturally as the categories come to the understanding, albeit with this distinction, that whereas the latter conduct us to truth—the concord, that is, of our concepts with the object—{{{ 427 }}} the former call forth a merely apparent yet irresistible semblance, whose deceptiveness scarcely the sharpest critique can fend off.

Whatever has its ground in the very nature of our powers is bound to be purposive and to accord with the right employment of those powers, so long only as we can ward off a {{ 671 }} certain misconstrual and light upon their genuine direction. The transcendental ideas, then, will in all probability possess their good and therefore ***immanent*** use, notwithstanding that, should their import be misjudged and should they be taken as concepts of real things, they may prove transcendent in their application and, for just that reason, deceptive. For what can be, in respect of the whole of possible experience, either ***overflying*** (_transzendent_) or ***native*** (_immanent_) is not the idea taken by itself but purely its use, depending on whether one aims it point-blank at an object presumed to answer to it, or merely at the employment of the understanding at large with regard to the objects it has to handle; and every error of subreption is on each occasion chargeable to a shortcoming of the power of judgment, and never to the understanding or to reason.

Reason is never related directly to any object; its bearing falls solely on the understanding, and by way of the understanding upon reason's own empirical use; hence it ***creates*** no concepts (of objects) but only ***orders*** them, conferring on them that unity which they are able to possess at their utmost reach, that is, in reference to the totality of the series—something the understanding does not regard at all, its regard falling only on the connection ***through which everywhere series*** of the conditions ***come to stand*** in keeping with concepts. Properly, then, reason has as its object nothing but {{ 672 }} the understanding together with its purposive deployment; and as the understanding gathers the manifold within the object into one by means of concepts, so reason on its side gathers the manifold of the concepts into one by means of ideas, positing a certain collective unity as the terminus of the understanding's operations, which apart from this are busied only with distributive unity.

Accordingly my thesis is this: the transcendental ideas are never of any constitutive use such that concepts of particular objects would be furnished through them, and wherever they are so construed they turn out to be mere {{{ 428 }}} ratiocinating (dialectical) concepts. They do, on the other hand, carry a use that is excellent and indispensably requisite, a regulative one—namely, to steer the understanding toward a certain goal, in prospect of which the guiding lines of all its rules run together into a single point that, though it is but an idea (_focus imaginarius_)—i.e., a point that the understanding's concepts do not in truth set out from, since it lies wholly past the confines of possible experience—yet one that nevertheless serves to secure for them the greatest unity alongside the greatest reach. Now out of this there does spring up in us the deception that these guiding lines had been cast off from some object lying, in its own right, outside the field of empirically possible cognition (in the way objects are viewed behind a mirror's plane); this illusion, however (which one is after all able to keep from {{ 673 }} misleading us), is for all that indispensably requisite if, alongside the objects set before our eyes, we mean at the same time to descry those lying far off behind us at our backs—that is, if in our situation we would drill the understanding out past every given experience (that portion of the whole of possible experience) and thus toward its greatest possible and most far-reaching enlargement.

Should we take in our cognitions of the understanding across their entire range, we discover that what reason, in a manner all its own, presides over and strains to accomplish is the ***systematic*** dimension of cognition, i.e., the hanging-together of that cognition out of one single principle. Such unity on reason's part invariably presupposes an idea—that of the form belonging to a whole of cognition, a form preceding the determinate cognition of the parts and holding within it the conditions for settling _a priori_, for each part, its position and its relation to the rest. This idea thus lays down as a postulate a complete unity of the understanding's cognition, whereby the latter becomes not a mere haphazard aggregate but a system cohering under necessary laws. Strictly one cannot say that this idea is a concept of the object; rather it is a concept of the thoroughgoing unity of those concepts, in so far as that unity does the understanding service as a rule. Concepts of reason of this sort are not scooped up out of nature; on the contrary we put our questions to nature by the lead of these ideas, and count our cognition defective {{ 674 }} for just so long as it fails to match them. It is granted that ***pure earth, pure water, pure air*** and so forth are hardly to be met with. The concepts of them are needed all the same (concepts which, so far as full purity goes, thus take their {{{ 429 }}} rise only in reason), the aim being to settle rightly the share each of these natural causes contributes to the appearance; and in this way one traces all matter back to earths (the mere dead weight, as it were), salts and combustible bodies (as the force), and lastly to water and air as vehicles (machines, so to speak, through which the former exert themselves), so as to render the chemical workings of matter upon one another on the model of a mechanism. For although nobody actually phrases it so, an influence of this kind exerted by reason over the classifications of the natural scientists is exceedingly easy to spot.

If we take reason to be a power for drawing the particular out of the universal, then either the universal already stands, in and of itself, certain and given, in which case nothing beyond the ***power of judgment*** is called for to carry out the subsumption, and the particular finds itself thereby determined of necessity. This is what I shall call reason's apodictic use. Or else the universal is conceded only ***problematically*** and is a bare idea; the particular stands certain, yet the universality of the rule for this consequence remains a problem: in which event a number of particular cases, all of them certain, get tried against the rule to see if they issue from it; and here, once it looks {{ 675 }} as though every particular case one might adduce does follow from it, the inference runs to the universality of the rule, and from that in turn to all cases, including those not given in themselves. Reason employed after this fashion is what I will term its hypothetical use.

Now reason's hypothetical use, starting out from ideas laid down at its base as problematic concepts, is not properly ***constitutive***—it is not, that is, so framed that from it, were one to judge with all strictness, the truth of the general rule assumed as hypothesis would ensue; for how could anyone come to know every possible consequence that, by ensuing from the one assumed principle, would go to prove its universality? It is, rather, merely regulative, its office being thereby to introduce, so far as may be, unity into the particular cognitions and by that means to bring the rule nearer to universality.

Reason's hypothetical use thus bears upon the systematic unity of the understanding's cognitions, and this unity is the ***assay-stone of the truth*** of the rules. Turned about, the systematic unity (as a bare idea) is no more than a ***projected*** unity, one to be looked upon not as given in itself but only as a problem; a unity, though, that answers the purpose of finding, for the manifold and particular use of the understanding, a principle {{{ 430 }}}, and of thereby steering that use even across cases not given and rendering it coherent.

{{ 676 }} From all this, however, one gathers only that the systematic unity—the rational unity, that is—of the understanding's manifold cognition is a ***logical*** principle, meant, wherever the understanding on its own does not reach as far as rules, to lend it a hand by way of ideas, and at once to furnish the diversity of its rules with a concord under one principle (a systematic concord) and thereby with coherence, so far as that admits of being done. But whether the makeup of the objects, or the nature of that understanding which cognizes them as such, is of itself determined toward systematic unity, and whether one might postulate this _a priori_, to a certain degree even without any regard to such an interest of reason, and so pronounce: all possible cognitions of the understanding (the empirical ones included) possess rational unity and stand beneath common principles from which, their diversity notwithstanding, they may be derived—that would constitute a ***transcendental*** principle of reason, one that would render systematic unity necessary not merely in a subjective and logical way, as a method, but objectively.

Let us throw light on this by taking a case of reason's employment. Among the various species of unity that fall under concepts of the understanding, there is also that of the causality of a substance, which is termed force. One and the same substance's differing appearances exhibit, on first inspection, so much unlikeness that at the outset one is nearly obliged to assume as many distinct forces in it as there are effects coming to the fore, {{ 677 }} much as in the human mind we meet sensation, consciousness, imagination, memory, wit, the power of discrimination, pleasure, desire, and so on. To begin with, a logical maxim bids us pare down this seeming diversity as far as we can, by tracking through comparison the concealed identity and looking to see whether imagination, joined with consciousness, might not be memory, wit, the power of discrimination, or perhaps even understanding and reason themselves. The idea of a ***fundamental force***—though as to whether any such thing exists logic reaches no determination whatever—is at the least the problem of a systematic representation of the multiplicity of forces. The logical principle of reason requires that this unity be carried through as far as can be, and the more the appearances of the one force and of the other are found identical among themselves, the likelier it grows that they are nothing but differing outward expressions {{{ 431 }}} of one and the very same ***fundamental force***, which (comparatively) may be styled their fundamental force. One goes about it in the same manner with the rest.

The comparative fundamental forces must, in their turn, be set against one another, so that by uncovering their concord we may draw them near to a single radical—that is, absolute—fundamental force. This rational unity is, however, purely hypothetical. One does not maintain that such a force must actually be come upon; one maintains only that it is to be sought for reason's sake, namely toward erecting certain principles for the several rules {{ 678 }} that experience may hold out to us, and that in this manner, wherever it can be done, systematic unity is to be brought into cognition.

It emerges, though, once one attends to the understanding's transcendental use, that this idea of a fundamental force as such is destined not merely as a problem for the hypothetical use, but puts forward an objective reality, whereby the systematic unity of a substance's several forces is postulated and an apodictic principle of reason erected. For even before we have so much as attempted the concord of the several forces—indeed even where, after every attempt, we fail to uncover it—we still take it for granted that such a one is there to be found; and this not solely, as in the case adduced, on account of the substance's unity, but wherever a good many are come upon, homogeneous though they be to a certain degree, as with matter in general, reason presupposes a systematic unity of manifold forces, since particular natural laws stand under more general ones, and the sparing of principles turns into not merely an economical principle of reason but an inner law of nature.

In fact there is likewise no seeing how a logical principle of the rules' rational unity could obtain, were a transcendental one not presupposed, through which a systematic unity of the kind, as clinging to the {{ 679 }} objects themselves, is taken _a priori_ to be necessary. For with what title could reason, in its logical use, insist that the manifold of forces which nature gives us to know be handled as a merely concealed unity and be traced back, so far as lies within them, to some fundamental force, if it were left open to reason to allow that it might equally well be the case that all forces are unlike in kind and that the systematic unity of their derivation does not square with nature? For then reason would be going clean against its own vocation {{{ 432 }}}, in that it would set before itself, as its goal, an idea flatly at variance with the constitution of nature. Neither may one say that reason had beforehand gleaned this unity, according to principles of reason, from the contingent makeup of nature. For the law of reason enjoining the search for it is a necessary one, since without that law we should have no reason at all, without reason no coherent use of the understanding, and, that being wanting, no adequate criterion of empirical truth—so that, with an eye to this last, we are simply obliged to take nature's systematic unity as objectively valid and necessary throughout.

This transcendental assumption we find, into the bargain, tucked away in a marvelous fashion within the principles of the philosophers, even if they have not always recognized it there, nor confessed it to themselves. That all the manifolds of individual things do not shut out identity of {{ 680 }} ***species***; that the sundry species have to be handled merely as varying determinations of a few ***genera***, and these again of higher ***families*** yet, and so forth; that consequently a certain systematic unity of all possible empirical concepts, in so far as they admit of derivation from higher and more general ones, has to be sought out: this is a rule of the schools, a logical principle, absent which no use of reason would obtain, for we can infer from the general to the particular only so far as general properties of things are set at the base, properties under which the particular ones stand.

That such a concord is met with in nature as well is something the philosophers take for granted in the familiar rule of the schools: that one is not to multiply first beginnings (principles) beyond necessity (_entia praeter necessitatem non esse multiplicanda_). What this says is that the nature of things itself holds out material for rational unity, and that the seemingly boundless diversity ought not to hold us back from surmising, behind it, a unity of basic properties out of which the manifold can be derived only by further determination. This unity, mere idea though it be, has in every age been chased after so keenly that occasion has been found sooner to curb the appetite for it than to spur it on. It was already no small thing that the chemists could bring all salts back to two chief genera, the acidic and the alkaline; they go so far as to try regarding even this difference {{ 681 }} as a mere variety, a differing outward expression of one and the same basic stuff. The various kinds of earths (the stuff of {{{ 433 }}} stones and even of metals) have been progressively brought down to three, at length to two; yet, not even satisfied with that, they cannot shake off the thought of surmising, behind these varieties, a single genus after all, indeed perhaps a principle shared alike by these and the salts. One might perhaps suppose this to be a merely economical knack of reason, bent on sparing itself trouble as much as it can, and a hypothetical venture which, in the event of success, lends to the presupposed ground of explanation a probability precisely by way of this unity. Yet an aim so self-interested is very readily told apart from the idea by which everyone takes for granted that this rational unity befits nature itself, and that here reason does not go begging but commands, albeit without being in a position to fix the limits of that unity.

Were there present among the appearances offered to us so vast a diversity—I do not mean in form (in that respect they may well resemble one another), but in content, i.e., as regards the multiplicity of existing beings—that even the very keenest human understanding, setting one against another, could not ferret out the slightest resemblance (a case one can perfectly well conceive), then the logical law of genera would find no place at all; indeed there would be no concept of {{ 682 }} genus, no general concept whatsoever, no understanding even, seeing that the understanding has to do with nothing but such concepts. So the logical principle of genera carries a transcendental one behind it, once it is to be brought to bear on nature (whereby here I mean nothing but the objects that are given to us). By that principle a homogeneity is of necessity presupposed to pervade the manifold of a possible experience (even though its degree is beyond our _a priori_ determining), for lacking it no empirical concepts, and hence no experience, could come about.

Over against the logical principle of genera, which lays down identity, there stands another, that of ***species***, which calls for multiplicity and difference among things despite their agreement under one and the same genus, and which makes it a precept for the understanding to attend no less to the one than to the other. This principle (of acuteness, or of the faculty of distinguishing) sharply reins in the levity of the first (of wit), and here reason evinces a twofold interest at war with itself, {{{ 434 }}} on the one side an interest in ***scope*** (in generality) touching the genera, on the other an interest in ***content*** (in determinacy) bearing on the multiplicity of species, since in the first case the understanding thinks a great deal ***beneath*** its concepts, whereas in the second it thinks so much the more ***within them*** {{ 683 }}. This too shows itself in the widely divergent cast of thought among the natural scientists, some of whom (the preeminently speculative sort), hostile as it were to unlikeness, keep looking out toward the unity of the genus, while the others (preeminently empirical heads) unremittingly labor to cleave nature into such a wealth of diversity that one would well-nigh have to relinquish the hope of appraising its appearances by general principles.

Underlying this latter cast of thought there plainly lies a logical principle too, one whose aim is the systematic completeness of all our cognitions, whenever I, setting out from the genus, climb down to the manifold that may be held beneath it, and in that way seek to procure breadth for the system, just as, in the former case, mounting up toward the genus, I seek to procure simplicity. For out of the sphere of a concept designating a ***genus*** it is no more to be made out how far the dividing of it may reach than it is out of the space that matter can take up. Hence every genus demands differing ***species***, and these in turn differing ***subspecies***; and since not one of the latter turns up but has, once more, a sphere of its own (a compass in the sense of a _conceptus communis_), reason in its full extension requires that no species be regarded as the lowest taken by itself, because—being always a concept that harbors within it only what several things have in common—such a concept cannot be through-and-through {{ 684 }} determined, and so cannot be referred immediately to an individual, and must therefore forever hold further concepts, i.e., subspecies, beneath it. This ***law of specification*** might be worded thus: _entium varietates non temere esse minuendas_.

Yet one readily sees that this logical law too would be void of sense and application did there not lie at its base a transcendental law of specification—which, to be sure, exacts of the things that can become our objects no real ***infinity*** in respect of their differences (for that the logical principle, asserting as it does merely the ***indeterminacy*** of the logical sphere in respect of possible division, gives no ground), but which {{{ 435 }}} does nevertheless charge the understanding with hunting, beneath every species that comes before us, for subspecies, and, for every difference, for smaller differences. For were there no lower concepts, neither would there be any higher ones. Now the understanding cognizes everything by way of concepts alone: and so, so far as its dividing reaches, never through bare intuition, but ever again through lower concepts. The cognition of appearances in their through-and-through determination (a cognition possible through the understanding alone) exacts an unceasingly prolonged specification of its concepts and an advance toward differences that always still remain, from which abstraction was made in the concept of the species, and in that of the genus still more.

{{ 685 }} Nor can this law of specification be taken on loan from experience; for experience can yield no disclosures reaching anything like so far. Empirical specification comes to a standstill soon enough in its sorting of the manifold, unless it has been guided by the already antecedent transcendental law of specification, as a principle of reason, to search such differences out and to go on surmising them even where they do not lay themselves open to the senses. That absorbent earths are of yet further kinds (calcareous and muriatic earths) called, for its discovery, for a forestalling rule of reason, which set the understanding the task of searching out the difference by presupposing nature rich enough for that difference to be surmised. For we possess an understanding only on the presupposition of differences in nature every bit as much as on the condition that its objects carry homogeneity within them, since it is precisely the manifoldness of what can be gathered under one concept that constitutes the use of that concept and the occupation that keeps the understanding busy.

Reason, then, makes the understanding's field ready for it: 1., by a principle of the ***homogeneity*** of the manifold under higher genera; 2., by a principle of the ***variety*** of the homogeneous under lower species; and, to bring the systematic unity to completion, it appends 3. a further law of the ***affinity*** of all concepts, one prescribing an unbroken {{ 686 }} passage from each and every species to every other by a stepwise swelling of difference. We may name them the principles of the ***homogeneity, specification*** and ***continuity*** of forms. This last takes its rise from uniting the first two, once one has brought the systematic coherence {{{ 436 }}} to completion within the idea, alike in the ascent to higher genera and in the descent to lower species; for then all the manifolds are of one kin, seeing that one and all they stem, across every degree of extended determination, from a single, supreme genus.

The systematic unity holding under the three logical principles can be made sensible to oneself in the following way. Any concept whatever may be looked upon as a point that, as the vantage of an onlooker, has a horizon of its own, i.e., a throng of things capable of being represented from it and, so to speak, surveyed at a glance. Within this horizon a throng of points must be specifiable without end, each of these having, once more, its own narrower field of view; that is, each species holds subspecies under it by the principle of specification, and the logical horizon is built up out of narrower horizons (subspecies) only, and not out of points bereft of any compass (individuals). But for differing horizons, i.e., genera, each fixed by just so many concepts, one can think a common horizon drawn, from which all of them together are surveyed {{ 687 }} as from a central point—the horizon that is the higher genus—until at last the highest genus is the general and true horizon, fixed from the vantage of the loftiest concept and gathering under it all the manifoldness as genera, species and subspecies.

To this loftiest vantage the law of homogeneity conducts me; to all the lower ones and their utmost variety, the law of specification. But since in this manner nothing empty lies within the entire compass of all possible concepts, and outside that compass nothing can be met with, there springs, from the assuming of that general field of view and the through-and-through division of it, the principle: _non datur vacuum formarum_, i.e., there do not exist diverse original and first genera standing, as it were, in isolation and cut off from one another (by some empty interval), but all the manifold genera are merely partitions within a single, supreme and general genus; and springing straight from this principle, its immediate consequence: _datur continuum formarum_, i.e., all the differences of species border upon one another and grant no passage between one and another by any leap, but only through all the smaller degrees of difference by which one can get from the one to the other; in short, no species or subspecies whatever counts, within reason's concept, as another's nearest neighbor; on the contrary, intermediate species {{{ 437 }}} {{ 688 }} remain forever possible, whose difference from the first and the second is smaller than the difference these bear to one another.

The first law, accordingly, wards off any extravagant plunge into the manifoldness of diverse original genera and commends homogeneity; the second, by contrast, once more curbs this leaning toward concord and bids us mark off the subspecies before ever we turn, with our general concept, to individuals. The third brings the pair into union, in that, amid the utmost manifoldness, it prescribes homogeneity all the same, by way of the stepwise passage from one species to the next—which betokens a kind of kinship among the differing branches, in so far as one and all they have sprung from a single stock.

This logical law of the _continui specierum_ (_formarum logicarum_), however, presupposes a transcendental one (_lex continui in natura_), lacking which the use of the understanding would only be led astray by that precept, for it might well take a course diametrically opposed to nature. This law must therefore rest upon grounds purely transcendental and not empirical. Were the latter the case, it would arrive later than the systems; whereas it is in truth what first brought forth whatever is systematic in the cognition of nature. Nor, behind these laws, lie hidden any designs upon a trial to be undertaken with them as bare experiments, though {{ 689 }} to be sure this coherence, where it holds good, supplies a mighty ground for holding the hypothetically devised unity to be well-founded, so that in this respect too they have their utility; rather, one plainly perceives in them that they judge the thrift of underlying causes, the multiplicity of effects, and a kinship of nature's members springing therefrom, to be in themselves rational and befitting nature, and that these principles accordingly bear their commendation directly, and not merely as knacks of method.

But one readily sees that this continuity of forms is nothing but an idea, one for which no congruent object can be pointed to in experience: ***not only*** on the score that the species in nature stand actually divided and must therefore of themselves make up a _quantum discretum_—so that, were the stepwise advance in their kinship continuous, it would have to embrace as well a true infinity of intervening members lodged within any two given species, {{{ 438 }}} which cannot be—***but also*** because we can put this law to no determinate empirical use whatever, seeing that by it not the least mark of affinity is signified telling us according to which mark, and how far, we are to seek out the sequence of degrees of their difference, but nothing beyond a general intimation that seek it we must.

{{ 690 }} Were we to reorder the principles just cited according to their sequence, so as to set them in keeping with the use of experience, the principles of systematic unity would stand roughly so: ***manifoldness***, ***kinship*** and ***unity***, each of them, however, taken as an idea at the highest pitch of its completeness. Reason presupposes the cognitions of the understanding, which are applied in the first place to experience, and looks for their unity by ideas—a unity ranging far beyond what experience can attain. The kinship of the manifold, its difference notwithstanding, under one principle of unity concerns not merely the things but, far more than that, the bare properties and forces of things. Thus, suppose that some experience (not yet fully set right) hands us the planets' course as circular, and we come upon deviations: we then surmise them to reside in whatever is able to alter the circle, by a constant law, through all the endless intervening degrees, into one of these divergent orbits; that is, the planets' motions that are not circles will come more or less near to the circle's properties and fall into the ellipse. The comets display a diversity of their paths greater still, since (as far as observation carries) they do not so much as come back on a circle; yet we conjecture a parabolic course, one that is nonetheless of the ellipse's kin and that, when the long axis of the latter is drawn out very far, cannot be told apart from it {{ 691 }} in any observation of ours. So it is that, following the lead of those principles, we come to a unity of the genera of these paths in their shape, and thereby further to a unity in the cause of all the laws of their motion (gravitation); from there we thereafter widen our conquests, undertaking to explain out of the same principle every variety and seeming departure from those rules as well, and in the end even to add more than experience can ever bear out—namely, following the rules of kinship, to think for ourselves cometary paths that are downright hyperbolic, along which these bodies quit our solar world altogether and, passing from sun to sun {{{ 439 }}}, knit together in their course the remoter tracts of a world-system that, for us, is boundless and that hangs together through one and the same moving force.

What is noteworthy in these principles, and what alone engages us, is this: that they appear to be transcendental, and that, although they hold nothing but ideas for the guidance of reason's empirical use—ideas which that use can trail only asymptotically, so to speak, i.e., by mere approximation, without ever coming up to them—they yet, as synthetic _a priori_ propositions, carry an objective though undetermined validity, serving as a rule of possible experience, and are indeed put to work with good fortune, as heuristic principles, in the elaboration of that experience, without {{ 692 }} one's being able for all that to bring off a transcendental deduction of them, which, as was made out above, is at all times impossible where ideas are concerned.

In the transcendental analytic we set apart, among the understanding's principles, the ***dynamical*** ones—as principles of ***intuition*** that are merely regulative—from the ***mathematical*** ones, which with respect to intuition are constitutive. This notwithstanding, the said dynamical laws are indeed constitutive with respect to ***experience***, in that they render possible _a priori_ the very ***concepts*** apart from which no experience takes place. Principles of pure reason, by contrast, cannot be constitutive even with respect to empirical ***concepts***, for no matching schema of sensibility can be supplied to them, and so they can own no object _in concreto_. Now if I dispense with such an empirical employment of them in the role of constitutive principles, how am I nonetheless to make good for them a regulative employment, and along with it some objective validity, and what significance could such a use bear?

The understanding makes up an object for reason, exactly as sensibility does so for the understanding. To render the unity of all possible empirical acts of the understanding systematic is a task of reason's, in the same way that the understanding ties the manifold of appearances together by concepts and brings it beneath empirical laws. The acts of the understanding, though, without schemata of sensibility, are ***indeterminate***; {{ 693 }} and just so the ***unity of reason*** is, in respect of the conditions under which, and the degree up to which, the understanding is to bind its concepts systematically, of itself ***indeterminate***. Yet although {{{ 440 }}} for a thoroughgoing systematic unity of all the understanding's concepts no schema can be tracked down in ***intuition***, an ***analogue*** of such a schema can and must for all that be supplied—the idea, namely, of the ***maximum*** of the partitioning and the unifying of the understanding's cognition within one principle. For the greatest and absolutely complete admits of being thought determinately, since all restricting conditions, which would give an indeterminate manifoldness, are set aside. The idea of reason is therefore an analogue to a schema of sensibility, yet with this difference: that applying the understanding's concepts to reason's schema does not, as elsewhere, amount to a cognition of the object itself (as when the categories are applied to their sensible schemata), but amounts only to a rule or principle for the systematic unity of all employment of the understanding. And since every principle that settles _a priori_ for the understanding a thoroughgoing unity of its employment holds good—though only indirectly—of the object of experience as well, the principles of pure reason will likewise possess objective reality in respect of that object; not, however, so as to ***determine*** anything in it, but only so as to indicate the procedure by which {{ 694 }} the understanding's empirical, determinate use in experience can be rendered thoroughly consonant with itself, through being brought, ***as far as may be***, into connection with the principle of thoroughgoing unity, and derived therefrom.

All subjective principles that are drawn, not from the object's constitution, but from reason's interest with a view to a certain attainable perfection in the cognition of that object, I call ***maxims*** of reason. Thus there exist ***maxims*** of speculative reason resting purely on that reason's speculative interest, however much it may look as though they were objective principles.

When principles that are merely regulative come to be regarded as constitutive, they may, as objective principles, fall into conflict; but if one regards them merely as ***maxims***, then there is no genuine conflict, only a differing interest of reason that occasions the parting of ways in thought. Reason has, in fact, but one single interest, and the quarrel among its maxims is nothing more than a difference—and a reciprocal delimiting—of the methods for giving that interest its satisfaction.

In this way, with ***this*** reasoner the interest {{{ 441 }}} in ***manifoldness*** carries more weight (following the principle of specification), while with ***that*** one it is the interest in ***unity*** (following the principle of aggregation). {{ 695 }} Each of the two believes he holds his judgment from an insight into the object, and yet grounds it purely on how firmly or loosely he cleaves to one or the other of the two principles, no one of which reposes on objective grounds, resting instead solely on reason's interest, and which might on that account be better termed maxims than principles. When I behold discerning men at odds with one another over the characterization of human beings, of animals or plants, indeed even of the bodies of the mineral realm—where the one party, say, assumes particular national characters grounded in descent, or else settled and heritable distinctions among families, races, and the like, while the others set their conviction on nature's having, in this respect, laid down dispositions wholly uniform, all distinction resting merely on outward contingencies—then I need only weigh the makeup of the object to grasp that it lies far too deeply concealed for either of them to be speaking from insight into the object's nature. It is nothing other than reason's twofold interest, of which the one side takes to heart, or affects, the one, and the other side the other—hence the difference of the maxims of natural manifoldness or of natural unity, maxims that may perfectly well be reconciled, but which, so long as they pass for objective insights, give rise not to strife alone but to obstacles besides, that hold truth up for a long while, until a means {{ 696 }} is hit upon of reconciling the interest at odds and setting reason at ease over the matter.

It stands the same way with the affirming or the impugning of the much-invoked law of the continuous ***scale*** of created things, a law set going by Leibniz and finely trimmed out by Bonnet, which is nothing beyond a compliance with the principle of affinity resting on reason's interest; for observation and insight into nature's arrangement could by no means hand it over as an objective assertion. The rungs of such a scale, as experience is able to point them out to us, stand much too far apart, and our supposedly little differences are commonly, in nature herself, gulfs so wide that upon observations of the kind (above all with a great manifoldness of things, where finding certain resemblances and approximations must always come easy) nothing at all is to be counted as designs of nature {{{ 442 }}}. The method, on the other hand, of hunting out order in nature by such a principle, and the maxim of taking such an order—indeterminately, though, as to where or how far—to be well-grounded in a nature as such, is assuredly a legitimate and admirable regulative principle of reason, one that as such reaches much further than experience or observation could ever match, yet without fixing anything, serving only to mark out for experience the road toward systematic unity.
