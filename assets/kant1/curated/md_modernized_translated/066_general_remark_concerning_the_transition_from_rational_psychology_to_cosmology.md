## {{{ 279 }}} General remark concerning the transition from rational psychology to cosmology.

The proposition: I think, or: I exist thinking, is an empirical proposition. Yet at the basis of such a proposition lies empirical intuition, and consequently the thought object as appearance; and so it seems as if, according to our theory, the soul is completely, even in thinking, transformed into appearance, and in such a way our consciousness itself, as mere illusion, must in fact refer to nothing.

Thinking, taken by itself, is merely the logical function, and thus pure spontaneity of the combination of the manifold of a merely possible intuition, and in no way represents the subject of consciousness {{ 429 }} as appearance, merely because it takes no account at all of the kind of intuition, whether it be sensible or intellectual. Thereby I represent myself to myself neither as I am, nor as I appear to myself, but rather I think myself only as any object in general, from whose kind of intuition I abstract. If I represent myself here as the ***subject*** of thoughts or also as the ***ground*** of thinking, then these modes of representation do not signify the categories of substance or of cause, for these are those functions of thinking (judging) already applied to our sensible intuition, which would indeed be required if I wanted to ***cognize*** myself. But now I want to become conscious of myself only as thinking; how my own self is given in intuition, that I set aside, and then it could be mere appearance to me, who thinks, but not insofar as I think; in the consciousness of myself in mere thinking I am the ***being itself***, of which, however, indeed nothing is thereby yet given to me for thinking.

But the proposition: I think, insofar as it says as much as: ***I exist thinking***, is not a mere logical function, but rather determines the subject (which is then at the same time an object) in respect of existence, and cannot take place without inner sense, whose intuition always yields the object not as a thing in itself, but merely as appearance. In it {{ 430 }} there is therefore already no longer mere spontaneity of thinking, but also receptivity of intuition, i.e., the thinking of myself applied to the empirical intuition of the very same subject {{{ 280 }}}. In this latter, the thinking self would then now have to seek the conditions of the use of its logical functions for categories of substance, cause, etc., in order not merely to designate itself as an object in itself through the 'I', but also to determine the kind of its existence, i.e., to cognize itself as a noumenon; which, however, is impossible, since inner empirical intuition is sensible and yields nothing but data of appearance, which furnish nothing to the object of ***pure consciousness*** for the knowledge of its separate existence, but can serve only for the purpose of experience.

But suppose there should be found in the sequel, not in experience, but in certain laws of the pure use of reason established _a priori_ and concerning our existence (not being merely logical rules), an occasion for presupposing ourselves entirely _a priori_ in respect of our own ***existence*** as ***legislative***, and as also determining this existence itself: then a spontaneity would thereby be discovered through which our reality would be determinable, without needing for this the conditions of empirical intuition; and here we would become aware that in the consciousness of our existence there is contained something _a priori_ which can serve to determine our only {{ 431 }} sensibly thoroughly determinable existence, in respect of a certain inner faculty, in relation to an intelligible (to be sure, only thought) world.

But this would nonetheless not advance all attempts in rational psychology in the least. For through that admirable faculty, which first of all reveals to me the consciousness of the moral law, I would indeed have a principle of the determination of my existence which is purely intellectual, but by means of which predicates? By no other than those which must be given to me in sensible intuition; and so I would end up again right where I was in rational psychology, namely, in the need for sensible intuitions in order to procure significance for my concepts of understanding, substance, cause, etc., through which alone I can have cognition of myself; but those intuitions can never help me beyond the field of experience. Meanwhile, in respect of practical use, which is after all always directed to objects of experience, I would nonetheless be authorized to apply these concepts to freedom and its subject, in accordance with their analogical significance in theoretical use, in that I understand by them merely the logical functions of subject {{{ 281 }}} and predicate, of ground and consequence, in accordance with which {{ 432 }} the actions or effects are so determined in conformity with those laws that they, simultaneously with the laws of nature, can always be explained in accordance with the categories of substance and of cause, even though they arise from an entirely different principle. This was only meant to be said for the prevention of the misunderstanding to which the doctrine of our self-intuition as appearance is easily exposed. In the sequel, one will have opportunity to make use of this.
