## {{{ 279 }}} General remark concerning the transition from rational psychology to cosmology.

That I think, or: that I exist in thinking, is an empirical proposition. But at the basis of any such proposition lies empirical intuition, and consequently also the object thought of as appearance, so that it seems as though, on our theory, the soul were wholly and entirely, even in its thinking, transformed into appearance, and in this way our consciousness itself, as a mere semblance, would in fact have to come to nothing.

Thinking, considered on its own, is nothing but the logical function, and thus pure spontaneity in the connecting of the manifold belonging to a merely possible intuition; and it presents the subject of consciousness in no way {{ 429 }} as appearance, for this one reason: that it pays no regard at all to how the intuition is given, whether sensibly or intellectually. By this I represent myself, to myself, neither as I actually am nor as I appear to my own view, but I think of myself simply as any object as such, abstracting from the kind of intuition through which it is given. If I here represent myself as the ***subject*** of thoughts, or likewise as the ***ground*** of thinking, these ways of representing do not stand for the categories of substance or of cause, since the latter are precisely those functions of thought (of judgment) as already applied to our sensible intuition—an application that would, to be sure, be called for if I meant to ***cognize*** myself. But for now I wish to be aware of myself merely as something that thinks; the way in which my own self is given in intuition I leave aside, and in that respect it might well be, for me who does the thinking though not so far as I think, nothing more than appearance; when I am aware of myself in the act of mere thought I am the ***being itself***, yet thereby nothing about it is yet given to me for thought.

The proposition, though: I think, taken to mean as much as: ***I exist thinking***, is no mere logical function, but instead determines the subject (which at once is then also object) in regard to existence, and it can have no place apart from inner sense, whose intuition invariably yields the object not as a thing in itself but only as appearance. In it {{ 430 }} there is accordingly no longer merely the spontaneity of thought, but a receptivity of intuition as well, that is, my thinking of myself as brought to bear on the empirical intuition of that very same subject {{{ 280 }}}. In this latter intuition, then, the thinking self would be obliged to look for the conditions under which it employs its logical functions as categories of substance, of cause, and so on, so as to mark itself out as an object in its own right not merely by means of the I, but also to determine the mode of its existence, that is, to cognize itself as noumenon—which, however, cannot be done, since the inner empirical intuition is sensible and hands over nothing beyond data of appearance, data that contribute nothing to the object of ***pure consciousness*** for a knowledge of its separate existence, and can be of use solely for the aims of experience.

But grant that there should turn up further on—not within experience, but within certain laws of the pure use of reason (laws that are more than merely logical rules) which stand fixed _a priori_ and bear on our existence—an occasion to presuppose ourselves, wholly _a priori_ and as regards our own ***existence***, to be ***legislative*** and to be ourselves the determiners of that existence: then a spontaneity would thereby come to light through which our actuality could be determined without any call for the conditions of empirical intuition; and at this point we would come to realize that within the consciousness of our existence there lies enclosed _a priori_ a certain something, one able to help determine our existence—everywhere determinable, otherwise, only {{ 431 }} through the senses—as regards a particular inner power, and in relation to an intelligible (albeit merely thought) world.

But this would nonetheless not in the least advance any of the attempts made in rational psychology. For through that admirable faculty which the consciousness of the moral law first reveals to me I would indeed have a principle for the determination of my existence, one that is purely intellectual, but by means of which predicates? By none other than those that have to be supplied to me within sensible intuition; and so I would end up back once more at the spot where I stood in rational psychology, namely at the requirement for sensible intuitions in order to lend meaning to my concepts of the understanding—substance, cause, and the like—by which alone I am able to have cognition of myself; those intuitions, however, can never carry me out past the field of experience. Yet in regard to practical use—which after all is always aimed at objects of experience—I would still be permitted to apply these concepts to freedom and to the subject thereof, following the meaning analogous to their theoretical use, inasmuch as by them I take to be meant nothing but the logical functions of subject {{{ 281 }}} and of predicate, of ground and of consequence, conformably to which {{ 432 }} the actions or effects come to be determined, in keeping with those laws, in such a way that they admit of being explained—jointly with the laws of nature—always in conformity with the categories of substance and of cause, even should they arise from an altogether different principle. This was meant to be said only in order to forestall the misunderstanding to which the doctrine of our self-intuition as appearance is easily exposed. In what follows there will be occasion to make use of it.
