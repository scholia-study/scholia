## I. On transcendental illusion

We designated dialectic in general, earlier on, as a ***logic of illusion***. This does not mean that it is a doctrine of ***probability***; for probability is truth all the same, only truth known through grounds that fall short, so that although such cognition is defective it is not therefore misleading, and hence there is no call to sever it from logic's analytic part. {{ 350 }} Even less should one conflate ***appearance*** with ***illusion***. For it is not the object, as it is intuited, but the judgment passed on it, as it is thought, that harbors truth or illusion. So it is quite right to say that the senses never err — though the reason is not that their judgments are always correct, but that they pass no judgment whatsoever. For this reason truth and error alike — and with them illusion, which lures us into error — are found nowhere but in judgment, i.e., solely in how the object stands related to our understanding. Where a cognition accords throughout with the laws of the understanding, error has no place. And a sensory representation, containing as it does no judgment at all, is equally free of error. Yet no natural force can, on its own, depart from the laws that govern it. It follows that error would arise neither in the understanding taken in isolation (with no other cause acting on it) nor in the senses taken on their own; not in the understanding, because whenever it proceeds strictly by its own laws the resulting effect (the judgment) is bound to conform to those laws. And conformity to the laws of the understanding is precisely what constitutes the formal side of every truth. Within the senses no judgment occurs, be it true or false. Since, then, we possess no source of cognition beyond these two, the conclusion is this: {{{ 235 }}} error comes to pass only through the unremarked bearing of sensibility upon the understanding, in consequence of which the subjective {{ 351 }} grounds of a judgment merge with the objective ones and pull the latter away from what they ought to determine [^*]; much as a body in motion would, left to itself, forever hold to a straight line in one and the same direction, yet, should a second force press upon it at once along another direction, veers off into curved motion. So as to tell apart the understanding's own characteristic operation from the force that mixes itself in, we shall have to treat the mistaken judgment as a diagonal lying between two forces that pull the judgment along two divergent directions and thereby, so to speak, span an angle between them, and then to break this compound effect down into the separate contributions of understanding and sensibility; and with pure judgments _a priori_ this has to be accomplished by way of transcendental reflection, through which (as was noted already) every representation gets allotted its proper place in the cognitive faculty suited to it, so that the bearing of the one faculty upon the other is likewise kept distinct.

It is no part of our task here to deal with empirical illusion (the {{ 352 }} optical sort, say), which crops up when otherwise sound rules of the understanding are put to empirical use and which leads the power of judgment astray under the sway of imagination; our concern, rather, is with ***transcendental illusion*** alone, an illusion that works upon principles never even framed for use in experience — had they been so framed, we should at least possess some test of their soundness — but that instead, in defiance of every caution critique may issue, sweeps us clean past the empirical employment of the categories and dangles before us the delusive prospect of enlarging the ***pure understanding***. Those principles that confine their application wholly within the limits of possible experience we shall term ***immanent***, while those meant to soar past these limits we shall term ***transcendent*** principles. By the latter, though, I have in mind not the ***transcendental*** {{{ 236 }}} employment or misemployment of the categories — that being a simple failing of a power of judgment insufficiently reined in by critique, one that keeps too little watch over the boundary of the ground where alone the pure understanding may carry on its game — but genuine principles that press us to level every one of those boundary markers and to seize for ourselves a wholly fresh ground acknowledging no line of demarcation anywhere. So ***transcendental*** and ***transcendent*** are by no means interchangeable. The principles of the pure understanding presented earlier are meant to have {{ 353 }} an empirical employment only, never a transcendental one — that is, an employment stretching past the limit of experience. A principle, by contrast, that clears these barriers away — indeed one that bids us cross them outright — counts as ***transcendent***. Should our critique manage to expose the illusion belonging to these presumptuous principles, then those principles of merely empirical use, set over against the latter, will be entitled to the name ***immanent*** principles of the pure understanding.

Logical illusion — the illusion at work in fallacious inferences — amounts to nothing more than an imitation of reason's form, and it springs merely from inattention to the logical rule. The moment that rule is brought to bear keenly on the case before us, the illusion dissolves without remainder. Transcendental illusion, on the other hand, persists undiminished even once it has been laid bare and its emptiness plainly grasped through transcendental critique (consider, for instance, the illusion lodged in the claim that the world had to begin at some point in time). The reason for this lies in the following: within our reason — taken subjectively, as one of the human powers of cognition — there reside fundamental rules and maxims for its employment that wear the full appearance of objective principles, and by which it happens that a subjective necessity — the necessity of joining our concepts in a certain way for the benefit of the understanding — comes to be mistaken for an objective necessity governing the determination of things in themselves. An ***illusion***, {{ 354 }} then, that admits of no avoidance whatever — no more than we can keep the sea from looking higher to us at its center than along the shore, since we behold the one by rays of light set higher than those disclosing the other, or, more strikingly still, no more than the astronomer himself can stop the rising moon from looking larger to him, notwithstanding that this illusion does not take him in.

Accordingly, the transcendental dialectic will rest content with laying bare the illusion of transcendent judgments and, at once, with guarding against {{{ 237 }}} its deceiving us; that this illusion should moreover (as the logical sort does) altogether vanish and stop being an illusion at all — this it can never bring about. For what confronts us here is a ***natural*** and inescapable ***illusion***, one that is grounded in subjective principles of its own yet foists them upon us as though they were objective; whereas logical dialectic, in dissolving fallacious inferences, contends merely with a slip in applying principles, or with a contrived semblance got up in imitation of them. Pure reason, then, harbors a dialectic that is natural and inescapable — not one into which some bungler tangles himself for want of knowledge, nor one that a sophist has cunningly devised to bewilder reasonable folk, but one bound up with human reason past all thwarting, an illusion that, even after we have uncovered its deceptive play, will never leave off deceiving reason afresh {{ 355 }} and thrusting it again and again into fleeting aberrations that stand in need of removal every time.

[^*]: Sensibility, when it is placed beneath the understanding as the object upon which the understanding exercises its function, yields real cognitions. That same sensibility, though, once it works its way into the very activity of the understanding and sways it toward a judgment, becomes the ground of error.
