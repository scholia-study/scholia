## {{{ 7 }}} {{ VII }} Preface to the Second Edition

Whether the treatment of the cognitions that belong to the business of reason travels the secure path of a science or not, can easily be judged from the result. If, after many preparations and arrangements have been made, it comes to a halt as soon as it approaches its goal, or, in order to reach it, must often go back again and strike out on another path; likewise, if it is not possible to bring the various co-workers into unanimity regarding the way in which the common aim is to be pursued: then one can always be convinced that such a study has by no means yet entered upon the secure path of a science, but is a mere groping about, and it is already a merit for reason to find this path if possible, even if much that was contained in the aim previously taken up without reflection must be given up as futile.

{{ VIII }} That logic has traveled this secure path already from the earliest times can be seen from the fact that since ***Aristotle*** it has not had to take a single step backwards, unless one wants to count the removal of some dispensable subtleties or the clearer determination of its presentation as improvements, which, however, belong more to the elegance than to the security of the science. What is further remarkable about it is that until now it has also not been able to take a single step forward, and therefore to all appearance seems to be closed and complete. For if some of the moderns thought to enlarge it by interpolating partly ***psychological*** {{{ 8 }}} chapters on the different cognitive powers (imagination, wit), partly ***metaphysical*** ones on the origin of cognition or the different kind of certainty according to the difference of objects (idealism, skepticism, etc.), partly ***anthropological*** ones on prejudices (their causes and remedies), this stems from their ignorance of the peculiar nature of this science. It is not an augmentation, but a deformation of the sciences, when one allows their boundaries to run into one another; the boundary of logic, however, is thereby determined quite exactly, in that it is a {{ IX }} science which exhaustively presents and strictly proves nothing but the formal rules of all thinking (whether it be _a priori_ or empirical, whatever origin or object it may have, and whatever accidental or natural obstacles it may meet with in our minds).

That logic has succeeded so well, it owes merely to its limitation, by which it is justified, indeed obliged, to abstract from all objects of cognition and their differences, and in which therefore the understanding has to deal with nothing more than itself and its form. It must naturally have been far more difficult for reason to enter upon the secure path of science when it has to deal not merely with itself, but also with objects; therefore that logic also constitutes, as a propaedeutic, only the vestibule of the sciences, as it were, and when one speaks of knowledge, one indeed presupposes a logic for judging them, but their acquisition must be sought in what are properly and objectively called sciences.

Insofar as there is to be reason in these, something in them must be cognized _a priori_, and its cognition can be related to its object in two ways, either merely to ***determine*** this object and its concept (which must be given from elsewhere {{ X }}), or to also ***make it actual***. The first is ***theoretical***, the other ***practical*** cognition of reason. Of both, the pure part, however much or little it may contain, namely that in which reason determines its object wholly _a priori_, must alone be expounded first, and that which comes from other sources must not be mixed with it; for it is bad economy to spend blindly whatever comes in, without being able later, when it comes to a standstill, to distinguish {{{ 9 }}} which part of the income can bear the expenditure and from which it must be cut.

***Mathematics*** and ***physics*** are the two theoretical cognitions of reason which are supposed to determine their ***objects*** _a priori_, the former entirely purely, the second at least in part purely, but then also according to the measure of other sources of cognition than that of reason.

***Mathematics*** has, from the earliest times to which the history of human reason reaches, traveled the secure path of a science among the admirable people of the Greeks. But one must not think that it was as easy for it as for logic, where reason has to deal only with itself, to hit upon, or rather to pave for itself, that royal road to {{ XI }}; rather I believe that it long remained (especially still among the Egyptians) in a state of groping about, and that this change is to be ascribed to a ***revolution***, brought about by the happy inspiration of a single man in an attempt, from which point onward the path that had to be taken could no longer be missed, and the secure path of a science was struck and prescribed for all time and to infinite distances. The history of this revolution in the mode of thinking, which was much more important than the discovery of the passage around the famous Cape, and of the fortunate man who brought it about, has not been preserved for us. Yet the legend handed down to us by ***Diogenes Laertius***, who names the reputed inventor of the smallest elements of geometrical demonstrations, which according to common judgment do not even stand in need of proof, shows that the memory of the change wrought by the first trace of the discovery of this new path must have seemed exceedingly important to mathematicians, and thereby became unforgettable. A new light broke upon the first person who demonstrated the ***isosceles triangle*** (whether he was called ***Thales*** or whatever else {{ XII }}); for he found that he must not trace what he saw in the figure, or even trace its mere concept, and read off its properties from it, as it were, but that he must produce it through what he himself thought into it and presented _a priori_ according to concepts (through construction), and that in order to know something securely _a priori_ he had to ascribe to the thing nothing except what followed necessarily from what he himself had put into it in accordance with its concept.

{{{ 10 }}} Natural science was much slower in finding the highway of science; for it is only about a century and a half since the proposal of the ingenious Bacon of Verulam partly occasioned this discovery, and partly further enlivened it, since one was already on its track, which likewise can be explained only by a rapidly occurring revolution in the mode of thinking. I will here consider only natural science insofar as it is founded on ***empirical*** principles.

When ***Galileo*** rolled his spheres down the inclined plane with a weight chosen by himself, or ***Torricelli*** made the air carry a weight which he had calculated beforehand to be equal to that of a known column of water, or in still more recent times ***Stahl*** transformed metals into calx {{ XIII }} and this again into metal, by withdrawing something from them and restoring it to them:[^*] a light broke upon all investigators of nature. They comprehended that reason has insight only into that which it produces after a plan of its own, that it must not allow itself to be kept, as it were, in nature's leading-strings, but must itself show the way with principles of judgment based upon fixed laws, constraining nature to give answer to questions of reason's own determining; for accidental observations, made in obedience to no previously thought-out plan, can never be made to yield a necessary law, which alone reason seeks and requires. Reason, holding in one hand its principles, according to which alone concordant appearances can be admitted as laws, and in the other hand the experiment which it has devised in conformity with these principles, must approach nature, in order to be taught by it, it is true, but not in the character of a pupil who listens to everything that the teacher chooses to say, but of an appointed judge who compels the witnesses to answer questions which he has himself formulated. And thus even physics owes the so advantageous revolution {{ XIV }} of its mode of thinking entirely to the happy thought, that while reason must seek in nature (not fictitiously ascribe to it) whatever it has to learn from nature, and of which it would know nothing by itself, it must do so in accordance with what reason itself puts into nature. Through this, natural science was first brought into the secure path of a science, whereas for so many centuries it had been nothing more than a mere groping about.

{{{ 11 }}} ***Metaphysics***, a completely isolated speculative cognition of reason, which elevates itself entirely above instruction from experience, and does so through mere concepts (not, like mathematics, through their application to intuition), where reason therefore is supposed to be its own pupil, has hitherto not been so favored by fate as to have been able to enter upon the secure path of a science, even though it is older than all the others, and would remain even if all the others were to be entirely swallowed up in the abyss of an all-destroying barbarism. For in it reason continuously comes to a halt, even when it wants to gain _a priori_ insight (as it pretends) into those laws which the commonest experience confirms. In it one must retrace one's path countless times, because one finds that it does not lead to where one wants to go, and as regards the unanimity of its adherents in their assertions {{ XV }}, it is still so far from it that it is rather a battlefield that seems quite uniquely designed to practice one's powers in mock combats, on which no fighter has ever yet been able to win even the smallest territory and to found a durable possession upon his victory. There is therefore no doubt that its procedure hitherto has been a mere groping about, and what is worst, among mere concepts.

What, then, is the reason that no secure path of science has yet been found here? Is it perhaps impossible? From whence then has nature afflicted our reason with the restless striving to trace it as one of its most important concerns? Still more, how little cause have we to place trust in our reason, if in one of the most important parts of our desire for knowledge it not merely forsakes us, but holds us off with false promises and in the end betrays us! Or if it has merely been missed hitherto, what indication can we use to hope that with renewed searching we will be more fortunate than others before us have been?

I should think that the examples of mathematics and natural science, {{ XVI }} which have become what they now are through a revolution brought about all at once, were remarkable enough to reflect on the essential element in the change of the mode of thinking that has been so advantageous to them, and to imitate them in this, at least as an experiment, so far as their analogy with metaphysics, as cognitions of reason, permits. Hitherto it has been assumed that all our cognition must {{{ 12 }}} conform to the objects; but all attempts to find out something about them _a priori_ through concepts that would enlarge our cognition have, on this presupposition, come to nothing. Let us therefore try once whether we do not get further with the problems of metaphysics by assuming that the objects must conform to our cognition, which already agrees better with the demanded possibility of an _a priori_ cognition of them, which is to establish something about objects before they are given to us. The situation here is the same as with the first thoughts of ***Copernicus***, who, failing to make good progress with the explanation of the celestial motions if he assumed that the entire host of stars revolved around the spectator, tried to see if he might not have greater success if he made the spectator revolve and left the stars at rest. In metaphysics {{ XVII }} one can now try a similar path regarding the ***intuition*** of objects. If intuition had to conform to the constitution of the objects, I do not see how we could know anything of them _a priori_; but if the object (as object of the senses) conforms to the constitution of our faculty of intuition, then I can quite well represent this possibility to myself. But because I cannot stop with these intuitions if they are to become cognitions, but must relate them as representations to something as object and determine this object through them, I can assume either that the ***concepts*** through which I bring about this determination also conform to the object, and then I am again in the same perplexity regarding the way in which I could know anything about it _a priori_; or else I assume that the objects, or what is the same thing, the ***experience*** in which alone they can be cognized (as given objects), conform to these concepts, then I immediately see an easier way out, because experience itself is a kind of cognition that requires understanding, the rule of which I must presuppose in myself even before objects are given to me, hence _a priori_, which rule is expressed in concepts _a priori_, to which therefore all objects of experience must necessarily {{ XVIII }} conform and with which they must agree. As for objects so far as they are thought merely through reason, and necessarily at that, but which (at least as reason thinks them) can never be given in experience, the attempts to think them (for they must be capable of being thought) will later provide a splendid touchstone {{{ 13 }}} of what we assume as the altered method of our mode of thinking, namely that we cognize _a priori_ of things only what we ourselves put into them.[^**]

This experiment succeeds as desired, and promises to metaphysics in its first part, where it occupies itself with concepts _a priori_ to which the corresponding objects can be given in experience appropriately {{ XIX }}, the secure path of a science. For after this change in the mode of thinking one can quite well explain the possibility of a cognition _a priori_, and, what is still more, provide the laws which lie _a priori_ at the foundation of nature, as the sum total of the objects of experience, with their satisfactory proofs, both of which were impossible according to the previous procedure. But from this deduction of our faculty of cognizing _a priori_ in the first part of metaphysics there emerges a strange result, and one that to all appearance is highly detrimental to the whole purpose of metaphysics, which occupies the second part, namely that we can never get beyond the boundary of possible experience with it, which is precisely the most essential concern of this science. But precisely here lies the experiment of a counter-check on the truth of the result of that {{ XX }} first estimation of our rational cognition _a priori_, namely that it reaches only to appearances, while leaving the thing in itself as something actual for itself, but uncognized by us. For that which necessarily drives us to go beyond the boundary of experience and of all appearances {{{ 14 }}} is the ***unconditioned***, which reason necessarily and with full right demands in things in themselves for everything that is conditioned, thereby demanding the series of conditions as complete. If now one finds that, if one assumes that our empirical cognition conforms to the objects as things in themselves, the unconditioned ***cannot be thought without contradiction*** at all; but that, on the contrary, if one assumes that our representation of things as they are given to us does not conform to these as things in themselves, but rather that these objects as appearances conform to our mode of representation, ***the contradiction falls away***; and that consequently the unconditioned must not be found in things insofar as we know them (as they are given to us), but rather in them insofar as we do not know them, as things in themselves: then it shows that what we initially assumed only as an experiment {{ XXI }} is well founded.[^***] Now there still remains for us, after speculative reason has been denied all advance in this field of the supersensible, to try whether there are not data in its practical cognition to determine that transcendent concept of reason of the unconditioned, and in such a way to reach beyond the boundary of all possible experience, in accordance with the wish of metaphysics, with our _a priori_ cognition, which is possible only for a practical purpose. And with such a procedure, speculative reason has nevertheless always at least made room for such an extension, even if it had to leave it empty, and we are therefore still at liberty, {{ XXII }} indeed we are even called upon by it, to fill it with practical data of reason, if we can.[^****]

{{{ 15 }}} The business of this Critique of pure speculative reason consists in that attempt to alter the previous procedure of metaphysics, and in undertaking a complete revolution with it, following the example of the geometers and natural scientists. It is a treatise on the method, not a system of the science itself; but it nevertheless maps out its entire outline, both in respect of {{ XXIII }} its boundaries, as well as its entire internal articulation. For it is the peculiarity of pure speculative reason that it can and should measure its own faculty according to the difference in the way it chooses objects for thinking, and even completely enumerate the various ways of setting itself problems, and thus trace out the whole outline for a system of metaphysics; because, concerning the first point, nothing can be ascribed to objects in _a priori_ cognition except what the thinking subject takes from itself, and, concerning the second, it is in respect of the principles of cognition a completely separate unity subsisting for itself, in which each member exists for the sake of all the others, and all for the sake of one, as in an organized body, and no principle can safely be taken in ***one*** relation without having simultaneously investigated it in its ***thoroughgoing*** relation to the entire use of pure reason. For this, however, metaphysics also has the rare fortune, which can fall to the lot of no other rational science that has to deal with objects (for ***logic*** is occupied only with the form of thinking in general), that if it is brought into the secure path of a science by this critique, it can fully embrace the entire field of the cognitions belonging to it {{ XXIV }} and thus complete its work and lay it down for posterity as a capital that can never be augmented, because it deals merely with principles and the limitations of their use, which are determined by those very principles. It is therefore also bound to this completeness as a fundamental science, and {{{ 16 }}} of it one must be able to say: _nil actum reputans, si quid superesset agendum_.

But what sort of a treasure is this, one will ask, that we intend to leave to posterity with such a metaphysics, purified by critique, but thereby also brought into a permanent state? Upon a cursory overview of this work, one might believe that its utility is only ***negative***, namely never to venture beyond the boundary of experience with speculative reason, and this is indeed its first use. This, however, immediately becomes ***positive***, when one becomes aware that the principles with which speculative reason ventures beyond its boundary in fact do not have the ***extension*** of our use of reason as their inevitable result, but rather, if one considers them more closely, its narrowing, since they actually threaten to extend the boundaries of sensibility, to which they properly belong, {{ XXV }} over everything, and thus to crowd out the pure (practical) use of reason entirely. Hence a critique that limits the former, although it is ***negative***, nevertheless, by thereby simultaneously removing an obstacle that limits the latter use or even threatens to annihilate it, is in fact of ***positive*** and very important utility, as soon as one is convinced that there is an absolutely necessary practical use of pure reason (the moral use), in which it inevitably extends itself beyond the boundaries of sensibility, for which it admittedly needs no assistance from speculative reason, but must nevertheless be secured against its counteraction, in order not to fall into contradiction with itself. To deny the ***positive*** utility of this service of critique would be equivalent to saying that the police yield no positive utility, because their main business is only to put a stop to the violence that citizens have to apprehend from citizens, so that everyone can carry on his affairs quietly and safely. That space and time are only forms of sensible intuition, and therefore only conditions of the existence of things as appearances, that we further have no concepts of understanding, and hence also no elements at all for the {{ XXVI }} cognition of things, except insofar as an intuition corresponding to these concepts can be given, consequently that we can have no cognition of any object as a thing in itself, but only insofar as it is an object of sensible intuition, i.e. as an appearance, is proved in the analytic part of the Critique; from which indeed follows the limitation {{{ 17 }}} of all possible speculative cognition of reason to mere objects of ***experience***. Nevertheless it is always reserved, which must be carefully noted, that we must at least be able to ***cognize*** these same objects as things in themselves, even if we cannot ***think*** them.[^*****] For otherwise the absurd proposition would follow from it {{ XXVII }} that there would be appearance without anything that appears. Now let us assume that the distinction necessitated by our critique between things as objects of experience and the very same things as things in themselves had not been made at all; then the principle of causality and hence the mechanism of nature in their determination would have to hold of all things in general as efficient causes. Of the very same being, e.g. the human soul, I would therefore not be able to say that its will is free, and yet at the same time is subject to natural necessity, i.e., is not free, without falling into an obvious contradiction, because I have taken the soul in both propositions ***in the very same meaning***, namely as a thing in general (as a thing in itself), and without previous critique could not have taken it otherwise. But if critique has not erred in teaching us to take the object in ***a twofold meaning***, namely as appearance or as a thing in itself; if the deduction of its concepts of the understanding is correct, and thus the principle of causality applies only to things taken in the first sense, namely insofar as they are objects of experience, but these very same things taken in the second meaning {{ XXVIII }} are not subject to it: then the very same will is thought in the appearance (visible actions) as necessarily conforming to the law of nature and to that extent ***not free***, and yet on the other hand, as belonging to a thing in itself, it is thought as not subject to that law, and hence as ***free***, without any contradiction occurring herein. Now although I cannot ***cognize*** my soul from the latter side through any speculative reason (still less {{{ 18 }}} through empirical observation), and consequently cannot ***cognize*** freedom as a property of a being to which I ascribe effects in the world of sense, because I would have to cognize such a being according to its existence and yet not as determined in time (which is impossible, because I cannot supply any intuition to my concept): nevertheless I can ***think*** freedom, i.e., the representation of it at least contains no contradiction in itself, if our critical distinction of the two modes of representation (the sensible and the intellectual) and the limitation of the pure concepts of the understanding arising from it, and hence also of the principles flowing from them, takes place. Now suppose that morality necessarily presupposes freedom (in the strictest sense) as a property of our will, by citing practical, original principles lying in our reason as _a priori_ ***data*** of the same, which would be absolutely impossible without the presupposition of {{ XXIX }} freedom, but that speculative reason had proved that it cannot be thought at all: then that presupposition, namely the moral one, would necessarily have to yield to the one whose opposite contains an obvious contradiction, consequently ***freedom*** and with it morality (for its opposite contains no contradiction, unless freedom is already presupposed) would have to give way to the ***mechanism of nature***. But as things stand, since I need nothing more for morality than that freedom simply should not contradict itself and can thus at least be thought, without my having any need to gain further insight into it, and that it thus places no obstacle in the way of the mechanism of nature of the very same action (taken in another relation): the doctrine of morality asserts its place and the doctrine of nature also its own, which however would not have occurred if critique had not previously taught us of our inevitable ignorance with respect to things in themselves and limited everything that we can ***cognize*** theoretically to mere appearances. This very same exposition of the positive utility of the critical principles of pure reason can be shown in respect to the concept of ***God*** and the ***simple nature*** of our ***soul***, which however I pass over for the sake of brevity. I therefore cannot {{ XXX }} even ***assume God, freedom and immortality*** for the sake of the necessary practical use of my reason, if I do not at the same time ***deprive*** speculative reason of its pretension to extravagant insights, because in order to arrive at these, it must make use of {{{ 19 }}} such principles which, since they in fact reach only to objects of possible experience, if they are nevertheless applied to what cannot be an object of experience, actually transform this into an appearance at all times, and thus declare all ***practical extension*** of pure reason to be impossible. I therefore had to suspend ***knowledge***, in order to make room for ***belief***, and the dogmatism of metaphysics, i.e., the prejudice that one can advance in it without critique of pure reason, is the true source of all unbelief conflicting with morality, which is always highly dogmatic. – If then it cannot be difficult to leave a legacy to posterity with a systematic metaphysics composed in accordance with the critique of pure reason, this is no gift to be valued lightly; whether one looks merely at the cultivation of reason through the secure path of a science in general in comparison with its {{ XXXI }} groundless groping and frivolous wandering without critique, or also at the better use of time by inquisitive youth, who receive so early and so much encouragement in ordinary dogmatism to conveniently reason about things of which they understand nothing, and into which they, like no one else in the world, will never have any insight, or even to set out on the invention of new thoughts and opinions and thus neglect the learning of thorough sciences; but most of all, if one takes into account the invaluable advantage of making an end to all objections against morality and religion in a ***Socratic*** manner, namely through the clearest proof of the ignorance of the opponents, for all future time. For some kind of metaphysics has always been in the world and will probably remain in it, but with it also a dialectic of pure reason, because it is natural to it, will be found therein. It is therefore the first and most important concern of philosophy to deprive it once and for all of all detrimental influence by stopping up the source of the errors.

With this important change in the field of the sciences and the ***loss*** that speculative reason must suffer in its hitherto imagined {{ XXXII }} possessions, everything nonetheless remains in the same advantageous state for the universal human concern and the utility that the world has hitherto drawn from the teachings of pure reason as it ever was, and the loss affects only the ***monopoly of the schools***, but in no way the ***interest of humanity***. I ask the most inflexible dogmatist {{{ 20 }}} whether the proof of the continuation of our soul after death from the simplicity of substance, whether that of the freedom of the will against the universal mechanism through the subtle, though powerless, distinctions of subjective and objective practical necessity, or whether that of the existence of God from the concept of a most real being (the contingency of the alterable and the necessity of a first mover), after they originated in the schools, have ever been able to reach the public and have the least influence on its conviction? Now if this has not happened, and also cannot ever be expected because of the unsuitability of the common human understanding for such subtle speculation; if instead, as regards the first point, the predisposition of his nature noticeable to every human being, never to be capable of being satisfied by the temporal (as insufficient for the predispositions of his whole vocation), has alone had to bring about the hope of a future {{ XXXIII }} life, in respect of the second the mere clear presentation of duties in opposition to all claims of inclinations has had to bring about the consciousness of freedom, and finally, as regards the third, the glorious order, beauty and providence that shine forth everywhere in nature have had to bring about the belief in a wise and great author of the world, alone and entirely producing the conviction spreading to the public insofar as it rests on rational grounds: then not only does this possession remain undisturbed, but it rather gains even more in respect through the fact that the schools are now instructed not to arrogate to themselves a higher and more extensive insight into a point that concerns the universal human concern than that which the great (for us most worthy of respect) multitude can also attain just as easily, and thus to limit themselves to the cultivation of these universally comprehensible and morally sufficient grounds of proof alone. The change thus concerns merely the arrogant claims of the schools, which would fain have themselves regarded here (as otherwise rightly in many other points) as the sole connoisseurs and preservers of such truths, of which they communicate only the use to the public, but keep the key to them for themselves (_quod mecum nescit, solus vult scire videri_). Nevertheless {{ XXXIV }} provision is also made for a more equitable claim of the speculative philosopher. He always remains the exclusive depositary of a science that is useful to the public without its knowledge, namely the critique of reason; for this can never become popular, but it also does not need {{{ 21 }}} to be, because, just as the finely spun arguments for useful truths do not enter the minds of the people, just as little do the equally subtle objections against them ever enter their heads; on the other hand, because the school, like every person rising to speculation, inevitably falls into both, it is bound by a thorough investigation of the rights of speculative reason to prevent once and for all the scandal that must sooner or later break out even to the people from the disputes in which metaphysicians (and as such finally also the clergy) inevitably entangle themselves without critique, and which even falsify their teachings afterwards. Through this alone can the very root be cut off from ***materialism***, ***fatalism***, ***atheism***, free-thinking ***unbelief***, ***fanaticism*** and ***superstition***, which can become universally harmful, and finally also from ***idealism*** and ***skepticism***, which are more dangerous to the schools and can hardly pass over to the public{{ XXXV }}. If governments indeed think it good to concern themselves with the affairs of scholars, it would be far more appropriate to their wise care for sciences as well as humanity to favor the freedom of such a critique, by which alone the labors of reason can be put on a firm footing, than to support the ridiculous despotism of the schools, which raise a loud cry over public danger when one tears up their cobwebs, of which the public has nevertheless never taken notice, and whose loss it can therefore also never feel.

Critique is not opposed to the ***dogmatic procedure*** of reason in its pure cognition, as science (for this must always be dogmatic, i.e., strictly proving from sure principles _a priori_), but to dogmatism, i.e., the presumption of advancing solely with a pure cognition from concepts (the philosophical) according to principles, such as reason has long had in use, without inquiring into the manner and the right by which it has arrived at them. Dogmatism is therefore the dogmatic procedure of pure reason ***without previous critique of its own faculty***. This opposition is therefore not to speak in favor of loquacious shallowness under the assumed {{ XXXVI }} name of popularity, or even of skepticism, which makes short work of all metaphysics; rather, critique is the necessary preliminary arrangement for the promotion of a thorough metaphysics as a science, which must necessarily be dogmatic {{{ 22 }}} and executed systematically according to the strictest demand, hence scholastically (not popularly), for this demand on it, since it pledges itself to execute its business entirely _a priori_ and thus to the complete satisfaction of speculative reason, is inexorable. In the execution of the plan that the critique prescribes, i.e., in the future system of metaphysics, we must therefore someday follow the strict method of the famous Wolff, the greatest among all dogmatic philosophers, who first gave the example (and by this example became the author of the spirit of thoroughness in Germany that is not yet extinguished), of how the secure path of a science is to be taken through lawful establishment of principles, clear determination of concepts, attempted strictness of proofs, prevention of bold leaps in inferences, who was also for that very reason eminently suited to put such a science as metaphysics into this condition, if it had occurred to him to prepare the field for himself beforehand through {{ XXXVII }} a critique of the organ, namely of pure reason itself: a defect that is to be attributed not so much to him as to the dogmatic mode of thinking of his age, and about which the philosophers of his time as well as of all previous times have nothing with which to reproach one another. Those who reject his manner of teaching and yet at the same time also the procedure of the critique of pure reason can have nothing else in mind than to throw off the fetters of ***science*** altogether, to transform work into play, certainty into opinion, and philosophy into philodoxy.

***Regarding this second edition***, I have, as is fitting, not wanted to let the opportunity for it pass without removing as much as possible the difficulties and the obscurity from which many misinterpretations may have sprung that have perhaps confronted acute men in the judging of this book, not without my fault. In the propositions themselves and their grounds of proof, as well as the form and the completeness of the plan, I have found nothing to alter; which is to be attributed partly to the long examination to which I had subjected them before I presented it to the public, partly to the constitution of the matter itself, namely the nature of a pure speculative reason, which contains a true articulation, wherein everything is an organ, namely {{ XXXVIII }} everything is for the sake of one and each single part is for the sake of all, and hence every weakness, however small, whether it be a fault (error) or a lack, must inevitably betray itself in use. In this unalterability {{{ 23 }}} this system will, as I hope, also maintain itself in the future. It is not self-conceit, but merely the evidence produced by the experiment of the equality of the result in proceeding from the smallest elements to the whole of pure reason and in returning from the whole (for this too is given for itself through the ultimate aim of reason in the practical sphere) to every part, since the attempt to alter even the smallest part immediately introduces contradictions not merely of the system, but of universal human reason, that justifies me in this trust. But in the ***presentation*** there is still much to do, and herein I have attempted improvements with this edition, which are intended partly to remove the misunderstanding of the Aesthetic, especially that in the concept of time, partly the obscurity of the deduction of the concepts of understanding, partly the supposed lack of sufficient evidence in the proofs of the principles of pure understanding, and finally the misinterpretation of the paralogisms advanced against rational psychology. Up to here (namely only to the end of the first chapter {{ XXXIX }} of the Transcendental Dialectic) and no further do my {{ XL }} alterations of the mode of presentation extend,[^******] because time was too short and I had encountered no misunderstanding on the part of competent and impartial reviewers with regard {{{ 24 }}} {{ XLI }} to the rest, who, even without my being allowed to name them with the praise they {{{ 25 }}} {{ XLII }} deserve, will already hit upon the consideration I have given to their reminders in their proper places by themselves. With this improvement, however, a small loss for the reader is connected, which could not be prevented without making the book far too voluminous, namely that various things, which although they do not essentially belong to the completeness of the whole, many a reader might yet be reluctant to miss, since they can otherwise be useful for another purpose, have had to be omitted or presented in an abbreviated form, in order to make room for my now, as I hope, more comprehensible presentation, which fundamentally alters absolutely nothing in regard to the propositions and even their grounds of proof, but yet departs here and there in the method of the exposition from the previous one in such a way that it could not be managed through interpolations. This small loss, which anyway can be replaced at anyone's pleasure by comparison with the first edition, is, as I hope, more than compensated for by the greater comprehensibility. I have observed in various public writings (partly on the occasion of the review of some books, partly in special treatises) with grateful pleasure that the spirit of thoroughness in Germany has not died out, {{ XLIII }} but has only been outshouted for a short time by the fashionable tone of a genius-like freedom in thinking, and that the thorny paths of critique, which lead to a scholastic, but as such alone durable and therefore highly necessary science of pure reason, have not hindered courageous and clear heads from mastering it. To these meritorious men, who so happily combine thoroughness of insight with the talent for a lucid presentation (of which I am not exactly conscious in myself), I leave the completion of my treatment, which is perhaps still defective here and there in respect to the latter; for in this case the danger is not that of being refuted, but rather of not being understood. For my part, I cannot enter into disputes from now on, although I will carefully heed all hints, whether from friends or opponents, in order to use them in the future execution of the system according to this propaedeutic. Since during these labors I have already advanced quite far in age (into my sixty-fourth year this month), I must, if I want to execute my {{{ 26 }}} plan of delivering the metaphysics of nature as well as of morals as confirmation of the correctness of the critique of speculative as well as practical reason, proceed economically with my time and await the {{ XLIV }} illumination of the obscurities that were initially barely avoidable in this work, as well as the defense of the whole, from the meritorious men who have made it their own. Every philosophical exposition can be pinched in individual places (for it cannot appear as armored as a mathematical one), while nevertheless the articulation of the system, considered as a unity, does not run the least danger thereby, for an overview of which, when it is new, only a few possess the agility of spirit, but still fewer, because every innovation is inconvenient to them, possess the inclination. Also, apparent contradictions can be picked out if one compares individual passages, torn out of their context, with each other, in any writing that proceeds primarily as free speech, which throw a detrimental light on it in the eyes of one who relies on the judgment of others, but which are very easy to resolve for one who has mastered the idea as a whole. Meanwhile, if a theory has stability in itself, then action and reaction, which initially threatened it with great danger, serve in time only to smooth out its unevennesses and, if men of impartiality, insight, and true popularity occupy themselves with it, also to provide it in a short time with the requisite elegance. Königsberg, in the month of April 1787.

[^*]: I do not here exactly follow the thread of the history of the experimental method, whose first beginnings are also not well known.

[^**]: This method, modeled on the natural investigator, thus consists in this: to seek the elements of pure reason ***in that which can be confirmed or refuted through an experiment***. Now no experiment can be made with the ***objects*** of the propositions of pure reason (as in natural science), especially when they venture beyond all boundary of possible experience: so it will be practicable only with ***concepts*** and ***principles*** that we assume _a priori_, by arranging them in such a way that the same objects can be considered ***on the one hand*** as objects of the senses and of the understanding for experience, but ***on the other hand*** yet as objects that one merely thinks, at most for the isolated reason striving beyond the boundary of experience, thus from two different sides. If it is now found that, when one considers things from that double standpoint, there is agreement with the principle of pure reason, but that from a single standpoint an inevitable conflict of reason with itself arises, then the experiment decides for the correctness of that distinction.

[^***]: This experiment of pure reason has much in common with that of the ***chemists***, which they sometimes call the experiment of ***reduction***, but generally the ***synthetic procedure***. The ***analysis of the metaphysician*** separated pure cognition _a priori_ into two very heterogeneous elements, namely that of things as appearances and then of things in themselves. The ***dialectic*** connects the two again in ***unanimity*** with the necessary rational idea of the ***unconditioned*** and finds that this unanimity never comes out otherwise than through that distinction, which is thus the true one.

[^****]: Thus the central laws of the movements of the heavenly bodies afforded established certainty to what ***Copernicus*** initially assumed only as a hypothesis, and simultaneously proved the invisible force connecting the universe (the Newtonian attraction), which would have remained forever undiscovered if the former had not dared, in a paradoxical but yet true way, to seek the observed movements not in the celestial objects, but in their spectator. In this preface I also propose the alteration in the mode of thinking put forward in the critique analogous to that hypothesis only as a hypothesis, even though in the treatise itself it is proved not hypothetically but apodictically from the constitution of our representations of space and time and the elementary concepts of the understanding, solely in order to make noticeable the first attempts at such an alteration, which are always hypothetical.

[^*****]: To ***cognize*** an object, it is required that I be able to prove its possibility (whether by the testimony of experience from its actuality, or _a priori_ through reason). But I can ***think*** whatever I want, provided only that I do not contradict myself, i.e., provided my concept is only a possible thought, even though I cannot answer for whether or not an object corresponds to it in the sum total of all possibilities. But to attribute objective validity (real possibility, for the former was merely logical) to such a concept requires something more. This something more, however, need not be sought in theoretical sources of cognition, it may also lie in practical ones.

[^******]: Proper addition, though only in the method of proof, I could call only that which I have made through a new refutation of psychological ***idealism*** and a strict (also, as I believe, the only possible) proof of the objective reality of outer intuition p. 275. However innocent idealism may be held to be in respect to the essential purposes of metaphysics (which in fact it is not), it always remains a scandal to philosophy and to universal human reason that the existence of things outside us (from which we derive the whole material of cognitions, even for our inner sense) must be accepted merely on ***belief***, and that, if it occurs to anyone to doubt it, no satisfactory proof can be opposed to him. Because there is some obscurity in the expressions of the proof from the third line to the sixth, I ask that this period be altered as follows:||| ***"This permanent, however, cannot be an intuition in me. For all grounds of determination of my existence that can be encountered in me are representations, and as such require themselves a permanent distinct from them, in relation to which their change, and consequently my existence in the time in which they change, can be determined."*** Against this proof one will presumably say: I am nevertheless immediately conscious only of what is in me, i.e., of my ***representation*** of outer things; consequently it always remains undecided whether there is anything corresponding to it outside me or not. But I am conscious of ***my existence in time*** (consequently also of its determinability in it) through inner ***experience***, and this is more than merely being conscious of my representation, yet it is identical with the ***empirical consciousness of my existence***, which is determinable only through relation to something that ***is*** connected with my existence ***outside me***. This consciousness of my existence in time is thus identically bound up with the consciousness of a relation to something outside me, and it is thus experience and not fiction, sense and not imagination, which inextricably connects the outer with my inner sense; for outer sense is already in itself the relation of intuition to something actual outside me, and its reality, as distinct from imagination, rests only on the fact that it is inextricably connected with inner experience itself, as the condition of its possibility, which happens here. If, with the ***intellectual consciousness*** of my existence in the representation ***I am***, which accompanies all my judgments and acts of understanding, I could simultaneously connect a determination of my existence through ***intellectual intuition***, then the consciousness of a relation to something outside me would not necessarily belong to this. Now, however, that intellectual consciousness indeed precedes, but the inner intuition, in which my existence can alone be determined, is sensible and bound to the condition of time, and this determination, however, and hence inner experience itself, depends on something permanent which is not in me, consequently on something outside me, in relation to which I must consider myself: so the reality of outer sense is necessarily connected with that of inner sense for the possibility of experience in general, i.e., I am just as certainly conscious that there are things outside me that relate to my sense, as I am conscious that I myself exist determined in time. To which given intuitions objects outside me actually correspond, and which therefore belong to outer ***sense***, to which they and not to the imagination are to be ascribed, must be decided in each particular case according to the rules by which experience in general (even inner experience) is distinguished from imagination, the proposition that there actually is outer experience always lying at the foundation. One can add this remark: the representation of something ***permanent*** in existence is not the same as the ***permanent representation***; for this can be very variable and changing, like all our representations and even the representations of matter, and yet it refers to something permanent, which must therefore be an external thing distinct from all my representations, whose existence is necessarily included in the ***determination*** of my own existence, and with it constitutes only a single experience, which would not even take place internally if it were not (in part) simultaneously external. The How? can be explained here just as little further as how we generally think the stationary in time, whose simultaneity with the changing produces the concept of alteration.
