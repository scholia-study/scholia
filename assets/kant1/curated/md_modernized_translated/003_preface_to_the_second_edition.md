## {{{ 7 }}} {{ VII }} Preface to the Second Edition

Whether or not the working-up of those cognitions that fall to reason's own enterprise is proceeding along the sure road of a science is something the results themselves soon reveal. Should it stall the moment it reaches its object, after all its many arrangements and preparations, or should it be forced again and again to double back and try a fresh route in order to arrive there; and should it likewise prove impossible to get the several collaborators to agree on how the shared intention ought to be carried through: then one may rest assured that a study of this kind has not yet come anywhere near the sure road of a science, but is merely fumbling in the dark, and reason already earns credit by tracking that road down wherever it can, even at the cost of surrendering as vain a good deal that had been comprised in the goal originally set up without due reflection.

{{ VIII }} As for logic, that it has been travelling this sure road ever since the most ancient days is evident from its having had no occasion, since the time of ***Aristotle***, to retreat by so much as a step—unless one chooses to reckon among its improvements the clearing-away of certain expendable subtleties, or a sharper delineation of what it teaches, refinements that pertain to the science's elegance rather than to its reliability. A further curious thing about it is that neither has it managed to advance a single step, so that, by every indication, it appears to stand closed off and finished. For when certain more recent writers imagined they could broaden it—by smuggling in now ***psychological*** {{{ 8 }}} chapters on the assorted powers of cognition (imagination, wit), now ***metaphysical*** ones on where cognition originates or on how certainty varies with the diversity of objects (idealism, skepticism, and the like), now ***anthropological*** ones on prejudice (its sources and its antidotes)—this only betrays how little they grasped what is distinctive in the nature of this science. To let the boundaries of the sciences bleed into one another is not to augment them but to deform them; and logic's own boundary is fixed with complete precision by this, that it is a {{ IX }} science charged with nothing beyond setting out in full, and proving with rigor, the merely formal rules of all thought (be that thought _a priori_ or empirical, whatever its source or its object, and whether the obstacles it meets in our mind are contingent or built in).

Logic's success on this score is owed entirely to its own narrowness, which authorizes it—indeed requires it—to leave out of account every object of cognition together with all their differences, so that within it the understanding is occupied with nothing beyond its own self and the form belonging to it. In the nature of the case things had to prove far more difficult for reason once it must grapple not with itself alone but with objects besides; and this is why logic, functioning as a propaedeutic, amounts as it were merely to the antechamber of the sciences, so that where genuine bodies of knowledge are concerned, though a logic is indeed presupposed for appraising them, one has to look for the acquiring of them in those disciplines that are, strictly and objectively, entitled to the name of science.

Now to the extent that reason is to be present in such disciplines, something within them has to be cognized _a priori_, and the cognition reason yields can bear on its object in either of two ways: it may serve merely to ***determine*** that object together with its concept (which must be supplied from some other quarter {{ X }}), or else to ***render it actual*** as well. The one is reason's ***theoretical***, the other its ***practical*** cognition. In both cases the pure portion—no matter how much or how little it may amount to—namely that portion in which reason settles its object wholly _a priori_, must be laid out first and by itself, with nothing drawn from other wells allowed to contaminate it; for it is poor housekeeping to pay out blindly whatever comes in, unable later, once the treasury falls short, to tell {{{ 9 }}} which part of the receipts is able to cover the outlay and which part one is obliged to trim it back from.

The two theoretical cognitions in which reason is to fix its ***objects*** _a priori_ are ***mathematics*** and ***physics***—the first with complete purity, the second purely at least in part, though there also as measured against sources of cognition lying outside reason itself.

Among that admirable people the Greeks, ***mathematics*** had already set out upon a science's sure road as far back as the recorded history of human reason will carry us. Yet one ought not suppose that hitting upon {{ XI }} that royal highway—or rather cutting it open for itself—came to mathematics as effortlessly as it came to logic, where reason deals with itself and nothing more; I incline instead to think that mathematics too long remained stuck in mere fumbling (above all still among the Egyptians), and that its turnabout is to be credited to a ***revolution*** which the lucky inspiration of one man carried through in a single trial, whereafter the route that had to be followed could nevermore be lost, and a science's sure march was launched and staked out for all ages and across unbounded reaches. No record has reached us either of the man fortunate enough to accomplish it or of the history of this upheaval in mankind's mode of thought—an upheaval of far greater moment than the finding of the sea-route around that celebrated headland. All the same, the story ***Diogenes Laertius*** has passed down to us, in which he supplies a name for the supposed discoverer of even the most trifling elements of geometrical proof—elements that, by ordinary reckoning, hardly call for proving at all—shows that the recollection of the shift set in motion by the first faint trace of this new road's discovery must have struck the mathematicians as momentous in the extreme, and so was rendered imperishable. Upon the first man to prove something of the ***isosceles triangle*** (be his name **Thales** or anything else one pleases {{ XII }}) a light broke; for it dawned on him that his task was not to pore over what the drawn figure presented to his eye, nor over the bare concept of the figure, reading off its properties as though from these, but rather to bring those properties forth by way of what he himself, guided by concepts, had thought into the figure and laid out in it _a priori_ (by construction), and that, to secure any _a priori_ knowledge, he was entitled to credit the thing with nothing save the consequences that followed of necessity from what, in keeping with his concept, he had himself deposited in it.

{{{ 10 }}} Natural science was much slower in striking the broad thoroughfare of science; for it is a mere century and a half or so since the recommendation of the shrewd Bacon of Verulam partly prompted this breakthrough and partly—one being already on its scent—gave it fresh impetus, a breakthrough that likewise admits of explanation only through a swiftly executed overturning of the mode of thought. My concern in the present remarks is with natural science solely in the measure that it rests on ***empirical*** principles.

The day ***Galileo*** sent his spheres rolling down the incline under a weight he had himself assigned them, or ***Torricelli*** got the atmosphere to hold up a weight he had reckoned in advance as matching a column of water of known height, or, later still, ***Stahl*** turned metals into their calx {{ XIII }} and the calx back into metal by taking something from them and handing it back:[^*] on that day a light broke upon every student of nature. It became clear to them that reason discerns only what it brings forth after a plan of its own devising, that it has to march ahead holding the principles of its judgments under fixed laws and force nature to reply to what it asks, instead of suffering itself to be towed along by nature as if kept on a leash; for otherwise observations gathered by chance, on no plan sketched beforehand, never hang together in that necessary law which reason is nonetheless after and stands in need of. Reason, then, must go to nature carrying in the one hand its principles, which alone can lend agreeing appearances the status of laws, and in the other the experiment it has contrived by their lead—going, to be sure, in order to learn from nature, yet not as a schoolboy content to have the master recite to him whatever the master likes, but as a duly appointed judge who obliges his witnesses to respond to the queries he sets before them. And thus even physics is beholden for the so beneficial overturning {{ XIV }} of its mode of thought to nothing but the notion of hunting in nature—conformably to whatever reason has itself laid into nature—for that which reason must be taught by nature and would, on its own, have no inkling of (rather than foisting such things upon her). It is by this means that natural science was at long last placed upon a science's sure march, having for so many hundreds of years done nothing but fumble blindly.

{{{ 11 }}} With ***metaphysics***—an entirely self-enclosed speculative cognition of reason that lifts itself clear above all schooling by experience, and does so through concepts alone (not, as mathematics does, by putting concepts to work upon intuition), so that here reason is to be its own pupil—fortune has thus far been too grudging for it ever to have gained a science's sure march, notwithstanding that it is more venerable than all the rest and would outlast them even were they, one and all, engulfed in the maw of a barbarism that devours everything. For within metaphysics reason keeps stalling without end, even where it presumes to grasp _a priori_ those very laws that the most everyday experience corroborates. Here one is compelled to turn back time without number, finding that the road fails to reach the destination one is bound for; and as to its adherents ever concurring in what they assert {{ XV }}, metaphysics falls so far short of this that it is rather an arena seemingly cut out for the express purpose of drilling one's powers in sham skirmishes, one on which no champion has yet succeeded in securing even a foot of ground or in resting upon his triumph any durable holding. Beyond dispute, then, its way of proceeding has so far been nothing but a fumbling in the dark—and, worse yet, a fumbling amid bare concepts.

Why is it, now, that no sure road of science has yet let itself be found in this quarter? Is such a road, perhaps, out of the question? From what source, then, has nature visited upon our reason this tireless urge to chase after that road as one of its weightiest affairs? More than that: how slender are our grounds for reposing trust in our reason, when, in one of the most consequential objects of our craving to know, it does not merely desert us but keeps us dangling with false shows and in the end plays us false! Or, if the road has thus far only been overlooked, what indication may we exploit so as to hope that this renewed pursuit will leave us luckier than those who went before us?

It is my view that the cases of mathematics and of natural science, {{ XVI }} which owe their present standing to a revolution brought off in one stroke, are striking enough to warrant our pondering the crucial ingredient in that recasting of the mode of thought which served them so well, and, at least by way of trial, our copying it here so far as their kinship with metaphysics, as cognitions of reason, will allow. Up to now the standing assumption has been that all our cognition has to {{{ 12 }}} regulate itself by the objects; and yet, on that footing, every effort to settle anything about them _a priori_ by means of concepts, and so to widen our cognition, ended in failure. So let the experiment be made, for once, of whether we might not get on better with metaphysics' tasks by supposing instead that the objects have to regulate themselves by our cognition—a supposition that already squares more readily with the sought-for possibility of an _a priori_ cognition of those objects, one meant to lay down something about them in advance of their being given to us. The case stands here exactly as with the opening idea of ***Copernicus***, who, once accounting for the heavenly motions refused to go well on the supposition that the whole stellar array wheels about the spectator, tried whether he might not fare better by setting the spectator in motion and letting the stars stay still. In metaphysics {{ XVII }}, likewise, one can attempt something of this sort where the ***intuition*** of objects is concerned. Were intuition obliged to regulate itself by how the objects are constituted, then I fail to grasp how anything about it could be known _a priori_; but if instead the object (taken as an object of sense) regulates itself by how our capacity for intuiting is constituted, then this possibility is one I can represent to myself quite readily. Since, however, I cannot come to rest at these intuitions if they are to turn into cognitions, but must instead refer them, as representations, to some object and pin that object down by their means, I may suppose either that the ***concepts*** by which I accomplish such pinning-down likewise regulate themselves by the object—whereupon I land back in the very perplexity of before, as to how I could know anything on this head _a priori_—or else that the objects, or (what comes to the same) the ***experience*** wherein alone they are cognized as objects that are given, regulate themselves by these concepts, and here I catch sight straightaway of a smoother resolution, since experience is itself a mode of cognizing that calls for understanding, whose rule I have to presuppose within me even prior to any object's being given, hence _a priori_, a rule voiced in _a priori_ concepts, to which accordingly every object of experience must of necessity {{ XVIII }} defer and with which it must fall into accord. As regards objects insofar as reason thinks them and thinks them of necessity, though they cannot (at any rate as reason conceives them) be given anywhere in experience, the endeavors to think them—for think them we assuredly must be able to do—will thereafter furnish a superb assay-stone {{{ 13 }}} for what we are taking on as the transformed method of the mode of thought, namely that of things we cognize _a priori_ only that which we have ourselves placed into them.[^**]

This attempt turns out as one would wish, and it holds forth to metaphysics, in its opening part—where it is busied, that is, with concepts _a priori_ whose answering objects can be supplied in experience to suit those {{ XIX }} concepts—the sure march of a science. For once the mode of thought has been so altered, the very possibility of a cognition _a priori_ is readily accounted for, and, more than this, the laws that underlie nature _a priori_—nature being the aggregate of the objects of experience—can be equipped with adequate proofs, both of which lay beyond reach under the procedure hitherto followed. But this deduction of our power of cognizing _a priori_ throws up, within metaphysics' opening part, a disconcerting upshot—one that looks highly damaging to the whole aim of the discipline, the aim taken up in the second part—namely that by this power we are never able to press past the frontier of experience that is possible, though this is precisely the discipline's most vital business. Yet just here resides the experiment furnishing a countercheck on the truth yielded by that {{ XX }} initial appraisal of our _a priori_ rational cognition, namely that such cognition bears upon appearances only, letting the thing in itself, by contrast, lie there real enough on its own account but unknown to us. For what drives us of necessity to overstep the frontier of experience and of every appearance {{{ 14 }}} is the ***unconditioned***, which reason, in things in themselves, insists upon—rightfully and inevitably—for whatever is conditioned, and by that insistence demands the sequence of conditions brought to completion. Suppose it now emerges that, once we posit our experiential cognition as regulating itself by the objects taken as things in themselves, the unconditioned ***simply cannot be thought free of contradiction***; whereas, once we posit that our representation of the things as given to us does not regulate itself by them as things in themselves, but that these objects rather, being appearances, regulate themselves by our manner of representing, ***the contradiction drops away***; and suppose it emerges, in consequence, that the unconditioned is not to be met with in things so far as we know them (so far as they are given us), but is indeed to be met with in them so far as we do not know them, as things in themselves: then it comes to light that what at the outset we merely posited by way of trial {{ XXI }} rests on firm ground.[^***] There remains open to us still, now that speculative reason has been refused any headway across this supersensible terrain, the trial of seeing whether within reason's practical cognition data may not turn up for pinning down that transcendent rational concept of the unconditioned, and thus—as metaphysics wishes—for getting out past the frontier of all possible experience by means of an _a priori_ cognition of ours that is, however, feasible only with a practical intent. And in a procedure of this kind speculative reason has at all events cleared us a space for such an enlargement, however much it had to leave that space vacant, so that we are still at liberty {{ XXII }}—indeed are positively summoned by reason itself—to fill it, if we are able, out of reason's own practical data.[^****]

{{{ 15 }}} The undertaking of this critique of pure speculative reason consists, then, precisely in that attempt to overhaul metaphysics' erstwhile procedure, working a thoroughgoing revolution in it after the pattern set by the geometers and the investigators of nature. It is a treatise concerning method, and not a system of the science as such; and yet it charts the entire contour of that science, alike with respect to {{ XXIII }} the science's bounds and with respect to its whole inner framework. For this is what is proper to pure speculative reason, that it is able—and is obliged—to take the gauge of its own capacity according to the diverse ways in which it selects objects for thinking, and moreover to run through in full the sundry ways of posing tasks to itself, thereby laying down the whole preliminary sketch for a system of metaphysics; because, touching the former, in cognition _a priori_ the objects can be granted nothing but what the thinking subject draws out of its own resources, and, touching the latter, reason forms—as regards the principles of cognition—a wholly self-standing unity complete in itself, in which each member, as within an organized body, is there for the sake of all the rest and all for the sake of the single one, and no principle may be adopted with assurance in ***one*** bearing without its having been examined at the same time in its ***all-round*** bearing upon the entire pure employment of reason. In recompense, though, metaphysics enjoys the singular good luck—such as can accrue to no other rational science having objects for its concern (for ***logic*** busies itself merely with the form of thinking as such)—that, having been set by this critique upon a science's sure march, it can take in to the full the entire province of the cognitions that fall to it {{ XXIV }} and so round off its labor and bequeath it to later ages, for their use, as a capital fund never open to increase, since it deals only with principles and with the limits upon their employment, limits fixed by those principles themselves. As a foundational science it is therefore bound over to this completeness as well, and {{{ 16 }}} of it one has to be able to say: _nil actum reputans, si quid superesset agendum_.

But then, it will be asked, what manner of treasure is this that we mean to hand down to those who come after us in a metaphysics thus refined by critique and thereby brought into a settled condition? Skimming this work, one will fancy one perceives that its worth is, after all, purely ***negative***—that of never hazarding, with speculative reason, past the frontier of experience—and that is indeed its first worth. This worth turns ***positive***, however, the instant one perceives that the principles with which speculative reason strays past its own frontier do not in reality yield ***enlargement*** but, examined at closer range, yield the contraction of our rational employment as their unfailing result, seeing that they genuinely threaten to spread the bounds of sensibility—where these principles in truth belong—{{ XXV }} across everything, and so to crowd out altogether the pure (practical) employment of reason. For that reason a critique reining the former employment in is to that degree indeed ***negative***; but inasmuch as it thereby, at one and the same time, removes a barrier that hems in the latter employment—or even threatens to undo it—it proves in fact of ***positive*** and quite weighty worth, the moment one grows convinced that pure reason has a practical use that is unconditionally necessary (the moral one), wherein reason unavoidably reaches out beyond sensibility's bounds, needing for this no help from the speculative side, yet obliged all the same to make itself safe against that side's counter-thrust, lest it fall out with itself. To refuse this service of critique its ***positive*** worth would come to much the same as pronouncing that a police force renders no positive benefit, on the ground that its chief occupation is, after all, merely to bar the door against the acts of violence which citizens have cause to dread at one another's hands, so that each may go about his business in peace and safety. That space and time are but forms of sensible intuition, and hence but conditions on the existence of things as appearances; that, further, we possess no concepts of the understanding, and thus no elements whatever, toward the {{ XXVI }} cognition of things save so far as an intuition answering to those concepts can be furnished; and that we can therefore have cognition of no object as a thing in itself, but only so far as it is an object of sensible intuition, i.e., as appearance—this is what the analytical part of the critique establishes; whence there of course results the confinement {{{ 17 }}} of all the speculative cognition reason can possibly have to nothing beyond objects of ***experience***. All the same—and this deserves careful note—there is throughout the reservation that, though we cannot ***cognize*** these selfsame objects as things in themselves too, we must at the very least be capable of ***thinking*** them.[^*****] For otherwise there would ensue the preposterous proposition {{ XXVII }} that there is appearance and yet nothing that appears in it. Let us now grant that the distinction our critique makes indispensable—between things as objects of experience and those very things as things in themselves—had never been drawn: then the principle of causality, and with it nature's mechanism in the determining of such things, would have to obtain, without any exception, of all things at large regarded as efficient causes. Of one and the very same being, then—the human soul, say—I could not maintain that its will is free while at once subject to natural necessity, i.e., unfree, without tumbling into a manifest contradiction, for in both claims I would have taken the soul in ***one identical sense***, that is, as a thing taken quite generally (as a thing in itself), nor, absent a prior critique, could I have taken it any differently. But if the critique has committed no error in teaching that the object is to be taken ***under two senses***, i.e., as appearance or as thing in itself; if the deduction of its concepts of the understanding holds good, so that the causal principle too reaches only to things taken in the first of these senses—so far, that is, as they count as objects of experience—while under the second sense {{ XXVIII }} those same things stand outside its reach: then one and the very same will is conceived, in the appearance (the actions open to view), as necessarily conforming to natural law and to that extent ***not free***, and yet, from the other side, as pertaining to a thing in itself and hence exempt from that law, and so as ***free***, with no contradiction cropping up in the process. Now granted that no speculative reason (still less {{{ 18 }}} any empirical observation) lets me ***cognize*** my soul as viewed from this latter side, and hence lets me cognize freedom either, as a property of a being to which I attribute effects within the world of sense—the reason being that I should then have to ***cognize*** such a being as determinate in its existence and yet not as determinate in time (impossible, since to my concept I can lay no intuition underneath)—still I am able to ***think*** freedom to myself: which is to say, the representing of it harbors, at the least, no self-contradiction, provided our critical severance of the two modes of representing (the sensible and the intellectual), together with the resulting curb upon the pure concepts of the understanding and thereby upon the principles issuing from them, holds. Grant now that morality of necessity takes freedom (in the most rigorous sense) for granted as a property belonging to our will, in that it adduces, as its own _a priori_ ***data***, practical and original principles lodged in our reason—principles that, without freedom presupposed, {{ XXIX }} would be flatly impossible—whereas speculative reason had proved freedom to be wholly unthinkable: then of necessity that moral presupposition would have to yield before the one whose contrary carries a manifest contradiction, and consequently ***freedom***, and morality along with it (its contrary carrying no contradiction unless freedom be already presupposed), would have to cede the field to nature's ***mechanism***. As matters stand, however, since for morality's sake I ask no more than that freedom not run counter to itself, and so at least admit of being thought—without my needing to see any further into it—so that it lays no impediment at all in the way of the natural mechanism of the very same action (taken under another bearing): the teaching of morality keeps its ground and the teaching of nature keeps its own, which could not have come to pass had critique not first schooled us in our unavoidable ignorance touching things in themselves and pared down everything we can ***cognize*** theoretically to appearances and nothing more. This very same treatment of the positive worth carried by critical principles of pure reason can be exhibited with respect to the concept of ***God*** and to the ***simple nature*** possessed by our **soul**, though for brevity's sake I let it pass. I cannot, then, so much as ***take up*** {{ XXX }} ***God, freedom, and immortality*** for the purpose of my reason's necessary practical use, unless I at the same time ***strip*** speculative reason of its pretension to insights that overreach, seeing that, in order to arrive at these, it is bound to lean upon such {{{ 19 }}} principles as—reaching in reality no further than objects of a possible experience—when nonetheless brought to bear on what can be no object of experience, really do convert this every time into an appearance, thereby pronouncing every ***practical enlargement*** of pure reason impossible. I had, accordingly, to remove ***knowledge*** so as to open a place for ***belief***; and the dogmatism of metaphysics—the prejudice, that is, that one may make one's way in it without a critique of pure reason—is the genuine wellspring of all that unbelief which contests morality and which is at every turn thoroughly dogmatic. – If, that being so, it need be no hard matter, with a systematic metaphysics drawn up to the measure of the critique of pure reason, to leave a legacy to those who follow, this is a present not to be lightly prized; be it that one looks merely to reason's cultivation by way of a science's sure march in general, set beside its {{ XXXI }} baseless fumbling and its heedless roving where critique is wanting, or to the better spending of the time of an inquisitive youth—one who, under the customary dogmatism, gets encouragement so early and so lavishly to reason at ease about matters it grasps not at all, and into which it, no more than anyone else alive, will ever gain any insight, or even to sally forth inventing thoughts and opinions of a novel kind and thus to let slip the learning of well-founded sciences—but most of all when one enters into the reckoning the priceless gain of putting paid, for all time to come, to every objection against morality and religion in the ***Socratic*** fashion, that is, by the plainest demonstration of the opponents' ignorance. For some metaphysics or another has been present in the world at all times, and doubtless will go on being met with there, and, bound up with it, a dialectic of pure reason as well, this being native to it. Philosophy's first and weightiest concern is therefore this: to deprive that dialectic, once and for all, of every harmful influence, and to do so by plugging up the very source of the errors.

Amid this weighty shift in the domain of the sciences, and the ***forfeiture*** that speculative reason must sustain in the holdings it had until now fancied {{ XXXII }} its own, everything touching the universal concern of mankind, and the benefit the world has thus far reaped from the doctrines of pure reason, abides nonetheless in just the favorable state it ever occupied; and the forfeiture strikes only the ***schools' monopoly***, never in the least the ***concern of humankind***. Let me put it to the most unbending dogmatist {{{ 20 }}}: the demonstration of our soul's persistence past death drawn from the simplicity of substance, the demonstration of the will's freedom against the mechanism of the universe drawn through those fine yet feeble distinctions between subjective and objective practical necessity, the demonstration of God's existence drawn from the concept of an all-most-real being (from the contingency of what is mutable and the need of a prime mover)—once these had gone forth from the schools, were they ever able to reach the general public and to sway its conviction by so much as a hair? Now if that has not come about—and, given the common human understanding's unfitness for speculation so fine-drawn, can never be looked for either—if, on the contrary, as to the first point, the bent of his nature that every person notices, of never being able to find contentment in the temporal (as falling short of the endowments of his whole vocation), must all on its own beget the hope of a life to come {{ XXXIII }}; as to the second, the merely lucid laying-out of the duties, set over against all the pretensions of the inclinations, must all on its own beget awareness of freedom; and, as to the third at last, the magnificent order, loveliness, and provident care that peer forth from nature on every side must all on its own beget belief in a wise and mighty maker of the world—these convictions, disseminating themselves among the public so far as they repose on grounds of reason, having to bring the whole of this about entirely of themselves: then not only does this possession stay undisturbed, but it gains all the more in esteem from the schools' being now taught to arrogate to themselves no loftier or more far-reaching insight, on a point bearing upon mankind's universal concern, than the insight to which the great throng (in our eyes the most estimable) can attain with equal ease, and so to hold themselves solely to the cultivating of these grounds of proof, universally intelligible and, for moral purposes, sufficient. The shift thus bears merely on the overweening claims of the schools, which would fain have themselves passed off, in this matter (as, with justice, they may elsewhere in much else), for the sole connoisseurs and keepers of truths of which they impart to the public only the use, reserving the key to those truths for themselves alone (_quod mecum nescit, solus vult scire videri_). For all that, {{ XXXIV }} a more equitable claim on the speculative philosopher's part is likewise provided for. He stays forever the sole trustee of a science that profits the public unbeknownst to it, namely the critique of reason; for that critique can never grow popular—nor has it any call {{{ 21 }}} to be so, since, just as the finely spun arguments on behalf of useful truths refuse to enter the people's heads, so the equally subtle objections against them never so much as occur to them; whereas, because the school, like every person who raises himself to speculation, is drawn without fail into both, the school is thereby under obligation, through a searching inquiry into the entitlements of speculative reason, to head off once and for all the scandal that must sooner or later break upon the people themselves out of the quarrels into which metaphysicians (and, as such, in the end presumably clergy as well) inescapably get tangled where critique is absent, quarrels that thereafter go on to falsify their own doctrines. By this critique alone can the taproot itself be severed of ***materialism***, of ***fatalism***, of ***atheism***, of freethinkers' ***unbelief***, of ***fanaticism*** and of ***superstition***—all of which may grow harmful far and wide—and, in the last place, of ***idealism*** too and ***skepticism***, which imperil the schools more than the rest and can scarcely find their way over into the public {{ XXXV }}. Should governments after all see fit to occupy themselves with the affairs of the learned, it would accord far better with their sage solicitude for the sciences and for mankind alike to lend their favor to the freedom of a critique of this sort—the one means by which reason's labors can be placed on a solid footing—than to prop up the laughable despotism of the schools, who set up a loud hue and cry over public peril whenever their gossamer webs are pulled apart, webs the public never took the slightest notice of and whose loss it can therefore never feel.

Critique does not set itself against reason's ***dogmatic proceeding*** within its pure cognition considered as a science (for such cognition must invariably be dogmatic, i.e., strictly demonstrative from secure _a priori_ principles); it sets itself rather against dogmatism, i.e., against the presumption of getting on with a pure cognition out of concepts (the philosophical sort), by principles of the kind reason has long been putting to use, and getting on with it single-handed, without first inquiring into the manner and the right whereby it came by them. Dogmatism, accordingly, is pure reason's dogmatic proceeding ***unaccompanied by any prior critique of its own capacity***. This opposition, then, is not meant to plead the cause of that garrulous superficiality which usurps the {{ XXXVI }} name of popularity, nor indeed of the skepticism that makes short shrift of the whole of metaphysics; on the contrary, critique is the requisite preparatory measure toward advancing a well-grounded metaphysics as a science, which must of necessity be executed dogmatically {{{ 22 }}} and, by the sternest demand, systematically—hence in a school-fit (not a popular) manner—for this demand laid upon it, given that it pledges to discharge its task entirely _a priori_ and so to the full contentment of speculative reason, admits of no relaxing. In carrying out the plan critique prescribes—that is, in the metaphysics yet to come—we shall one day be obliged to take after the exacting method of the celebrated Wolff, greatest among all the dogmatic philosophers, who first supplied the model (and by that model became the begetter of that spirit of thoroughness, not yet snuffed out, in Germany) of how a science's sure march is to be won through the orderly settling of principles, the distinct fixing of concepts, the striving after rigor in the proofs, and the forestalling of rash leaps in one's inferences—a man for that very reason eminently fitted to raise a science, such as metaphysics is, into this condition, had it but occurred to him to make the ground ready beforehand through a {{ XXXVII }} critique of the instrument, namely of pure reason itself: a lack chargeable not so much to him as to the dogmatic cast of thought of his age, and over which the philosophers of his own day and of every earlier day have no grounds for reproaching one another. Those who cast aside his manner of instruction and, in one breath, the procedure of the critique of pure reason as well can be harboring no other design than to throw off the shackles of ***science*** entirely—to turn labor into pastime, certitude into mere opinion, and philosophy into philodoxy.

***Turning now to this second edition***, I have, as was only proper, been unwilling to forgo the opening it affords for setting right, so far as may be, the difficulties and the obscurity out of which sundry misreadings may have arisen—misreadings that sharp-witted men have run up against in appraising this book, perhaps not without blame on my side. In the propositions themselves and in their probative grounds, and no less in the form and in the completeness of the plan, I have found nothing to change; a fact to be ascribed partly to the long testing I had put them through before submitting the work to the public, partly to the very make-up of the subject—the nature, that is, of a pure speculative reason, which comprises a genuine framework wherein everything is an organ, wherein, namely, {{ XXXVIII }} all is for the sake of the one and each single thing for the sake of all, so that even the merest frailty, whether a fault (an error) or a shortcoming, cannot but give itself away in the using. In this unalterability {{{ 23 }}} the system will, I trust, go on holding its own hereafter as well. It is no self-flattery but merely the plain evidence—produced by the experiment showing the result to come out the same whether one proceeds outward from the slightest elements up to the whole of pure reason or works back from the whole (for this too is given on its own through reason's ultimate aim in the practical sphere) to each several part—that warrants me in such confidence, since any effort to recast even the smallest part at once conjures up contradictions, not of the system merely, but of universal human reason. In the ***manner of exposition***, by contrast, much still remains outstanding, and on this head I have, with the present edition, essayed improvements meant to redress in part the misunderstanding of the Aesthetic, chiefly the one lodged in the concept of time, in part the obscurity of the deduction of the concepts of the understanding, in part the alleged want of ample evidence in the proofs of the principles of the pure understanding, and in part, finally, the misconstruing of the paralogisms laid to the charge of rational psychology. Only so far as this—namely, no further than to the close of the first main division {{ XXXIX }} of the Transcendental Dialectic—do my {{ XL }} revisions of the mode of exposition reach,[^******] because time ran short and, as regards {{{ 24 }}} {{ XLI }} the remainder, no misapprehension on the part of knowledgeable and unbiased examiners had come my way—examiners who, even though I may not name them with the {{{ 25 }}} {{ XLII }} praise that is their due, will discover for themselves, each in its place, the heed I have paid to their reminders. Bound up with this improvement, though, is a slight loss to the reader, one not to be averted save by swelling the book to an unwieldy bulk: namely that various matters which, while not indispensable to the completeness of the whole, many a reader would nonetheless be loath to go without—since they may be serviceable toward another end—have had to be dropped or rendered in curtailed form, so as to make way for my presentation, now, I hope, more readily grasped, which at bottom leaves the propositions and even their probative grounds entirely untouched, yet here and there parts company with the earlier one in its method of delivery so widely that mere insertions could not have brought it off. This slight loss, which anyone may in any event make good at pleasure by collating with the first edition, is more than offset, I hope, by the greater ease of comprehension. In various published pieces (on occasion where I have reviewed certain books, on occasion in treatises of their own) I have registered with thankful satisfaction that the spirit of thoroughness in Germany is not extinct, {{ XLIII }} but has only for a little while been drowned out by the modish note of a genius-like license in thinking, and that the thorn-strewn tracks of critique—which lead to a science of pure reason that is school-fit yet, being such, alone lasting and therefore in the highest degree needful—have not kept spirited and lucid minds from making themselves master of it. To these deserving men, who so felicitously wed thoroughness of insight with a gift for luminous presentation (a gift I am hardly aware of possessing myself), I leave the completing of my working-out, which in the latter respect is here and there still wanting; for in the present case the danger is not of being refuted but rather of not being understood. As for myself, from this point on I can enter into no controversies, albeit I shall heed with care every hint, whether it come from friends or from adversaries, so as to turn it to account in the future working-out of the system agreeably to this propaedeutic. Since in the course of these labors I have already moved on rather deep into old age (this very month into my sixty-fourth year), I must, if I mean to carry through my {{{ 26 }}} plan of furnishing the metaphysics of nature no less than that of morals, by way of vindicating the soundness of the critique of speculative and of practical reason alike, husband my time and look to have the {{ XLIV }} clearing-up both of the obscurities scarcely to be shunned at the start of this work, and the defending of the whole, come from those deserving men who have taken it up as their own. In this or that passage any philosophical exposition may be nipped at (it cannot come forward armor-plated as the mathematical can), while the framework of the system, regarded as a unity, runs thereby not a particle of danger—a system for the surveying of which, when it is new, few command the nimbleness of mind, and fewer still the appetite, all novelty coming to them at an ill time. Seeming contradictions, too, can be picked out—by pitting isolated passages, wrenched from their setting, one against another—in any writing that runs on chiefly as free discourse; such contradictions cast, in the eyes of one who leans on others' verdicts, an unfavorable light upon it, yet come apart with great ease for anyone who has taken possession of the idea as a whole. Meanwhile, where a theory has soundness within itself, the action and reaction that at first menaced it with grave danger come in time to serve only for grinding down its unevennesses and—should men of impartiality, of insight, and of true popularity take it in hand—for procuring it, before long, the elegance it still requires as well. Königsberg, in April 1787.

[^*]: I am not, in what precedes, keeping strictly to the thread of the history of the experimental method, whose earliest origins are moreover not well established.

[^**]: This method, taken over from the natural scientist, comes down therefore to this: to look for the elements of pure reason in ***whatever an experiment can confirm or refute***. Now no experiment can be run upon the ***objects*** of pure reason's propositions (as it can in natural science) so as to test them, above all where those propositions are ventured out past every bound of possible experience: it will thus be practicable only with the ***concepts*** and ***principles*** we adopt _a priori_, by so contriving them that the selfsame objects admit of being viewed from two different sides—***on the one hand*** as objects of sense and of understanding belonging to experience, ***on the other*** as objects merely thought, at most for a reason that stands apart and strains beyond experience's bound. Should it then be found that agreement with pure reason's principle obtains when the things are viewed under that doubled aspect, whereas under a single aspect there springs up an unavoidable strife of reason with itself, the experiment pronounces in favor of that distinction's being the right one.

[^***]: This experiment of pure reason has a great deal in common with that of the ***chemists***, an experiment they at times call the trial by **reduction**, but in general the ***synthetic proceeding***. The ***metaphysician's analysis*** had parted pure cognition _a priori_ into two elements of very unlike kind, namely that of things as appearances and next that of things in themselves. The ***dialectic*** then binds the two once more into ***accord*** with reason's indispensable idea of the ***unconditioned***, and discovers that this accord can issue from nothing but that same distinction—which is therefore the genuine one.

[^****]: Thus the central laws governing the motions of the heavenly bodies lent settled certainty to what ***Copernicus*** had at the outset taken up only as a hypothesis, and at once made good the unseen force (Newton's attraction) that holds the world-edifice together—a force that would have stayed hidden forever had the former not ventured, in a fashion at odds with sense yet true for all that, to seek the observed motions not in the heavenly bodies but in the one who beholds them. In this preface I likewise advance the recasting of the mode of thought expounded in the critique—analogous to that hypothesis—as itself no more than a hypothesis, notwithstanding that in the treatise proper it is proved, from how our representations of space and of time are constituted and from the understanding's elementary concepts, not by hypothesis but apodictically, my purpose being merely to flag the first essays at such a recasting, essays that are in every instance hypothetical.

[^*****]: To cognize an ***object*** there is demanded of me that I be in a position to establish its possibility (whether out of its actuality, on the testimony of experience, or _a priori_ by way of reason). ***Think***, however, I may whatever I please, provided only that I do not contradict my own self—that is, provided my concept amounts to a possible thought—even if I am in no position to warrant whether or not, within the totality of all that is possible, an object answers to it as well. But to confer upon such a concept objective validity (real possibility, the former having been but the logical kind), something further is demanded. This further something, though, need by no means be looked for in theoretical sources of cognition; it may just as well reside in practical ones.

[^******]: A genuine addition, albeit only in the manner of proof, is the one and only thing I could point to, the addition I have made, at p. 275, by a fresh refutation of psychological ***idealism*** together with a rigorous proof—the sole possible one, as I take it—of the objective reality of outer intuition. However harmless idealism may be reckoned with respect to metaphysics' essential ends (harmless it in fact is not), it stays a scandal to philosophy and to universal human reason all the same that the existence of the things outside us—from which, after all, we get the entire matter for our cognitions, even for our inner sense—should have to be taken on ***belief*** alone, with no adequate proof to be set against anyone who should take it into his head to call it in doubt. Since some obscurity attaches to the wording of the proof from the third line down to the sixth, I would ask that this period be recast thus:||| ***„This abiding thing, however, cannot be an intuition within me. For the grounds determining my existence that are to be found within me are one and all representations, and, as such, themselves stand in need of something abiding, distinct from them, in reference to which their alternation—and so my existence throughout the time in which they alternate—may be determined.“*** The likely rejoinder to this proof is: surely I am immediately aware only of what lies within me, i.e., of my ***representation*** of outer things; and hence it stays undecided whether or not anything answering to it exists beyond me. Yet of ***my existence in time*** (and thus of the determinability of that existence therein) I am aware through inner ***experience***, and this amounts to more than being merely aware of my representation, being nonetheless one and the same as the ***empirical awareness of my existence***, which is determinable solely by way of a relation to something that, tied to my existence, ***lies beyond me***. This awareness of my existence in time is bound up, then, identically with the awareness of a relation to something beyond me, and it is accordingly experience and not invention, sense and not imagination, that knits the outer inseparably to my inner sense; for outer sense is already of itself a referring of intuition to something actual beyond me, and its reality, as against imagining, rests on nothing but this, that it comes to be joined inseparably with inner experience itself, as the condition under which that experience is possible—which is what happens here. Could I, alongside the ***intellectual awareness*** of my existence in the representation ***I am*** that attends all my judgments and acts of understanding, at the same time bind up a determining of my existence by ***intellectual intuition***, then the awareness of a relation to something beyond me would form no necessary part of it. But since, as it is, that intellectual awareness does come first, while the inner intuition in which alone my existence admits of being determined is sensible and tied to a temporal condition; and since this determining, and hence inner experience itself, hangs upon something abiding that is not within me, and so upon something beyond me over against which I must consider myself as standing in relation: outer sense's reality is of necessity conjoined with inner sense's for the possibility of experience at all—which is to say, I am just as surely aware that things exist beyond me bearing on my sense as I am aware that I myself exist as determined in time. But which of the intuitions given actually have objects beyond me answering to them, and so belong to outer ***sense***, to which and not to imagination they are to be assigned, has to be settled case by singular case by the rules whereby experience as such (inner experience included) is told apart from imagining, the proposition that outer experience really exists lying always at the base of it. One may append here yet this remark: representing something ***abiding*** in existence is not one and the same with an ***abiding representing***; for the latter can be highly mutable and shifting, like all our representations, the representations of matter not excepted, and it refers all the while to something abiding, which must therefore be a thing set apart from every one of my representations and outer, whose existence is drawn of necessity into the ***determining*** of my own existence and forms with it but a single experience, one that would not even come to pass inwardly were it not, in part, at once outward. The How? is no more open to further explaining here than is our thinking, in general, of that which stands fast in time, whose coexistence with what shifts gives rise to the concept of alteration.
