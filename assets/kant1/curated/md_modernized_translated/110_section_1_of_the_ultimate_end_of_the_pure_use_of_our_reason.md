## Section 1. Of the ultimate end of the pure use of our reason

Reason is driven by a propensity of its nature to go beyond its use in experience, to venture in a pure use and by means of mere ideas to the outermost bounds of all cognition, and only first to find peace in the completion of its circle, in a self-subsisting systematic whole. Is this endeavor now grounded merely on its speculative, or rather uniquely and solely on its practical interest?

I will now set aside the good fortune of pure reason in a speculative regard, and ask only about those problems the solution of which constitutes its ultimate end, whether it reaches this or not, and in respect of which all other ends have merely the value of means. These {{ 826 }} highest ends must, according to the nature of reason, in turn have unity, in order to promote in a unified way that interest of humanity which is subordinate to no higher interest.

The final aim to which in the end the speculation of reason in its transcendental use is directed concerns three objects: the freedom of the will, the immortality of the soul, and the existence of God. In respect of all three, the merely speculative interest of reason is only very small, and in regard to it a {{{ 519 }}} tiring labor of transcendental investigation, struggling with unending obstacles, would hardly be undertaken, because one cannot make any use of all the discoveries that might be made here that would prove its utility _in concreto_, i.e., in the investigation of nature. The will may be free, but this can concern only the intelligible cause of our volition. For as regards the appearances of its manifestations, i.e., actions, we must never explain them otherwise than all other appearances of nature, namely, in accordance with its immutable laws, according to an inviolable basic maxim without which we cannot exercise any reason in its empirical use. Second, the spiritual nature of the soul (and with it its immortality) may perhaps be comprehended, yet no account can be made of this either as an explanatory ground for the appearances of this life, or for the special constitution {{ 827 }} of our future state, because our concept of an incorporeal nature is merely negative and does not in the least expand our cognition, nor does it offer any suitable material for consequences, except perhaps for such as can hold only as fictions, which are not permitted by philosophy. Third, even if the existence of a supreme intelligence were proven, we would indeed be able to make comprehensible the purposiveness in the world-constitution and order in general, but we would by no means be authorized to derive any special arrangement and order from it, or, where it is not perceived, boldly to infer it; since it is a necessary rule of the speculative use of reason not to bypass natural causes and abandon what we can learn about from experience in order to derive something that we know from that which completely exceeds all our acquaintance. In a word, these three propositions always remain transcendent for speculative reason, and have no immanent use at all, i.e., one that is permissible for objects of experience, and thus useful to us in some way, but, considered in themselves, are entirely idle and moreover extremely difficult exertions of our reason.

If, then, these three cardinal propositions are not at all necessary for our ***knowing***, and yet are insistently recommended to us by our reason, {{ 828 }} their importance must really concern only the ***practical***.

{{{ 520 }}} Practical is everything that is possible through freedom. But if the conditions for the exercise of our free choice are empirical, then in this case reason can have no other than regulative use, and can serve only to bring about the unity of empirical laws; as, e.g., in the doctrine of prudence, the unification of all ends that are given to us by our inclinations into the single end, ***happiness***, and the coordination of the means for attaining it, constitutes the entire business of reason, which for this reason can deliver no other than ***pragmatic*** laws of free conduct for the attainment of the ends recommended to us by the senses, and thus no pure laws determined completely _a priori._ In contrast, pure practical laws, the end of which is given by reason completely _a priori,_ and which do not command under empirical conditions but rather command absolutely, would be products of pure reason. The ***moral*** laws, however, are of this kind; hence they alone belong to the practical use of pure reason and permit of a canon.

The entire preparation of reason, therefore, in its cultivation, which one can call pure philosophy, is in fact directed only to these three problems. These themselves, however, have in turn their more remote aim, namely, ***what is to be done*** if the will is free, if there is a {{ 829 }} God, and if there is a future world. Since this now concerns our conduct in relation to the highest end, the ultimate aim of nature, which provides for us wisely in the constitution of our reason, is properly directed only to the moral.

But caution is necessary in order that we do not, while casting our eyes on an object that is foreign[^*] to transcendental philosophy, wander off into episodes and violate the unity of the system, and yet, on the other side, by saying too little about its new material, fail to provide clarity or conviction. I hope to accomplish both by keeping as close as possible to the {{{ 521 }}} transcendental, and setting completely aside what might be psychological, i.e., empirical, here.

And here it is first to be noted that for now I will make use of the concept of freedom only in a practical sense, and will set aside the concept of freedom in its transcendental significance, which cannot be presupposed empirically as an explanation of appearances, {{ 830 }} but is itself a problem for reason, as having been dealt with above. A choice, namely, is merely ***animal*** (_arbitrium brutum_) if it cannot be determined otherwise than through sensible impulses, i.e., ***pathologically***. That choice, however, which can be determined independently of sensible impulses, and thus through motivating causes that are represented only by reason, is called ***free choice*** (_arbitrium liberum_), and everything that is connected with this, whether as ground or consequence, is called ***practical***. Practical freedom can be proven through experience. For not merely that which stimulates, i.e., directly affects the senses, determines human choice, but we have a power to overcome the impressions on our sensible desire-faculty through representations of that which is itself useful or harmful in a more remote way; but these deliberations about what is desirable in regard to our whole state, i.e., what is good and useful, rest on reason. Reason therefore also gives laws which are imperatives, i.e., objective ***laws of freedom***, and which say ***what ought to happen***, even if it perhaps never does happen, and are thereby distinguished from ***laws of nature***, which deal only with ***what does happen***, on which account they are also called practical laws.

{{ 831 }} But whether in these actions, through which it prescribes laws, reason is not itself determined in turn by other influences, and whether that which in regard to sensible impulses is called freedom might not in turn be nature in respect of higher and more remote efficient causes, does not concern us in the practical, since we ask reason here only for the ***rule*** of conduct, but is a merely speculative question which we can set aside as long as our aim is directed to action or omission. We thus cognize practical freedom through experience as one of the natural causes, namely a causality of reason in the determination of the will, whereas {{{ 522 }}} transcendental freedom requires an independence of this reason itself (in regard to its causality to begin a series of appearances) from all determining causes of the sensible world, and to that extent seems to be contrary to the law of nature, and thus to all possible experience, and so remains a problem. For reason in its practical use, however, this problem does not belong, so we have to do in a canon of pure reason with only two questions that concern the practical interest of pure reason and in respect of which a canon of its use must be possible, namely: is there a God? is there a future life? The question concerning transcendental freedom concerns merely speculative knowledge, which we can set aside as completely indifferent when {{ 832 }} we have to do with the practical, and about which sufficient discussion is already to be found in the Antinomy of Pure Reason.

[^*]: All practical concepts concern objects of satisfaction or dissatisfaction, i.e., of pleasure and displeasure, hence at least indirectly concern objects of our feeling. But since this is not a power of representing things, but lies outside our entire cognitive power, the elements of our judgments, insofar as they relate to pleasure or displeasure, hence those of practical judgments, do not belong in the sum total of transcendental philosophy, which has to do solely with pure cognitions _a priori._
