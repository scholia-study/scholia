## Second Analogy. Principle of succession in time in accordance with the law of causality

***All alterations take place conformably to the law linking cause and effect.***

## {{{ 167 }}} Proof.

(That all appearances following one upon another in time are, one and all, nothing but ***alterations***, i.e. a successive being and non-being of the determinations of the substance that endures — so that the being of this substance, coming after its own non-being, or its non-being, coming after its {{ 233 }} existence, in short, that the substance itself neither comes into being nor passes away — all this the earlier principle has demonstrated. One might just as well have phrased it thus: ***All change (succession) among appearances is nothing but alteration***; for a substance's coming-to-be or ceasing-to-be does not count as its alteration, given that the very concept of altering demands that one selfsame subject be posited as existing — and thus as enduring — under two contrary determinations. With this advance notice in place, here is the proof).

What I perceive is that appearances come one after another — that at some given time the things are in a state whose contrary held in the state before. So what I am really doing is binding together two perceptions within time. Yet such binding is no achievement of sense and intuition on their own; it arises here from a synthesizing power of imagination, which fixes inner sense as regards the relation in time. Now this power is free to link the two states just mentioned in two opposite orders, letting either precede the other in time; for time on its own admits of no perception, and one cannot read off from the object, by reference to time and as if empirically, which member comes first and which after. Hence all I am aware of is that my imaginative power sets down the one before and the other after, not that within the object itself the one state runs ahead of {{ 234 }} the other; put differently, bare perception leaves the ***objective relation*** among the appearances that follow each other undecided. For this relation to be known as something settled, the connection between the two states has to be conceived so that it fixes, as necessary, which of them is to be set first and which second, ruling out the reverse. Now a concept bringing with it a necessity of synthetic unity can be nothing other than a pure concept of the understanding, absent from perception itself; and in the present case it is the concept governing the ***relation of cause and effect***{{{ 168 }}}, by which the first fixes the second, in time, as what results from it — not as a thing that could merely run ahead in fancy (or fail to be perceived altogether). It follows that experience itself — namely empirical cognition of appearances — is possible only where we place the succession of appearances, and with it every alteration, under the causal law; and so those appearances, taken as objects of experience, are themselves possible only under this same law.

Apprehending the manifold that an appearance offers is at every turn successive. One representation of a part comes after the next. Whether the parts likewise succeed each other in the object is a further matter for reflection, not already settled by the former. Granted, anything at all — indeed each representation, so far as we are conscious of having it — may be termed an object; yet just what {{ 235 }} that term signifies when applied to appearances — taken not as representations that are themselves objects, but only as designating an object — demands more searching inquiry. To the extent that they, simply as representations, are also objects for consciousness, they cannot be marked off from the apprehending of them — their intake into imagination's synthesis; and so the verdict must be that the mind always brings forth the manifold of appearances one item after another. Were appearances things in themselves, no one could ever infer, from the sequence in which the representations of their manifold arrive, the way that manifold is joined together in the object. After all, our dealings extend to our representations and nothing more; what things in themselves might be — leaving aside the representations by which they act upon us — falls wholly outside the range of what we can cognize. So, although appearances are not things in themselves and yet remain the sole material offered us for cognition, my charge is to say what kind of temporal combination attaches to the manifold in the appearances themselves, even while, in apprehension, its representation runs on successively. Take, for instance, a house that stands before me: my apprehending of the manifold it presents proceeds bit by bit. The question now is whether that house's manifold is, in its own right, likewise successive — something no one, of course, would concede. {{ 236 }} Yet the moment I lift my concepts of an object to their transcendental import, the house ceases to be any thing in itself and becomes merely an appearance — a representation, that is — with a transcendental {{{ 169 }}} object left unknown; so what am I really asking when I ask how the manifold could be joined within the appearance itself (which, after all, is nothing on its own)? In this setting, whatever lies within the successive apprehending counts as representation, while the appearance handed to me — though it is no more than the aggregate of those representations — counts as their object, one with which the concept I extract out of the apprehended representations ought to square. It quickly becomes plain that, because truth is cognition's agreement with its object, the only thing open to question here is the formal requirements of empirical truth, and that an appearance, set over against the apprehended representations, can be presented as the object marked off from them solely when it falls under a rule setting it apart from any other apprehension and making some one manner of combining the manifold obligatory. Whatever in the appearance holds the condition for this compulsory rule of apprehending — that is the object.

Let us now carry on with the problem before us. That something occurs — i.e. that some thing, or state, arises which was not there before — is nothing we can empirically {{ 237 }} take in unless it is led by an appearance lacking that state; for an actuality succeeding an empty stretch of time, a coming-to-be with no prior state of things, is as unapprehensible as empty time is on its own. So each apprehending of an event amounts to a perception that comes after some other. Yet, because matters stand this way throughout every synthesis of apprehending — as my earlier house-example brought out — this feature alone does not set one apprehension apart from the rest. I remark, however, this further point: if, within an appearance harboring some occurrence, I label the earlier perceptual state A and the later one B, then in apprehension B can come only after A, while the perception A can only run ahead of B and never trail it. Suppose I watch a ship carried down the current. My perceiving of its downstream position comes after my perceiving of its upstream position along the river's flow, and there is no way, in apprehending this appearance, for the ship to register first downstream and only afterwards upstream. Thus the order of the perceptual sequence within apprehension is here fixed, and the apprehending is tied down to it. By contrast, in the earlier {{{ 170 }}} case of the house my apprehending perceptions could start from the roof and close at the base, or equally {{ 238 }} start from the bottom and finish at the top, and just as well run through the manifold of empirical intuition rightward or leftward. Across that string of perceptions, then, no fixed order compelled me to any particular starting point in apprehending if I was to bind the manifold together empirically. With the perception of what occurs, however, such a rule is present without fail, and it renders the order of the successive perceptions (in apprehending this appearance) ***necessary***.

Here, accordingly, I am obliged to read the ***subjective sequence*** of my apprehending off the ***objective sequence*** of the appearances; otherwise the former stays entirely loose and tells no one appearance apart from the next. Taken by itself the subjective sequence establishes nothing regarding how the manifold hangs together in the object, since it is wholly a matter of caprice. The objective sequence, by contrast, lies in that ordering of the appearance's manifold whereby apprehending the one member (the thing that occurs) trails apprehending the other (the antecedent) ***in accordance with a rule***. This alone licenses me to assert of the appearance itself — not just of my act of apprehending — that a sequence resides in it, which comes to the same as saying I can conduct the apprehending in no other order than exactly this one.

By such a rule, then, whatever in general leads up to an {{ 239 }} event must contain the condition for a rule under which that event invariably and of necessity ensues; the reverse route, though, is closed to me — I cannot start from the event and pin down (by apprehending) what went before. For out of a later moment no appearance travels back to the earlier one, though it does bear on ***some preceding one*** or other; whereas from a given time the passage forward to the determinate subsequent time cannot be escaped. So, being after all a thing that comes after, it has of necessity to be referred to some other, antecedent thing that it succeeds under a rule — necessarily, that is — so that the event, qua conditioned, points reliably to a condition, and this condition in turn settles the event.

Imagine that nothing at all precedes an event on which it would have to follow by a rule; then every perceptual sequence would reside {{{ 171 }}} in apprehension alone — would be simply subjective — and would fix nothing objectively about which perception truly ought to rank as prior and which as posterior. On these terms we would be left with a mere play of representations answering to no object whatever; that is, our perceiving would fail to separate one appearance from another in point of temporal relation, since apprehending runs the same course everywhere and so nothing within the appearance {{ 240 }} pins it down in a way that would make a determinate sequence objectively necessary. Hence I shall decline to say that two states succeed each other in the appearance, and say only that one act of apprehending trails another — a thing wholly ***subjective***, settling no object, and therefore incapable of passing for cognition of any object at all (not even within the appearance).

Whenever, then, we experience that something takes place, we invariably take for granted that some antecedent exists which it succeeds by a rule. Lacking that, I would never predicate 'following' of the object, for the bare succession within my apprehending, unless a rule fixes it in relation to our experience, entitles me to no succession in the object. It is thus always by appeal to a rule — one whereby the appearances in their succession, i.e. as they come to pass, are governed by the antecedent state — that I turn my subjective synthesis (of apprehending) into an objective one; and only on this single assumption does experience of an occurring thing become possible at all.

Admittedly this looks like a clash with everything that has ever been observed about how our use of the understanding proceeds — the view being that only by noticing and comparing the agreeing sequences whereby many events trail preceding appearances were we first guided {{ 241 }} to hit upon a rule under which certain events forever come after certain appearances, and thereby first moved to fashion for ourselves the concept of a cause. So construed, the concept would be empirical through and through, and the rule it furnishes — that whatever comes about has some cause — would prove no less contingent than experience is: its universality together with its necessity would in that case be a mere fiction, devoid of genuine universal force, resting as it would not upon anything _a priori_ but merely upon induction. The situation, though, is the same as with the other pure representations {{{ 172 }}} _a priori_ (space and time, say): we can draw them out of experience as clear concepts only because we had already laid them into experience and by their means first made experience come about. True enough, the logical clarity of this representation — a rule fixing the series of events, i.e. the concept of a cause — comes within reach only after we have employed it in experience; yet regarding it as the condition for the synthetic unity of appearances throughout time was all the same what grounded experience itself, and so ran ahead of experience _a priori_.

What it comes down to, therefore, is showing by an example that never, not even in experience, do we assign the succession (of an event, wherein something arises that formerly was not) to the object, and mark it off from the subjective succession belonging to our {{ 242 }} apprehending, save where a rule underlies things and forces us to keep to this arrangement of the perceptions in preference to any other — indeed that this very forcing is what makes the representation of a succession in the object possible in the first place.

Within us there are representations that we are able to become aware of. But however widely this awareness reaches, and however exact or meticulous it is, they never cease to be mere representations — inner determinations of our mind standing in some temporal relation or other. By what route, then, do we get to setting an object over against these representations, or crediting them, beyond their subjective reality as modifications, with some objective reality or other, I know not what? Objective meaning cannot lie in a relation to some further representation (namely of what one would like to call the object), else the question simply recurs: how does this second representation, for its part, step outside itself and gain objective meaning over and above the subjective meaning that belongs to it as a determination of the mind's condition? When we probe what fresh quality the ***relation to an object*** bestows on our representations, and what standing they acquire by it, we discover that it accomplishes only this: it makes the joining of the representations necessary after a certain fashion {{ 243 }} and places them under a rule; and, the other way around, only because a certain ordering is necessary in the temporal relation of our representations does objective meaning accrue to them.

Throughout the synthesis of appearances the manifold of representations comes ever one after another. By this, however, no object whatever gets {{{ 173 }}} represented, for a succession shared by every apprehension alike keeps nothing marked off from anything else. The instant, though, that I notice or assume within this succession a bearing on the antecedent state, from which the representation issues by a rule, something is thereby represented as an event, as what comes to pass — that is, I cognize an object that has to be lodged at one definite spot in time, a spot that the preceding state permits it to receive in no other way. So, in perceiving that something occurs, my representation carries first of all that something goes before it, since it is exactly by reference to this that the appearance gets its place in time — namely, that of coming to exist after an earlier time in which it did not. Its fixed temporal place within this relation, however, it can obtain only if something is assumed in the antecedent state upon which it invariably — that is, by a rule — comes after; whence it follows, first, that I may not turn the series around and set what occurs ahead of that which it succeeds, and, second, that once the preceding state {{ 244 }} is posited, this determinate event ensues unfailingly and of necessity. In this way there arises an ordering among our representations wherein the present (so far as it has come to be) directs us to some antecedent state as a correlate — as yet undetermined though it be — of this given event, a correlate that all the same bears determiningly on the event as its consequence and knits it necessarily to itself along the time-series.

Now if it belongs to the necessary law of our sensibility — hence to a ***formal condition*** shared by all perceptions — that earlier time determines later of necessity (seeing that I reach the later by no path but the earlier), then it is likewise an inescapable ***law of the empirical representation*** of the time-series that whatever existence falls in the later time is settled by the appearances of the time gone by, and that these, as events, occur only to the extent that the earlier appearances mark out for them their existence in time, i.e. lay it down by a rule. For ***in the appearances alone are we able empirically to know this continuity within the interconnection*** of times.

Understanding is requisite for all experience and its very possibility, and its first contribution here is not to render the representation of objects distinct but rather to make the representation of an object {{ 245 }} possible in the first place. It brings this about by carrying the order of time {{{ 174 }}} over to the appearances and their existence, allotting to each — as a member that follows — a temporal place fixed _a priori_ relative to the appearances before it, a place without which it would fail to accord with time itself, which apportions to every one of its parts its place _a priori_. This assignment of place cannot, however, be drawn from any relation of the appearances to absolute time (that being no object of perception); on the contrary, the appearances have to determine one another's temporal places and make such placing necessary within the order of time — meaning that whatever follows or occurs must come, under a universal rule, after what the antecedent state held; and out of this grows a series of appearances which, through the understanding, produces and renders necessary, in the field of possible perceptions, that very order and unbroken connection already found _a priori_ in the form of inner intuition (time), where every perception would have to take its place.

So that something occurs is a perception belonging to a possible experience — one made actual once I treat the appearance as fixed as regards its temporal place, and thus as an object retrievable at any time within the nexus of perceptions {{ 246 }} under a rule. And this rule — for pinning something down in the order of time — runs thus: within the antecedent there must be found the condition under which the event forever (i.e. necessarily) ensues. Hence the principle of sufficient reason is what grounds possible experience — that is, the objective cognition of appearances as they stand related in time's succession.

The proof-ground of this principle, however, hinges on nothing but the following points. Every empirical cognition involves imagination's synthesis of the manifold, forever successive; within it, that is, the representations come always one upon another. Yet in imagination this sequence carries no fixed order (nothing dictating what shall go first and what after), and the run of successive representations may be traversed backward no less than forward. Should this synthesis, though, be one of apprehension (of the manifold given in a particular appearance), then the order does stand fixed in the object — or, put more exactly, there resides in it an order of the successive synthesizing that determines an object, an order by which something has necessarily to precede {{{ 175 }}} and, that being posited, the other has necessarily to ensue. Accordingly, for my perception to hold the cognition of an event — of a thing that genuinely occurs — it has to be an empirical judgment wherein one conceives the succession as settled: as presupposing, that is, another {{ 247 }} appearance in time which it succeeds necessarily, or by a rule. Were it otherwise — were I to posit the antecedent and yet have the event not follow it of necessity — I should have to regard the thing as no more than a subjective sport of my fancies, and, should I still picture something objective in it, dub it a bare dream. So the relation among appearances (viewed as possible perceptions), whereby the later member (what occurs) has its existence fixed necessarily, and by a rule in time, through something earlier — in short, the relation of cause to effect — constitutes the condition for the objective validity our empirical judgments possess in regard to the run of perceptions, and hence for their empirical truth, and so for experience. The principle asserting a causal relation across the succession of appearances therefore holds good of every object of experience as well (given the conditions of succession), precisely because it is what grounds the very possibility of any such experience.

At this point, though, a further misgiving surfaces, and it needs clearing away. Our formulation confines the principle of causal linkage among appearances to their succession, yet in applying it one notices that it fits their coexistence too, cause and effect being capable of simultaneity. Consider the warmth in a {{ 248 }} room, which one does not encounter out in the open air. Casting about for its cause, I light upon a stove that has been fired up. This stove, as the cause, coexists with its effect, the room's warmth; so no time-order separates cause from effect here — they are contemporaneous — and still the law stands. Most of nature's operative causes run alongside their effects, and any time-order among the latter springs merely from the cause's inability to discharge its full effect in one moment. Even so, at the moment when the effect first emerges it always coincides with its cause's causality, for had that cause left off being a moment sooner, the effect would never have come about. One must be careful to see that what counts is time's ***order*** and not its ***elapsing*** {{{ 176 }}}: the relation holds even where no time has passed. The interval between the cause's causality and its immediate effect may shrink to the ***vanishing*** point (making them contemporaneous), and yet how the one stands to the other stays forever determinable in terms of time. Should I take as cause a ball resting on a stuffed cushion and denting a hollow into it, it is contemporaneous with its effect. All the same, I tell the two apart by the temporal relation in the dynamical linkage between them. Lay the ball onto the cushion, and the hollow succeeds its formerly even surface; but let the cushion already bear (from I know not where) {{ 249 }} a hollow, and no leaden ball comes after it.

Time-order, then, really is the one empirical mark of the effect as against the causality of the cause going before. A glass is what causes water to climb above its own level surface, even though the two appearances are contemporaneous. For no sooner do I scoop the water out of some larger vessel with the glass than something happens, namely the turning of the flat level it kept there into the concave one it takes on within the glass.

Such causality points onward to the concept of action, action to that of force, and by this route to the concept of substance. Because I have no wish to clog my critical project — aimed strictly at the springs of synthetic cognition _a priori_ — with dissections that bear only on clarifying concepts (not enlarging them), I hand their thorough discussion over to some future system of pure reason, even though analysis of this kind already abounds in the familiar handbooks of the subject. What I cannot pass over, however, is the empirical mark of a substance, so far as substance appears to disclose itself not by the appearance's persistence but, more readily and better, through action.

{{ 250 }} Wherever action is present — and with it activity and force — substance too is present, and it is in substance alone that we must locate the seat of that teeming wellspring of appearances. Well and good as far as it goes; but when it comes to spelling out what one means by substance, and doing so without the faulty circle, the account is not so cheaply given. How, from the action, is one to conclude at once to the ***persistence*** of the agent — that being, after all, so essential and distinctive a {{{ 177 }}} sign of substance (phaenomenon)? Yet on the strength of what we said earlier the question resolves without any such trouble, however insoluble it would prove in the customary way (of working merely analytically upon one's concepts). Action, by its very meaning, denotes how the subject of causality stands to the effect. And because every effect lies in what comes to pass — thus in the changeable, which time marks off by succession — its final subject is ***the abiding***, the substratum underlying all that shifts, i.e. substance. For by the causal principle actions constitute at all times the primary ground for every change among appearances, and so cannot reside in a subject that is itself changing, since that would call for yet further actions and a further subject to determine the change. On this basis action now establishes substantiality as a sufficient {{ 251 }} empirical mark, sparing me any prior search for the substance's persistence by way of compared perceptions — a search that could not, in any event, be carried through this way with the completeness that the scope and rigorous universality of the concept demand. For it follows securely that the primary subject of the causality behind all coming-to-be and passing-away cannot, within the field of appearances, itself come to be or pass away — an inference that terminates in empirical necessity and abidingness in existence, and thus in the concept of a substance as appearance.

Whenever an event occurs, the sheer coming-to-be — leaving aside what the thing is that comes to be — already forms, on its own, a topic for inquiry. The passage out of a state's non-being into that state — even supposing the state carried no quality within the appearance — stands by itself as something requiring examination. As was set out under heading A, this arising touches not the substance (which does not arise) but its state. It is thus alteration only, and not a springing-up out of nothing. Where such a springing-up is taken as the effect of an external cause, it is termed creation, which we cannot admit as an event among appearances, since its bare possibility would at once cancel the unity of experience; though if I view all things not as phenomena but as things in themselves and as {{ 252 }} objects of the mere understanding, they may — for all their being substances — be looked on as hanging, in their existence, upon an external cause; but that would then drag along {{{ 178 }}} wholly other senses of the words and would not suit appearances taken as possible objects of experience.

In what way anything whatever admits of alteration; how it can come about that a state at one temporal point gives way to its opposite at another — of all this we hold not the faintest concept _a priori_. What it takes is familiarity with real forces, obtainable only by experience — the moving forces, say, or, amounting to the same thing, certain successive appearances (motions) that betoken such forces. The form of any alteration, however — the condition on which alone it can go forward as the emergence of another state (no matter what its content, i.e. no matter which state is being altered) — and therefore the succession of the states themselves (the thing that happens) can all the same be weighed _a priori_ under the causal law together with the conditions of time.[^*]

{{ 253 }} Let a substance move from a state a to another state b: the moment of the second differs from the moment of the first state and comes after it. In the same way the second state, as a reality (within the appearance), is set off from the first — in which that reality was absent — just as b is set off from zero; that is, even were b to diverge from a in magnitude alone, the alteration amounts to a coming-to-be of b - a, absent from the antecedent state, relative to which the latter equals 0.

The query, accordingly, is this: in what manner a thing crosses over from a state = a to another = b. Two moments are always parted by a time, and the two states holding in them always by a difference possessing magnitude (since the parts of appearances are, once more, magnitudes throughout). Hence each crossing from one state to the next takes place within a time lying between two moments, the earlier settling the state the thing quits, the later the state it reaches. The pair of them, therefore, bound the time of an alteration — and so the intermediate state between the two states — and {{{ 179 }}} in that capacity form part of the alteration as a whole. Every alteration, moreover, has a cause that displays its causality across the entire time through which the alteration runs. So this cause brings forth its alteration not {{ 254 }} in a flash (all at once, in one moment) but over a span of time, such that, as the time swells from the opening moment a to its close in b, the reality's magnitude (b - a) is generated too, passing through every lesser degree lying between the first and the last. Every alteration, then, can happen solely through an unbroken action of causality, which — so far as it stays uniform — one terms a moment. Out of such moments the alteration is not composed; rather they beget it as their effect.

Here, then, we have the law governing the continuity of all alteration, whose basis is this: that neither time nor the appearance within time is built out of smallest parts, and that even so, in altering, the thing's state moves through all those parts — as elements — to its second state. It holds that there is ***no difference*** of the real within the appearance, just as no difference among the magnitudes of times is ***the smallest***; and so the fresh state of reality springs from the first — wherein that reality was lacking — across all its infinite degrees, whose mutual differences are, all told, tinier than the one between 0 and a.

Whatever service this proposition might render in the study of nature is nothing that concerns us at present. How a proposition of this sort, seemingly such an enlargement of our cognition of nature, can be possible fully _a priori_ — that emphatically calls for our scrutiny, even where the plain evidence shows it {{ 255 }} to be genuine and correct, so that one might fancy oneself let off the question of how it came to be possible. For groundless claims to enlarge our cognition by pure reason are so various that one has to adopt it as a universal principle to stay utterly wary on this head, and, lacking papers that can supply a solid deduction, to credit and take up nothing of the kind, however lucid the dogmatic proof.

Any growth in empirical cognition, any forward step of perception, is merely a further determining of inner sense — a progress in time — no matter what the objects are, appearances or pure intuitions. This progress through time settles everything while itself receiving no further determination from anything {{{ 180 }}}; its parts, that is, are given within time and by time's synthesis, never in advance of it. On that account, each passing-over in perception to a temporal successor is a determining of time through the bringing-forth of this perception; and because that time is at every point and throughout a magnitude, what we have is the bringing-forth of a perception as a magnitude, running through all degrees — none of them least — from zero up to its settled degree. Hereby the possibility now comes to light of knowing, as to their form {{ 256 }}, a law of alterations _a priori_. We do no more than forestall our own apprehending, whose formal condition, residing in us ahead of every appearance that is given, has surely to be knowable _a priori_.

And so, precisely as time holds the sensible condition _a priori_ for the possibility of an unbroken passage of the existent to what comes next, so too, through the unity of apperception, the understanding supplies the _a priori_ condition for the possibility that all the places of the appearances within this time be continuously determined — by way of the chain of causes and effects, whereby the former unfailingly drag the existence of the latter after them, and thereby lend the empirical cognition of time-relations validity for every time (universally), and hence objective validity.

[^*]: Mark this carefully: I am talking not about the alteration of certain relations in general, but about alteration of state. Thus a body in uniform motion in no way alters its state (of motion); it does so, though, when its motion waxes or wanes.
