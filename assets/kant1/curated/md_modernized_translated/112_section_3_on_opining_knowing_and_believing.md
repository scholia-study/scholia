## {{ 848 }} Third Section. On opining, knowing, and believing

Assent is an event within our understanding, one that may indeed rest on objective grounds while also requiring subjective causes in the mind of whoever is judging. Should it hold good for everyone, so far as he but possesses reason, then its ground is objectively sufficient, and the assent is at that point termed ***conviction***. {{{ 532 }}} Should its ground lie merely in the special character of the subject, it is termed ***persuasion***.

Persuasion is nothing but a semblance, since the ground of the judgment, residing purely in the subject, is held to be objective. For that reason such a judgment likewise carries only private validity, and the assent admits of no communication. Truth, by contrast, reposes on accordance with the object, in regard to which the judgments of every understanding must accordingly agree (_consentientia uni tertio consentiunt inter se_). Whether an assent amounts to conviction or to mere persuasion has, then, its outward touchstone in whether it can be shared and found valid for the reason of every human being; for at that point there is at any rate a surmise that the ground of the concurrence of all judgments, {{ 849 }} in spite of the differences among the subjects themselves, will lie in the common ground, that is, the object, with which they therefore one and all harmonize and so establish the truth of the judgment.

Persuasion, then, admittedly cannot be told apart subjectively from conviction whenever the subject keeps the assent in view merely as an appearance of its own mind; yet the trial one runs by applying the grounds of it, valid for us, to the understanding of another—to see whether upon a foreign reason they work exactly the effect they work upon ours—is nonetheless a means, subjective though it remains, not indeed of producing conviction, but of laying bare the sheer private validity of the judgment, that is, something in it that is nothing but persuasion.

If, over and above this, we are able to unfold the subjective ***causes*** of the judgment that we mistake for its objective ***grounds***, and can thereby account for the deceptive assent as an event in our mind without requiring for it the character of the object: then we strip the semblance bare and are on that account no longer taken in, though still to some degree tempted so long as the subjective cause of the semblance cleaves to our nature.

I am able to ***assert*** nothing—in other words, to enunciate as a judgment binding for everyone {{ 850 }} of necessity—save what produces conviction. A persuasion I may retain for myself if I find it agreeable, yet I can neither, nor ought I to wish to, make it count outside myself.

Assent, or the subjective validity of a judgment in relation to conviction (which holds objectively at the same time), possesses the following {{{ 533 }}} three grades: ***opining, believing***, and ***knowing***. ***Opining*** is an assent consciously deficient ***both*** subjectively ***and*** objectively. Where the latter is merely sufficient subjectively while being reckoned objectively deficient, it bears the name ***believing***. Lastly, an assent sufficient subjectively as well as objectively bears the name ***knowing***. Sufficiency on the subjective side is termed ***conviction*** (for myself), sufficiency on the objective side ***certainty*** (for everyone). I shall not linger over the clarification of concepts as easy to grasp as these.

I dare never presume to ***opine*** unless I ***know*** at least something whereby the judgment, in itself no more than problematic, gains a link with truth which, incomplete though it be, still amounts to more than a capricious fabrication. Beyond this, the law governing such a link must be certain. For if in regard to it I too have nothing beyond opinion, then the whole is but a game of imagination lacking the least bearing on truth. Within judgments drawn from pure reason there is no leave whatever to ***opine***. For inasmuch as they draw no support from grounds of experience {{ 851 }} but everything is meant to be cognized _a priori_, where all is necessary, the principle of the linkage calls for universality and necessity, and thus for entire certainty, in default of which no lead toward truth is to be had at all. Hence it is preposterous to opine in pure mathematics; one must know, or else abstain from judging altogether. Matters stand the same way with the principles of morality, since one dare not risk an action on the bare opinion that something is ***permitted***, but has to know it.

In the transcendental employment of reason, on the other hand, opining is naturally too meager, yet knowing likewise too much. With a purely speculative aim, then, we cannot judge here at all: for subjective grounds of assent, such as can bring about belief, merit no approbation in speculative questions, since they neither keep themselves clear of every empirical aid nor allow of being conveyed to others to the same degree.

It is solely within a ***practical relation***, however, that a theoretically deficient assent can ever be called belief. This practical aim is now either the aim of ***skill*** or that of ***morality***, the former bearing on arbitrary and contingent purposes, the latter, however, on purposes utterly necessary.

The moment an end is laid down, the conditions for reaching it become hypothetically necessary. This necessity is {{{ 534 }}} {{ 852 }} subjective, yet no more than comparatively sufficient, in case I am aware of no other conditions whatever under which the end might be reached; but it is unqualifiedly and universally sufficient once I know for certain that no one could be acquainted with other conditions leading to the end laid down. In the former case my supposition, together with the assent to certain conditions, is a merely contingent belief, whereas in the latter it is a necessary one. A physician confronted with a patient in danger has to do something, yet does not recognize the disease. He attends to the symptoms and concludes, for want of anything better, that it is consumption. His belief is, even by his own verdict, merely contingent; someone else might well hit the mark more accurately. Such contingent belief, which nevertheless underlies the real employment of the means toward certain actions, I call ***pragmatic belief***.

The customary touchstone for telling whether what someone maintains is bare persuasion, or at any rate subjective conviction—that is, firm belief—is ***wagering***. Not seldom a person delivers his propositions with so assured and intractable an obstinacy that he appears to have shed every worry about error. A wager pulls him up short. At times it emerges that his persuasion, while worth staking a single ducat, does not extend to ten. The one he will {{ 853 }} still hazard readily enough, but faced with ten he suddenly grows aware of what had escaped him before, namely that he may, after all, quite possibly have gone wrong. Let a person picture to himself that he is to wager the fortune of his entire life upon it, and his triumphant verdict shrinks drastically, he turns exceedingly diffident, and only then does it dawn on him that his belief does not carry so far. Pragmatic belief, accordingly, has but a degree, which—depending on how large the interest in play may be—can prove great or else small.

But since—granting that we can set nothing whatever afoot with respect to an object, so that the assent stays merely theoretical—we can nonetheless in many instances entertain and picture an enterprise for which we fancy ourselves to hold adequate grounds, supposing there were some means of settling the certainty of the thing, there arises in purely theoretical judgments an ***analogue*** of practical ones, to whose assent the word ***belief*** is suited, and which we may christen ***doctrinal belief***. Were it feasible to settle the point through any experience at all, I would readily stake everything I own upon it {{{ 535 }}} that inhabitants exist on at least one or another of the planets within our sight. For that reason I declare it to be no mere opinion but a robust belief (on whose rightness I would already hazard many of life's benefits) that dwellers in other worlds exist as well.

{{ 854 }} We must now own that the doctrine of God's existence pertains to doctrinal belief. For although, as regards the theoretical knowledge of the world, I have nothing ***to dispose of*** that would of necessity presuppose this thought as a condition of my explanations of the world's appearances, being obliged rather to employ my reason as though everything were mere nature: the purposive unity is for all that so weighty a condition of the application of reason to nature that I—experience furnishing me, besides, abundant instances of it—can by no means pass it over. For this unity, however, I am acquainted with no other condition that would render it my clue for the study of nature than to presuppose that a supreme intelligence has arranged all things after the wisest ends. It follows that presupposing a wise author of the world is a condition of an aim which, though contingent, is nonetheless far from trivial—namely, that of having a guide in the investigation of nature. The upshot of my inquiries so frequently corroborates the serviceability of this presupposition, while nothing can be brought decisively against it, that I would be saying far too little were I to call my assent a mere opining; rather, even within this theoretical bearing it may be said that I steadfastly believe in a God; yet in that case this belief, taken strictly, is nonetheless not a practical one, but has to be termed a doctrinal belief, such as the ***theology*** of nature (physico-theology) {{ 855 }} must of necessity generate at every turn. In view of that very wisdom, considering the superb outfitting of human nature together with the shortness of life so ill-fitted to it, there is likewise ample ground to be met with for a doctrinal belief in the future life of the human soul.

In such cases the profession of belief is a profession of modesty on the ***objective*** side, yet at once of steadfast confidence on the ***subjective***. Were I to call the merely theoretical assent here so much as a hypothesis that I should be entitled to adopt, I would thereby be pledging myself already to {{{ 536 }}} command a fuller concept of the makeup of a world-cause and of another world than I am truly able to set forth; for of anything I adopt even only as a hypothesis I must be acquainted at least with enough of its properties that I may fabricate ***not its concept*** but ***only its existence***. The word belief, however, bears solely upon the guidance an idea affords me and upon the subjective influence toward furthering my operations of reason, which keeps me holding fast to that idea, even though I am unable to render account of it with a speculative aim.

But merely doctrinal belief harbors something wavering in itself; one is often unseated from {{ 856 }} it by difficulties cropping up in speculation, though one unfailingly comes back to it time and again.

With ***moral belief*** the case stands quite differently. For here it is downright necessary that something come to pass, namely that I render obedience to the moral law in every particular. The end is here fixed beyond escape, and by all my insight only one solitary condition is possible under which this end hangs together with the sum of all ends and thereby holds practical validity—namely, that a God and a world to come exist; I know, moreover, with full certainty that no one is acquainted with other conditions conducting to the same unity of ends beneath the moral law. But since, then, the moral precept is at once my maxim (reason, indeed, enjoining that it be so), I shall unfailingly believe in an existence of God and in a life to come, and I rest assured that nothing can set this belief tottering, seeing that thereby my very moral principles would be toppled, principles I cannot forswear without becoming loathsome in my own eyes.

In this manner, once all the ambitious designs of a reason roving past the frontiers of every experience have been thwarted, there yet remains enough for us to have reason to rest content with it for practical purposes. Admittedly no one, to be sure, will be in a position to plume himself that he ***knows*** {{ 857 }} there to be a God and there to be a life to come; for if he knows this, he is precisely the man I have long been in search of. Every piece of knowledge (where it concerns an object of mere reason) admits of communication, and I might therefore even hope, through his tuition, to behold my own knowledge stretched to so marvelous an extent. No—the conviction is not a ***logical*** but a ***moral*** certainty, and because it reposes on subjective grounds (those of the moral cast of mind), {{{ 537 }}} I must not so much as declare: ***it is*** morally certain that a God exists, etc., but instead: ***I am*** morally certain, etc. Which is to say: my belief in a God and in another world is woven together with my moral cast of mind so tightly that, just as little as I stand in danger of forfeiting the latter, so little do I fear that the former could ever be torn from me.

The one misgiving that presents itself in all this is that this belief of reason props itself upon the presupposition of moral dispositions. Should we set this aside and take up someone utterly indifferent toward moral laws, then the question reason throws up turns into a mere problem for speculation and can at that point be shored up, to be sure, with forceful grounds drawn from analogy, yet not with such as the most obstinate craving for doubt would have to yield to.[^*] There is, however, no human being {{ 858 }} free of all interest where these questions are concerned. For though a lack of good dispositions may sever him from the moral, still even in this case enough is left over to make him ***dread*** a divine existence and a hereafter. For nothing further is called for here than that he be at the very least incapable of pretending to any ***certainty*** that ***no*** such being and ***no*** life to come are to be found—which, since it would have to be proved by pure reason and thus apodictically, would oblige him to establish the impossibility of both, a thing that assuredly no reasonable person can shoulder. This would be a ***negative*** belief, one able to bring about not indeed morality and good dispositions, but nonetheless the analogue of them—that is, forcibly to hold in check the eruption of the evil ones.

But is this the whole of it, someone will say, that pure reason brings off in throwing open vistas past the bounds of experience? no more than a pair of articles of belief? So much as this even ordinary {{{ 538 }}} {{ 859 }} understanding could well have brought off, without calling the philosophers into consultation over it!

I have no wish here to vaunt the merit that philosophy possesses, through the toilsome exertion of its critique, on behalf of human reason; suppose its issue were even found to be merely negative; for something on that head will still come up in the following section. But do you really insist that a cognition bearing on all human beings should outstrip ordinary understanding and be unveiled to you by philosophers alone? This very thing you find fault with is the finest corroboration of the soundness of the assertions advanced hitherto, inasmuch as it brings to light what could not be foreseen at the outset—namely, that nature, in matters of concern to human beings without distinction, is not to be accused of any partial parceling out of her gifts, and that the loftiest philosophy, when it comes to the essential ends of human nature, can carry things no further than the guidance she has vouchsafed even to the most ordinary understanding.

[^*]: The human mind takes (as I believe inevitably occurs in every rational being) a natural interest in morality, even if this interest is not undivided and practically preponderant. Reinforce and augment this interest, and you will find reason highly docile and itself more enlightened, apt to join the speculative interest to the practical one as well. But unless you first take care to make people good, at least halfway along, you will likewise never turn them into sincerely believing people.
