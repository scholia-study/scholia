## I. Solution of the cosmological idea of the totality of the composition of appearances of a cosmic whole.

Here, as with the remaining cosmological questions, the ground of reason's regulative principle lies in this proposition: that within the empirical regress ***no experience of an absolute limit***, and thus of no condition as one that would be ***empirically absolutely unconditioned***, can be met with. But this rests on the following: that any such experience would need to harbor within itself a bounding of the appearances through nothing, or the void, on which the prolonged regress might come to strike by way of a perception—something that cannot be.

This proposition now, which asserts neither more nor less than that in the empirical {{ 546 }} regress I reach at every step only a condition that must itself, in turn, be counted as empirically conditioned, carries the rule _in terminis_: that, no matter how far up the ascending series I may have come, I have on every occasion to inquire after some higher member of the series, whether or not this comes to be known to me through experience.

Now, to solve the first cosmological problem, nothing more is needed than to settle the following: whether, in the regress toward the cosmic whole's unconditioned magnitude (with respect to time and to space), this never-bounded ascent is to be called a ***regression into the infinite***, or merely an ***indeterminably continued regress*** (_in indefinitum_).

The bare, general representation of the whole succession of past world-states, and equally of the things that exist together in cosmic space, is nothing in itself but a possible empirical regress, one that I entertain, though as yet indeterminately, and by means of which alone the concept of such a chain of conditions running up to the given perception can first take shape {{{ 355 }}}.[^*] Now, at every point I possess the cosmic whole in concept alone, {{ 547 }} and in no way (as a whole) within intuition. Its magnitude, therefore, affords me no inference to that of the regress, such that I could fix the latter by the former; instead I have first to fashion for myself, out of the magnitude of the empirical regress, a concept of how large the world is. Yet concerning that regress I never learn more than this: that from any member already given in the series of conditions I am bound to advance, empirically, to one that lies higher (further off). Thus the magnitude of the whole of the appearances is thereby not determined absolutely at all, and accordingly one also cannot say that this regress proceeds ***into the infinite***, because this would ***anticipate*** the members which the regress has not yet reached and depict their multitude as so vast that no empirical synthesis could ever reach it, and would in consequence ***determine*** the world's magnitude ahead of the regress (if only negatively)—which cannot be. For no intuition presents the world to me (in its totality), so that even its magnitude remains, before the regress, wholly ungiven. This being so, of the world's magnitude taken in itself there is nothing whatever we may assert—not even that a _regressus in infinitum_ occurs within it; we are left only to seek the concept of that magnitude by the rule which governs the empirical regress inside it. And what that rule enjoins amounts to no more than this: that no matter how far we have pushed into the series of empirical conditions, at no point are we to posit an absolute limit, but are to place each appearance, as {{ 548 }} something conditioned, under another that serves as its condition, and so to press on toward the latter—precisely the _regressus in indefinitum_, which, since it settles no magnitude in the object, stands sufficiently apart from the _in infinitum_.

I therefore cannot say: the world is ***infinite*** with respect to past time or to space. For any such concept—magnitude taken as an infinity already given—is of empirical origin, and therefore, once the world is considered as an object of the senses, flatly impossible. Nor shall I say: the regress from a given perception {{{ 356 }}} onward to everything that this bounds within a series, in past time no less than in space, proceeds ***into the infinite***; for this presupposes the world's infinite magnitude; nor: it is ***finite***; for the absolute limit is likewise empirically impossible. It follows that I can pronounce nothing upon the object of experience in its entirety (the world of sense), but only upon the rule whereby experience, fitted to its object, ought to be set up and carried onward.

On the cosmological question of the world's magnitude, then, the first, negative answer is this: the world has no initial beginning in time and no outermost boundary as regards space.

For, were it otherwise, it would find its bounds on the one side in empty time and {{ 549 }} on the other in empty space. Now since, as appearance, it can be neither of the two in itself—for an appearance is not a thing in itself—there would have to be possible a perception of the bounding by absolutely empty time or empty space, through which these ends of the world would come to be given within a possible experience. Yet an experience of that kind, utterly void of content, is not to be had. Hence an absolute limit of the world is empirically, and consequently also utterly, impossible[^**].

There emerges from this, in the same stroke, the ***affirmative*** reply: that the regress through the series of the world's appearances, being a way of determining how large the world is, runs _in indefinitum_—which amounts to saying that the world of sense has no absolute magnitude, while the empirical regress (the sole avenue by which the world can be presented on the side of what conditions it) is governed by a rule, namely to move from any member of the series, taken as a conditioned, ever {{ 550 }} onward to one yet more distant (be it by firsthand experience, by the thread that history supplies, or by the linkage of causes with their effects), and never to hold oneself back from widening the possible empirical employment {{{ 357 }}} of the understanding—this being, indeed, the genuine and unique task of reason in dealing with its principles.

A determinate empirical regress that would run on unceasingly within some given kind of appearances is not thereby laid down—for instance, that beginning from a living human being one would have to climb ever upward through a line of ancestors without expecting a first couple, or through the series of heavenly bodies without conceding an outermost sun; rather, all that is commanded is the passage from appearances to appearances, even should these furnish no actual perception (when it is, in degree, too faint for our consciousness to amount to experience), since they belong for all that to possible experience.

All beginning is in time, and all limit of the extended is in space. But space and time have their place only within the world of sense. Hence conditionality attaches only to appearances ***in the world***, while the ***world*** itself is neither conditioned nor limited in any unconditioned fashion.

For just this reason—and because the world can at no time be had ***as a whole***, and even the series of conditions belonging to a given conditioned cannot, qua world-series, be furnished ***as a whole***—the concept of the world's magnitude is yielded only by way of the regress, and not in advance of it within a collective {{ 551 }} intuition. The former, however, resides always solely in the ***determining*** of magnitude and hence delivers no ***determinate*** concept—thus no concept, either, of a magnitude that, measured against some given standard, would be infinite; it does not, then, run into the infinite (as though this were something given), but into an indeterminate reach, in order to supply a magnitude (belonging to experience) that only through this regress attains actuality.

[^*]: Hence this world-series admits of being neither greater nor lesser than that possible empirical regress in which its concept has its sole foundation. And since this can furnish no determinate infinite, and just as little a determinately finite (something absolutely bounded): it is on this account clear that the world's magnitude can be taken by us as neither finite nor infinite, since the regress (whereby that magnitude is represented) permits neither of the two.

[^**]: One will notice: that the proof has here been conducted in an entirely different manner than the dogmatic one above in the antithesis of the first antinomy. In that place we had, in keeping with the ordinary, dogmatic manner of representation, allowed the world of sense to pass for a thing given in itself, as to its totality, ahead of any regress, and had refused it—supposing it did not fill out all time and all spaces—any determinate position whatever in the two. Hence the inference too was different from here, namely, its actual infinity was inferred.
