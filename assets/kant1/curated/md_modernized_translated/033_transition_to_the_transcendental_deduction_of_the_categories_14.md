## §14. Transition to the transcendental deduction of the categories.

There are only two cases possible in which synthetic representations and their objects can meet, relate to each other necessarily, and, as it were, encounter each other: either when {{ 125 }} the object alone makes the representation possible, or when the representation alone makes the object possible. If it is the former, then this relation is only empirical, and the representation is never possible _a priori_. And this is the case with appearances in respect of that in them which belongs to sensation. But if it is the latter, then since the representation in itself (for we are not here speaking of its causality by means of the will) does not produce its object as to its ***existence***, the representation is nevertheless determinant in respect of the object _a priori_ if and only if through it alone is it possible to cognize something ***as an object***. There are, however, two conditions under which alone the cognition of an object is possible: first, ***intuition***, through which the object is given, though only as appearance; second, the ***concept***, through which an object is thought that corresponds to this intuition. But it is clear from what has been said above that the first condition, namely that under which alone objects can be intuited, does in fact lie at the foundation of objects _a priori_ in the mind as to their form. With this formal condition of sensibility, therefore, all appearances necessarily agree, because they can appear only through it, i.e., be empirically intuited and given. Now the question arises whether concepts _a priori_ do not also precede as conditions under which alone something, even if not intuited, is nevertheless thought as an object in general; for if so, all {{ 126 }} empirical cognition of objects is necessarily in agreement with such concepts, because without their presupposition nothing is possible as an ***object of experience***. But now all experience contains, in addition to the intuition of the senses, through which something is given, a ***concept*** of an object that is given in intuition or appears: accordingly, concepts of objects in general will lie at the foundation of all empirical cognition as conditions {{{ 105 }}} _a priori_: consequently, the objective validity of the categories, as concepts _a priori_, will rest on the fact that through them alone is experience (as far as the form of thinking is concerned) possible. For then they relate necessarily and _a priori_ to objects of experience, because only by means of them can any object of experience be thought at all.

The transcendental deduction of all concepts _a priori_ has therefore a principle toward which the entire investigation must be directed, namely this: that they must be recognized as conditions _a priori_ of the possibility of experience (whether of the intuition that is met with in it, or of the thinking). Concepts that yield the objective ground of the possibility of experience are for that very reason necessary. The development of experience, however, in which they are met with, is not their deduction (but their illustration), because they would thereby be only accidental {{ 127 }} in it. Without this original relation to possible experience, in which all objects of cognition occur, their relation to any object at all could not be understood.

The famous **Locke**, from the lack of this consideration, and because he met with pure concepts of the understanding in experience, also derived them from experience, and yet proceeded so ***inconsistently*** that he dared to attempt cognitions with them that go far beyond all limits of experience. **David Hume** recognized that in order to be able to do the latter, it was necessary that these concepts must have their origin _a priori_. But since he could not explain at all how it is possible that the understanding must think concepts, which in themselves are not combined in the understanding, as necessarily combined in the object, and since it did not occur to him that perhaps the understanding, through these concepts themselves, could be the author of the experience in which its objects are encountered, he derived them, forced by necessity, from experience (namely from a subjective necessity arisen from frequent association in experience, which is ultimately falsely held to be objective, i.e., ***custom***), but subsequently proceeded very consistently in declaring it to be impossible to go beyond the boundary of experience with these concepts and the principles they occasion. {{{ 106 }}} {{ 128 }} The ***empirical*** derivation, however, on which both hit, cannot be reconciled with the reality of the scientific cognitions _a priori_ that we possess, namely ***pure mathematics*** and ***general natural science***, and is therefore refuted by the fact.

The first of these two famous men opened the gates wide to ***enthusiasm***, because reason, once it has claims on its side, no longer allows itself to be kept within bounds by indefinite praise of moderation; the second surrendered himself entirely to ***skepticism***, since he believed he had discovered so universal a delusion of our faculty of cognition, which was yet held to be reason. — We are now about to make an attempt to see whether we cannot guide human reason successfully between these two cliffs, assign to it definite limits, and yet keep the entire field of its appropriate activity open for it.

Beforehand, I will only premise the ***explanation of the categories***. They are concepts of an object in general, through which its intuition is regarded as ***determined*** in respect of one of the ***logical functions*** of judgment. Thus the function of the ***categorical*** judgment was that of the relation of the subject to the predicate, e.g., all bodies are divisible. Yet in respect of the merely logical use of the understanding, it remained undetermined which of the two concepts {{ 129 }} one wanted to give the function of the subject and which that of the predicate. For one can also say: some divisible is a body. But by the category of substance, when I bring the concept of a body under it, it is determined: that its empirical intuition in experience must always be considered only as subject, never as a mere predicate; and similarly for all the other categories.
