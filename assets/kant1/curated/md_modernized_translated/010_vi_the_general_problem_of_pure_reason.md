## {{{ 39 }}} {{ 19 }} VI. The general problem of pure reason.

One stands to gain a great deal already if a whole multitude of inquiries can be subsumed under the formula of a single task. For in this way one lightens not only one's own business, by fixing it precisely for oneself, but also, for anyone else who cares to examine it, the verdict on whether or not we have done justice to our undertaking. The genuine task of pure reason is thus comprised in this question: **How are synthetic judgments _a priori_ possible?**

The reason metaphysics has so far persisted in such a shaky state of uncertainty and contradictions lies solely in this: that no one let this task, and perhaps even the distinction between ***analytic*** and ***synthetic*** judgments, enter their thoughts sooner. It is on the resolution of this task, or on an adequate proof that the possibility whose clarification {{{ 40 }}} it demands does not in fact take place at all, that the standing and falling of metaphysics now depends. ***David Hume***, who among all philosophers approached this task most closely, still thought it through with nowhere near enough precision and never in its full generality, but stopped short at the synthetic proposition linking an effect to its causes (_Principium_ {{ 20 }} _causalitatis_), and he took himself to have established that any such proposition is wholly impossible _a priori_; on his reasoning, all that we term metaphysics would amount to nothing more than a delusion of fancied rational insight into what has really been borrowed from experience alone and has, by force of habit, acquired the semblance of necessity — a claim that demolishes all pure philosophy, one he would never have arrived at had our task in its generality stood before his eyes, for he would then have grasped that, on his own argument, there could be no pure mathematics either, since this assuredly contains synthetic propositions _a priori_, a claim from which his sound understanding would surely have saved him in that case.

The resolution of the above task also embraces, at one stroke, the possibility of putting pure reason to use in grounding and executing every science that holds a theoretical cognition _a priori_ of objects, in other words the reply to the following questions:

**How is pure mathematics possible?**

**How is pure natural science possible?**

Of these sciences, seeing that they are really given, it is surely proper to ask **how** they are possible; for that they have to be possible {{ 21 }} their actuality itself proves.[^*] As for ***metaphysics***, however, its poor progress up to now, together with the fact that of no single one presented so far can it be said, as regards its essential purpose, that it really exists, must with good reason make anyone doubt its possibility.

{{{ 41 }}} Still, this ***kind of cognition*** must in a certain sense be counted as given after all, and metaphysics, even if not as a science, is real as a natural disposition (_metaphysica naturalis_). For, propelled by a need of its own and not spurred on by any mere vanity of knowing a great deal, human reason advances irresistibly to questions of a kind that no empirical use of reason, and no principles drawn from such use, can ever answer; and so, in every human being, the moment reason widens within them as far as speculation, some metaphysics or other has existed at every period and will forever persist there. And here too the question presents itself: ***How {{ 22 }} is metaphysics possible as a natural disposition?*** in other words, out of the nature of universal human reason, how do the very questions arise that pure reason raises for itself and that its own need compels it to answer as best it can?

Since, however, every attempt made so far to answer these natural questions — for instance, whether the world had a beginning or has existed from all eternity, and the like — has invariably run into inescapable contradictions, one cannot leave things at the bare natural disposition toward metaphysics, that is, at the pure faculty of reason itself, from which indeed some metaphysics or other (be it what it may) always springs, but it must instead be possible to arrive with it at certainty, whether in knowing or in not knowing the objects, that is, either by reaching a decision upon the objects that its questions concern, or upon the power and impotence of reason to pronounce judgment on anything with regard to them, and hence either to extend our pure reason reliably, or to fix determinate and secure bounds upon it. This final question, issuing from the general task set out above, would properly run: **How is metaphysics possible as a science?**

The critique of reason thus leads in the end, of necessity, to science, whereas the dogmatic use of it without any critique leads instead to baseless {{ 23 }} assertions, against which equally plausible ones may be set, and so to ***skepticism***.

Nor need this science be of any vast, off-putting length, since it deals not with objects of reason, whose manifold is infinite, but solely with itself, with tasks that spring wholly from its own bosom and are set before it not by the nature of things distinct from it, but by its own {{{ 42 }}} nature; for once it has first fully acquainted itself with its own capacity in regard to the objects that may present themselves to it in experience, it must become an easy matter to mark out completely and reliably the compass and the boundaries of its use as ventured beyond every limit of experience.

One can therefore, and indeed must, treat all the attempts hitherto made to establish a metaphysics ***dogmatically*** as though they had never occurred; for whatever in the one or the other is analytic — mere analysis, that is, of the concepts residing _a priori_ in our reason — is not yet the goal at all, but only a groundwork for metaphysics properly so called, whose aim is to enlarge one's cognition _a priori_ synthetically, and it is useless for that aim, since it does no more than lay out what already lies in these concepts, and not how we come to have such concepts _a priori_, {{ 24 }} so that we might thereafter determine as well their legitimate use in respect of the objects of all cognition whatsoever. Little self-renunciation, moreover, is called for in surrendering all these pretensions, since reason's contradictions with itself — which cannot be denied and are, in the dogmatic procedure, unavoidable as well — long ago robbed every metaphysics to date of its standing. More firmness will be needed here, so as not to be deterred, inwardly by the difficulty and outwardly by the resistance, from at last bringing a science indispensable to human reason, one whose every upshooting stem may indeed be lopped off but whose root cannot be eradicated, through a treatment wholly opposed to the one pursued so far, into a thriving and fruitful growth.

[^*]: As for pure natural science, some might still cast doubt on this latter point. One need only consult the various propositions found at the very outset of physics proper (empirical physics) — such as that concerning the permanence of one and the same quantity of matter, that concerning inertia, the equality of action and counteraction, and the like: one is soon persuaded that they make up a _Physicam puram_ (or _rationalem_) which fully merits being presented in isolation, as a distinct science, throughout its whole extent, be that extent narrow or wide.
