## {{{ 39 }}} {{ 19 }} VI. The general problem of pure reason.

One already gains a great deal if one can bring a multitude of investigations under the formula of a single problem. For thereby one not only lightens one's own business, by determining it exactly for oneself, but also the judgment of anyone else who wants to examine it, as to whether we have satisfied our plan or not. The proper problem of pure reason is now contained in the question: **How are synthetic judgments _a priori_ possible?**

That metaphysics has hitherto remained in so vacillating a state of uncertainty and contradictions is to be ascribed merely to the cause that one did not earlier let this problem, and perhaps even the distinction between ***analytic*** and ***synthetic*** judgments, come to mind. On the solution of this problem, or on a satisfactory proof that the possibility which it requires to be explained {{{ 40 }}} in fact does not occur at all, now rests the standing and falling of metaphysics. ***David Hume***, who among all philosophers came closest to this problem, but was far from conceiving of it determinately enough and in its universality, but merely stopped at the synthetic proposition of the connection of the effect with its causes (_Principium_ {{ 20 }} _causalitatis_), believed he had brought out that such an _a priori_ proposition is entirely impossible, and according to his inferences everything that we call metaphysics would come down to a mere delusion of an alleged rational insight into that which is in fact merely borrowed from experience and has through habit taken on the appearance of necessity; a claim destructive of all pure philosophy upon which he would never have hit if he had had our problem in its universality before his eyes, since he would then have comprehended that according to his argument there could also be no pure mathematics, because this certainly contains synthetic propositions _a priori_, a claim from which his good understanding would then surely have protected him.

In the solution of the above problem there is at the same time included the possibility of the pure use of reason in the foundation and execution of all sciences that contain a theoretical cognition _a priori_ of objects, i.e., the answering of the questions:

**How is pure mathematics possible?**

**How is pure natural science possible?**

Of these sciences, since they are actually given, it can now fittingly be asked: **how** they are possible; for that they must be possible {{ 21 }} is proved by their actuality.[^*] As far as ***metaphysics*** is concerned, however, its poor progress hitherto, and the fact that of not a single one thus far presented can it be said, as regards its essential purpose, that it is actually present, must leave everyone with grounds to doubt its possibility.

{{{ 41 }}} Now, however, this ***kind of cognition*** is in a certain sense nevertheless also to be regarded as given, and metaphysics is actual, even if not as a science, yet as a natural predisposition (_metaphysica naturalis_). For human reason, without being moved to it by mere vanity of much knowledge, driven by its own need, proceeds unstoppably to such questions as cannot be answered by any experiential use of reason and principles borrowed thence; and thus there has actually always been some metaphysics in all human beings, as soon as reason extends itself in them to speculation, and it will also always remain therein. And now the question concerning this too is: ***How {{ 22 }} is metaphysics as a natural predisposition possible?*** i.e., how do the questions that pure reason raises for itself, and which it is driven by its own need to answer as well as it can, arise from the nature of universal human reason?

But since in all previous attempts to answer these natural questions, e.g., whether the world has a beginning, or is from eternity, etc., unavoidable contradictions have always been found, one cannot let it rest at the mere natural predisposition to metaphysics, i.e., the pure faculty of reason itself, out of which indeed some metaphysics (be it what it may) always grows, but rather it must be possible to bring it to certainty, either in the knowledge or the non-knowledge of the objects, i.e., either in the decision regarding the objects of its questions, or regarding the capacity and incapacity of reason to judge anything in respect of them, thus either to extend our pure reason with reliability, or to set determinate and secure limits for it. This last question, which flows from the above general problem, would rightly be this: **How is metaphysics as a science possible?**

The critique of reason therefore leads finally necessarily to science; the dogmatic use of it without critique, on the contrary, leads to groundless {{ 23 }} assertions, against which one can oppose equally plausible ones, and hence to ***skepticism***.

Nor can this science be of great, daunting prolixity, because it has to deal not with objects of reason, the manifoldness of which is infinite, but merely with itself, with problems that spring entirely from its own womb and are not proposed to it by the nature of things that are distinct from it, but by its own {{{ 42 }}} nature; since it must then become easy, once it has completely come to know its own faculty in respect of the objects that may present themselves to it in experience, to determine completely and securely the extent and the limits of its attempted use beyond all boundaries of experience.

One can therefore and must regard all attempts made thus far to bring about a metaphysics ***dogmatically*** as undone; for what is analytic in one or the other, namely mere analysis of the concepts that reside _a priori_ in our reason, is not yet at all the purpose, but only a preparation for metaphysics proper, namely to extend its _a priori_ cognition synthetically, and it is unsuited for this, because it merely shows what is contained in these concepts, but not how we arrive at such concepts _a priori_ {{ 24 }}, in order to then also be able to determine their valid use in respect of the objects of all cognition in general. It also requires only little self-denial to give up all these claims, since the undeniable contradictions of reason with itself, which are also inevitable in the dogmatic procedure, have long since stripped every previous metaphysics of its reputation. More steadfastness will be needed not to let oneself be deterred by the difficulty within and the resistance without, from at last promoting to a prosperous and fruitful growth, through another treatment entirely opposed to the previous one, a science indispensable to human reason, from which one can indeed chop off every sprouted stem, but whose root one cannot eradicate.

[^*]: Some might still doubt this latter point concerning pure natural science. But one need only look at the various propositions that occur at the beginning of proper (empirical) physics, such as that of the permanence of the same quantity of matter, of inertia, of the equality of action and reaction, etc.: then one will soon be convinced that they constitute a _physica pura_ (or _rationalis_), which well deserves to be established separately as its own science in its narrow or wide, but yet complete extent.
