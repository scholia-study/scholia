## {{{ 146 }}} Section 3. Systematic representation of all synthetic principles of pure understanding

That principles occur anywhere at all is to be ascribed solely to the pure understanding, which is not only the faculty of rules {{ 198 }} in respect of that which happens, but is itself the source of principles, in accordance with which everything (which can only come before us as an object) necessarily stands under rules, because without such, cognition of an object corresponding to them could never belong to appearances. Even laws of nature, when they are considered as principles of the empirical use of understanding, carry with them an expression of necessity, and therefore at least the presumption of a determination from grounds that are valid _a priori_ and prior to all experience. But without distinction, all laws of nature stand under higher principles of the understanding, since they only apply these to particular cases of appearance. These alone therefore yield the concept which contains the condition and, as it were, the exponent for a rule in general, while experience yields the case that stands under the rule.

That one should take merely empirical principles for principles of the pure understanding, or vice versa, of this there can indeed properly be no danger; for the necessity according to concepts that characterizes the latter, and the lack of which in every empirical proposition, however universally it may hold, is easily perceived, can easily prevent this confusion. But there are pure principles _a priori_ which I would nevertheless not uniquely attribute to the pure understanding, because they are drawn not from {{ 199 }} pure concepts, but from pure intuitions (albeit by means of the understanding); the understanding, however, is the faculty of concepts. Mathematics has such principles, but their application to experience, hence their objective validity, indeed the possibility of such synthetic _a priori_ cognition (the deduction of the same), nevertheless always rests on the pure understanding.

Therefore, I shall not include among my principles those of mathematics, but indeed those upon which the possibility {{{ 147 }}} and objective validity _a priori_ of the latter are grounded, which are consequently to be regarded as principles of these principles, and which proceed ***from concepts*** to intuition, but not ***from intuition*** to concepts.

In the application of pure concepts of the understanding to possible experience, the use of their synthesis is either ***mathematical*** or ***dynamical:*** for it is directed partly merely to the ***intuition,*** and partly to the ***existence*** of an appearance in general. But the conditions _a priori_ of intuition are thoroughly necessary in respect of a possible experience, while those of the existence of the objects of a possible empirical intuition are in themselves only contingent. Therefore the principles of the mathematical use will be unconditionally necessary, i.e., apodictic, but those of the dynamical use, to be sure, will also carry with them the character of an _a priori_ necessity, but only under the condition of empirical thinking in an experience, {{ 200 }} hence only mediately and indirectly, and consequently they will not contain that immediate evidence (though without prejudice to their certainty, which is universally related to experience) that is peculiar to the former. Yet this will be better judged at the conclusion of this system of principles.

These titles I have chosen with caution, in order not to leave unnoticed the differences in respect of the evidence and the exercise of these principles {{{ 148 }}}. But it will soon be shown: that, as regards both {{ 201 }} the evidence and the determination of appearances _a priori_ according to the categories of ***magnitude*** and ***quality*** (if one attends merely to the form of the latter), their principles differ notably from the two others, in that the former are capable of an intuitive, the latter of a merely discursive, though in both cases capable of a complete certainty. I shall therefore call the former the ***mathematical,*** the latter the ***dynamical*** principles.[^*] But it will be well noted: that I here {{ 202 }} have in view as little the principles of mathematics in the one case as the principles of general (physical) dynamics in the other, but rather only those of the pure understanding in relation to the inner sense (without distinction of the representations given in it), through which indeed the former altogether obtain their possibility. I therefore name them more in regard to their application than on account of their content, and I now proceed to the consideration of them in the very same order in which they are presented in the table.

[^*]: All ***combination*** (_conjunctio_) is either ***composition*** (_compositio_) or ***connection*** (_nexus_). The former is the synthesis of the manifold of what does ***not necessarily*** belong to ***one another***, as, e.g., the two triangles, in which a square is divided by the diagonal, do not in themselves necessarily belong to one another; and of this kind is the synthesis of the ***homogeneous*** in everything that can be ***mathematically*** considered (which synthesis can in turn be divided into that of ***aggregation*** and ***coalition***, of which the former is directed to ***extensive,*** the other to ***intensive*** magnitudes). The second combination (_nexus_) is the synthesis of the manifold insofar as it ***necessarily*** belongs to ***one another***, as, e.g., the accident to some substance, or the effect to the cause —, hence represented as connected _a priori_ even though as ***heterogeneous***; which combination, because it is not arbitrary, I therefore call ***dynamical***, because it concerns the combination of the ***existence*** of the manifold (which can in turn be divided into the ***physical***, of appearances among one another, and the ***metaphysical***, their combination in the faculty of cognition _a priori_).
