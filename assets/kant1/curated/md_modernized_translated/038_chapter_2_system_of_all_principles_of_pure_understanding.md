## {{{ 140 }}} Chapter 2. System of all principles of pure understanding

We have in the preceding chapter considered the transcendental power of judgment only in accordance with the general conditions under which alone it is authorized to employ the pure concepts of the understanding for synthetic judgments. Now our business is: to exhibit in systematic connection the judgments that the understanding actually brings about _a priori_ under this critical caution, for which our table of categories must without doubt provide the natural and secure guidance. For it is precisely these whose relation to possible experience must constitute all pure cognition of the understanding _a priori_, and whose relation to sensibility in general {{ 188 }} will for that reason exhibit all transcendental principles of the use of the understanding completely and in a system.

Principles _a priori_ bear this name not merely because they contain in themselves the grounds of other judgments, but also because they themselves are not grounded in higher and more general cognitions. This property, however, does not always exempt them from a proof. For although this could not be conducted further objectively, but rather lies at the ground of all cognition of its object, yet this does not prevent a proof from the subjective sources of the possibility of a cognition of the object in general from being possible, indeed even necessary, since the proposition would otherwise nevertheless carry the greatest suspicion of a merely surreptitious assertion.

Secondly, we will limit ourselves merely to those principles that relate to the categories. The principles of the transcendental Aesthetic, according to which space and time are the conditions of the possibility of all things as appearances, likewise the restriction of these principles, namely, that they cannot be related to things in themselves, do not therefore belong in our staked-out field of investigation. Likewise, mathematical principles constitute no part of this system, because they are drawn only from intuition, and not from the {{{ 141 }}} {{ 189 }} pure concept of the understanding; yet their possibility, since they are nevertheless synthetic judgments _a priori_, will necessarily find a place here, not indeed to prove their correctness and apodictic certainty, which they do not need at all, but only to make the possibility of such evident cognitions _a priori_ comprehensible and to deduce it.

But we shall also have to speak of the principle of analytic judgments, and this indeed in contrast with that of synthetic judgments, as those with which we are properly occupied, because this very contrast frees the theory of the latter from all misunderstanding and places it clearly before our eyes in its peculiar nature.
