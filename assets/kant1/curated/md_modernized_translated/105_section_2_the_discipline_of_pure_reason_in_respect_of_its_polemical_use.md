## {{{ 484 }}} Section 2. The discipline of pure reason in respect of its polemical use

Reason must submit itself to criticism in all of its undertakings and cannot curtail the freedom of criticism through any prohibition without harming itself and drawing upon itself a suspicion detrimental to it. Now there is nothing so important with respect to utility, nothing so sacred, that it might exempt itself from this testing and surveying examination, which respects no person. Upon this freedom rests the very existence of reason, which wields no dictatorial authority, but whose verdict is on every occasion nothing more than the concurrence of free citizens {{ 767 }}, every one of whom must be free to utter his misgivings—indeed his _veto_—without any reserve.

But even though reason can never ***refuse*** itself to criticism, it does not at all times have cause to ***dread*** it. But pure reason, in its dogmatic (as against mathematical) employment, is not so assured of scrupulously observing its highest laws that it could avoid presenting itself with diffidence—nay, with the wholesale surrender of every pretended dogmatic authority—before the scrutinizing gaze of a loftier, judicial reason.

Matters stand quite otherwise when its business is not with the judge's censure but with the pretensions of a fellow citizen, against which it has merely to defend itself. For since these wish to be every bit as dogmatic, albeit in denying, as the former in affirming: there arises a justification _κατ' ἄνθρωπον_ that safeguards against every encroachment and yields a titled holding, one that need fear no alien presumptions, notwithstanding that it admits of no adequate proof _κατ' ἀλήθειαν_ on its own account.

What I mean by reason's polemical employment is the vindication of its propositions in the face of the dogmatic denials brought against them. What is at stake here is not whether its claims might, for all that, turn out false, but solely that nobody could at any time uphold the {{ 768 }} contrary with apodictic certainty (or even with any stronger show of plausibility). For we do not then hold our possession merely on sufferance {{{ 485 }}}, if we have before us a title to it, though not a sufficient one, and it stands wholly assured that at no time could anyone establish the unlawfulness of this holding.

There is something disheartening, something that weighs one down, in the very fact that an antithetic of pure reason should exist to begin with, and that reason—which is, after all, the highest tribunal over every dispute—should come to blows with its own self. True, we had before us above just such a seeming antithetic of it; but it emerged that this rested upon a confusion—for, following the ordinary prejudice, one had taken appearances to be things in themselves and had then required an absolute completeness of their synthesis in this manner or that (though it was, on either manner, equally impossible), a completeness that can in no wise be looked for from appearances. So at that time there existed no genuine ***contradiction of reason*** with its own self in these propositions: that a series of appearances ***given in themselves*** possesses an absolutely first commencement, and, conversely: that such a series is, plainly and ***in itself***, devoid of any commencement whatever; for the two claims are perfectly compatible, since ***appearances***, regarded in respect of their existence (as appearances), amount ***in themselves*** to nothing whatsoever—that is, to something self-contradictory—so that positing them must, in the nature of the case, drag contradictory inferences in its train.

But no such confusion can be pleaded, and thereby {{ 769 }} the conflict of reason settled, if, for instance, one were to assert on the theist's part: ***there is a highest being***, and on the atheist's, to the contrary: ***there is no highest being***; or in psychology: whatever thinks is of an absolute and abiding unity and so is marked off from all perishable material unity, against which another might set: the soul is not an immaterial unity and cannot be spared from perishing. For in this case the object under question is clear of everything alien that would conflict with its nature, and the understanding is here concerned with ***things in themselves*** alone, and not with appearances. Here, then, a true conflict would indeed be met with, if only pure reason on the negating side had something to say that came close to the ground of an assertion; for as regards the critique of the proofs of the one who dogmatically affirms, one may well grant it to him without on that account giving up these propositions, which at least {{{ 486 }}} have the interest of reason on their side, something the opponent cannot appeal to at all.

I do not, to be sure, share the opinion that outstanding and thoughtful men (Sulzer, for one) have so frequently voiced whenever they sensed the frailty of the proofs offered up to now: that one may hope some day yet to hit upon self-evident demonstrations for the pair of cardinal tenets of our pure {{ 770 }} reason—that a God exists, that a life to come awaits. On the contrary, I am certain that it never will. For where is reason to find the ground for synthetic claims of that sort, claims bearing on no objects of experience nor on their intrinsic possibility? Yet it is likewise certain, with apodictic force, that no man shall ever step forth capable of maintaining the ***opposite*** with even the faintest semblance, still less dogmatically. For seeing that he could make it out through pure reason alone, he would have to set about proving: that a highest being, that the subject thinking within us as pure intelligence, is ***impossible***. But from what quarter is he to draw the cognitions that would license him to judge so synthetically about things lying past all possible experience? We may accordingly rest wholly unperturbed that anyone will one day prove the contrary to us, and on that score have no call to devise proofs framed by the rules of the schools, being free rather to embrace those propositions that sit quite comfortably alongside our reason's speculative concern in its empirical employment and that furnish, besides, the one and only avenue for reconciling it with the practical concern. For the opponent (who here must not be regarded merely as a critic) we have our _non liquet_ in readiness, which must infallibly disconcert him, while we do not refuse the retortion of it upon ourselves, since we constantly hold in reserve the subjective {{ 771 }} maxim of reason, which the opponent necessarily lacks, and under whose protection we can look upon all his blows in the air with calm and indifference.

So construed, there is properly no antithetic of pure reason whatever. For its one and only battlefield would have to be looked for within pure theology and pure psychology; yet that terrain sustains no fighter clad in full armor and bearing weapons one would have cause to dread. He can come forward only with mockery or braggadocio, which can be laughed at as child's play. This is a comforting remark that gives reason fresh courage; for on what else {{{ 487 }}} would it rely, if it, which alone is called to remove all errors, were itself in disarray, without being able to hope for peace and quiet possession?

Everything that nature itself ordains is good for some purpose. Even poisons serve to overpower other poisons that are generated in our own humors, and hence may not be missing from a complete collection of remedies (_Officin_). The objections against the persuasions and self-conceit of our merely speculative reason are themselves set as tasks by the nature of this reason, and must therefore have their good determination and purpose, which one must not cast to the winds. Why has providence placed some objects, even though they are bound up with our highest interest, so high above us that we are permitted almost only to encounter them in an indistinct and {{ 772 }} self-doubted perception, whereby prying glances are more provoked than satisfied? Whether it be useful to venture bold determinations with respect to such prospects is at least doubtful, perhaps even harmful. But always and without any doubt it is useful to set the inquiring as well as the examining reason into complete freedom, so that it may attend to its own interest unhindered, an interest that is promoted just as much by its setting bounds to its insights as by its extending them, and that always suffers when foreign hands interfere in order to steer it, against its natural course, toward forced purposes.

Let your opponent, then, only show reason, and combat him solely with the weapons of reason. For the rest, be untroubled about the good cause (the practical interest), for it is never brought into the game in a purely speculative contest. The quarrel then brings to light no more than a certain antinomy of reason which, resting as it does on reason's own nature, must of necessity be heard out and scrutinized. It cultivates that reason by weighing its object from two sides, and rectifies its judgment by hemming it in. What comes into contention here is not the ***matter***, but the ***tone***. For enough is left you still to speak that language, vindicated in the face of the keenest reason, of a steadfast ***belief***, though you have been forced to relinquish {{ 773 }} the language of ***knowledge***.

{{{ 488 }}} Were one to put the question to the cool-headed David Hume, fashioned as he genuinely was for a balanced judgment: what induced you to sap, by means of painstakingly excogitated scruples, the persuasion so consoling and serviceable to man—that its rational insight reaches as far as the assertion and the determinate concept of a highest being?, he would reply: nothing beyond the design of carrying reason further in its self-knowledge, coupled with a certain resentment at the constraint one would fain lay upon reason, in that one makes much of it while at the same time barring it from a frank avowal of the weaknesses that come to light for it in the examination of itself. Turn instead to Priestley—a man given over wholly to the principles that govern the ***empirical*** exercise of reason and disinclined toward any transcendent speculation—and ask what motive forces prompted him to pull down the freedom and immortality of our soul (with him the hope of a life hereafter amounts only to the anticipation of a miraculous re-awakening), two such load-bearing columns of all religion, he who is himself a devout and ardent teacher of religion: he could return no other answer than this—the interest of reason, which suffers loss when one seeks to withdraw certain objects from the laws of material nature, the sole laws that {{ 774 }} we are able to know and determine with precision. It would look unjust to cry down the latter—who contrives to square his paradoxical thesis with the ends of religion—and to wound a well-meaning man because he can no longer orient himself the moment he has strayed beyond the field of natural science. But this indulgence must fall no less to the equally well-disposed Hume, faultless as regards his moral character, who is unable to forsake his abstracted speculation, persuaded on good grounds that its object resides entirely beyond the confines of natural science, within the realm of pure ideas.

What, then, ought to be done in this situation, above all in view of the peril that appears to menace the public welfare as a result of it? There is nothing more natural, nothing more equitable, than the course you are obliged to adopt in the matter. Just let these people go about it; if they show talent, if they show deep and novel inquiry, in a word, if they only show reason, then reason wins every time. If you resort to other means than those of an unconstrained reason, if you cry high treason and call together the common public, which understands nothing of such subtle elaborations, {{{ 489 }}} as it were to help put out a fire, you make yourselves ridiculous. For it is by no means a question of what here works to the advantage or disadvantage of the common good, but only of how far reason may advance in its speculating that abstracts from every interest, {{ 775 }} and whether anything at all is to be reckoned upon here, or whether it must sooner be surrendered outright to the practical. Instead, therefore, of striking in with the sword, look on rather at this dispute calmly from the secure seat of criticism, a dispute that must turn out laborious for the combatants, entertaining for you, and—given a certainly bloodless outcome—profitable for your insights. For there is something thoroughly preposterous in demanding illumination of reason while dictating to it in advance the side on which its verdict must inevitably fall. Besides, reason is already of its own accord so thoroughly curbed and kept in check by reason that you have not the slightest need to muster armed patrols in order to mount civic resistance against whichever party's alarming preponderance strikes you as perilous. Within this dialectic no triumph arises over which you would have any reason to feel disquiet.

Reason, moreover, is greatly in need of such a dispute, and it would be desirable that it had been conducted earlier and with unrestricted public permission. For all the sooner would a mature critique have come about, upon whose appearance all these quarrels must fall away of themselves, since the disputants would learn to see through the delusion and prejudices that had set them at odds.

There is a certain impurity in human nature that in the end, like everything that comes from nature, must after all contain a predisposition to good {{ 776 }} ends, namely an inclination to conceal one's true dispositions and to put on display certain assumed ones that one holds to be good and creditable. It is quite certain that human beings, through this propensity both to conceal themselves and to assume an appearance advantageous to them, have not merely ***civilized*** themselves, but gradually, to a certain degree, ***moralized*** themselves, because no one could penetrate the veneer of decency, respectability, and propriety, and thereby discovered, in the ostensibly authentic instances of the good he beheld about him, a schooling toward his own betterment. But this predisposition to represent oneself as better than one is, and to express dispositions one does not have, serves only, as it were, ***provisionally*** to bring man out of crudeness and at least to let him first assume the ***manner*** of the good that he knows; {{{ 490 }}} for afterward, once the genuine principles have been developed and have passed into one's way of thinking, that falsehood must gradually be vigorously combated, because otherwise it corrupts the heart and does not let good dispositions come up under the choking weed of fair appearance.

It grieves me to detect this identical impurity, dissembling, and hypocrisy even within the pronouncements of the speculative cast of mind, in which, after all, men meet with far fewer impediments, and {{ 777 }} no gain at all, in disclosing the avowal of their thoughts, as fairness would have them do, in an open and unconcealed way. For what can be more detrimental to insights than to communicate to one another even mere thoughts in falsified form, to conceal doubts that we feel against our own assertions, or to give an air of evidence to proofs that do not satisfy even ourselves? As long, however, as it is merely private vanity that instigates these secret intrigues (which is commonly the case in speculative judgments that have no special interest and are not easily capable of apodictic certainty), the vanity of others resists it ***with public sanction***, and things come at last to where the purest disposition and sincerity would have brought them, though far earlier. But where the common public supposes that subtle reasoners are bent on nothing short of unsettling the very base of public welfare, there it appears not merely in keeping with prudence but even sanctioned, indeed positively praiseworthy, to prop up the good cause by means of specious grounds rather than to concede to its putative adversaries so much as the advantage of pitching our tone down to the moderation of a merely practical conviction and forcing us to own the want of speculative and apodictic certainty. Meanwhile I should think that with the intention of upholding a good cause nothing in the world could well be worse combined than underhandedness, dissimulation, and deceit. That, in weighing up {{ 778 }} the reasons belonging to a bare speculation, all should go on honestly is assuredly the least one is entitled to ask. Yet could one but reckon with assurance on even this little, then the strife of speculative reason touching those weighty questions concerning God, the immortality of the soul, and freedom would long ago have been decided, or {{{ 491 }}} would reach its close before long. So it is that uprightness of disposition frequently runs contrary to how good the cause itself may be, and such a cause has, it may be, more candid and honest adversaries than champions.

I therefore presuppose readers who have no wish to see a just cause upheld by unjust means. With respect to them it is now settled that, on our principles of criticism, provided one attends not to what actually occurs but to what ought in fairness to occur, there can properly be no polemic of pure reason at all. For how are two persons to carry on a quarrel over a thing whose reality neither can exhibit in any actual, or even so much as possible, experience, over whose idea each broods in solitude so as to extract from it something ***more*** than mere idea—to wit, the very actuality of the object? By what means do they intend to extricate themselves from the dispute, given that neither is able to render his own case straightforwardly intelligible and certain, and each is confined to assailing and rebutting what the other maintains? For such is the destiny that befalls every assertion of pure reason: reaching past the {{ 779 }} conditions of any possible experience—outside which no attestation of truth is anywhere to be encountered—while yet being obliged to employ those laws of the understanding earmarked solely for empirical application, laws without which not a single stride in synthetic thought is feasible, these assertions perpetually offer their adversary a weak flank, and each turns the other's exposed side to his own account.

One may look upon the critique of pure reason as the genuine tribunal for all disputes belonging to it; for the critique is not itself caught up in those latter disputes, which bear directly upon objects, but is set in place to fix and pass judgment on the rights of reason as such, in keeping with the founding principles of its first establishment.

Deprived of it, reason stands, so to say, in a natural condition, and has no means of pressing or safeguarding its claims and pretensions save through ***war***. Criticism, on the contrary, which takes all its rulings from the founding statutes of its own establishment, whose authority none can call in question, secures for us the tranquillity of a lawful state, wherein our disputes are to be pursued in no other manner than by ***litigation***. That which puts an end to the feuds in the former condition is a ***victory*** boasted of by both parties, upon which there commonly ensues but an {{ 780 }} unstable peace, imposed by the magistrate who interposes himself {{{ 492 }}} between them, whereas in the latter it is the ***sentence*** which, striking here at the very wellspring of the disputes, is bound to afford a peace that lasts forever. The interminable quarrels of a purely dogmatic reason likewise drive one at last to seek repose in some critique of this very reason and in a body of legislation founded upon it; exactly as Hobbes contends: that the natural condition is one of wrong and violence, which we are bound of necessity to quit so as to place ourselves under a lawful constraint that alone confines our freedom in such fashion that it may stand together with the freedom of every other and, by that very means, with the common good.

To this freedom there also belongs that of publicly exhibiting for judgment one's thoughts and one's doubts, which one cannot resolve oneself, without being decried on that account as a restless and dangerous citizen. This is already contained in the primordial entitlement of human reason, which acknowledges no arbiter save the common reason of mankind at large, wherein each person holds a vote; and since every betterment our condition admits of must issue from this source, an entitlement of the kind is sacred and must not be pared down. Then, too, it is most imprudent to raise the cry of danger {{ 781 }} over certain bold claims or brazen assaults directed at those who already enjoy the concurrence of the largest and worthiest portion of the community; for to do so is to lend them a weight they ought by no means to possess. Should word reach me that some extraordinary intellect claims to have argued out of existence the freedom of the human will, the prospect of a life to come, and God's existence, I grow keen to open the book, since I count on his gifts to carry my own insights further. Yet this much I hold beforehand with complete assurance, that he will have achieved none of it; not that I fancy myself already holding unassailable proofs of these weighty propositions, but because transcendental critique, having laid bare to me the whole inventory belonging to our pure reason, has thoroughly persuaded me that, precisely as reason proves wholly unequal to affirmative claims in this domain, so it will command as little—indeed still less—wherewith to advance any negative claim about these questions. For whence is the self-styled freethinker to procure his cognition that, say, no highest being exists? Such a claim falls beyond the province of any possible experience, and hence past the frontier of everything human insight can reach as well. As for the dogmatic {{{ 493 }}} champion of the good cause who takes the field against this foe, I would decline to read him altogether, aware ahead of time that he assails his rival's spurious grounds merely to clear a passage for his own—and besides, a commonplace illusion yields nowhere near the stuff for fresh remarks that a strange and cleverly devised one supplies. The religious antagonist, on the other hand, dogmatic after his own fashion, would afford my critique the {{ 782 }} very occupation it could wish for, and an occasion to correct its principles yet further, with nothing whatever to be dreaded on his behalf.

But the young, who are consigned to academic instruction, ought at the very least to be cautioned against writings of this sort and held back from an early familiarity with such perilous propositions, before their faculty of judgment has ripened—or rather, before the doctrine one means to plant in them has struck firm root—so that it may stoutly withstand every inducement to the reverse, from whatever quarter it may issue?

If matters had to remain with the dogmatic procedure in affairs of pure reason, and the dispatching of opponents had to be genuinely polemical, i.e., so constituted that one entered into combat and armed oneself with proofs for opposed assertions, then to be sure nothing would be more advisable ***for the moment***, while at the same time nothing would prove emptier or more barren ***in the long run***, than to set youth's reason under wardship for a season and to keep it, at least that long, safe from seduction. But if subsequently either curiosity or the fashionable tone of the age plays such writings into their hands: will that youthful persuasion then still hold up? One who carries nothing but dogmatic arms to fend off his opponent's assaults, and has no idea how to unfold the {{ 783 }} concealed dialectic residing every bit as much in his own bosom as in his rival's, watches specious grounds that boast the charm of novelty rise up against specious grounds that possess it no longer, and that rather awaken the suspicion of a youthful credulity turned to abuse. He fancies there is no surer way to prove himself grown past the nursery's tutelage than to override those kindly meant warnings; and, inured to dogmatism, he swallows in deep draughts the poison that dogmatically ruins his principles.

Precisely the reverse of what is counseled here ought to take place in academic teaching, though naturally only where a searching grounding in the critique of pure reason has first been laid down. {{{ 494 }}} For if one is to set its principles to work as early as may be and to exhibit their adequacy against the most formidable dialectical semblance, it is altogether requisite to aim the assaults so dreadful to the dogmatist at his own reason—feeble as yet, but illuminated by critique—and to let him try his hand at testing the adversary's baseless claims one by one against those principles. It can be no difficulty at all for him to dissolve them into sheer vapor, and thus he feels early on his own power, against such harmful illusions, which in the end must lose all semblance for him, {{ 784 }} to secure himself completely. Now although the very same blows that strike down the enemy's edifice must be just as ruinous to his own speculative construction, should he perhaps think to erect one: yet he is entirely untroubled about this, since he has no need at all to dwell in it, but still has before him a prospect into the practical field, where he can with good ground look for firmer soil upon which to raise his reasonable and wholesome system.

And so, in the end, no genuine polemic exists within the field of pure reason. The two sides alike are shadow-fencers grappling with their own shadows, for they venture past nature, where nothing lies ready for their dogmatic clutch that could be grasped and held fast. They may fight to their heart's content; the shades they cleave asunder knit themselves whole again in a moment, after the manner of Valhalla's heroes, ready to divert themselves afresh in bloodless bouts.

But there is also no admissible skeptical use of pure reason, which one might call the principle of ***neutrality*** in all its disputes. To set reason at odds with its own self, to furnish it with arms on either side, and thereupon to watch its fiercest clash with unruffled derision, makes a poor showing when viewed dogmatically, and carries about it the look of a gloating and malignant temper. If, however, one considers the invincible delusion and the boasting of the sophists, who will {{ 785 }} let themselves be moderated by no criticism, then there really is no other counsel than to set against the braggadocio on the one side another that rests on the very same rights, so that reason may at least be given pause by the resistance of an enemy, in order to cast some doubt on its pretensions and to give criticism a hearing. But to let matters rest entirely with these doubts and {{{ 495 }}} to make it one's aim to recommend the conviction and confession of one's ignorance not merely as a remedy against dogmatic self-conceit, but at the same time as the way to bring the conflict of reason with itself to an end, is an entirely futile design and can by no means be suited to procuring for reason a state of repose, but is at most only a means of awakening it from its sweet dogmatic dream in order to draw its condition into more careful examination. Since, however, this skeptical manner of extricating oneself from a vexatious affair of reason seems to be, as it were, the short road to attaining a lasting philosophical repose, or at any rate the highway that those gladly take who imagine they lend themselves a philosophical air through a scornful disdain for all investigations of this order, I judge it needful to set out this manner of thought in the light peculiar to it.
