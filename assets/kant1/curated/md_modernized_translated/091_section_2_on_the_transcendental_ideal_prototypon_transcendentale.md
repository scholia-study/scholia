## Section 2. On the transcendental ideal (Prototypon transcendentale).

As regards whatever it does not itself contain, every ***concept*** remains undetermined, and it falls under the principle of ***determinability***: namely, that of ***any two*** predicates contradictorily opposed to each other, only one can attach to it—a principle that follows from the law of contradiction and is therefore purely logical, one that sets aside all content of cognition and attends to nothing beyond the logical form of that cognition.

Every ***thing***, however, considered in respect of its possibility, is subject besides to the principle of ***exhaustive determination***, whereby, among ***all possible predicates of things***—so far as these are set against their contraries {{ 600 }}—one must attach to it. This does not rest on the law of contradiction alone; for, beyond the relation between two conflicting predicates, it regards each thing further in relation to ***the whole of possibility***, taken as the aggregate of all predicates of things as such, and, since it presupposes this as an _a priori_ condition, it exhibits every thing as drawing its own possibility from the share {{{ 386 }}} it holds in that entire possibility.[^*] The principle of exhaustive determination therefore bears on the content, not on the logical form alone. It is the principle governing the synthesis of all those predicates that are to constitute a thing's complete concept, rather than the merely analytic representation given through one of a pair of opposed {{ 601 }} predicates; and it carries a transcendental presupposition with it, namely that of the ***matter for all possibility***, which is to hold _a priori_ the data for each thing's ***particular*** possibility.

The proposition ***everything existing is exhaustively determined*** asserts not only that, of every pair of ***given*** predicates opposed to one another, but also of all ***possible*** predicates, one always attaches to it; through this proposition the predicates are not just set in logical relation among themselves, but the thing itself is placed in transcendental comparison with the aggregate of all possible predicates. It means as much as this: to have complete cognition of a thing, one must cognize everything possible and, by that means, determine the thing either affirmatively or negatively. Exhaustive determination is on this account a concept that we are never able to present _in concreto_ in the whole of its totality, and it accordingly rests upon an idea seated exclusively in reason, which lays down for the understanding the rule for its complete employment.

Now even though this idea of the ***aggregate of all possibility***—insofar as it underlies, as its condition, the exhaustive determination of every thing—still remains itself undetermined as to the predicates that might make it up, so that through it we think nothing more than an aggregate of all possible predicates taken generally, we do nonetheless discover, on closer scrutiny, that this idea, as the primordial concept, casts out a multitude of {{ 602 }} predicates that stand already given as derived from others, or that cannot coexist alongside one another, and that it clarifies itself up to a {{{ 387 }}} concept determined throughout _a priori_, thereby coming to be the concept of a single object that the mere idea exhaustively determines, and which must therefore be called an ***ideal*** of pure reason.

When we weigh all possible predicates not just logically but transcendentally—that is, in terms of the content thinkable in them _a priori_—we discover that some of them represent a being and others a bare non-being. Logical negation, marked out merely by the little word 'not', never in truth belongs to a concept as such, but only to the concept's relation to another within a judgment, and so it falls far short of being adequate to designate a concept in respect of its content. The term ***not-mortal*** can in no way convey that thereby a bare non-being of the object is represented; it leaves the entire content undisturbed. A transcendental negation, by contrast, denotes non-being in itself, and to it there stands opposed transcendental affirmation, which is a something whose very concept already expresses a being and is on that account termed reality (thinghood), since through it alone, and to the extent it reaches, objects are something (things), whereas the negation opposed to it denotes a mere deficiency, and, {{ 603 }} where it alone is thought, the cancellation of every thing is what gets represented.

Now no one is able to think a determinate negation without laying the opposed affirmation at its basis. Someone born blind cannot frame even the faintest representation of darkness, since he has none of light; the savage none of poverty, since he is unacquainted with prosperity.[^**] One who is ignorant possesses no concept of his own ignorance, since he possesses none of science, and so forth. Hence the concepts of negations are, all of them, derived as well, while the realities furnish the data and, so to say, the matter—the transcendental content—for the possibility and exhaustive determination of all things.

If, accordingly, the exhaustive determination in our reason is founded upon a {{{ 388 }}} transcendental substratum—one holding, so to speak, the entire stock of stuff from which every possible predicate of things may be drawn—then that substratum is none other {{ 604 }} than the idea of a totality of reality (_omnitudo realitatis_). All genuine negations are, on that footing, nothing beyond ***limits***—a name they could not bear unless the unlimited (the All) lay at their basis.

Moreover, by this complete possession of reality the concept of a ***thing in itself*** is likewise represented as exhaustively determined, and the concept of an _entis realissimi_ amounts to the concept of an individual being, since out of all the mutually opposed predicates that are possible, one—precisely the one belonging to being outright—is met with in its determination. It is therefore a transcendental ***ideal*** underlying that exhaustive determination which is met with necessarily in whatever exists, and forming the highest and complete material condition of its possibility—the condition to which, in respect of their content, all thinking of objects whatever must be led back. It is, besides, the sole ideal proper to which human reason can attain, for in this one case alone a concept that is in itself universal, applying to a thing, is by its own means exhaustively determined and is recognized as the representation of an individual.

Reason's logical determining of a concept proceeds by way of a disjunctive inference, whose major premise sets out a logical division (namely the partitioning of the sphere of a universal concept) {{ 605 }}, whose minor premise confines that sphere to a single part, and whose conclusion thereby fixes the concept. A universal concept of reality as such admits of no _a priori_ division, since apart from experience we are acquainted with no determinate species of reality that would fall under that genus. The transcendental major premise of the exhaustive determination of all things is on this account nothing else than the representation of the aggregate of all reality—not a concept that merely holds all predicates, by their transcendental content, ***beneath itself***, but one that embraces them ***within itself***; and the exhaustive determination of any given thing depends upon a limiting of this ***All*** of reality, whereby a part of it is assigned to the thing and the remainder shut out, which accords with the disjunctive major premise's either/or and with the determining {{{ 389 }}} of the object by one of the members of that partition in the minor premise. Consequently, the way reason employs itself when it makes the transcendental ideal the ground of its determining of all possible things runs parallel to the way it proceeds within disjunctive inferences; and this was just the proposition I set out above as the ground for the systematic division of all transcendental ideas, on which basis these are produced in parallel with, and corresponding to, the three kinds of inference of reason.

It goes without saying that reason, for the purpose here in view—namely, simply to represent to itself the necessary exhaustive determination of things—does not presuppose the existence of a being answering to the ideal {{ 606 }}, but only the idea of such a being, so as to trace, from an unconditioned totality of exhaustive determination, the conditioned one—namely, that of what is limited. For reason, then, the ideal is the archetype (_Prototypon_) of all things, which collectively, as defective copies (_ectypa_), draw from it the stuff of their possibility and, coming nearer to it in greater or lesser degree, yet at all times fall short of reaching it by an infinite distance.

In this way the entire possibility of things (of the synthesis of the manifold with respect to its content) is looked upon as derived, and only the possibility of that which encloses all reality within itself is looked upon as original. For all negations (which are, after all, the sole predicates whereby everything else may be marked off from the supremely real being) amount to nothing but restrictions of a greater reality and, in the end, of the supreme reality; they thus presuppose it and, in respect of content, are merely derived from it. The whole manifold of things is but so many correspondingly manifold ways of restricting the concept of the supreme reality, which serves as their common substratum, in the same manner that every figure is possible only as a distinct way of restricting infinite space. For this reason the object of reason's ideal, an object residing solely within reason, is likewise named the ***original being*** (_ens originarium_); insofar as nothing stands above it, the ***supreme being*** (_ens summum_); and {{ 607 }} insofar as everything else, being conditioned, ranks beneath it, the ***being of all beings*** (_ens entium_). Yet all of this designates, not the objective relation of an actual object to other things, but that of the ***idea*** to ***concepts***, and it leaves us wholly in the dark concerning the existence of a being of such surpassing distinction.

And since one likewise cannot say that an original being is composed of many derived {{{ 390 }}} beings—each of these presupposing it and hence unable to constitute it—the ideal of the original being will have to be conceived as simple as well.

Strictly put, then, the deriving of every other possibility from this original being cannot be viewed as a ***restriction*** of its supreme reality, nor, so to speak, as a partitioning of that reality; for in that event the original being would come to be viewed as a mere aggregate of derived beings, and this, by what precedes, is impossible—however much, at the very start, in a first rough silhouette, we did picture it so. It would be more accurate to say that the supreme reality underlies the possibility of all things as a ***ground*** rather than as an ***aggregate***, and that the manifoldness of those things rests, not on any restriction of the original being itself, but on its full sequel—to which our whole sensibility, along with all reality in appearance, would then belong, though such reality cannot belong, as an ingredient, to the idea of the supreme being.

{{ 608 }} Should we now press this idea of ours yet further, hypostatizing it, we shall find ourselves able, by means of the bare concept of the supreme reality, to determine the original being as unique, simple, all-sufficient, eternal, and so on—in short, to fix it, in its unconditioned completeness, through all the predicaments. The concept of a being of this kind is the concept of ***God*** as thought in a transcendental sense; and so the ideal of pure reason forms the object of a transcendental ***theology***, exactly as I noted above as well.

This employment of the transcendental idea would, even so, already transgress the bounds of its determination and permissibility. For reason had laid it down, merely as the ***concept*** of all reality, at the basis of the exhaustive determination of things as such, without requiring that all this reality be given objectively and itself make up a thing. This last is nothing but a figment, whereby we draw together and give reality to the manifold of our idea within an ideal, as though in a particular being—a thing we have no title to do, not even so much as to admit outright the possibility of such a hypothesis; just as, moreover, whatever conclusions issue from an ideal of the kind have nothing to do with, and exert not the least influence on, the exhaustive determination of things at large, for whose sake alone the idea was ever required.

{{{ 391 }}} {{ 609 }} Describing the procedure of our reason together with its dialectic does not suffice; one has, in addition, to try to uncover the sources from which it springs, so that this semblance may itself be accounted for, in the manner of a phenomenon of the understanding; for the ideal we are discussing rests upon a natural idea, and not a merely arbitrary one. Hence I raise the question: what leads reason to look upon all possibility of things as derived from one single possibility lying at the basis—that of the supreme reality—and then to presuppose this as contained within a particular original being?

The answer presents itself unbidden out of what was transacted in the transcendental analytic. The possibility of objects of the senses consists in a relation of those objects to our thinking, wherein something (that is, the empirical form) admits of being thought _a priori_, while that which makes up the matter—the reality within appearance (answering to sensation)—has to be given, in default of which the object could not so much as be thought and its possibility, accordingly, could not be represented. Now an object of the senses allows of exhaustive determination only when it is held up against all the predicates of appearance and, through them, represented affirmatively or negatively. But since that which makes up the thing itself (within appearance)—the real, that is—must be given therein, in default of which it could not be thought at all, while that wherein the real of all appearances is given is the single all-embracing {{ 610 }} experience, it follows that the matter for the possibility of all objects of the senses has to be presupposed as given within one aggregate, upon whose limiting alone the whole possibility of empirical objects, their mutual difference, and their exhaustive determination can be founded. Now in point of fact no objects other than those of the senses can be given to us, and given nowhere save within the setting of a possible experience; nothing, in consequence, counts as an object ***for us*** unless it presupposes the aggregate of all empirical reality as the condition of its possibility. By force of a natural illusion we come to regard as a principle valid for all things whatever what in truth holds good only of those given as objects of our senses. In consequence, by striking out this restriction, we shall take the empirical principle underlying our concepts of how things are possible as appearances to be a transcendental principle of the possibility of things in general.

{{{ 392 }}} That we go on, afterwards, to hypostasize this idea of the aggregate of all reality comes about because we dialectically convert the ***distributive*** unity belonging to the understanding's use in experience into the ***collective*** unity of one experiential whole, and, within this whole of appearance, imagine to ourselves a single thing holding all empirical reality within it, {{ 611 }} which thing, by way of the transcendental subreption already spoken of, then gets mistaken for the concept of a thing positioned at the pinnacle of the possibility of all things—furnishing, for their exhaustive determination, the real conditions.[^***]

[^*]: By this principle, that is to say, every thing gets referred to a shared correlate, namely the whole of possibility, which—were it (the stuff, that is, for all possible predicates) to be met with in the idea of one single thing—would prove an affinity among everything possible, by way of the identical ground of that thing's exhaustive determination. The ***determinability*** of every ***concept*** falls under the **universality** (_Universalitas_) of the principle excluding a middle between two opposed predicates, whereas the ***determination*** of a ***thing*** falls under the **totality** (_Universitas_), that is, the aggregate of all possible predicates.

[^**]: The observations and computations of those who study the stars have taught us many things worthy of wonder, but surely the weightiest is that they have laid bare for us the abyss of ***ignorance*** that human reason, lacking such knowledge, could never have imagined to be so vast, and reflection on which is bound to produce a great shift in how we determine the ultimate ends of our use of reason.

[^***]: This ideal of the most real being of all is thus, notwithstanding that it is a mere representation, first ***realized***—that is, turned into an object—next ***hypostasized***, and at last, by a natural advance of reason toward the completion of unity, even ***personified***, as we shall presently set out; for the regulative unity of experience is grounded not on the appearances themselves (on sensibility taken alone), but on the linking of their manifold by the ***understanding*** (in one apperception), so that the unity of the supreme reality and the exhaustive determinability (possibility) of all things comes to seem to reside in a supreme understanding—hence in an ***intelligence***.
