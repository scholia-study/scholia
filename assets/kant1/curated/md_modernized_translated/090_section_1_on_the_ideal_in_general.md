## Section 1. On the ideal in general.

We saw above that through pure ***concepts of the understanding***, apart from all conditions of sensibility, no objects whatsoever can be represented, because the conditions for their objective reality are lacking, and nothing but the mere form of thinking is met with in them. Nonetheless they admit of being exhibited _in concreto_ once one applies them to appearances; for it is in these that they properly find their material for the concept of experience, which is itself nothing but a concept of the understanding taken _in concreto_. ***Ideas***, though, lie at a still greater remove from objective reality than the ***categories*** do; for there is no appearance to be met with wherein they could be exhibited _in concreto_. They embrace a completeness of a certain sort, one that no empirical cognition that is possible {{ 596 }} can ever attain, and here reason has in mind nothing but a systematic unity, to which it seeks to approximate the empirically possible unity without ever quite reaching it.

Yet still farther removed from objective reality than the idea seems to be that which I call the ***ideal***, and under which I take the idea to hold not only _in concreto_ but indeed _in individuo_ — that is, as a single thing determinable, or even determined, through the idea alone.

Humanity in its entire perfection comprises not merely the enlargement of every essential property attaching to this nature — the properties that make up our concept of it — as far as full agreement with its ends, this being what our idea of perfected humanity would amount to, but likewise all that, over and above this concept, pertains to the idea's thoroughgoing determination; for of every pair of opposed predicates only one alone can, in the end, befit the idea of the most perfect human being. What is an ideal for us was for Plato an ***idea of the divine understanding***, a single object within the {{{ 384 }}} pure intuition of that understanding, the most perfect specimen of every kind of possible being and the original ground of all copies in appearance.

{{ 597 }} Without soaring quite so high, however, we must concede that human reason harbors ideals no less than ideas — ideals that, while devoid of the creative force of the ***Platonic*** ones, do wield ***practical*** force (as regulative principles) and underlie the possibility that certain ***actions*** attain their perfection. Moral concepts are not wholly pure concepts of reason, because something empirical (pleasure or displeasure) underlies them. Even so, with regard to the principle by which reason sets bounds to a freedom that is in itself lawless (that is, when one attends merely to their form), they may perfectly well serve as instances of pure concepts of reason. Virtue, and human wisdom together with it, taken in their full purity, are ideas. The sage of the Stoic, by contrast, is an ideal, that is, a human being existing merely in thought yet corresponding entirely to the idea of wisdom. Just as the idea furnishes the ***rule***, so in such an instance the ideal serves as the ***archetype*** by which the copy is determined throughout; and we hold no other yardstick for our deeds than the bearing of this divine human being within us, with whom we compare and assess ourselves and thereby grow better, even though we can never attain it. These ideals, granted that one may be reluctant to concede them objective reality (existence), are nonetheless not to be dismissed as figments of the brain; on the contrary, they yield an indispensable {{ 598 }} standard for reason, which stands in need of a concept of what is, in its own kind, entirely complete, so as to assess and measure out, by that standard, the degree and the defects of the incomplete. But to want to realize the ideal in an example, that is, in appearance — as, say, the sage in a novel — is impracticable and has, moreover, something absurd and little edifying about it, since the natural limits that continually impair the completeness in the idea render all illusion in such an attempt impossible and thereby make even the good that lies in the idea suspect and akin to a mere fabrication.

Such is the case with the ideal of reason, which must at all times rest on determinate concepts and serve as the rule and archetype, whether of compliance or of assessment. Matters stand quite otherwise with the creatures of the imagination, about which no one can give an account or offer any intelligible concept — ***monograms***, as it were, that are only scattered features, determined by no assignable rule, {{{ 385 }}} features that make up rather a sketch hovering, so to speak, amid the mean of diverse experiences than any determinate image, of the sort that painters and physiognomists claim to carry in their heads, and which are meant to be an uncommunicable shadow-image of what they produce, or indeed of what they appraise. One might, albeit only loosely, term them ideals of sensibility, since they are meant to serve as the unreachable pattern for possible {{ 599 }} empirical intuitions while nonetheless supplying no rule susceptible of explication or testing.

Reason's intent in its ideal, on the other hand, is thoroughgoing determination according to rules _a priori_; and so it frames for itself an object supposed to be wholly determinable on the basis of principles, even where the conditions adequate for this are missing in experience, so that the concept itself turns out transcendent.
