## Section 7. Critical decision of the cosmological conflict of reason with itself.

The whole antinomy into which pure reason falls turns upon a single dialectical inference: if something conditioned is given, then so too is the entire series of the conditions on which it depends; now, the objects of sense are presented to us as conditioned; consequently, etc. Through this rational inference—whose major premise looks so natural and compelling—there arise, according to the different kinds of conditions (within the synthesis of appearances) so far as these make up a series, precisely as many cosmological ideas; and these ideas demand the absolute totality of such series, thereby throwing reason inevitably into discord with itself. Before we lay bare the deceptive character of this sophistical argument, however, {{ 526 }} we must first equip ourselves for the task by correcting and pinning down certain concepts that turn up within it.

***First***, this much is plain and beyond any doubt: that once the conditioned is given, we are thereby handed a regress through the series {{{ 343 }}} of all its conditions as ***our task***; for the concept of the conditioned already entails as much, since by it a thing is referred back to a condition, and, should that condition itself be conditioned, to one still further removed, and thus onward through every member of the series. This proposition, accordingly, is analytic, and it rises above all dread of any transcendental critique. It amounts to a logical postulate of reason—to track by means of the understanding, and to prolong as far as one can, that linkage of a concept with its conditions which is already inherent in the concept as such.

***Furthermore***: supposing that both the conditioned and its condition are things in themselves, then, when the former has been ***given***, the regress to the latter is not merely ***set as a task***—rather, that latter is in fact co-***given*** along with it; and, because this applies to each member of the series alike, the complete series of conditions, and with it the unconditioned too, is at once co-given—or, more precisely, is taken for granted the moment the conditioned (which only that series rendered possible) is given. Here the synthesis linking the conditioned to its condition is one performed by the mere understanding, which represents things ***as they are*** while paying no regard to whether, or how, we might arrive at cognizance of them. {{ 527 }} With appearances, by contrast—which, being nothing more than representations, are simply not given until I gain cognizance of them (i.e., until I arrive at the appearances themselves, since they amount to nothing beyond empirical cognizances)—I cannot assert in that same sense that, the conditioned being given, all the conditions belonging to it (as appearances) are given along with it; and I am therefore in no position whatever to conclude to the absolute totality of their series. For ***appearances***, within the very apprehension of them, are in themselves nothing but an empirical synthesis (one carried out in space and in time), and for that reason they are given solely ***within it***. Now it in no way follows that, given the conditioned (in appearance), the synthesis making up its empirical condition is co-given and presupposed thereby; on the contrary, such a synthesis occurs for the first time only within the regress, and never independently of it. What one may quite properly say in such a case, however, is that on this side a ***regress*** toward the conditions—that is, an ongoing empirical synthesis—is commanded or ***set as a task***, and that conditions supplied by this regress can never run short.

This makes it plain that the major premise of the cosmological syllogism {{{ 344 }}} construes the conditioned in the transcendental sense proper to a pure category, while the minor premise construes it in the empirical sense of an understanding-concept brought to bear on mere appearances; and so the {{ 528 }} dialectical sleight lodged within it is the one known as _Sophisma figurae dictionis_. This deceit, however, is nothing artificially manufactured, but rather a wholly natural delusion of ordinary reason. For by means of it we take for granted (in the major premise) the conditions together with their series, ***sight unseen*** as it were, the instant something is presented as conditioned—this being merely the logical requirement that complete premises be posited for a given conclusion; and in the tie between the conditioned and its condition no temporal ordering shows up at all: the two are assumed, in themselves, to be given ***all at once***. It is, moreover, just as natural (in the minor premise) to treat appearances as if they were things in themselves—objects, that is, delivered to the understanding on its own—exactly as was done in the major premise, seeing that I had set aside every condition of intuition, the sole conditions under which objects can be given to us. Yet in proceeding thus we had passed over a noteworthy distinction between these concepts. The joining of the conditioned to its condition, along with the whole series of those conditions (in the major premise), brought with it no restriction by time whatever and no notion of succession. The empirical synthesis, on the other hand—the series of conditions within appearance, the one subsumed under the minor premise—is of necessity successive, its members given only in temporal succession; and so ***I*** was unable, in this case, to presuppose the absolute ***totality*** of the synthesis and of the series it represents here {{ 529 }} in the way I could in the other, for there every member of the series stands given in itself (with no temporal condition attached), while here the members become available only by way of the successive regress, a regress that exists only insofar as one genuinely carries it through.

Now that a false step of this kind has been fastened upon the argument that both sides had jointly placed at the base (of the cosmological assertions), the two disputing parties may with justice be turned away as litigants who ground their demand on no valid title. Still, this does not so far settle their contest as to have proven them—both, or at least one of the pair—mistaken in the very thing asserted (in the conclusion), even granting that he could not rest it on solid grounds of proof. Indeed, nothing looks more evident than this: that where one party maintains the world had a beginning and the other maintains the {{{ 345 }}} world never began but reaches back through all eternity, surely one of the two has to be in the right. But if that holds, then—precisely because the evidence weighs the same on either hand—it still proves impossible ever to make out on which side the right resides; and the quarrel persists just as it did, even after the parties have been ordered to hold their peace before reason's court of law. No route is left, then, for bringing the dispute to a thorough close that will satisfy both sides, save this: that, given how elegantly each can demolish the other, they be brought in the end to see that their quarrel is about nothing at all, and that a certain transcendental illusion has conjured up for them an actuality in a place where {{ 530 }} none can be met with. It is precisely this way of laying to rest a quarrel admitting of no verdict that we now propose to follow.

***

The ***Eleatic*** ***Zeno***, a dialectician of great subtlety, had already been rebuked in the strongest terms by Plato as a mischievous sophist, for this: that, in order to show off his art, he would prove one and the same thesis by means of specious arguments only to knock it down again, moments later, with others fully as strong. His claim was that ***God*** (though in his mouth this was presumably no more than the world) is neither the one nor the other of finite and infinite, is as little in motion as at rest, and stands neither in likeness nor in unlikeness to anything else whatsoever. It struck those who passed judgment on him here as though he had wished flatly to reject a pair of mutually contradictory claims—something plainly absurd. For my part, though, I do not see that the charge can justly be laid at his door. The first among these claims I shall shortly bring under closer scrutiny. As regards the others: supposing he took the term ***God*** to mean the ***universe***, then he was surely bound to hold that it neither abides fixed in its location (at rest) nor shifts out of it (moves), for all locations lie within the universe alone, so that ***the universe*** has itself ***no location*** at all. If the totality of the world encloses within it all that there is, then, just so far, it bears neither resemblance nor its opposite to any ***other thing***, since beyond it lies ***no other thing*** against which it might be measured. Whenever two mutually {{ 531 }} opposing judgments rest on a condition that cannot be granted, then, despite the conflict between them (which for all that is no real contradiction), the two collapse together, since there collapses the very condition on which alone either proposition was meant to stand.

{{{ 346 }}} Should someone declare that every body either has a good smell or has no good smell, a third possibility comes into play—namely, that it gives off no smell whatever (has no scent)—and thus the two opposing statements may both turn out false. Were I instead to say that it is either sweet-smelling or else not sweet-smelling (_vel suaveolens vel non suaveolens_), the two judgments would then stand in contradictory opposition, and only the former is false, while its contradictory counterpart—that some bodies are not sweet-smelling—takes in as well those bodies which ***give off no smell at all***. In the earlier contrast (_per disparata_) the accidental condition belonging to the concept of bodies (their odor) ***stayed*** in force alongside the opposing judgment, and so went uncancelled by it, which is why that opposing judgment was not the contradictory counterpart of the first.

Suppose, then, that I assert the world to be, as regards space, either infinite or not infinite (_non est infinitus_); in that event, if the first statement is false, its contradictory opposite—that the world is ***not infinite***—has to be true. By this I should be cancelling merely an infinite world {{ 532 }} while setting up no other in its place, no finite one, that is. If, however, the claim went: the world is either infinite or else finite (non-infinite), then the pair of them might both be false. For then I am looking upon the world as something fixed in itself as to its magnitude, inasmuch as, within the opposition, I do more than cancel infinity (and with it, it may be, the world's entire independent existence): I attach to the world a determination, treating it as a thing real in its own right—and this too may well be false, in case the world is ***not a thing in itself in any sense***, and hence is not given, as to magnitude, either as infinite or as finite. Let me be permitted to name an opposition of this sort the ***dialectical*** kind, and the opposition of contradiction the analytic ***opposition***. And so, of two judgments opposed in the dialectical manner, both may be false—just because the one, rather than simply contradicting its fellow, asserts something over and above what contradiction calls for.

Take the two propositions—that the world is infinite as to magnitude, and that the world is finite as to its magnitude—and treat them as contradictory opposites: one then supposes the world (the whole series of appearances) to be a thing existing in itself. For it goes on being just that, no matter whether it is the infinite or the finite regress within the series of its appearances that I cancel. Should I, on the other hand, strike out this presupposition—this transcendental illusion—and refuse the world any status as a thing in itself, the contradictory clash of the two claims is thereby transformed {{{ 347 }}} into {{ 533 }} one that is merely dialectical; and since the world enjoys no existence in itself whatever (apart from the regressive series of my representations), it exists as a whole neither ***infinite in itself*** nor ***finite in itself***. One meets with it solely in the empirical regress that runs through the series of appearances; taken on its own, it is nowhere to be found. And so, this series being at all times conditioned, it is never given in its entirety; the world, accordingly, forms no unconditioned whole, and thus has no existence as a whole of that kind—whether its magnitude be infinite or finite.

Whatever has here been said regarding the first of the cosmological ideas—that is, the absolute totality of magnitude within appearance—applies equally to all the others. The series of conditions can be met with only in the regressive synthesis as such, and not on its own in the appearance, as though the latter were a self-subsistent thing given ahead of all regress. I shall therefore have to say, likewise, that the number of parts within any given appearance is, in itself, neither finite nor infinite—for an appearance is not anything self-subsistent, and its parts first come to be given by, and in the course of, the regress of the decomposing synthesis, a regress never delivered as flatly ***complete***, be it finite or infinite. Precisely the same is true of the series of causes stacked one upon another, or of conditioned existence reaching up toward an existence unconditionally necessary, which can at no point {{ 534 }} be taken, as to its totality, for something either finite or infinite in itself; for as a series of subordinated representations it has its being only in the dynamical regress, whereas before that regress, as a self-standing series of things in themselves, it is incapable of existing at all.

Thus, then, is the antinomy of pure reason laid to rest in respect of its cosmological ideas—by the demonstration that it is nothing but dialectical, a clash produced by illusion, arising because the idea of absolute totality (valid only as a condition of things in themselves) has been carried over to appearances, which have their existence merely in representation and, when they form a series, in the successive regress, but which apart from this have no existence whatever. Turned around, however, this antinomy can also be put to a real use—critical and doctrinal in kind, though certainly not dogmatic: it lets us establish, by an indirect route, the transcendental ideality of appearances, for anyone who might find {{{ 348 }}} the direct demonstration in the transcendental aesthetic insufficient. That demonstration would take the shape of the following dilemma. Were the world a whole subsisting in itself, it would have to be either finite or else infinite. But the one option and the other are alike false (by the arguments set out above, those of the antithesis in the first case and of the thesis in the second). It is therefore {{ 535 }} likewise false that the world (the totality of all appearances taken together) constitutes a whole that subsists in itself. And from this it follows, in turn, that appearances as such amount to nothing beyond our representations—which is exactly the point we were making in speaking of their transcendental ideality.

This observation carries weight. It shows us that the foregoing proofs of the fourfold antinomy were no empty conjuring-tricks but soundly grounded—on the supposition, that is, that appearances, or a world of sense taking them all up within it, were things in themselves. The clash among the propositions derived from that supposition, however, discloses that a falsity is lodged in it, and in this way brings us to uncover the genuine character of things considered as objects of the senses. The transcendental dialectic, then, offers no aid whatever to skepticism, though it does aid the skeptical method, which may cite it as a specimen of its great value, once one allows reason's arguments to confront one another in their utmost freedom—arguments that, even if in the end they fail to deliver what was being sought, will yet on every occasion yield something useful, something that serves to set our judgments right.
