## Section 3. On the arguments of speculative reason in inferring the existence of a supreme being.

Despite this pressing need of reason to presuppose something capable of serving the understanding as the full basis underlying the thoroughgoing determination of its concepts, reason still remarks the ideal and merely fabricated character of such a presupposition far too readily {{ 612 }} to let itself be talked, on that ground alone, into straightaway accepting a mere self-made product of its own thinking as an actual being — unless it were driven by something else to seek, somewhere, its point of rest in the _regressus_ that leads from the conditioned (which is given) up to the unconditioned — a thing that, considered in itself and according to its bare concept, is indeed not given as actual, yet which alone can bring to completion the series of conditions traced out to their grounds. Such is the natural path that every human reason travels, even the most vulgar, although not every {{{ 393 }}} one endures to the end of it. It sets out not from concepts but from ordinary experience, and thereby places something existing at its base. This soil, however, sinks away unless it is supported by the unshakable rock of the absolutely necessary. And this rock itself hovers without support if there is still empty space beside and beneath it, and if it does not itself fill everything, leaving no further room for any ***why*** — in other words, unless it is infinite as regards reality.

If anything whatsoever exists, be it what it may, then one must likewise grant that something ***necessarily*** exists. For whatever is contingent exists only on the condition of something else serving as its cause, and from that cause the inference runs on further until it arrives at a cause that is not contingent and, just for that reason, is there necessarily and without any condition. That is the argument by which reason underpins its advance to an original being.

{{ 613 }} Reason now casts about for the concept of a being fit for such a prerogative of existence as unconditioned necessity is, not so much in order thereupon to infer that being's existence _a priori_ from its concept (for had it the boldness to do so, it might conduct its inquiry solely among bare concepts and would not need to lay any given existence at the base), but solely so as to locate, among all concepts of possible things, that one which harbors nothing at odds with absolute necessity. For that something absolutely necessary must after all exist it already regards as settled by the earlier inference. If, then, it is able to remove everything that will not square with this necessity save a single thing, then that thing is the absolutely necessary being — whether or not one can grasp its necessity, that is, derive it from its concept alone.

Now that thing whose concept contains within it the "because" answering to every "why", which is defective in no part and in no respect, and which everywhere suffices as a condition, seems on precisely this account to be the being that fits absolute necessity, since, holding of itself all the conditions for everything possible, it stands in need of no condition of its own — is indeed not even susceptible of one — and hence satisfies the concept of unconditioned necessity in at least one part, wherein no other concept can equal it, since such a concept, being deficient and standing in need of {{ 614 }} completion, exhibits in itself no such mark of independence from all further conditions. It is true that from this one still cannot yet {{{ 394 }}} safely conclude that whatever fails to contain within it the highest condition, complete in every regard, must therefore itself be conditioned as to its existence; but it does after all lack in itself that one distinguishing mark of unconditioned existence which reason has at its command for cognizing any being as unconditioned through an _a priori_ concept.

The concept of a being possessing the highest reality would therefore, among all concepts of possible things, be the one that fits best the concept of an unconditionally necessary being; and even though it does not entirely satisfy the latter, we nonetheless have no choice, but see ourselves constrained to cling to it, since we dare not cast to the winds the existence of a necessary being, and yet, granting it, are unable to find anything in the entire field of possibility that could put forward a better-grounded claim to such a prerogative in existence.

The natural path of human reason, then, is fashioned as follows. To begin with, it satisfies itself that ***some*** necessary being exists. In such a being it cognizes an unconditioned existence. It then seeks the concept {{ 615 }} of what is independent of every condition, and it finds this in that which is itself the sufficient condition of all else — that is, in what contains all reality. But the unlimited all amounts to absolute unity, and it brings with it the concept of a single, namely the supreme, being; and so reason infers that the supreme being, being the primordial ground of all things, is there absolutely and of necessity.

A certain solidity cannot be disputed to this concept when what is at issue is ***resolutions*** — that is, once one has conceded the existence of some necessary being and settled that one must commit to a position on where to place it; for then one can make no more fitting choice, or rather one has no choice whatever, but is constrained to give one's vote to the absolute unity of complete reality, taken as the primordial wellspring of possibility. But if nothing impels us to a resolution, and we would rather leave this whole affair undecided until the full weight of the proofs forces our assent — that is, if the concern is merely the ***assessment*** of how much we know of this task and how much we only fancy ourselves to know — then the foregoing inference appears in a far less advantageous shape and requires favor to make good the deficiency of its rightful claims.

{{{ 395 }}} For even supposing we allow everything to pass just as favorably as it here lies before us — namely, first, that from some given existence (at most also {{ 616 }} merely my own) a valid inference to an unconditionally necessary being's existence takes place; and second, that I am bound to regard a being which contains all reality, and thus every condition as well, as absolutely unconditioned, so that the concept of the thing that answers to absolute necessity is thereby found — it still in no way follows from this that the concept of a restricted being — one lacking the highest reality — is on that account in conflict with absolute necessity. For even though in its concept I do not meet with the unconditioned that already brings with it the totality of conditions, it still by no means follows from this that its existence must for just that reason be conditioned; any more than, in a hypothetical inference of reason, I may say: where a certain condition (here, that of completeness according to concepts) is lacking, there too the conditioned is lacking. Rather, it will remain open to us to let all the remaining restricted beings count just as much as unconditionally necessary, notwithstanding that we cannot derive their necessity from the general concept we have of them. In this manner, however, this argument would have supplied us with not even the least concept of what properties a necessary being possesses, and would have achieved nothing at all.

Even so, there clings to this argument a certain weight and a prestige of which it cannot at once {{ 617 }} be deprived on account of this objective inadequacy. For assume there are obligations perfectly correct in the idea of reason, yet lacking all reality of application to ourselves — that is, without incentives — in the absence of a presupposed supreme being that could impart force and weight to the practical laws: then we too would be under an obligation to follow those concepts which, granting they may not be objectively sufficient, still outweigh the rest by the standard of our reason, compared with which we know nothing better or more compelling. Here the duty to choose would, through a practical addition, tip the irresolution of speculation out of its equilibrium; indeed reason, before its own bench as the most indulgent of judges, would find no justification if, under pressing motives — however defective its insight — it had not followed these grounds of its judgment, of which we at any rate know none more reliable.

{{{ 396 }}} Though this argument is in fact transcendental, resting as it does on the inner inadequacy of what is contingent, it is nevertheless so plain and natural that it suits the most ordinary human sense the moment it is once brought to it. One sees things alter, arise and perish; they, then, or at least their state, must have a cause. But of every cause that may ever be given in experience {{ 618 }} this very same thing can again be asked. Now where should we more justly place the ***supreme*** causality than where the ***highest*** causality likewise resides — that is, in that being which originally holds within itself the sufficiency for every possible effect, and whose concept, moreover, comes about very readily through the single stroke of an all-embracing perfection. This highest cause we then take to be absolutely necessary, since we find it flatly necessary to climb up to it, and find no ground to pass on still further beyond it. Hence it is that among all peoples we see some sparks of monotheism glimmer through even the blindest polytheism — a thing to which not reflection and profound speculation, but only a gradually intelligible natural course of the common understanding, has led.

## Only three ways of proving God's existence out of speculative reason are possible.

Every route one might strike out on toward this end either sets out from determinate experience and from the particular constitution of our sensible world cognized by means of it, ascending from there, by laws of causality, to a highest cause lying outside the world; or it lays only indeterminate experience, that is, some existence or other, empirically at the base; or, finally, abstracting from all experience whatever, it concludes, entirely _a priori_, to the existence of a {{ 619 }} highest cause out of mere concepts. The first of these is the ***physico-theological*** proof, the second the ***cosmological***, and the third the ***ontological***. There are no more of them than this, nor can there be more.

I shall make it evident that reason achieves just as little by the one route (the empirical) as by the other (the transcendental), and that it unfurls its wings to no purpose when it seeks to rise above the sensible world by the mere might of speculation. As concerns the order in which these kinds of proof must be laid before examination, it will be exactly the reverse of the one {{{ 397 }}} that reason, in gradually widening itself, takes, and in which we too have presented them at the outset. For it will emerge that, although experience furnishes the first occasion for it, it is the ***transcendental concept*** alone that steers reason in this striving of its and stakes out, in all such attempts, the goal it has set before itself. I shall accordingly start with the examination of the transcendental proof, and afterward observe what the empirical, added on, might contribute toward enlarging its probative force.
