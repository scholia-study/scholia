## Chapter 2. The Antinomy of Pure Reason

We showed, in the introduction to the present portion of our work, that all transcendental illusion of pure reason has its basis in dialectical inferences, for which logic furnishes the schema by way of the three formal kinds of inference of reason in general, roughly as the categories, for their part, encounter their logical schema within the four functions belonging to every judgment. The ***first kind of these*** ratiocinating inferences was directed at the unconditioned unity of the ***subjective*** conditions of all representations whatever (the subject, that is, or the soul), corresponding to the ***categorical*** inferences of reason, whose major premise, in its capacity as principle, asserts the relation that a predicate bears to {{ 433 }} a ***subject***. The ***second kind*** of dialectical argument will therefore, following the analogy with ***hypothetical*** inferences of reason, make the unconditioned unity of the objective conditions in appearance into its content, just as the ***third kind***, which is to come up in the next chapter, takes for its theme the unconditioned unity of the objective conditions of the possibility of objects in general.

What is noteworthy, however, is that the transcendental paralogism brought about only a one-sided illusion with respect to the idea concerning the subject of our thinking, while for asserting the opposite not the least illusion, drawn from concepts of reason, will present itself. The advantage falls {{{ 282 }}} entirely to the side of pneumatism, even though pneumatism cannot deny its inherited flaw of evaporating, for all the illusion in its favor, into sheer mist under critique's trial by fire.

Things fall out quite otherwise once we bring reason to bear on the ***objective synthesis*** of appearances, where, though it supposes it can assert its principle of unconditioned unity with a great show of plausibility, it soon entangles itself in contradictions of such a kind that it finds itself constrained, in its cosmological intent, to withdraw from its demand.

For at this point human reason displays a new phenomenon, namely: a thoroughly natural antithetic, one over which no one need brood {{ 434 }} or contrive to set snares, but into which reason lapses of itself, and indeed unavoidably, whereby it is preserved, on the one hand, from the slumber of an imagined conviction such as a merely one-sided illusion produces, yet is at the same time led into the temptation either of surrendering to a skeptical despair, or of assuming a dogmatic defiance and stubbornly fixing its mind on certain assertions while refusing to grant the grounds of the opposite a hearing and their due. Both spell the death of a sound philosophy, though the former could perhaps still be termed the ***euthanasia*** of pure reason.

Before we allow the scenes of the schism and of the disruptions to be witnessed that this conflict of laws (antinomy) of pure reason gives rise to, we mean to set down certain considerations capable of elucidating and vindicating the method that we employ in dealing with our object. To all transcendental ideas, insofar as they have to do with the absolute totality within the synthesis of appearances, I give the name ***world-concepts***, partly because of just this unconditioned totality (upon which the concept of the world-whole itself reposes, though that concept is in turn only an idea), and partly because they bear merely on the synthesis of appearances, and hence on the empirical synthesis, whereas the absolute totality in the synthesis of the {{ 435 }} conditions of all possible things whatever will give rise to an ideal of pure reason, one wholly distinct from the world-concept, albeit standing in relation to it. Accordingly, just as the paralogisms of pure reason supplied the foundation for a dialectical psychology, so likewise the antinomy of pure reason is to hold up before us the transcendental principles of a putative pure (rational) cosmology, not so as to find them valid and adopt them as our own, but rather—as {{{ 283 }}} the very designation of a conflict of reason already makes plain—so as to present them, being an idea that admits of no reconciliation with appearances, in their dazzling yet deceptive illusion.
