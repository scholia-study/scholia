## Chapter 2. The Antinomy of Pure Reason

We have shown in the introduction to this part of our work that all transcendental illusion of pure reason rests on dialectical inferences, the schema of which logic provides in the three formal kinds of syllogisms of reason in general, just as the categories find their logical schema in the four functions of all judgments. The ***first kind of these*** ratiocinating syllogisms was directed at the unconditioned unity of the ***subjective*** conditions of all representations in general (of the subject or the soul) in correspondence with the ***categorical*** syllogisms of reason, whose major premise asserts as a principle the relation of a predicate to {{ 433 }} a ***subject***. The ***second kind*** of dialectical argument will therefore, in analogy with ***hypothetical*** syllogisms of reason, make the unconditioned unity of the objective conditions in appearance its content, just as the ***third kind***, which will occur in the following chapter, has as its theme the unconditioned unity of the objective conditions of the possibility of objects in general.

It is, however, remarkable that the transcendental paralogism produced a merely one-sided illusion in regard to the idea of the subject of our thinking, and that not the least illusion from concepts of reason is to be found for the assertion of the opposite. The advantage is {{{ 282 }}} entirely on the side of pneumatism, although the latter cannot deny its hereditary defect of dissolving into mere vapor in the fiery trial of critique, despite all the illusion favoring it.

The matter is quite different when we apply reason to the ***objective synthesis*** of appearances, where it indeed thinks to assert its principle of unconditioned unity with much illusion, but soon entangles itself in such contradictions that it is compelled, in cosmological regard, to desist from its demand.

For here a new phenomenon of human reason reveals itself, namely, a completely natural antithetic, which no one needs to ruminate over {{ 434 }} and artificially lay snares for, but into which reason of itself and indeed unavoidably falls, and is thereby preserved, to be sure, from the slumber of an imaginary conviction produced by a merely one-sided illusion, but at the same time is tempted either to abandon itself to a skeptical hopelessness, or to adopt a dogmatic defiance and set its head stiffly upon certain assertions, without granting a hearing and justice to the grounds of the opposite. Both are the death of a healthy philosophy, although the former could at best still be called the ***euthanasia*** of pure reason.

Before we let the scenes of discord and disruption be seen, which this conflict of laws (antinomy) of pure reason occasions, we want to provide certain explanations that can clarify and justify the method we employ in the treatment of our subject. I call all transcendental ideas, insofar as they concern absolute totality in the synthesis of appearances, ***world concepts***, partly because of this very unconditioned totality on which also the concept of the world-whole rests, which is itself only an idea, partly because they relate solely to the synthesis of appearances, and thus to the empirical one, whereas, on the other hand, the absolute totality in the synthesis of the {{ 435 }} conditions of all possible things in general will give rise to an ideal of pure reason, which is entirely distinct from the world concept, although it stands in relation to it. Hence, just as the paralogisms of pure reason laid the foundation for a dialectical psychology, so the antinomy of pure reason will place before our eyes the transcendental principles of a pretended pure (rational) cosmology, not in order to find them valid and to appropriate them, but rather, as {{{ 283 }}} the very designation of a conflict of reason indicates, in order to represent them in their dazzling but false illusion as an idea that cannot be reconciled with appearances.
