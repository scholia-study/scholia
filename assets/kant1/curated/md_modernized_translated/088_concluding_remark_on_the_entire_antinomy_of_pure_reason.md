## Concluding remark on the entire antinomy of pure reason.

So long as, by means of our concepts of reason, we have for an object nothing more than the totality of conditions within the world of sense, together with whatever may be done with respect to them in the service of reason: our ideas are, to be sure, transcendental, and yet ***cosmological***. But the moment we place the unconditioned (which is, after all, the real point at issue) in that which lies wholly beyond the world of sense, and thus beyond every possible experience, the ideas turn ***transcendent***: they do not merely subserve the completing of the empirical employment of reason (an idea that forever remains one that can never be carried through, and yet must be pursued), but detach themselves from it entirely and set up objects of their own, the material of which is not taken from experience, and the objective reality of which likewise depends, not on the bringing of the empirical series to completion, but on pure _a priori_ concepts {{{ 382 }}}. Transcendent ideas of this kind possess a merely intelligible object, one that it is certainly allowable to admit as a transcendental object about which, moreover, nothing is known, but for the thinking of which — that is, in order to think it as a thing {{ 594 }} determinable through the predicates that distinguish it and that belong to it inwardly — we possess on our part neither any grounds for its possibility (as something independent of every concept of experience) nor the least justification for assuming any such object, so that it amounts to a mere thought-entity. And yet, among all the cosmological ideas, it is precisely the one that occasioned the fourth antinomy which drives us to hazard this step. For the existence of appearances — an existence in no way grounded in itself, but on the contrary always conditioned — summons us: to cast about for something differing from all appearances, that is, for an intelligible object, in which this contingency might cease. But since, once we have allowed ourselves the liberty of positing, beyond the field of sensibility taken as a whole, an actuality subsisting on its own account, appearances can then be viewed only as contingent ways of representing intelligible objects on the part of beings that are themselves intelligences: nothing further is left to us than the analogy according to which we press the concepts of experience into service so as to fashion at least some concept of intelligible things, of which, in themselves, we have not the least acquaintance. Since the contingent becomes known to us in no other way than through experience, whereas what is at issue here are things that are meant to be no objects of experience whatsoever, we shall be obliged to draw our acquaintance with them from that which is in itself necessary, from pure concepts of things as such. The first step we {{ 595 }} take beyond the world of sense accordingly obliges us to open our new acquaintance with an inquiry into the being that is absolutely necessary, and, out of the concepts belonging to that being, to work out the concepts of all things in the measure that they are merely intelligible; and this attempt we mean to undertake in the chapter that follows.
