## I. Of Logic in General

Our cognition arises from two fundamental sources of the mind, the first of which is the capacity to receive representations (receptivity of impressions), the second the faculty of cognizing an object through these representations (spontaneity of concepts); through the former an object is ***given*** to us, through the latter it is, in relation to that representation (as a mere determination of the mind), ***thought***. Intuition and concepts therefore constitute the elements of all our cognition, so that neither concepts without an intuition corresponding to them in some way, nor intuition without concepts can yield a cognition. Both are either pure or empirical. ***Empirical***, if sensation (which presupposes the actual presence of the object) is contained therein; ***pure***, however, if no sensation is mixed with the representation. The former may be called the matter of sensible {{{ 75 }}} {{ 75 }} cognition. Therefore pure intuition contains solely the form under which something is intuited, and pure concept alone the form of thinking of an object in general. Only pure intuitions or concepts alone are possible _a priori_, empirical ones only _a posteriori_.

If we wish to call the ***receptivity*** of our mind to receive representations, insofar as it is affected in any way, ***sensibility***, then on the contrary, the faculty of producing representations ourselves, or the ***spontaneity*** of cognition, is the ***understanding***. Our nature is so constituted that ***intuition*** can never be other than ***sensible***, i.e. contains only the way in which we are affected by objects. On the contrary, the faculty of ***thinking*** the object of sensible intuition is the ***understanding***. Neither of these properties is to be preferred to the other. Without sensibility no object would be given to us, and without understanding none would be thought. Thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind. Therefore it is just as necessary to make one's concepts sensible (i.e. to attach the object to them in intuition), as to make one's intuitions understandable (i.e. to bring them under concepts). These two faculties or capacities also cannot exchange their functions. The understanding is capable of intuiting nothing, and the senses of thinking nothing. Only from their {{ 76 }} unification can cognition arise. But for this reason one must not confuse their respective contributions, but rather one has great cause carefully to separate and distinguish each from the other. Hence we distinguish the science of the rules of sensibility in general, i.e., aesthetic, from the science of the rules of the understanding in general, i.e., logic.

Logic can now, in turn, be undertaken with a twofold aim, either as the logic of the general or of the particular use of the understanding. The first contains the absolutely necessary rules of thinking, without which no use of the understanding at all takes place, and thus goes upon this regardless of the diversity of the objects to which it may be directed. The logic of the particular use of the understanding contains the rules for thinking correctly about a certain kind of objects. The former can be called elementary logic, the latter, however, the organon of this or that science. The latter is for the most part sent ahead in the schools as a propaedeutic to the sciences, although according to the course of human reason it is the latest stage which it {{{ 76 }}} reaches only when the science has long been finished and requires only the finishing touch for its correction and completion. For one must already know the objects in a fairly high degree if {{ 77 }} one wants to state the rules as to how a science of them might be brought to pass.

General logic is now either pure or applied logic. In the former we abstract from all empirical conditions under which our understanding is exercised, e.g., from the influence of the senses, the play of the imagination, the laws of memory, the power of habit, inclination, etc., hence also from the sources of prejudice, indeed even in general from all causes from which certain cognitions arise or might be foisted upon us, because they merely concern the understanding under certain circumstances of its application, and, in order to know these, experience is required. A ***general*** but ***pure*** logic has therefore to do with nothing but principles _a priori_, and is a ***canon of the understanding*** and of reason, but only in respect of the formal part of their use, be the content what it may (empirical or transcendental). But a ***general logic*** is called ***applied*** when it is directed to the rules of the use of the understanding under the subjective empirical conditions taught to us by psychology. It has therefore empirical principles, although it is general insofar as it concerns the use of the understanding without distinction of objects. For this reason it is also neither a canon {{ 78 }} of the understanding in general, nor an organon of particular sciences, but merely a cathartic of common understanding.

In general logic, therefore, the part which is to constitute the pure doctrine of reason must be entirely separated from that which constitutes applied (although still general) logic. The former alone is properly science, although short and dry, as the scholastic presentation of an elementary doctrine of the understanding requires. In this, therefore, logicians must at all times have two rules before their eyes.

1) As general logic, it abstracts from all content of the cognition of the understanding and from the diversity of its objects, and has to do with nothing but the mere form of thinking.

2) As pure logic, it has no empirical principles, hence it draws nothing (as one has sometimes persuaded oneself) from psychology, which {{{ 77 }}} thus has no influence at all on the canon of the understanding. It is a demonstrated doctrine, and everything in it must be completely certain _a priori_.

What I call applied logic (contrary to the common meaning of this word, according to which it is supposed to contain certain exercises to which pure logic gives the rule) is a representation of the understanding and of the rules of its necessary use in concreto, namely under {{ 79 }} the contingent conditions of the subject, which can hinder or promote this use, and which are one and all given only empirically. It treats of attention, its impediments and consequences, the origin of error, the state of doubt, of hesitation, of conviction, etc.; and to it general and pure logic is related as pure ethics, which contains merely the necessary moral laws of a free will in general, is related to the doctrine of virtue proper, which considers these laws under the obstacles of feelings, inclinations, and passions to which humans are more or less subject, and which can never yield a true and demonstrated science, because it, just as much as that applied logic, requires empirical and psychological principles.
