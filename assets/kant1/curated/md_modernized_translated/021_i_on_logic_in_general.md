## I. On Logic in General

Our cognition springs from two basic sources of the mind: the first is the reception of representations (the receptivity of impressions), and the second is the power to cognize an object through such representations (the spontaneity of concepts); by way of the former an object gets ***given*** to us, while by way of the latter that same object, considered in its bearing on the representation (as nothing more than a determination of the mind), comes to be ***thought***. Intuition and concepts, accordingly, make up the elements of the whole of our cognition, in such a way that concepts deprived of an intuition answering to them in some fashion, and no less intuition deprived of concepts, prove unable to furnish any cognition. Both alike are either pure or else empirical. They count as ***empirical*** whenever a sensation (one that presupposes the object's actual presence) is contained in them; as ***pure***, by contrast, whenever nothing of sensation has been mixed into the representation. This latter component one may term the matter belonging to sensible {{{ 75 }}} {{ 75 }} cognition. Pure intuition, on this account, holds nothing but the form under which a thing is intuited, and pure concept holds solely the form for thinking an object as such. Only pure intuitions or concepts are possible _a priori_, whereas empirical ones are possible merely _a posteriori_.

Suppose we choose to give the ***receptivity*** of our mind — its capacity for receiving representations in so far as it is affected in some way or other — the name ***sensibility***: then, by contrast, the power to bring forth representations of its own accord, the ***spontaneity*** of cognition, constitutes the ***understanding***. Our nature is so constituted that ***intuition*** can never be anything but ***sensible***, that is, it embraces solely the way objects come to affect us. The power to ***think*** the object of a sensible intuition, on the other hand, constitutes the ***understanding***. Neither of these two properties merits being ranked above the other. Absent sensibility, not a single object would be given us; absent understanding, not one would be thought. A thought without any content is empty; an intuition without any concept is blind. Hence it is every bit as necessary to render one's concepts sensible (that is, to attach to them their object in intuition) as it is to render one's intuitions intelligible (that is, subsuming them under concepts). These two faculties, or capacities, are likewise unable to trade their functions. The understanding is capable of intuiting nothing, the senses of thinking nothing. It is solely from this, {{ 76 }} from their uniting, that cognition can arise. Even so, one may by no means blend their respective shares on that account; on the contrary, there is strong reason to keep each carefully apart from the other and to tell them apart. And so we mark off the science that furnishes the rules of sensibility as such — that is, aesthetics — from the one that furnishes the rules of the understanding as such, namely logic.

Now logic, in its turn, admits of being pursued with two different aims: either as a logic of the general employment of the understanding or as one of its particular employment. The first sort holds those rules of thinking that are unconditionally necessary, rules apart from which the understanding gets no employment whatever, and it thus addresses the understanding without any regard to how the objects it may be trained upon differ among themselves. The logic of the particular employment of the understanding holds the rules for thinking rightly about a determinate kind of object. The first of these may be termed elementary logic, whereas the second is the organon of one science or another. In the schools it is usually placed first, serving as a propaedeutic for the sciences, even though, in the order that human reason actually travels, it is the very last thing reason {{{ 76 }}} attains, reached only once a science stands long completed and wants no more than a finishing touch toward its correction and its perfection. For one has to be acquainted with the objects already to a rather high degree if {{ 77 }} one means to lay down the rules by which a science concerning them might be established.

General logic, for its part, is either pure or applied. Within the first of these we leave out of account every empirical condition under which our understanding is brought into play — for example, the influence of the senses, the play of the imagination, the laws of memory, the force of habit, inclination, and so on — and along with them the sources of our prejudices, indeed, quite generally, all those causes out of which certain cognitions may arise in us or come to be foisted on us, for they touch the understanding only under particular circumstances attending its application, circumstances that can be known only by way of experience. A logic that is both ***general*** and ***pure*** thus concerns itself exclusively with principles that are _a priori_, and it amounts to a ***canon of the understanding*** and, no less, of reason, though only as regards the formal side of their employment, no matter what the content may happen to be (whether empirical or transcendental). A ***general logic***, by contrast, comes to be called ***applied*** once it is aimed at the rules for employing the understanding under conditions of a subjective, empirical kind — the sort that psychology instructs us in. It therefore rests on empirical principles, even though it counts as general in so far as it bears on the employment of the understanding irrespective of any difference among objects. That is likewise why it constitutes no canon {{ 78 }} of the understanding at large, nor any organon for the particular sciences, but merely a cathartic serving the common understanding.

Within general logic, then, the portion meant to make up reason's pure doctrine has to be set wholly apart from the portion that makes up the applied (yet still general) logic. This first portion is, strictly speaking, the only genuine science here, however terse and dry it may be — as terse and dry as the scholastically correct exposition of an elementary theory of the understanding demands. In dealing with this first portion, accordingly, logicians must at every moment keep two rules in view.

1) Being general logic, it leaves aside all the content of the understanding's cognition together with the differences among its objects, and it is occupied with nothing beyond the sheer form of thinking.

2) Being pure logic, it possesses no empirical principles at all, so that (contrary to what people have now and then talked themselves into) it borrows nothing from psychology, which {{{ 77 }}} accordingly exerts no influence of any kind upon the canon of the understanding. It is a demonstrated body of doctrine, and everything within it has to be wholly certain _a priori_.

What I, for my part, call applied logic (against the ordinary sense of the term, on which it would take in certain exercises for which pure logic supplies the rule) is a representation of the understanding and of the rules for its necessary employment in concreto — that is, under {{ 79 }} the accidental conditions belonging to the subject, conditions capable of impeding or of aiding that employment, and given, one and all, only empirically. It treats of attention, of what obstructs it and what follows from it, of the origin of error, of the condition of doubt, of misgiving, of conviction, and the like; and to it general and pure logic stands related just as pure morality — which holds nothing but the necessary moral laws of a free will as such — stands related to the doctrine of virtue proper, a doctrine that weighs those laws against the resistance of the feelings, the inclinations, and the passions, to which human beings find themselves subject to a greater or lesser degree, and that is never able to furnish a genuine and demonstrated science, since it, exactly like that applied logic, stands in need of empirical and psychological principles.
