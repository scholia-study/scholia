## Third Analogy. Principle of coexistence in accordance with the law of reciprocity or community

***All substances, to the extent that they can be perceived as coexisting in space, stand in thoroughgoing reciprocity.***

## Proof.

Two things are ***simultaneous*** if, in empirical intuition, perceiving the one can, upon the {{ 257 }} perception of the other, follow ***reciprocally*** {{{ 181 }}} (which, within the temporal succession of appearances, is impossible, as the second principle already established). Accordingly I may cast my perception first upon the moon and afterwards upon the earth, or in reverse order first upon the earth and then upon the moon, and it is just because the perceptions of these objects admit of following one another in turn that I declare them to coexist. Coexistence, now, consists in the manifold's existing within one and the same time. Time itself, however, cannot be perceived, such that from things being posited in the same time one might gather that the perceptions of them can succeed one another in turn. Hence the imagination's synthesis in apprehension would exhibit each of these perceptions merely as one present in the subject while the other is absent, and conversely, yet not as showing that the objects coexist—that is, that if the one is there the other too is there within the same time, and that this is necessary if the perceptions are to be capable of succeeding one another in turn. A concept of the understanding concerning the mutual sequence of the determinations of these things, coexisting outside one another, is therefore demanded, so that one may assert the mutual sequence of perceptions to be grounded in the object, and so represent the coexistence as objective. But the relation of substances in which the one harbors determinations {{ 258 }} whose ground lies in the other is the relation of influence, and where each in turn harbors the ground of the other's determinations, it is the relation of community, or reciprocity. The coexistence of substances in space, then, admits of being cognized in experience only on the supposition of a reciprocity obtaining among them; and this reciprocity is accordingly likewise the condition under which the things themselves are possible as objects of experience.

Things coexist to the extent that they exist within one and the same time. But by what does one recognize this: that they lie in one and the same time? By the order in the synthesis by which this manifold is apprehended remaining indifferent, that is, by its being able to run from A through B, C, D to E, or in reverse from E to A as well. For were it successive in time (in the order commencing at A and terminating at E), it would be impossible to set the apprehension going from the perception of E and to travel back to A, since A belongs to elapsed time and can therefore no longer be an object of apprehension.

Now suppose that, within a manifold of substances as appearances, {{{ 182 }}} each were wholly isolated—that is, none acted upon another or received influences from it in turn: then I maintain that their ***coexistence*** would form no object of any possible perception, {{ 259 }} and that the existence of the one could, by no route of empirical synthesis, lead over to the existence of the other. For should you conceive them as sundered by a wholly empty space, the perception advancing from the one to the other in time would indeed fix the latter's existence through a succeeding perception, yet could not settle whether the appearance objectively succeeds the first or is instead together with it.

Beyond bare existence, then, there must be something more, by which A assigns to B its position in time and, reciprocally, B again to A, since only on this condition can substances so conceived be represented in experience as ***coexisting***. Now that alone assigns to another its position in time which is the cause of it, or of its determinations. Every substance, accordingly (given that only in respect of its determinations can it be an effect), must contain within it the causality of certain determinations in the other and, at once, the effects of the other's causality—that is, they must stand in dynamical community (whether directly or through intermediaries), if coexistence is ever to be cognized in some possible experience. But everything is necessary in respect of the objects of experience without which {{ 260 }} experience of those very objects would be impossible. It is therefore necessary that all substances in the field of appearance, to the extent they coexist, stand in thoroughgoing community of reciprocity with one another.

In our language the word community is equivocal and may signify as much _communio_ as _commercium_. Here we take it in the latter acceptation, as a dynamical community, apart from which even local community (_communio spatii_) could never be cognized empirically. Our experiences readily show that only continuous influences, present at every point of space, are able to guide our sense from one object across to another; that the light which plays between our eye and the heavenly bodies sets up a mediate community between us and those bodies, and so demonstrates their coexistence; that we can alter no place empirically (that is, perceive such alteration) unless matter on every side renders possible for us the {{{ 183 }}} perception of our own position, and that this position, solely through its reciprocal influence, can exhibit its coexistence and, reaching thereby to the remotest objects, their coexistence as well (though only mediately). Without community every perception (of appearance in space) stands cut off from the rest, and the chain of empirical representations—that is, experience—would, upon a new object, commence wholly afresh {{ 261 }}, without the previous chain being in the slightest connected with it or standing in any relation of time. I have no wish hereby to refute empty space at all: it may well subsist wherever perceptions simply fail to reach, so that no empirical cognition of coexistence occurs there; but in that case it is, for all our possible experience, no object whatever.

The following may serve by way of clarification. Within our mind all appearances, as belonging to one possible experience, must stand in a community (_communio_) of apperception; and to the extent the objects are to be represented as bound together in coexisting, they must reciprocally fix their position in one time and thereby make up a whole. Should this subjective community rest upon an objective ground, or be referred to appearances as substances, then the perception of the one must, as ground, render the perception of the other possible, and vice versa, in order that the succession forever present in perceptions qua apprehensions not be imputed to the objects, but that these instead admit of being represented as coexisting. This, however, is a reciprocal influence—that is, a real community (_commercium_) of substances—apart from which, accordingly, the empirical relation of coexistence could not occur in experience. By means of this {{ 262 }} commercium the appearances, in so far as they lie outside one another yet in interconnection, make up a composite (_compositum reale_), and composites of such a kind become possible in manifold ways. The three dynamical relations, out of which all the others spring, are thus that of inherence, that of consequence, and that of composition.

***

Such, then, are the three analogies of appearances. They are nothing more than principles for fixing the existence of appearances in time in keeping with all three of its modes: their relation to {{{ 184 }}} time itself, viewed as a magnitude (the magnitude of a thing's existence, namely its duration); their relation within time as a series (successively); and finally their relation within it as the sum of all existence (togetherness). This unity of time-determination is dynamical through and through; time, that is, is not looked upon as that wherein experience would directly assign to each existence its place—an impossibility, given that absolute time is nothing perceivable by which appearances could be held together—but rather the rule of the understanding, whereby alone the existence of appearances can gain synthetic unity in respect of temporal relations, assigns to each of them its place in time, and does so _a priori_ and with validity for any and every time.

By nature (understood empirically) we mean the interconnection of appearances, as regards their existence, under necessary rules—that is, under laws. {{ 263 }} There are, then, certain laws—and indeed _a priori_ ones—that first render a nature possible at all; the empirical laws can obtain and be discovered only through experience, and this in conformity with those original laws by which experience itself first grows possible. Our analogies, then, properly present the unity of nature in the interconnection of all appearances under certain exponents, which give voice to nothing but the relation of time (in so far as it embraces all existence within itself) to the unity of apperception—a unity realizable solely in synthesis conducted by rules. Together, therefore, they declare: all appearances lie within a single nature and must so lie, for without this _a priori_ unity there could be no unity of experience, and hence no determination of the objects contained in it.

Yet concerning the mode of proof we have employed with these transcendental laws of nature, together with its distinctive character, a remark must be made that is at the same time of great weight as a precept for any other attempt to demonstrate propositions that are intellectual and at once synthetic _a priori_. Had we set out to prove these analogies dogmatically, that is, from concepts {{ 264 }}—namely, that whatever exists is met with solely in what is enduring; that every event presupposes something in the foregoing state upon which it follows by a rule; and finally that, in the manifold which is together, the states stand in mutual relation and are together by a rule (stand in community)—every exertion would have been utterly futile. For from a given object and its existence one simply cannot reach the existence of another, or its manner of {{{ 185 }}} existing, through bare concepts of the things in question, however one may choose to dissect them. What, then, was left to us? The possibility of experience, as a cognition wherein all objects must in the end be capable of being given to us, if their representation is to possess objective reality for us. Within this third element, whose essential form lies in the synthetic unity of apperception across all appearances, we came upon conditions _a priori_ for the thoroughgoing and necessary determination in time of everything existing in appearance—conditions without which even empirical time-determination would be impossible—and upon rules of synthetic unity _a priori_ by which we were able to anticipate experience. For lack of this method, and beguiled by the notion of proving dogmatically synthetic propositions that the understanding's empirical use commends as its principles, it has repeatedly come to pass that the principle of sufficient reason has had a proof attempted for it, always in vain. {{ 265 }} To the remaining two analogies no one gave a thought, however constantly they were drawn upon in silence,[^*] because the guiding thread of the categories was absent—the thread which alone can uncover and make conspicuous every gap in the understanding, whether in concepts or in principles.

[^*]: The unity of the world-whole, wherein all appearances are meant to be linked, is plainly a mere corollary of the covertly presupposed principle of the community of all substances that exist together: for were they isolated, they would not, as parts, compose a whole; and were their linkage (the reciprocity of the manifold) not already necessary on account of their coexistence, then from the latter, taken as a merely ideal relation, there would be no inferring to the former as something real. Although we have shown in the appropriate place: that community is, properly, what makes an empirical cognition of coexistence possible at all, and that one therefore, properly speaking, infers back only from the latter to the former as its condition.
