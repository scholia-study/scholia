## Third Analogy. Principle of coexistence in accordance with the law of reciprocity or community

***All substances, insofar as they can be perceived in space as simultaneous, are in thoroughgoing reciprocity.***

## Proof.

***Simultaneous*** are things when in empirical intuition the perception of the one can ***reciprocally*** {{ 257 }} follow {{{ 181 }}} upon the perception of the other (which, as has been shown in the second principle, cannot happen in the temporal succession of appearances). So I can direct my perception first to the moon and afterwards to the earth, or conversely first to the earth and then to the moon, and for this reason, because the perceptions of these objects can reciprocally follow one another, I say they exist simultaneously. Now coexistence is the existence of the manifold in the same time. But one cannot perceive time itself in order to infer from the fact that things are posited in the same time that their perceptions can follow one another reciprocally. The synthesis of the imagination in apprehension would therefore only indicate each of these perceptions as one that is present in the subject when the other is not, and vice versa, but not that the objects are coexistent, i.e., that if the one is, the other is also in the same time, and that this is necessary in order that the perceptions can reciprocally follow upon one another. Consequently, a concept of the understanding of the reciprocal succession of the determinations of these things existing simultaneously outside one another is required, in order to say that the reciprocal succession of the perceptions is grounded in the object, and thereby to represent coexistence as objective. Now, however, the relation of substances in which the one contains determinations {{ 258 }} the ground of which is contained in the other is the relation of influence, and, if reciprocally this contains the ground of the determinations in the other, it is the relation of community or reciprocity. Thus the coexistence of substances in space cannot be cognized in experience otherwise than under the presupposition of a reciprocity among them; this is therefore also the condition of the possibility of the things themselves as objects of experience.

Things are simultaneous insofar as they exist in one and the same time. But by what does one cognize that they are in one and the same time? When the order in the synthesis of the apprehension of this manifold is indifferent, i.e., can go from A through B, C, D to E, or also conversely from E to A itself. For if it were successive in time (in the order that begins with A and ends in E), then it is impossible to begin the apprehension in the perception of E and to proceed backward to A, because A belongs to past time and can therefore no longer be an object of apprehension.

Suppose now that, in a manifold of substances as appearances, {{{ 182 }}} each of them were completely isolated, i.e., none acted on another nor received reciprocal influences from it, then I say: that their ***coexistence*** would be no object of a possible perception, {{ 259 }} and that the existence of the one could by no path of empirical synthesis lead to the existence of the other. For if you think of them as separated by a completely empty space, then the perception that proceeds from the one to the other in time would indeed determine the existence of the latter by means of a succeeding perception, but would not be able to distinguish whether the appearance objectively follows upon the former, or is rather simultaneous with it.

There must therefore be something besides mere existence by which A determines for B its place in time, and conversely also B for A, because only under this condition can the substances thought of be represented empirically as ***existing simultaneously***. Now only that determines for another its place in time which is the cause of it or of its determinations. Consequently, every substance (since it can be a consequence only in respect of its determinations) must contain in itself the causality of certain determinations in the other, and at the same time the effects of the causality of the other, i.e., they must stand in dynamical community (immediately or mediately) if coexistence is to be cognized in any possible experience. Now, however, everything in respect of the objects of experience is necessary, without which {{ 260 }} the experience of these objects themselves would be impossible. Thus it is necessary for all substances in appearance, insofar as they are simultaneous, to stand in thoroughgoing community of reciprocity with one another.

The word community is ambiguous in our language, and can signify as much as _communio_, but also as _commercium_. We employ it here in the latter sense, as a dynamical community, without which even local community (_communio spatii_) could never be empirically cognized. It is easy to notice from our experiences that only the continuous influences in all places of space can guide our sense from one object to another, that the light which plays between our eye and the celestial bodies effects a mediate community between us and them and thereby proves the coexistence of the latter, that we cannot empirically change any place (perceive this change) without matter everywhere making the {{{ 183 }}} perception of our position possible, and that this matter, only by means of its reciprocal influence, can demonstrate their simultaneity and thereby, even to the most remote objects, their coexistence (although only mediately). Without community, every perception (of the appearance in space) is broken off from the other, and the chain of empirical representations, i.e., experience, would with a new object begin entirely from the beginning {{ 261 }} without the previous one being able to hang together with it in the least or to stand in a temporal relation. I do not by any means intend to refute empty space by this: for it may well exist where perceptions do not reach at all, and where therefore no empirical cognition of coexistence takes place; but in that case it is no object at all for all our possible experience.

The following can serve as illustration. In our mind, all appearances, as contained in a possible experience, must stand in a community (_communio_) of apperception; and insofar as objects are to be represented as connected as existing simultaneously, they must reciprocally determine their position in one time and thereby constitute a whole. If this subjective community is to rest on an objective ground, or be referred to appearances as substances, then the perception of the one must, as ground, make possible the perception of the other, and so conversely, in order that the succession which is always in perceptions as apprehensions may not be attributed to the objects, but that these can be represented as existing simultaneously. This is, however, a reciprocal influence, i.e., a real community (_commercium_) of substances, without which, therefore, the empirical relation of coexistence could not take place in experience. Through this {{ 262 }} _commercium_, appearances, insofar as they are outside of one another and yet stand in connection, constitute a composite (_compositum reale_), and composites of this kind are possible in many ways. The three dynamical relations from which all others arise are therefore those of inherence, of consequence, and of composition.

***

These, then, are the three analogies of appearances. They are nothing other than principles of the determination of the existence of appearances in time according to all three of its modes: the relation to {{{ 184 }}} time itself as a magnitude (the magnitude of existence, i.e., duration), the relation in time as a series (one after another), and finally also in it as a sum total of all existence (simultaneous). This unity of time-determination is thoroughly dynamical, i.e., time is not regarded as that in which experience would immediately determine its place for every existence, which is impossible, because absolute time is no object of perception by which appearances could be held together; but the rule of the understanding, through which alone the existence of appearances can obtain synthetic unity according to relations of time, determines for each of them its place in time, consequently _a priori_ and validly for each and every time.

By nature (in the empirical sense) we understand the connection of appearances as regards their existence according to necessary rules, i.e., according to laws. There are therefore certain laws, and indeed _a priori_, which first of all make a nature possible; the empirical ones can take place and be found only by means of experience, and indeed in consequence of those original laws, in accordance with which experience itself first of all becomes possible. Our analogies therefore really represent the unity of nature in the connection of all appearances under certain exponents, which express nothing other than the relation of time (insofar as it contains all existence in itself) to the unity of apperception, which can only take place in synthesis according to rules. Together they say: all appearances lie in one nature, and must lie therein, because without this _a priori_ unity no unity of experience, and consequently no determination of objects in it, would be possible.

An observation, however, must be made concerning the mode of proof which we have employed for these transcendental laws of nature, and their peculiarity, which must at the same time be very important as a prescription for every other attempt to prove intellectual and at the same time synthetic propositions _a priori_. Had we wished to prove these analogies dogmatically, i.e., from concepts: {{ 264 }} namely, that everything which exists is to be found only in that which is permanent, that every event presupposes something in the previous state upon which it follows according to a rule, and finally, that in the manifold which is simultaneous, the states in relation to one another are simultaneous according to a rule (stand in community), then all effort would have been entirely in vain. For one cannot pass from an object and its existence to the existence of another or its mode of {{{ 185 }}} existence through mere concepts of these things at all, however much one may analyze them. What, then, remained to us? The possibility of experience as a cognition in which all objects must at last be capable of being given to us if their representation is to have objective reality for us. In this third, now, the essential form of which consists in the synthetic unity of the apperception of all appearances, we found _a priori_ conditions of the thoroughgoing and necessary time-determination of all existence in appearance, without which even empirical time-determination would be impossible, and we found rules of synthetic unity _a priori_ by means of which we could anticipate experience. For want of this method, and under the delusion of wishing to prove dogmatically synthetic propositions which the empirical use of the understanding recommends as its principles, it has then come to pass that a proof of the principle of sufficient reason has so often, but always in vain, been attempted. {{ 265 }} No one has thought of the other two analogies, although one indeed always made use of them silently,[^*] because the guide of the categories was lacking, which alone can discover and make noticeable every gap of the understanding both in concepts and in principles.

[^*]: The unity of the world-whole, in which all appearances are to be connected, is obviously a mere consequence of the tacitly assumed principle of the community of all substances that are simultaneous: for if they were isolated, they would not as parts constitute a whole, and if their connection (reciprocity of the manifold) were not already necessary for the sake of coexistence, one could not infer from the latter, as a merely ideal relation, to the former as a real one. Although we have shown in its proper place: that community is actually the ground of the possibility of an empirical cognition of coexistence, and that one therefore actually only infers from the latter to the former as its condition.
