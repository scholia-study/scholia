## {{ 556 }} Concluding remark on the solution of the mathematical-transcendental ideas, and preliminary remark on the solution of the dynamical-transcendental ideas.

When we presented the antinomy of pure reason across all the transcendental ideas in a single table, and there pointed out the ground of this conflict along with the one means of removing it—which lay in the fact that both of the mutually opposed claims were pronounced false: we everywhere represented the conditions as belonging to their conditioned in keeping with relations of space and time, this being the usual assumption of ordinary human understanding, upon which, indeed, that conflict rested in its entirety. In this respect, too, every dialectical representation of totality within the series of conditions belonging to a given conditioned was, through and through, of ***the same kind***. It was always one series, in which the condition, together with the conditioned, was joined as a member of that same series and thereby rendered ***homogeneous***, so that the regress could never be thought as completed—or else, were this to occur, a member that is in itself conditioned would falsely have to be assumed as a first, and hence as unconditioned. Thus, while it was not everywhere the object, i.e., the conditioned, {{ 557 }} it was nevertheless the series of conditions leading up to it {{{ 361 }}} that came under consideration merely with respect to its magnitude, and here lay the difficulty, a difficulty that could be removed by no settlement but solely by severing the knot altogether, namely that reason rendered it, for the understanding, either ***too long*** or ***too short***, with the result that the understanding could never come up to reason's idea.

Here, however, we have overlooked an essential difference prevailing among the objects—that is, among those concepts of the understanding that reason seeks to elevate into ideas—inasmuch as, by our table of the categories set out above, two of these signify a ***mathematical*** synthesis of the appearances, while the other two signify a ***dynamical*** one. Up to this point this could well pass without notice, since, just as in the general presentation of all the transcendental ideas we always remained under conditions ***within appearance***, so too in the two mathematical-transcendental ones we had no other ***object*** than one within appearance. Now, though, in advancing to those ***dynamical*** concepts of the understanding that are to be adapted to the idea of reason, that distinction takes on weight and discloses to us a wholly new outlook upon the litigation in which reason finds itself entangled; and this litigation, which previously, as built upon false presuppositions on both sides, had {{ 558 }} been ***dismissed***, can now—since perhaps in the dynamical antinomy there occurs a presupposition of the kind that can subsist together with reason's pretension, and since the judge makes good the deficiency of the legal grounds that both parties had failed to recognize—be ***settled*** to the satisfaction of both sides from this standpoint, which is something that could not be done in the dispute of the mathematical antinomy.

To be sure, the series of conditions are all homogeneous insofar as one looks merely to their ***extent***: whether they are adequate to the idea, or whether they are too large or too small for it. But the concept of the understanding that underlies these ideas contains either merely a ***synthesis of the homogeneous*** (something that is presupposed in every magnitude, alike in its composition and in its division), or else one ***of the heterogeneous***, which can at least be admitted in the dynamical synthesis both of the causal connection and of the necessary with the contingent.

This is how it comes about that into the mathematical linkage of the series of appearances there can enter no condition other than a ***sensible*** one—that is, such as itself forms part of the series; whereas the {{{ 362 }}} dynamical series of sensible conditions does, for all that, admit a heterogeneous condition, one that forms no part of the series but, as merely ***intelligible***, {{ 559 }} lies outside it, whereby reason is given satisfaction and the unconditioned is placed above the appearances, without on that account throwing the series of the latter—conditioned as it always is—into disorder, or breaking it off in a manner contrary to the principles of the understanding.

Now, in that the dynamical ideas permit a condition of the appearances outside the series of those appearances—that is, a condition which is not itself an appearance—there comes about something wholly distinct from the upshot of the mathematical antinomy. For the latter brought it about that both dialectical counter-assertions had to be declared false. By contrast, the thoroughly conditioned character of the dynamical series, which is inseparable from them as appearances, when linked with the condition that is indeed empirically unconditioned but also ***non-sensible***, can satisfy the ***understanding*** on the one side and ***reason*** on the other,[^*] and, since the dialectical arguments that sought unconditioned totality in mere appearances in one way or another thereby fall away, the propositions of reason, on the other hand, taken in the sense thus {{ 560 }} corrected, ***can both be true***; something that can never occur in the case of the cosmological ideas, which concern merely mathematically unconditioned unity, because in their case no condition of the series of appearances is met with except one that is itself also an appearance and, as such, constitutes yet another member of the series.

[^*]: For among ***appearances*** the understanding permits no condition that would itself be empirically unconditioned. But if an ***intelligible*** condition, one that hence did not belong within the series of appearances as a member, could be thought for a conditioned (within appearance) without thereby interrupting the series of empirical conditions in the slightest: then such a one might be granted the status of ***empirically unconditioned***, so that thereby no interruption would befall the empirical, continuous regress at any point.
